With 170mm of travel in the front, 160mm in the rear, and Fazua's Ride 60 motor system, the Decoy SN – or Supernatural – is expanding YT's Decoy family.

The "MX" in the model name reveals that the Decoy SN runs on a mixed wheel setup, with a 29" front and 27.5" rear, without the option for changing the rear wheel to another size. Looking at YT's current lineup, the Decoy SN is aimed at the enduro category and therefore comes closest to the character of the non-motorized Capra, although in terms of geometry and features, the Decoy SN is clearly an evolution of that model.

Form language is an important part of the package, and YT's Decoy SN is not only nice to look at but does an excellent job of concealing that it's an e-bike. From the drive side, you have to look twice to spot any sign of the Fazua Ride 60 motor. The motor’s positioning, especially the way it extends into the downtube, is a smart solution and has been cleanly integrated with YT's skid plate seamlessly connecting to the down tube bash guard.

Frame & Motor Details

Made from ultra high modulus carbon fibers, the frame alone, without the motor, battery, and shock, weighs 3,038g in size L. The Decoy SN's frame is tested to ASTM Category 4 (enduro) standards.

The cable routing enters the frame through the sides of the head tube, using co-molded inner tube bonding inside for easy cable installation. The lower two entry ports are directly connected to the carbon-molded guides, but since the top ports are open in the head tube area, you can easily swap the rear brake cable from the left to the right-hand side if required.

There's no cover, but due to its alignment and spacing I never had issues with little rocks getting caught between the chainstay bridge and the main frame.


The bike comes with a YT bottle cage that holds a full-size water bottle, though the maximum capacity varies with different frame sizes. On the size L, I was able to fit a 750ml bottle, which came close to the shock but caused no issues. Two extra mounts on the underside of the top tube offer the option to mount accessories. The Crankbrothers TS2 Tube Stash + kit, which holds a spare tube and tire lever with valve tools, is included with the Core 4 spec and can be purchased aftermarket for other models.

YT says they have no issues with shock durability and their shock yoke due to implementing a short enough version (60mm) to prevent putting massive extra stress on the absorber. In fact, even Fox, who doesn’t allow their coil shock to be used on certain bike models with long yokes, gave them a thumbs up on their design.

In addition to the soft chainstay and seatstay protectors, the bike comes with a clear protective film on its sides – a nice detail. There's also a clear protective film in the midsection of the downtube area, above the thicker frame protection in the lower section.

The YT Decoy SN relies on Fazua's Ride 60 system, which delivers a maximum torque of 60Nm and is coupled with an integrated 430Wh battery. The motor weighs 1,960g, and the 430Wh integrated battery weighs 2,200g. The charge port is located in the lower seat tube area and is protected by a sturdy magnetic cover that doesn’t rattle.

Geometry & Sizing


Five sizes are available, ranging from S to XXL. The short seat tube across the range allows riders to select a size based on their riding style rather than being limited by height. Seat tube lengths start at a short 390mm for size S, with size XXL measuring 460mm. Add an extra 5mm to those measurements due to the seat collar.

The seatpost insertion depths are as follows: S: 285mm, M: 290mm, L: 310mm, XL: 325mm, and XXL: 340mm.

A change of 0.3 degrees of the head tube angle and 0.2 to 0.3 degrees (depending on the frame size) of the seat tube angle is minimal. The most noticeable change is the 4mm drop in bottom bracket height. This is especially important because, with the Continental 2.4" tires used, the actual measured bottom bracket height is 339mm in the regular setting and 335mm in the low setting. This makes it 5mm lower than the numbers suggest on paper.

Additionally, the head angle measures slightly slacker than indicated in the charts.




Suspension Design

The V4L system is based on a four-bar linkage layout with a Horst link and a shock yoke.

V4L suspension layout with a Horst link and yoke.

The leverage curve starts at about 3.03, gradually decreasing to 2.16 without any erratic changes throughout the travel. This results in a 28.7% progression overall. The initial 3:1 ratio at the start of the stroke improves suppleness, while the higher support in the mid-stroke slightly tapers off towards the end of the travel. The average leverage ratio is 2.46:1.

Leverage ratio

Anti-squat remains above 100% within the sag window for all gear combinations. At 30% sag – the recommended value – the range varies from 110% (34/10 gearing) to 133% (34/51 gearing). In theory, these values should extend the suspension rather than compress it when pedaling.

Anti-rise
Anti-squat

Anti-rise stays below 100% at all points, starting around 78% at the beginning of travel and nearing 60% at full travel. This lower level means braking has relatively little impact on suspension performance, keeping it active. The narrow range helps the bike remain predictable under braking.

YT states that their setup caters well to both coil and air shocks, which is reflected in their choice of components across different spec levels.

Test Bike Setup

I fall into YT's recommendation for a size M frame. However, with the short seat tube on the Decoy SN, I was able to go for a size L, allowing me to ride my preferred reach of 475mm without compromise.

YT recommends setting the sag for the Decoy SN at 30% while seated. In my case, that required a 300 lbs spring, which worked perfectly. I was close to the recommended damping settings, with one click slower on the high speed rebound (HSR 6) and slightly faster on the low speed rebound (LSR 15). I ran the low speed and high speed compression almost fully open (LSC 15, HSC 7), staying within the window of recommended settings.

I set up the Fox 38 with Grip X2 damping at 20% sag by the end, down from 15%, which Fox considers its plush setting. I used two volume spacers during most of the rides but removed one toward the end of the testing period to experiment further with the settings. While the air pressure was slightly lower than chart recommendations to achieve the 20% sag, the damping values were close to the guidelines, though I ran the high speed rebound slightly slower – LSR 14, HSR 6, LSC 18, HSC 8.

YT spec'd the Decoy SN with a 50mm stem, feeling it offered a good balance with the 44mm fork offset. To each their own, and it's a quick and inexpensive fix, but that opinion doesn’t align with my personal preference. To me, longer stems come from a road bike context, and the longer the stem, the more the ends of the bars are offset from the steer tube axis when turning, which ultimately impacts balance. While a 50mm stem isn’t exactly long, I’m more accustomed to the direct and predictable steering that a shorter stem offers. After some back and forth, I stuck with a 32mm stem for most of the testing period.

Although there’s nothing inherently wrong with YT’s provided bottle cage, I swapped it for a Fidlock system, as I’m used to grabbing my bottle with my right hand and find less fiddling involved when removing and replacing the bottle. I also switched out the SDG saddle for an SQlab model after spending more hours than necessary in discomfort.


Testing Info

After its initial launch on bike park tracks in Saalbach and Leogang, Austria, most of the testing took place in the sunny and dry conditions of Finale Ligure, Italy, with some additional time spent on my home trails around Vienna, Austria.




Climbing

YT refers to the Fazua system as a mid-support solution rather than calling their bike a light e-bike, and I have to agree with their assessment—at least when comparing it to something like the TQ HPR50 or Specialized SL motor. The power that the Ride 60 motor delivers feels like real support, more than just a gentle push from an invisible helping hand, especially in its highest setting. That’s not to say it matches the power of a full-power e-bike, but if you’re a reasonably fit rider, you can keep up with your buddies on full-power motors—unless they’re deliberately trying to drop you.

A major advantage of Fazua’s Ride 60 is its low noise emissions. There’s no annoying whine, and even pushing the motor doesn’t significantly increase the noise level. After several hundred kilometers of riding, the motor's sound hasn’t changed, which is something I’ve noticed happening with motors from other manufacturers.

The Decoy SN’s steep seat tube angle puts you in an efficient pedaling position, which is also advantageous on steeper inclines. However, I couldn’t resist pushing the saddle forward on its rail, as I prefer the benefits of a really steep seat tube.

Ride Modes / Motor Performance

I’m not a fan of the names for the ride modes, so I’ll refer to Breeze as Low, River as Medium, and Rocket as High mode. I suppose I can live with the name of the Boost function, which delivers a maximum of 450 watts for 12 seconds (or until you stop pedaling).

The system shuts down fairly quickly when taking a break. While it's not a huge issue, it does become tedious to go through the startup procedure more than once during a ride. The startup process takes a few seconds, but after a five-minute break, it takes longer, and the bike starts in 'No Support' mode. For breaks shorter than five minutes, it starts back up in its lowest riding mode. An upcoming software update will make it start in the lowest riding mode all the time.

It would be nice to have the option via the app to choose which support mode the bike starts in or adjust the shutdown time, but Fazua currently has no plans to implement this feature.

I like the general concept of the ring controller, but Fazua could have made it sturdier. The plastic components appear a bit flimsy and the actuation of the ride modes could be more defined. On a few occasions, it didn’t register an input on the first try.

Like any other motor, you can find reports of failures, but throughout the testing period, covering nearly 500 kilometers and encountering some extremely hot days (over 40°C) with the sun beating down on long climbs, I didn’t encounter any issues with the Ride 60 motor or battery, including heat management. I don’t know how the motor handles prolonged wet conditions but it's sealed according to IP54 standards.

If you ever run into problems with the motor, since YT is a direct-to-consumer brand, your first point of contact would be YT’s Customer Care section on their website. From there, the team will assist you. YT customers also have access to Fazua’s worldwide Premium Dealer network, which YT’s customer service can facilitate.


The Fazua Ride 60 is one of the less power-hungry systems on the market, making its range with a relatively small 430Wh battery quite impressive. A 90kg rider should be able to cover around 1,200 meters of elevation in its highest ride mode. With a balanced mix of the different ride modes, it’s possible to reach over 1,500 meters of elevation.

Once Fazua's range extender battery is released – now delayed until 2025 – you'll be able to enjoy even longer days in the saddle. The Decoy SN is already prepped to work with the range extender.
While YT saves weight with the non-removable battery, it’s something to consider if you can't charge the bike where it's stored, or during road trips where the charging situation is unknown. Obviously, swapping out a battery during a ride loop isn’t an option either.

On the majority of climbs, even those with technical sections full of loose rocks, I had no major issues with the system getting me to the top, as long as I was using the correct gear. The bike doesn’t perform as well if you're powering into an uphill section in too high of a gear, something a full-power motor might be able to overcome.

Thanks to the high sensitivity of the suspension and the coil shock – possibly due to the kinematic setup – you’ll notice a fair amount of bob from the suspension when rolling on asphalt or smooth fire roads. Adding more low-speed compression helps, but I’m not a fan of the ride quality when overdoing it, so I regularly used the platform lever on the shock for extended climbs in these conditions. However, on trails or rougher fire roads, I never felt the need to tame the suspension. I loved how the rear wheel’s grip let me plow over obstacles without disrupting my pedal stroke.

As expected, the Fazua Ride 60 system isn’t ideal for tackling highly technical uphill sections. While Boost mode is helpful in short segments, it doesn’t do much for extended technical climbs. In fact, when the Boost cuts out after twelve seconds in the middle of a critical obstacle – which happened to me a few times, as I enjoy tackling uphill challenges beyond simple fire road climbs – it can be frustrating. Additionally, the two-second delay before Boost activates can be tricky to time perfectly. On unknown tracks, you might not even have time to initiate it when you need it. Fortunately, Fazua has announced a new controller for 2025, which will allow Boost mode to be activated via an extra button – a welcome improvement.

Since the Ride 60 system lacks an overrun feature, it’s also more challenging to clear tricky rock slabs without risking a pedal strike, especially with the Decoy SN’s low bottom bracket, which is even lower than the listed specifications.

With relatively short chainstays, the Decoy SN isn’t a class leader in keeping the front end glued to the ground on steep climbs, but this doesn’t mean it’s a poor climber by any means. In fact, I was surprised by the types of trails I could ascend with the Ride 60’s support, even though it required more effort and skill than a full-power motor and sometimes turned extreme uphill challenges into a hit-and-miss struggle.

When dismounting becomes necessary, the Ride 60’s walk function could use significant improvement. Its slow initiation and execution led me to abandon it after a few tries, opting to push or carry the bike myself instead.

As with many e-bikes that feature only five bars to indicate battery charge, I find Fazua’s LED Hub to be an annoyingly inaccurate source of information. When there’s only one bar left, it’s unclear if you have 20% or 2% charge remaining, making it a guessing game whether you’ll make it home if you add in that little extra loop or not.

Reading YT's slogan to "ditch the display and free yourself from distractions," I don’t really get what they’re trying to say. I never considered knowing how much battery I have left a distraction, apart from the fact that if I want to know the exact charge I have to pull out my cell phone to look up Fazua's Rider app that can show the exact value. Now that's a real distraction. You could also opt for displaying the info on a Garmin device or similar, but that would clutter the cockpit somewhat. Just give us a display in the top tube that shows the percentage, it really isn't that hard.

At least, if the battery does run out, the system weight of the Decoy SN did not make it an absolute horror to pedal without motor assistance. I tried once, not necessarily by choice …

The app also acts as a hub to download different ride mode presets (which works really well) of how the power unfolds for the different ride modes. You also have the possibility to fully customize each ride mode. While the Fazua Ride 60 feels quite natural even in the most powerful settings – I spent most of my riding time using the downloaded Mountainbike Power profile – you have the opportunity to sacrifice some initial power and acceleration for some truly smooth engagement of the power band.




Descending

It took YT a few extra years to shorten their seat tube lengths in relation to reach, but they've finally reached a point where smaller riders can size up a frame or two if they want to, without having to compromise much on seatpost drop.

Except for the size S frame (42mm), all other frames have a 52mm difference between reach and seat tube length. However, that difference alone wouldn’t matter much if the seatpost insertion depth was lacking, but luckily, the Decoy SN has plenty of room. On the size L, you can fit the latest version of the YT Postman with 200mm of drop and smooth engagement, all the way down to the collar.

With a head angle of 64.2-degrees in the regular setting and 63.9-degrees in the low setting, which measured about 0.4 degrees slacker, the Decoy SN’s numbers are on the slack side but neither number makes the bike feel like it’s pushing into extreme territory. As a package, the geometry feels nicely balanced.

After gathering a good amount of riding impressions, I switched to the low geometry setting and spent most of the testing time in that configuration. I frequently encountered technical trails with tricky rock slabs to navigate on both climbs and descents, where I would have appreciated a few extra millimeters of bottom bracket height. Even with the thankfully short 160mm e*thirteen Helix cranks and being extra cautious, pedal strikes with the lower than claimed bottom bracket height were inevitable.

That said, I preferred the slacker head angle for the added stability at speed even if the difference is not that big to begin with. The bike feels highly planted while cornering, regardless of which geometry setting you’re in.

Thanks to YT’s forward-thinking design, the zero stack 56 headset cup on top means you can install an angle set. If the Decoy SN were my personal bike, I’d consider running the regular geometry setting for a higher bottom bracket, then use an angle set to bring back the slacker head angle.

YT is known for injecting a certain amount of playfulness into their bikes, whether it's a trail bike or a World Cup downhill machine. The chainstay length reflects that philosophy. Although, 442mm (or 443mm in the low setting) on all sizes is what their longest size-specific chainstay on the current Capra 29 measures, so you could say the Decoy SN is on the longer side of YT’s spectrum.
Overall, for size L, the Decoy SN has a nice front-to-rear center balance that allows it to carve into tight, snaking turns but won’t scare you when the downhill gets fast and rowdy. I wouldn't mind a slightly longer rear center for some extra weight on the front, but it's hard to fault the SN's character. For sizes XL and XXL, it’s reasonable to assume that the weight bias shifts more onto the rear wheel.



When I first hopped on the bike, it was immediately apparent that the Decoy SN is stiff and precise in its line choice, yet it finds a good middle ground by being compliant enough in the right places to avoid feeling harsh.

I’m also impressed by how the Decoy SN’s kinematics strike a balance between hugging the ground in almost any situation and staying playful, making it relatively easy to pop off obstacles when you want to get the bike airborne. The constant rate of the suspension curve likely plays a role in that. It’s quite hard to bottom out the rear suspension, yet it feels like you’re making good use of the travel in just about any situation. I can only recall two instances during the entire test period where I bottomed out the rear end hard, but in both cases, it was well deserved.

I took the SN on some seriously rough tracks and never once felt concerned about the suspension's ability to handle what was ahead. The tune YT chose for Fox’s DHX2 coil shock is probably another big factor for the rear suspension’s smooth performance. It handles both fast and slow hits with equal poise and rarely gets flustered.

What stands out about the bike’s versatility is that, despite its capability on tough terrain, it never felt out of place on mellow trails. In fact, its playful nature translated well to flowy trails too.

At 21.1 kg (46.42 lbs.) with pedals, the Decoy SN might not be the lightest mid-power e-bike out there, but it certainly feels light compared to some of the heavier e-bike options when riding it. Generally speaking, most e-bikes in the 23 kg range handle pretty well—they don’t push into corners too aggressively and can be maneuvered without feeling like they’re fighting you. However, the magic number seems to be right around where YT's Decoy SN sits, or below, as it’s hard to find situations where the extra weight becomes a negative on the downhills. If anything, it makes the bike feel nicely planted in corners and through rough sections.

Lifting the front wheel over a ledge at the top of a steep chute feels natural, as does initiating a drop when you can only roll to the edge slowly. When you speed into a tight, winding trail, it’s hard not to grin at how easily the Decoy SN can be thrown from side to side beneath you, with plenty of standover height for free movement. The ability to run posts with a long drop – something still not standard on many bikes today – greatly enhances that freedom of movement.

One of the standout features is how quiet the bike is on the downhills. For one, the motor doesn’t rattle at all, which is a big win in my book. The cables are well managed, with internal guides that suppress any noise, and the soft chainstay protectors, with air bubbles for extra cushioning, do an excellent job. YT mentioned that they intentionally equipped the bike with the GX Transmission derailleur, even on their top model, because its battery sits on top of the derailleur, rather than on the backside like it does with the XX and XO models, resulting in less bounce. While I haven’t spent enough time on those higher-end models to form a strong opinion, it makes sense in theory. Also, I’d pick the GX’s more protected battery placement over the pricier options any day anyway.

The loudest part of the bike is the I9 hub’s freewheel, but even with its many engagement points, it’s still one of the less obnoxious freehub sounds out there to deal with. I fully agree with YT’s decision to run a higher stack height with the 40mm riser bar. The bike’s modern geometry supports a higher front end without much compromise, offering that extra bit of confidence when the trail gets steeper.




How Does It Compare

YT Decoy SN
Trek Slash+

I haven't ridden a bike from another manufacturer in the same riding category with a similar motor type, but I have ridden the TQ HPR 50, and Dario gave a pretty clear picture of how the new Slash+ feels, so we decided to compare these two models head to head.

Looking at the numbers, both bikes use mixed wheel setups, but the Trek Slash+ offers a more adjustable geometry than the Decoy SN. The headset can accommodate a +/- 1-degree or 0-degree cup, which almost completely isolates the adjustment from the rest of the geometry. Additionally, a flip chip at the shock mount allows for altering the overall rear end progression.

With a mid-high pivot, 170mm of travel at both the front and rear, and the ability to push the head angle below 63 degrees, the Slash+ is massively composed at tackling the roughest trails. On the other hand, the YT Decoy SN should have the edge in terms of liveliness and all-around versatility.

The biggest difference between the two bikes, however, is the motor: the Slash+ features the TQ HPR 50. Its 10Nm less torque compared to the Decoy SN’s motor is more noticeable on the trail, than the number suggests. While the power transfer feels very smooth and natural, you’ll need to put in more effort to climb hills and navigate steep technical sections. On the plus side, the Slash+ comes with a 580Wh battery, so it's going to go further than the Decoy SN with its 430Wh battery.

While the Fazua Ride 60 motor is impressively quiet, the TQ HPR 50 reigns as the king of stealth, making the Slash+ the closest thing to a regular bike in terms of noise. Plus, the display integrated into the top tube provides the exact battery percentage and other key data.




Which Model is the Best Value?


It feels like prices for bikes from direct-distribution manufacturers have come down a bit in recent months, and fortunately, the Decoy SN is no exception. The Core 4 flagship model, equipped with Fox Factory suspension, isn’t exactly cheap, but when you consider the spec list and compare it to similarly equipped bikes on the market, it’s much easier on your wallet.

With RockShox ZEB and Vivid Ultimate air suspension, the Core 3 likely offers the best value for money, coming in at $1,000 less than the Core 4. The frame is identical to that of the top model. Although I haven't had a chance to ride the new S1000 Eagle Transmission derailleur, I doubt there will be a noticeable difference compared to the GX derailleur from the Core 4. My only gripe is that while the TRP DH Evo brakes are reliable, they don’t deliver the same massive stopping power as the SRAM Mavens.

At $6,499, the Core 2 still has a a smart spec, with a mechanical Shimano XT derailleur and no-nonsense tires and wheels.

Technical Report

Adjustments The flip chips – one at the top and one at the bottom of the yoke – are small and a bit fiddly to work with, but since you’ll likely only change the setting once or twice to find your preferred setup, it’s not a big deal. That said, swapping them on the trail might not be ideal. In my one attempt, I was hindered by the linkage, which didn’t allow me to turn my wide multi-tool at all (unbolting the shock in the front and compressing the rear end solved that issue but I won't try to repeat that ordeal).

Crankbrothers Synthesis wheels: I didn’t encounter any issues during the test period, even though rough rock sections were a near-daily occurrence, and I did hear the rim bottoming out on rocks once in a while. On top of that, their damping characteristics are top-notch.

Continental Kryptotal tires The tread clearly resembles a competitor's 3-2-3 pattern, and I’m totally fine with that. The use of the SuperSoft compound in the front is a welcome choice, especially now that it’s available with Conti’s lighter Enduro casing, which is quite sturdy. I didn’t have any issues with defects while running my usual air pressures in both the front and rear. Given how impressed I am with the minimal wear, even after serious abuse on sharp rocks and lots of hardpack riding, I’d personally switch to the SuperSoft compound in the rear as well for a bit more traction everywhere. I had a few instances where the rear tire slid out on sharply angled rock slabs, and I could do without moments like that.

Maven SLVR brakes: The Mavens are always predictable and ready when you need them. At the same time, they offer great modulation. So, if someone suggests they have too much power, I’d counter that they simply need to learn to pull them less at lower speeds or on surfaces that require a gentler touch.

I was initially concerned that the higher force required to initiate the brake lever might be a drawback with SRAM’s new stoppers, but I hardly gave it a second thought during most of my rides. Occasionally, on long and consistently steep technical trails, I wished for less strain on the lever, but overall, the significant braking power more than makes up for the extra breakaway force and helps alleviate fatigue far more than the extra effort required at the lever.

Pinkbike's Take
YT says that the Decoy SN is the best enduro bike they've ever made. A daring statement, but when taking the motor support out of the equation after having ridden the latest generation of Capra, I agree. The Decoy SN is a bike that you never have to second-guess, regardless of the trail conditions. With its supportive suspension it does well in rough conditions while remaining reactive enough to not act like a bore on smoother tracks. It's just flat-out fun when carving turns, although its low bottom bracket height requires extra attention in rocky sections.

Adding the motor back into the equation, the Fazua Ride 60 may not rival a full-power system, but it delivers enough punch to offer solid support on climbs, with enough range for longer days in the saddle. While the bike’s overall weight isn’t record-breaking, it’s low enough that it doesn’t negatively impact the ride quality. Overall, if you're satisfied with, or even seek the compromise, of a bit less power and a more natural ride feel, the Decoy SN should be high on your list of contenders for a good time on just about any kind of trail.
