The Rocky Mountain Instinct in this year's Field Test was a bit of a head-scratcher. On paper, it ticked all the boxes: geometry, features, and adjustment, all at an impressive weight, but when riding the bike it quickly became apparent that the shock tune wasn't to our liking.

A shock tune is much like an opinion - what works best for you might well spell trouble for somebody else. At Pinkbike, we do try and be self-aware of our habits. A good rule of thumb is that if we're nearing the limit of the compression range on either end, that doesn't necessarily mean something is wrong - some testers generally prefer more compression damping, while others prefer less. Rider weight and riding style plays a role in this equation as well. But if we're at the limit of the range of adjustments, and still not near our preference, then that will be mentioned in the review.

The Instinct is a $160 shock rebuild from being a very well-rounded bike.

This is largely how it played out with the Instinct review, only my desire for more compression damping was universal across the testers. Yes, you could increase the spring rate, but then you had difficulty accessing the latter stages of the travel. It was frustrating, as there are a lot of good things about the Rocky, but, as we all know, you don't ride spec sheets or CAD drawings. Bike riding happens in the real world, and that's where the Instinct loses its edge.

Kaz and I decided to embark on a day of testing to see how much we could improve the Rocky Mountain, or at least bring it closer in line with our own preferences, by tuning the compression alone. We had three tunes: the CS30, CS40, and CS50. Our test was simple: blind testing, the same air pressure with the same shock volume, and back-to-back laps. Kaz could then inform us of his preferred tune, as well as let the audience know what he liked, too. Of course, nobody is immune from innate bias and it's impossible to completely safeguard any run-and-gun test. That said, it represents something that anyone could do should they also have the opportunity to try different tunes.

Trails like this are on the upper limit of what a trail bike will be ridden on. However, we also felt the bike was underdamped on a whole variety of different trails.

Kaz, after a day of test laps, preferred the CS50 - which was the firmest tune. After seeing how much fun it was, I went home to Squamish, put some different color cable ties on the shocks, some tape over the serial numbers, took the o-rings off, and repeated the test. My findings aligned with his. It felt more supported and, as he put it, less "busy."

When testing bikes, especially in a group test, it's important to review the bikes we receive and not see them as a platform to build out, change, alter, and adjust endlessly until they are perfect. Simply put, that's not replicating a lot of people's experience when buying a bike, nor is it reasonable to expect to have to change fundamental aspects just to make it the performance product that it's intended to be. Especially when you factor in the thousands of dollars you may have parted with for your brand-new mountain bike.

The cost in Canada for Fox to service and re-valve your shock would be $230, or $160 in the US. That's not to mention there are many independent tuners that would also be able to help.

Any tune is just a preference, and it's not to say that if you enjoy the light rebound compression tune on your bike then there is some good reason why you shouldn't. But it is also worth noting that on the same trails, on the same hill, and often on the same day, not all the bikes on this year's Field Test came under fire in the same way, and that when you have a tune that suits your preference, it will give you more confidence and control when descending.

 @thekaiser: I have a Cane Creek and this approach is not ideal. If you look at Ohlins, you will notice that they have lots of tunes yet the dampers are twin-tubes like CC. This is because High speed knobs are not really changing the high speed, they change the speed at which it kicks in. The best analogy is in coil spring preload, you can adjust sag, but spring the curve gradient is the same. So the H knobs effectively move the curve higher, but the curve gradient does not change. So adjusting the shimstack is more effective than a knob, just like using correct spring rate is better than using preload.
