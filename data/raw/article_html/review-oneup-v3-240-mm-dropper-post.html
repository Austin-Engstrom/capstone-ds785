The OneUp V3 is arguably the best all-around droppers on paper. It’s one of the longest-travel, shortest stack, lowest weight and least expensive on the market. But I wanted to see how it fared in the real world.

It's no longer top-of-the-class in every area, as more affordable long-travel options have come to market recently, but it's still the benchmark because it scores well in every area.

The V3 came out in early 2024, claiming to be more reliable, smoother and 60-70 g lighter than the V2, but the comparison is a little more complicated against the new competitors.

After eighteen months of use on two different bikes in its most extreme 240 mm length, it’s time to give the verdict. In short, it’s still the one I'd recommend.

OneUp V3 Dropper Details

• Travel: 90mm / 120mm / 150mm / 180mm / 210mm / 240mm (tested)
• Total Length: 295mm / 350mm / 415mm / 480mm / 545mm / 610mm
• Travel can be reduced by 10 or 20 mm
• Compressed Stack Height: 30mm (25mm for 34.9)
• 27.2 / 30.9 / 31.6 / 34.9mm diameters
• 2-year warranty
• MSRP: $269.99 USD / $364.99 CAD / €291.99 / £291.99 ($302.37 with remote)
• Weight: 612 g (actual, 240x31.6 mm, post only)
• More info: oneupcomponents.com


How does it compare?

Until recently, the 240 mm OneUp dropper was the longest travel dropper available. That crown has since been stolen (marginally) by the 242 mm Wolf Tooth Resolve rev2 and the 250 mm RockShox Reverb AXS, and equalled by the cable-operated Fox Transfer, but the V3 remains one of the longest options, and I suspect more than ample for most riders.

Below is a selection of droppers with more than 210 mm of travel to see how the specs stack up.


While the V3 is no longer the longest, it's still the lightest and the lowest stack height, if only by a little. The compressed stack height is measured from the bottom of the collar to the centre of the saddle rails with the post fully compressed - this metric can be compared between droppers with different travel. If you're limited by the length of external space between the seat clamp and the saddle rails at pedalling height, a shorter compressed stack will allow you to run a longer travel dropper post (the V3 can be reduced in travel by 10 or 20 mm if it's too long).

The total length is important if it's the internal insertion depth that limits your maximum seatpost length, but that's harder to compare between seatpost with different travel. The 240 mm V3 is just 1 mm shorter than the 240 mm Fox Transfer, but 23 mm longer than the Wolf Tooth Resolve V2. OneUp has a calculator to determine the maximum length of dropper that will fit your frame.

The OneUP V3 is no longer exceptional in any area, but the combination of traits makes it exceptionally well-rounded.

Servicing

OneUp recommends a 120-hour service interval for a clean and grease & 350 hours for a full rebuild. They say this takes less than ten minutes using standard tools: a 14mm wrench, 5mm hex, and 2mm hex.


I've not attempted the full rebuild yet, because my post doesn't need it and I want to test its longevity. The fully sealed cartridge design was a concern to me, given that other posts need occasional adjustment of the air pressure to maintain return speed, but the V3 has shown no signs of slowing down over the 18-month test period. OneUp says that doing without a Schrader valve eliminates the main source of leaks in refillable systems, and using pure nitrogen maintains pressure better than air, too.

Worst case, you can get a replacement cartridge (along with all the other spare parts) from OneUp for $41.99.

How much travel do you need?

Not everyone needs a 240 mm seatpost, but at 191 cm / 6'3" tall, I find having beyond 200 mm of drop to be a distinct advantage. It allows me to move around the bike, absorb compressions and ride more fluidly. I don't think this is a result of steep seat tubes so much as that modern bikes require a more centred riding position, whereas with old-school bikes, you were swinging off the back behind the saddle. I also think modern enduro bikes are so capable that we're riding trails that used to be called DH tracks with 150-170 mm travel, so the extra room between your legs is appreciated.


Performance

There isn't too much separating modern cable-operated dropper posts in terms of performance, since the details are largely ironed out by now. Nevertheless, the V3 returns swiftly, even in grotty conditions, and makes an audible top-out so you know it’s fully extended before sitting down. If anything, the V3 post was a little slower to return when it was new. I gave the seal a quick grease about a year ago, and it's been enthusiastic ever since.

After eighteen months without a proper service, my 240 mm version is sometimes slightly sticky at full extension, and benefits from a little forward scoot to start it compressing smoothly, but it takes minimal effort to overcome this. I have a 213 mm Bike Yoke Revive on test, which is noticeably smoother to get moving in a butt-to-butt test, but that's not a fair comparison as it's shorter travel and much newer. While you can make it stick by pushing your weight back in the saddle, this is a non-issue on the trail and likely results from my deliberate withholding of TLC.

There's about a millimetre of squish when the post is set to mid-travel, but this isn't noticeable, and the post is solid at full extension, with no sagging and minimal play.

The dropper remote is particularly ergonomic and neat, with its rubber thumb pad and cable-end holder.

All this should be expected these days, but some droppers still fail to return swiftly and consistently, especially in cold weather or after a few months of use, or at least require adjustment of the air spring pressure to ensure reliable return speed. The fact that the V3 has maintained its smooth and swift performance even in the 240 mm length makes it more impressive.


Pros

+ Still a great balance of cost, weight, stack and travel
+ Healthy return, audable topout
+ No play or squish after eighteen months

Cons

- PNW & SDG offer alternatives with similar travel for less money, while the Wolf Tooth Resolve V2 has a shorter insertion depth
- Slight sticking from full extension if you abuse it




Pinkbike's Take

While OneUp's V3 post is no longer in a class of its own, it's still the benchmark. It's not just light and compact for the travel, it also performs solidly and has stood up well to an extended test period.— Seb Stott

venturavin (Sep 9, 2025 at 12:22)
 This is a pretty common misconception that happens with any high-volume product. Like the OneUp dropper or the Specialized Enduro, for instance. You see loads of anecdotal issues reported online, far more even than for competitors, which would lead you to believe they are more prone to failure. However there are simply way way more of them out in service. And nobody goes online to report their product is working as it should, only to report issues.

For example, if there are 1000 OneUp droppers and 200 Bike Yokes (ratio pulled from my behind, but wouldn't be surprised if it's even more of a delta) and both brands have a 3% failure rate, that is 6 failed Bike Yokes and 30 failed OneUps. So as you read through the forums you get the impression that OneUp fails 5x more than Bike Yokes. And this effect is even more pronounced because you are seeing real world reports from real people, so emotionally this carries a lot of weight. And then it is compacted even further as this news is spread around the forums/comments section, as folks report what they are seeing. Confident that they are reporting very high quality information taken from real world sources, but unknowingly they are actually building on this snowball of misinformation.

Sorry for the rant, but I have been observing this effect since the Kona Stinky days and it is bothersome.
