Last year Specialized revamped nearly their whole mountain bike lineup - new versions of the Epic, Stumpjumper, and Status models were all launched, and a DH race bike was teased as well. Granted, there’s still no new Enduro, but I’ve stopped holding my breath on that one - hopefully the wait is worth it when it finally emerges.

For 2025 the focus shifts to the motorized side, starting with the Turbo Levo. The new Levo picks up where the previous generation left off, and now has more power (thanks to a new motor), an 840 Wh battery, and there’s even in-frame storage. The 150mm of rear travel and 160mm fork are the same as before, and there’s still the wide range of possible geometry configurations that Specialized has become known for.

There are four complete models, with prices starting at $7,999 for the Levo 4 Comp Carbon, and going all the way up to the S-Works model shown here, which retails for a whopping $13,499 USD.

You can also go the frame-only route, still something of a rarity when it comes to eMTBs, assuming the $7,499 asking price of the S-Works frame doesn’t make you spit out your coffee. Similar to what Bosch has done in the past by offering a Race edition motor, Specialized’s S-Works motor is more powerful than the ‘regular’ Specialized 3.1 motor found in the other three models, with a max torque of 111 Nm and max power of 720 watts, compared to the still-impressive 101 Nm torque and 666 watt power of the other models.

Frame Details

The Levo has an entirely new carbon frame, with the battery accessed by a door on the side of the downtube. In recent years we’ve seen more and more e-bikes released with batteries that are aren’t easily removed, and in many cases unbolting the entire motor is required to pull them out. That’s typically done to save weight, since there aren’t any extra grams from the latching mechanism of a door, and there's no need to reinforce the rest of the frame to compensate for that big port.

In this case, Specialized has made battery swaps about as easy as possible - all that’s required is a hex key. An 840 Wh battery is the stock configuration, but there’s a 600 Wh option available for riders who want to save some weight (the 840 Wh battery is 9.3 lb), and there’s also a 280 Wh range extender that sits in the bottle cage. No matter which battery is installed, there’s room for storing a spare tube or other repair supplies in the head tube area. A small zippered bag is supplied, and a magnet holds it into place. You do need to pull the battery out to get to those goodies, so it’s not really where I’d put snacks; think of it more like the spare tire and jack that get crammed under the trunk of a car.

It’s also possible to remove the internal battery entirely and run the Levo solely on the range extender. In that setting the motor power is reduced by 50%. You could also ride the bike with no batteries installed at all, but that’s a move reserved for masochists, since the total weight with that setup is still 43 pounds.

Motor

There’s a bit of an arms race going on at the moment when it comes to eMTB motors - max torque and wattage outputs are increasing, as companies inch closer and closer to the limit of Class 1 ebikes. Regulations vary, but in general a Class 1 ebike must be pedal assist only, with a maximum assisted speed of 20 mph. The motor’s power must be limited to 1 horsepower, or 750 watts.

With the Levo, the S-Works motor has a max output of 720 watts, and a max torque of 111 Nm. The previous levo had 90 Nm of torque and 565 watts of power, so the increase is substantial. Keep in mind that the increased torque and power does drain the battery more quickly, so it’s a good thing that 840 Wh is the stock option.

85 - 90 Nm of torque and 600 watts of power used to seem seem like the standard, until DJI arrived on the scene with their Avinox motor, which has 105 Nm of torque and 850 W of peak power, and a Boost mode that bumps those numbers up even further for a 30 second burst. There are rumors that Bosch will be increasing the output of their Gen 5 motors later this year, but that hasn’t officially been confirmed.

As for the actual construction of the motor (it’s manufactured by Brose), it uses larger, full metal gears that have undergone a surface treatment to increase their durability. Care was taken to reduce the amount of noise and vibrations produced by the motor, and so far that seems to have paid off out on the trail - I didn’t find the whir of the motor to be overly loud or annoyingly whiny.




Display / Modes

Specialized’s Mastermind top tube display has always been one of the best, and that continues with the Levo. There’s a huge range of customizable screens, and Bluetooth devices can also be connected to it. The motor modes can easily be customized in the app, where there are presets that can skew the output to use less battery, or to provide maximum assistance for riders that want to cover the most distance in the shortest amount of time.

Along with the Eco, Trail, and Turbo, and Auto modes, Micro Tune and Dynamic Micro Tune can be accessed from the remote. Dynamic Micro Tune has become the mode I use the most - it gives access to all the motor’s power when you pedal hard, but saves battery by doling out less power when you’re not mashing on the pedals. You can select the amount of assistance in increments of 10, so 10/100, 20/100, 30/100, etc… will show up at the top of the screen.

The handlebar remote has a new shape, with a small toggle the sticks up out of the middle between the power and walk buttons. It took me some time to get used to the toggle - I still prefer the old Levo’s two button switch - but after a month of riding it’s feeling much more familiar.



Playing with fire

There’s a menu option on the Levo that’s going to raise some eyebrows, and rightly so. By pressing a few buttons on the controller, you can increase the Levo’s max speed limit to 28 mph, effectively turning it into a Class 3 e-bike, at least on the versions sold in the US. (Class 3 e-bikes are pedal-assisted and have a max assisted speed of 28 mph).

A large Class 3 logo shows up on the display to let others know that you’ve unlocked the cheat code. It’s mainly designed for riders that have a paved road commute to the trailhead, and in that scenario it really is nice to have, since you don’t get that lurching sensation when the 20mph limit is reached.

That said, I think Specialized are playing with fire by enabling this mode; it’s like leaving a bowl of Halloween candy out with a "Take one" sign - I’m sure there will be plenty of riders who use the mode in areas where it’s not allowed. It’s not going to affect average climbing speeds, since it’s rare that you’d be able to go up any sort of incline at 20mph, but on more rolling terrain it will bump up the average speed that riders can maintain.

We’ll see if it sticks around - I’d vote that it gets removed, even if that means it takes a few more minutes of commuting to get to the trails.


That little nubbin helps keep the range extender in place.

A 280 Wh range extender is available aftermarket, bumping the total battery capacity up to 1120 Wh.

Super Charger

Specialized are also releasing a 12 amp “Super Charger” battery charger that can take the battery level from 0% to 80% in less than an hour, a handy feature for all the forgetful riders out there. Along with the fast charge mode, it also has an Eco mode that’s designed to help increase the lifespan of the battery.


Geometry

There’s no shortage of geometry possibilities with the Levo, but the base numbers aren’t that much different than the last Levo. The head angle can be altered by + or - 1-degree by swapping out the upper headset cup, the bottom bracket height can be raised or lowered via a flip chip at the rear shock mount, and the chainstay length can be changed by 9mm, from 435 to 443mm with the flip chips on the rear chainstay. The nice thing is that the adjustments can be done fairly independently, rather than having one change dramatically alter all of the other geometry numbers.



Suspension Design

The Levo 5 uses a 210 x 55mm shock to deliver 150mm of rear travel via a Horst Link layout. It's equipped with a piggyback version of the two-chamber Genie shock that debuted on the Stumpjumper last year - for a deeper dive on the inner workings of that shock, Dario Digiulio has the scoop here. The bike is also compatible with standard shocks as well.

Test Bike Setup

195 psi in the Fox Genie shock put me right at 30% sag, and I stuck with that amount for the duration of testing. I trimmed the bars down to 770mm, and the only other change I made was a swap to a 200mm front rotor on the SRAM Maven brakes, downsizing from the stock 220 setup.

Testing Info

Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, as winter faded away and hints of spring started to show up. That meant the bike saw plenty of muddy rides until the sun started to stay out long enough to create heroic conditions.

Climbing

I’ll start with the motor performance, since that’s going to be the big question on many riders’ minds. Is the extra ‘oomph’ noticeable? It certainly is, especially on really steep climbs - there’s no drop off in the level of assistance, just a consistent push to get you to the top. On extra-techy sections of trail, the type with moves that slow speeds down to a crawl, the torque is especially impressive - pushing down on the pedals results in an instant surge of power, perfect for getting up and over rooty steps. There’s absolutely zero delay, even when you’re starting from a standstill. The cadence required to make the most out of the motor isn’t wildly fast either - Specialized says it's around 80 rpm, and I didn’t feel like I needed to alter my usual pedal speed that much to maintain a good level of assistance.

I didn’t have too many pedal strikes, but riders in rockier areas may want to swap out the fancy 160mm carbon cranks for something shorter, and made of metal.

The overall speed of the bike is what really stands out - I knew I was getting places faster than usual, but it wasn’t until looking at my stats that I’d realize just how much vert I’d knocked out in a short amount of time. As always, range amounts will vary depending on rider weight and terrain - if you get Turbo drunk it’s possible to use all 840 watts in under two hours, but by staying in Trail mode a little more often (which is still very quick even in the stock setting) the range increases significantly. I was able to get around 23 miles of range and 4,000 vertical feet of climbing out of a full battery, and that’s using a mix of Turbo and Trail modes.

As far as the overall climbing position goes, I don’t have any complaints - the slightly steeper seat angle was appreciated, and the higher rise bars help create an upright and comfortable setup.



Descending

The Levo and the Stumpjumper share similar geometry, and I’ve spent hours and hours on both bikes in the past, so it didn’t take long to get up to speed on the latest Levo. I tried a few different geo settings for experimentation’s sake and ended up preferring the long chainstay, low bottom bracket, and neutral head angle position - in that setting the front end didn’t feel overly sluggish on more rolling terrain, while still being slack to feel right at home in the steeps.

There’s been plenty of questions about the Genie shock, as is typically the case whenever a company produces something that deviates from the norm. Realistically, the design is simply a way to add a little more tunability to the air spring’s curve and to increase the bottom out resistance available, even on a bike with a more linear leverage curve. The Levo comes with one volume spacer in the outer chamber, and I stayed with that setup - the shock and fork felt balanced, with a good level of support to keep the bike from feeling too mushy or wallowy. I didn’t have any harsh bottom-outs either, which isn’t all that surprising, since the shock’s design makes that almost impossible, but it is a handy feature to have when you’re plummeting back to earth aboard a 50+ pound machine.

On that note, it’s probably time to discuss the Levo’s weight. Yes, there’s now an 840 Wh battery in that big down tube, but these days 52 pounds isn’t all that light. Look at the recently launched Norco Sight VLT - it’s 49 pounds with an 800 Wh battery, and that’s a high pivot design with an idler pulley. To be fair, that weight is with a Lyrik and trail casing tires, so it’s not exactly a totally even comparison. Or take the Transition Regulator, which comes in a 47 pounds with a 600 Wh battery - add a range extender onto that and you’re still looking at a sub-50 pound bike.

Am I being picky? Maybe a little, but weight does make a difference in how a bike handles, and the Levo doesn’t feel nearly as maneuverable and energetic as the Regulator, despite having very similar geometry. It takes more effort to toss it around, and it feels more, well, e-bikey than some of the other options out there. Part of me wishes Specialized had given the Levo more travel - I’d be a little more lenient when it comes to weight if this was designed to be a DH smasher as opposed to more of an all-rounder. It’s approved for up to a 180mm fork - maybe we’ll see a longer travel version released that can take advantage of that capability, since the geometry is already pretty much there.

Don’t get me wrong, the Levo’s no lethargic pig - it’s still a blast to plow through chopped up and rutted descents, and it does a very good job of making the most out of that 150mm of travel. Not surprisingly, it has a lot in common with the previous model, with nice and neutral trail manners, at least if you ride with a little more aggression than you would on a non-motorized bike.

How Does It Compare?

I had the Transition Regulator on hand at the same time as the Levo, so let’s start with that comparison. Both bikes have 150mm of travel with a 160mm fork, although the travel can be increased on the Regulator by running a longer stroke shock. The Regulator doesn’t have as many geometry options as the Levo, but I have a feeling that the settings most riders will end up at will be pretty similar between the two. The Regulator does have longer chainstays - 448 or 450mm vs 443mm for the Levo in the long setting.

Other than the slight geometry differences, it’s the motor, battery size, and weight that separates the two - the Regulator uses Bosch’s gen 5 motor with a 600 Wh battery and checks in at 47.25 pounds, while the Levo’s Specialized motor ant 840 Wh battery puts it at 52.3 pounds.

All the stats and numbers aside, out on the trail I felt more comfortable on the Regulator - it’s easier to toss around thanks to that lower weight, all while still maintaining excellent traction. I prefer the slightly longer chainstays as well - they add an element of centered stability that’s not quite as accessible on the Levo. The Levo’s suspension is calm and composed, but even with that fancy Genie shock the Levo’s suspension never felt quite as supple and ground hugging as the Regulator.

On paper it looks like the Specialized motor should blow the Bosch out of the water, but that’s not really the case out in the real world. The Specialized motor does an excellent job of maintaining a consistent level of assistance on steep climbs, and the extra torque is noticeable in situations when you’re almost stalled out and need to put the power down to keep moving forward, but the Bosch is no slouch in that department either. I’ll put it this way - I didn’t feel like the Specialized motor allowed me to get up anything that I couldn’t also climb with the Bosch motor.

Overall, the Levo and the Regulator are approaching the task of creating an all-mountain eMTB from two different angles. The Levo has a bigger battery capacity, a higher max power output, and a wider range of geometry options, while the Regulator saves weight at the cost of battery size and ease of battery access. For me, the Regulator ticks more of the boxes - it’s closer to the dream of a 40ish pound, full-power eMTB with enough battery for big rides.

The Amflow PL Carbon is also a part of nearly every eMTB conversation these days, and for good reason - it’s a 44 lb bike with an 800 Wh battery and a motor that puts out 105 Nm of torque with 850 watts of peak power. To be fair, that weight is for a lightweight build with Fox 36; it'd be a few pounds heavier with the same parts as the Levo 4. At the moment, it’s not easy to obtain that bike in the US, but several companies will be announcing bikes that use the DJI motor in the coming days - we’ll get a real-world comparison going as soon as possible.


Which Model is the Best Value?

At the risk of sounding like a broken record, the best value is always going to be a bike without a motor - Stumpjumper 15 alloy frames are currently on sale for $1,500. Digging into the spec sheets of the Levo 4, the Levo 4 Expert model is the one that catches my eye. It has Fox's Performance Elite suspension, Maven silver brakes, Roval alloy wheels with DT Swiss 370 hubs, and SRAM's wireless GX Transmission drivetrain. It's a solid build kit that doesn't need any immediate upgrades. Granted, for $9,499 you'd hope it'd be fully dialed. Value probably isn't the term I would use here, but that build kit is the closest to how I'd build up a bike of my own if I was trying to maximize the performance without going fully bankrupt.



Technical Report


Fox Transfer Neo dropper post: The Neo post’s response time is incredibly fast - it feels instant, and a slight push of the trigger is all it takes to lower or raise the seat. It’s held up well to plenty of mud and grit, and I’ve managed to remember to charge the battery often enough to keep it from dying out on the trail. That’s really my only tiny nitpick (other than the post's sky high price) - I wish the it was hardwired to the main battery somehow, so that there was only one battery to charge. Otherwise it’s currently the best wireless dropper post that I’ve used, a list that includes the newest RockShox Reverb AXS, KS Lev Circuit, and the TranzX EDP01.

Butcher Grid Gravity Tires: It’s great to see proper tires spec’d on a full power eMTB - the higher speeds and heavier overall system weight mean that DH tires are the way to go to prevent flats or vague handling. Specialized’s T9 compound isn’t the absolute stickiest, but it holds its own when things are wet and slimy, and the tread pattern is easy to get along with in a variety of conditions.

SRAM Maven brakes: Mavens are ideal for slowing down big e-bikes. Sometimes, almost too good - after having a couple of unwanted front wheel slides I swapped out the 220mm rotor for a 200 in order to tone things down a little. I’d rather have too much power than not enough, and for most riders the 220 / 200 combo will be fine, but if you find yourself overbraking, swapping to a smaller or thinner rotor should do the trick.

Pinkbike's Take

I spent a lot of time on the previous Levo, and back in 2021 I called it ‘the new benchmark.” At the time, it was a much more refined offering than the other options on the market. Four years later and the eMTB landscape isn't as desolate looking - it’s as if a sleepy village with one restaurant morphed into a bustling metropolis with no shortage of good food options. That makes it harder to stand out, and even with the extra torque and power the Levo 4 doesn't tower over the competition. Still, the new motor is very impressive, as is the bike's adaptability - for riders who want an e-bike that can be turned into almost anything, the Levo could be the one.— Mike Kazimer
