“You might not like it at first.” That’s not the way a bike is typically introduced at a press camp, but the new Orbea Rallon RS isn’t your typical e-bike. In fact, it’s barely an e-bike - its TQ HPR40 motor and 290 Wh battery provide a subtle amount of assistance, as opposed to acting like an electric shuttle truck. While the fastest, most powerful e-bikes on the market essentially drag riders to the top of a hill, the Rallon RS feels like someone is lightly pushing on your back. And I mean lightly - Orbea’s description of it feeling like a tailwind isn’t far off.

The Rallon RS is more than just a lightly powered eMTB, though; Orbea are using it as a way to showcase a level of electronic integration that hasn’t really been present in the marketplace until now. Yes, “integration” is one of those words that runs the risk of feeling like it was lifted from a mid-level manager's LinkedIn post, but it’s accurate in this case - the dropper post, shock, derailleur, and motor are all powered by the same battery.

On top of that, they communicate with each other - the shock can be set to open up when the dropper post is lowered, and the level of motor assistance can be reduced at the same time.

Before diving into all the extra-techy details, let’s take a moment to go over the basics. The Rallon RS is essentially a motorized Rallon, which means it has 170mm of rear travel, a 180mm fork, and can run either a 29” or 27.5” rear wheel by swapping out the upper shock link.

There are only two models of the Rallon RS, and they’re both expensive. The Rallon RS Team models is 10,199 USD, and the Rallon RS LTD is a whopping $14,999 - that price difference is mainly due to the cost of the Fox Neo electronic suspension components. Total weight for my size large review bike was a svelte (for an e-bike) 40.1 lb / 18.1 kg.

Frame Details

The small form factor of the TQHPR 40 motor and the 290 Wh battery size make it hard to tell that the Rallon RS is an e-bike - it’s really only the flap covering the charging port on the top of the downtube and glow of the LED on the handlebar remote that let curious onlookers know there’s something else going on here.

The HPR40 motor weighs just 1170 grams, and offers 200 watts of peak assistance and 40 Nm of torque. There are two modes, Range and Rally, plus an additional mode called Ultra that delivers an even more subtle level of assistance. Orbea says that riders can expect to get 1200 - 1800 meters of vert out of a single charge, or around 2-3 hours of ride time (I was able to get even more than that during my testing). If the battery does run out of juice it’s not that bad pedaling the RS without assistance - there are non-motorized enduro bikes on the market that are about the same weight.

There's no screen to stare at - lights on the remote indicate the battery perccentage and motor mode.

The small size of the HPR40 motor allowed Orbea to keep the suspension layout almost identical to that of the non-motorized Rallon.

To help with the integration that I mentioned earlier, Orbea developed a gateway, a small box that all of the elements plug into so that they can communicate with each other using the CAN bus protocol. It also leaves the door open for adding on more electronic accessories in the future - for example, lights that are also powered by the battery could be an option.

The Smart Controller remote on the left side of the handlebar allows riders to choose their suspension mode and motor mode without pulling out their phone, or looking at any screen at all. Just below those two buttons is a small lever that activates the electronic dropper posts. The Smart Controller and dropper are hardwired to the battery, which reducea the number of extra batteries to keep track of charging.

The short, uninterrupted seat tube makes if possible to run the longest dropper posts currently available - the size small and medium frames can fit a 210mm post, and the L and XL a 240mm. All models use Orbea’s own electronic dropper, the MC10-RS; the post has an optoelectronic Time-of-Flight sensor which relays the position of the post to the suspension. Depending on how the bike is set up, fully lowering the dropper can be used as the signal to fully open the suspension.



Geometry

The Rallon RS’ geometry is almost identical to that of the non-motorized Rallon EN. The head angle sits at 64.25-degrees, the seat tube angle is a steep 79.25-degrees, and the chainstays measure 445mm for all sizes.

The lower shock mount has a flip chip that’s used to change the bottom bracket height by 7mm, and the head angle by .5-degrees. There’s also an angle adjusting headset that can be used to alter the head angle by +/- .75-degrees.

There aren’t really any outliers on the geometry chart - Orbea did their homework, and the result is a bike that gets it right when it comes to putting the rider in a good position for steep climbs and descents.


Suspension Design

Thanks to the motor and the electronic shock, the Rallon RS doesn’t need to have excessive levels of anti-squat in order to make it an efficient climber, although the numbers aren’t as low as what you’ll find on the DH bike. The anti-squat percentage sits around 89% at sag, and drops as the bike goes through its travel.

Anti-rise begins around 67% and drops slightly deeper in the travel, but overall remains fairly consistent, free of any large changes that would be noticeable while riding.

Test Bike Setup

Other than swapping out the Fizik grips for ones that felt better in my hands (Peaty’s Monarch grips in this case), I tested the bike in its stock configuration. The Maxxis Highroller III / DHR II tire combo worked well in Spain and at home, a good choice for late season rides that have a mix of trail conditions.


Test Locations

I was able to test the Rallon just down the road from Orbea's headquarters in the Basque Country, as well as at home in Bellingham, Washington. The trails in the Navarra region reminded me a little of the East Coast of the US - the birch trees put on a show with their golden fall colors, and the trails were a fun mix of fast and swoopy with some steeper, techier sections to keep things interesting.

Climbing

As Matt Beer mentioned in his review of the regular Rallon, the climbing position of the Rallon RS is very upright thanks to the tall stack and steep seat tube angle. For riders coming from a bike with more relaxed geometry it may feel a little strange at first, but it makes a lot of sense for steep climbs, and even on flatter approaches the tall front end helps prevent it from feeling like there’s too much pressure on your hands.

As far as how much assistance the motor provides, that’s where the Rallon takes a different turn compared to most e-bikes. Orbea calls it a “rider dominant” design, as opposed to “motor dominant,” which describes most eMTBs on the market. In other words, it doesn’t feel like the motor is doing all that much. I’d equate each motor mode as the rough equivalent of shaving 4 pounds of weight off the bike. So in Ultra mode it feels like a 36 pound bike, in Range it becomes a 32 pound bike, and in Rally it's a 28 pound bike.

Compared to a full or even mid-powered eMTB there’s never really the sensation that you’re getting towed up the hill - no matter the mode, you’ll still be doing at least some work. The upside is that the 290 Wh battery lasts a really long time, since it’s not being called upon to support massive wattage or torque outputs. On one ride I tallied up close to 6,000 vertical feet of descending over the course of 30 miles without fully draining the battery.


The HPR40 motor uses a single clutch design, which makes it possible to mount a chainring directly to the cranks, unlike most mid- and full-power motor designs, where the chainrings are mounted separately. That makes it handy for lubing the chain, since rotating the cranks backwards springs the chainring backwards the same way it would on a non-motorized bike, but there is a noticeable downside to the design.

On rolling, more technical terrain, the type where you might need to take the occasional half pedal stroke, there’s a slight delay between when the rider stops pedaling and when the motor stops applying force to the cranks. This results in a strange sensation at the pedals - it’s almost like skid-stopping on a fixed gear bike, where you can still feel the force of the chain pushing against your feet. It doesn’t happen all the time, and it’s more noticeable in the Rally motor mode, but when it does it’s very disconcerting.

If you’re riding winch and plummet style trails that go straight up and then straight down it won’t be all that apparent, but on rolling, technical terrain I’d call it a substantial drawback. It’s detrimental enough that I made sure I’d turned the motor to the ‘off’ position before descending to avoid feeling what I can only describe as the nastiest pedal kickback imaginable.

The kickback sensation aside, Fox's Neo electronic suspension system does a great job of making the Rallon RS into a very efficient climber - it firms up the shock automatically, providing a good platform to push against, and keeping the bike from sagging too deep into its travel. The motor output may not be extreme, but it does provide enough assistance to tame steeper, more technical climbs, although, once again, it doesn’t really hold a candle to a full power bike - you won’t be scaling vertical faces, or seeking out strange, seemingly impossible ascents with this level of output.



Descending

This is hands down one of the best descending mountain bikes I’ve been on recently. The tall stack and the weight centered around the bottom bracket give it an incredibly confident presence on the trail - it’s very, very good. That shouldn’t come as a total surprise, though, since its non-motorized counterpart snagged the 2025 Bike of the Year award.

There are some positioning similarities to the Raaw Madonna, a bike whose descending performance I’ve had high praise for in the past. The tall front end plays an important part in the equation, along with enough bottom bracket drop and moderately long chainstays. The motor weight and battery weight are low in the frame, and the overall system weight feels ideal for high speed, rough downhill descents.

The upright descending position can create some really entertaining moments - changing directions is less about turning the handlebars and more about tipping the bike slightly. I’ve used the analogy before, but there were moments on the Rallon RS where it felt like I was carving on skis; this bike can rail corners with the best of them.

The 40 pound weight is noticeably easier to maneuver than the heavier 50+ pound full-power e-bikes out there - jumping feels very natural, and steep corners are handled with ease. The suspension feels more supportive than the Rallon DH bike, something that Matt mentioned in his review of the Rallon enduro bike as well. There’s a predictable platform to push off of, and the 170mm of rear travel is very well managed - it’s accessible when necessary, but there isn’t any wallowing or diving sensation in the initial portion of the travel.

We've seen enduro bikes drop in travel slightly over the last couple of seasons, but I'm a fan of the Rallon's 180mm fork and 170mm of travel. It helps boost the confidence levels when blasting into a big mess of rocks, and while those higher speeds are where it shines, on maneuvers where you need to slow to a crawl and get lined up to thread the needle down a slimy rock roll it never felt like a handful.

On top of the actual handling characteristics, the Rallon RS is a very quiet bike - there's no cable rattle, and no motor clanking. There's a little chainslap from the XTR wireless derailler, but otherwise it's a nearly silent ride.


How Does It Compare?

At the moment, there really aren’t that many bikes in this category, at least not with this much travel. The Specialized Kenevo comes to mind, but even that motor received a bump up to 50 Nm of torque a couple of years ago.

I’d say that the Trek Slash+ comes the closest when it comes to overall descending performance, but you’ll be able to get back up to the top much faster on it thanks to the TQ HPR60 motor system. Both bikes do an excellent job of smoothing out the rough stuff, but I'd give the point to the Slash+ and its high pivot design when it comes to just how well it erases obstacles. The flip side is that the Slash+ feels a little more glued to the ground; the Orbea is slightly peppier when it comes to popping off those bonus side hits.


Who Is the Rallon RS For?

This is a tough one. I’m sure there are a few riders out there who think this is the perfect bike for them, and it very well may be, but there are a few things to keep in mind.

• If you think you’ll be able to keep up with riders on full powered e-bikes, you won’t. There’s simply no way to easily bridge the gap between the max output of the TQ HPR40 motor and something like a full-power Bosch or Avinox motor. The on-trail difference between 40 Nm and a motor with 100 or 120 Nm of torque is huge, and you’ll quickly be left in the dust.

• This could be a good way to make up for differing fitness levels if the fitter riders are on non-motorized bikes. I can see a parent or a coach using this bike to keep up with a hyperactive group of groms. Both riders will still get a workout, but the one on the e-bike won’t be quite as shelled at the end of a big ride.

• It does make steep climbs easier, just not by a massive amount. The difference the motor makes is similar to what you’d experience by running XC tires instead of DH casing tires - it’s noticeable, but not earth-shattering. During the test period I did the first half of a ride with the motor off, riding with friend on non-motorized bikes. After those initial laps, which totaled 2,300 vert of climbing, I turned the motor on and headed out on my own to repeat what we’d just done. The thing is, that second lap didn’t feel all that different. Yes, it was easier, but not that much easier. That’s the part of this bike that’s tricky to wrap my brain around; the motor assistance is subtle enough that I didn’t miss it all that much when it wasn’t on.




Technical Report

XTR brakes: Compared to Mavens, the XTR brakes don’t offer the same amount of power, or the level of adjustment. I’d call them adequate, but not exceptional for the Rallon’s capabilities. The Galfer rotors help bump up the stopping power a little, but for a downhill-oriented, 40 pound bike I’d go with Mavens.

Fox Neo Float X2: I’m still not really sold on electronic suspension, but I will say that the Neo system does work quite well. It’s nearly invisible - the bike firms up when climbing, and opens up while descending without any odd delays. It’s also very tunable, and diving into the options on the app makes it possible for riders to really customize the setup.




Pros

+ Top notch descending capabilities
+ Impressive amount of tuning potential thanks to connected motor, dropper, and shock.
+ Excellent range considering battery size

Cons

- Very low amount of assistance from motor
- Pedal feedback due to HPR40 motor design on rolling terrain
- Expensive, especially considering how similar it feels to the non-motorized version.




Pinkbike's Take

The Rallon RS is going to be a polarizing bike, and I’d put myself in the camp of riders who think it sort of misses the mark. There are aspects of it that are highly commendable - running all of the electronics off of one battery is something I hope catches on, and the dropper post / suspension interaction is a feature that’s been on my wishlist for over a decade.

The fact that this is an e-bike that doesn’t really feel much like an e-bike is what I haven’t been able to fully come to terms with. For the record, I’m a big fan of TQ’s HPR60 motor - Yeti’s MTe, or the Trek Slash+ are bikes that nail it when it comes to delivering the ride experience I’d expect from a lightweight e-bike. Those bikes are easier to handle than their full-powered counterparts, and they have enough “oomph” that it feels like the motor is actually doing something.

At the end of the day, I wish that Orbea had gone with the HPR 60 on the Rallon RS. If weight was a primary concern they could have stuck with the 290 Wh battery and offered the option to run TQ’s 160 Wh range extender. As it is, the HPR40 puts this bike in a pretty odd middle ground. Sure, it’s only 5 pounds heavier than the non-motorized Rallon, but that sentiment goes the other way too - it’s 5 pounds heavier and thousands of dollars more expensive without really bringing that many benefits to the table.

As a proof of concept, I’m very interested to see where the ideas that are showing up on the Rallon RS go in the future, but the underpowered motor makes it a bit of a non-starter for me, no pun intended. More than anything, my time on the Rallon RS left me scheming ways to get my hands on the regular, non-motorized version, since this is one of the best descending enduro bikes I’ve been on in recent memory.— Mike Kazimer
