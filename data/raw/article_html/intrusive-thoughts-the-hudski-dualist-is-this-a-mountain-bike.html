Intrusive Thoughts: The Hudski Dualist - Is This a Mountain Bike?

Mar 26, 2026
by Dario DiGiulio

This is Shadow Dario. He does not adhere to manufacturer recommendations. He follows the whims of his impulses, however misguided they may be. Such ideas do not constitute advice, nor should they be taken too seriously. Seen here are the results of those fever dreams and runaway ideas, those intrusive thoughts.



A bike is a bike, but not all bikes are mountain bikes. I've done my fair share of underbiking, pushing the line of what makes logical sense for the goal at hand, and though it's not always pleasant, it's usually very fun. Embracing that ethos is Hudski, a brand committed to making a bike that does just about everything pretty damn well.

With no suspension to speak of, but the ability to run mountain bike components from tip to tail, the Hudski Dualist begs the question of what it really takes to be called a mountain bike.

Dualist Details

• 0mm travel, 0mm fork
• Aluminum frame, carbon fork
• 29" wheels, or 27.5"+
• 67° head angle, 74.5° seat angle
• 415-495mm reach, 570-645mm stack
• Chainstay and fork offset adjustment
• 4 sizes available
• Pricing: $2,700 complete, $1,600 frameset
• Weight: XXX
• hudskibikes.com

Frame Details

Speaking to Brian Szykowny, one of the chief minds behind Hudski, it's clear that he has a deep love for mountain biking, with a resume that reflects plenty of time in the industry. Taking the principles of MTB and applying them to a multipurpose chassis is simple on paper, but deciding which details are important becomes a unique challenge.

On face value, the Dualist is a hybrid, a touring bike, an ATB, a rigid mountain bike. In keeping with those categories, the frame is covered in bottle bosses, accessory mounts, and additional anchor points for racks and other accessories. If you're looking to carry a ton of shit on a bike, boy do I have a solid option for you.

Many, many mounts.
Fork included. Maybe you could strap some mass dampers to this puppy to take the edge off.

Considering the utilitarian nature of the Dualist, I'm a bit annoyed at the fact that it uses internal routing for the derailleur and both brakes. It makes for a clean look, but robs the bike of some of the parts-bin-magic that it exudes otherwise. Of course, most folks aren't swapping brakes on a regular basis, so this is just a picky gripe.

One detail that sets the Dualist apart from many other hybrid-esque framesets out there is the fact that it uses Boost spacing front and rear. This opens up a wide array of aftermarket options, and means that you may be able to share a wheelset between your mountain bike and this rigid ripper. The Dualist frame and fork have also been tested to ASTM 3 standards, so you can treat it like the little mountain bike is just might be.

Hudski implemented some very clever frame adjustments, in the form of front and rear dropout flip chips, allowing the user to modify both the rear center and fork offset of the bike. Both chips are self-contained, and don't require any spare parts storage. Swapping can be done trailside, should you feel so inclined.

Two-position rear dropout, with UDH compatibility in both.
Two fork offsets to choose from.


Geometry

The Dualist's geometry is adjustable, but none of the changes are going to alter the roomy and upright fit of the bike. Reach figures are in line with typical bikes of that size, and stack heights are tall, especially considering the riser bars that come stock.

The 67° head angle may look a bit steep, but with no fork travel to compress and steepen, it feels less drastic than you might expect. Same goes for the 74° seat angle, which remains consistent and comfortable even on steeper climbs. Steering geometry can be changed by altering the fork's offset, with 44mm and 54mm positions available to those that want to experiment.

50mm risers keep you nice and tall.

Hudski's clever UDH flip chip allows you to alter the chainstay length by 15mm, opting between 435mm and 450mm. The stability and handling tradeoffs are exactly as you'd expect, with the shorter option likely working best for the smaller two sizes in the range, and the longer catering to the lankier folks.

Dominant headtube.

As the Dualist isn't suspension corrected, you'd be smart to stick with the Hudski carbon fork up front. There are other rigid forks with a similar axle-to-crown measurement out there, should other mounting options or features appeal to you.


Suspension Design

Hope you're feeling supple, because there's not much to take the edge off here. Tire pressure matters a whole lot more on a rigid bike, and can drastically alter your experience. In the past, I've had luck with heavier casing tires run at low pressures, and may even recommend inserts to folks looking to rip the Dualist down rough trails on a regular basis.

For riding dirt, I had luck with around 22-23psi up front, and 25-26psi out back. The EXO casing tires definitely do some wiggling, but you're thankful for it when things get chattery. Being that this is an Intrusive Thought, I also ran some stupid tire combos, with the Schwalbe Albert Gravity radials front and rear standing out as a surprisingly comfortable option for those looking to add some serious heft.

Long dropper opens up a world of possibilities.
Resin-only brakes close some of those doors.

Who What Where Why?

What excited me about the Dualist right off the get go was the open-ended nature of how you can build it. One of the great joys of tinkering with bikes is the vast expanse of build options you can play with, and a frame like this does little to limit you. Does a similar hardtail frame offer more or less the same experience? Absolutely. But there's something nice about the limited nature of the rigid frame, as it removes the distraction of trying to make the bike a high-performance object.


As you may have seen before, I've done my fair share of hardtail tinkering, and do love the mix of simplicity and performance you can get out of a good build in that category. However, a hardtail brushes against the speed and performance you can get out of a full-suspension trail bike, and therefore tempts you to keep beefing it up in pursuit of achieving the same experience.

The rigid nature of the Dualist - and bikes like it - doesn't encourage as much aspiration, and instead feels happy to just be a bike that you can ride anywhere. That anywhere does include singletrack, goofy little jumps, and random cutties through town. But you're rarely under the impression that you're going as fast as you might on a 170mm enduro bike, even though you might feel just as scared.


The Dualist is far from my only time on a rigid 29" bike, thanks in part to my semi-secret bikepacking history. Please allow a brief trip down memory lane. My first bike in that category was an original Surly Karate Monkey. I toured the Great Divide Mountain Bike Route on that glorious brown machine, and rode it as a mountain bike for a few months afterwards.

That was a while ago, but I remember it being sturdy and comfortable enough, though some frame details left me wanting for a more modern option.

I don't have many photos of the Karate Monkey, but did do a BMX race mid-tour, getting third place and ending up on the local news.

After passing the steel bike onto a friend, I wanted something lighter and quicker, landing on a Kona Private Jake. Definitely not a mountain bike - more of a CX bike as gravel hadn't been invented yet - but still quite fun on mellow trails. I rode that bike everywhere, and treated it like a mountain bike, which is why it eventually broke during a race in the Sierras.

Not a mountain bike.
A lesson learned the hard way. This pine bough fix actually worked, and got me out with limited drama.

Enter the current build: a Specialized Chisel hardtail with a carbon fork I bought on Aliexpress, spray painted in my back yard. I learned from my mistakes, split the difference, and actually ended up on something fairly close to what Hudski is selling as an accessible complete option.


The build you see above is a few years old, with parts changing here and there as needs and wants shifted. The Chisel is suspension corrected, but with the massive rigid fork it ends up a little steeper than the Hudski's head angle. Note: this is the older Chisel frame, so the geo is a bit steeper and shorter than the current option - I'd probably prefer the current if given the option.

I'll spin up a Staff Ride on the Chisel at some point soon, but the short build notes end up looking similar to the Dualist on a few fronts. Dropper post, fast rolling light tires, brakes that do enough, a short stem and riser bar, and room for cargo.

Being able to run a 120mm fork feels like a worthwhile consideration, to me.

Riding the Dualist

What the Dualist lacks in travel, it makes up for in stability. That can be altered if you decide to run the rear dropout in the short position, and pop the fork into the longer offset position, but for me the 450mm chainstay and 44mm offset felt like the sweet spot.


As a system, the frame and cockpit make for a very roomy fit, with plenty of space to move around on the bike as need be. This feels like a more critical feature than on a well-designed full suspension, as your ability to shift weight and take forces through your arms and legs is what keeps your bones from getting rattled. A 200mm dropper post gets the saddle way out of the way, and adds more room for body English.

I understand the reasoning behind keeping the Dualist rigid, but can't help but wonder how good it could be if you could squeeze a 120mm fork in there. You'd lose out on some of the massive frame storage, and it would make less sense as a MTB/gravel split build, but the curiosity remains.

That said, the Dualist might just be the perfect parts bin bike for the avid mountain biker. Your parts should fit, the feel is familiar, but the use case is different enough to inspire some different riding. Cruise around town, explore mellower trails, maybe even pack up some gear and go sleep in a field.

The lack of specificity is part of what makes the Dualist special, and feels like the positive conclusion of an exercise in restraint.


If I didn't already have my own contraption of a similar sort, I'd probably pick up a Dualist frame to play around with. For those searching for some rigid fun, a parts bin project, or a very well-designed camping bike, the Hudski Dualist is worth a look.

As to whether it counts as a mountain bike or not, that's up to you.
