Review: Giro's New Montaro III Helmet

Aug 8, 2025
by Mike Kazimer  
Share
Tweet

The Montaro is one of the most popular helmets in Giro’s lineup, which undoubtedly put a little extra pressure on the design team to get things right for version 3.0. The newest iteration is still intended to be an all-round trail helmet, but the overall look has changed; the profile is more angular, and the lines of the two position visor flow a little more naturally into the rest of the helmet.

There’s secure sunglasses storage in two places for riders that stash their shades on the climbs (my hand is raised), and a Fidlock magnetic buckle to make the already-easy process of buckling your helmet even easier. A dial at the rear of the helmet is used to fine tune the fit, with three different vertical positions to adjust how low the helmet sits.

Montaro III Details

• MIPS Evolve liner
• Two-position visor, sunglasses storage
• Weight: 445 grams (medium)
• Sizes: S, M, L
• Colors: black, white, sage, stone
• CPSC, CE, and NTA8776 certified
• MSRP: $215.95
• giro.com
The Montaro III is CPSC and CE certified, and it also meets the NTA e-bike certification. Unfortunately, it doesn't have a five-star rating from Virginia Tech. Virginia Tech recently reset their safety threshold, which meant that over 100 formerly 5-star helmets were bumped down to four stars, including the Montaro III. According to Virginia Tech, "This is not because their performance has worsened, but rather the bar for top-tier performance has risen."





FIT & FUNCTION

Most of my rides with the Montaro III have been on warmer, humid summer days. The fit is excellent for my oval shaped head - it’s fairly deep feeling, but it’s nice and secure even when blasting down rougher trails. The glasses storage works great too - I typically use the rear vent holes to slide the glasses arms into, where they’re held very securely in place.

On paper, the weight is on the heavier side, but that wasn’t too noticeable on the trail - the depth of the helmet helps keep it from feeling too tall and tippy. It's worth playing around with the vertical height adjustment feature in order to make sure the helmet sits in the right spot. There are three positions, and a quick push or pull on the mechanism is all it takes to slide it into a different position.

The overall amount of ventilation is good, and even on hot days the helmet didn't feel over stifling. My main gripe has to do with the thin padding around the brow - it doesn’t take much sweat for it to become saturated and start dripping, usually right onto my glasses. As I mentioned, it’s been pretty humid lately, but even still, the Montaro III doesn’t do the best job of dealing with sweat - I’ve been tempted to find some sort of stylish headband to help deal with the persistent perspiration, and that's not something I've had to do with other helmets.



WEIGHT & PRICE

The Montaro III is on the heavier side of the spectrum for a trail helmet, weighing in at 445 grams for a size medium. The $216 price tag is on the higher end as well, although everything seems to be getting more expensive these days, and there are plenty of examples of pricier mountain bike helmets out there.

Fox Speedframe RS: $279 / 408 grams
Giro Montaro III: $215.95 / 445 grams
Mavic Deemax: $200 / 395 grams
Specialized Ambush: $180 / 360 grams
Lazer Coyote: $109 / 340 grams
Specialized Tactic: $110 / 380 grams
POC Tectal: $180 / 352 grams
Giro Source: $130 / 351 grams




Pros

+ Good coverage, comfortable fit
+ Very effective glasses storage

Cons

- On the heavier side
- No 5-star Virginia Tech rating
- Thin brow padding



Pinkbike's Take
I don’t want it to seem like I’m being overly nitpicky, but there are a lot of very good helmets on the market right now, and it’s the little things that help one stand out from another. The Montaro III gets high marks for its overall comfort and secure sunglasses storage, but the lack of a 5-star Virginia Tech rating and sub-par sweat mitigation take it down a notch. — Mike Kazimer

Snfoilhat (Aug 8, 2025 at 9:11)
 @mikekazimer: here’s an example of how a helmet designer acting in good faith could misuse the VT ratings. Imagine a multi-density design that doesn’t just vary radially like some helmets already do, but varies such that the six zones of the VT test get special treatment. All design is about managing tradeoffs with cost, weight, other facets of performance, but this choice seems very evidence based because the six zones were established by VT after looking at crashed helmets. So by designing to the test you get a safer helmet, right? The problem is the VT test doesn’t measure safety directly but indirectly. And the VT test is not sensitive to what’s happening to the helmet and the wearer at zones outside the six. It’s not sensitive to the bicycle crash as multi-impact series of interrelated events. So it is possible that the real world safety of that hypothetical helmet is lower than the test would rate it to be in comparison to other helmets designed to the broad principles of safe deceleration but not specifically designed to ace the test. Don’t let a metric become a target.
