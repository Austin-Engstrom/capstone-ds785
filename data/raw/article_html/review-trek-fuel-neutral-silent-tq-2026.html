Trek's approach to the lightweight eMTB is simple: take a successful mountain bike, and add a motor. Of course, that process isn't so simple in practice, but at least it makes the bikes a little easier to understand. In this case, we have the electrified version of the brand-new Fuel platform, which encompasses three sub-model options depending on build kit and link choice.

Though I've been testing the EX variant, there are many different permutations one can create from the base frame, by using the rocker link and shock mount kits that Trek is selling alongside the bike.
Fuel+ Details

• 145-160mm frame travel, 150-170mm fork
• MX or full 29"
• Aluminum or carbon frame
• 63.4-64.5° head angle
• TQ HPR60 motor
• 580Wh battery
• 60 Nm torque, 350W max power
• Price: $6,000 – $11,700 USD
• Weight: 43.24lbs / 19.61kg (model tested)

The mid-travel lightweight eMTB isn't necessarily the segment of the electric mountain bike market that gets people stoked, but something like the Fuel+ EX might be the best choice for a lot of riders out there.—Dario DiGiulio



Frame Details

Trek's new Fuel platform is simultaneously very simple, and very complicated. There is a common base frame shared between the EX (tested), MX, and LX sub-models, with the rocker link and lower shock mount determining which of those three options you end up with. To throw some sand in the engine, there's also a secret menu containing more than thirty permutations of all those rocker, link, and shock combinations, making for a very wide array of options for those that want to experiment.

Instead of drowning in choice, I opted to stick to the bog-standard Fuel+ EX, exactly as it comes from factory. Without getting into all the possible permutations of the Fuel+, here are the broad overviews of the three sub-models.

The Fuel+ EX is a 29" bike with 145mm of frame travel and a 150mm fork. It uses a 205x60mm shock. Large geo: 485mm reach, 64.5° head angle, 77.3° seat angle, 443.5mm chainstay length.

The Fuel+ MX is an MX bike with 150mm of frame travel and a 160mm fork. It uses a 205x60mm shock. Large geo: 481.5mm reach, 64.2° head angle, 77.0° seat angle, 440.4mm chainstay length.

The Fuel+ LX is a 29" bike with 160mm of frame travel and a 170mm fork. It uses a 205x65mm shock. Large geo: 473.2mm reach, 63.4° head angle, 76.3° seat angle, 443.5mm chainstay length.

Those who want to parse through all 32 secret menu permutations can do so here.



The Fuel+ does away with the headtube angle adjustment that the previous model touted, instead opting for a standard press-in ZS headset. If angle adjustment is desired, aftermarket options exist, but generally it's nice to see Trek moving away from the proprietary parts required to make the previous system work.

The frame features internal cable routing, accessory mounts on the top tube, and room for a water bottle or range extender on all sizes. The small models do feature a curved top tube to accommodate all of these features. All sizes feature a continuous straight seat tube, for maximum dropper post insertion and adjustability.


Motor & Battery

Trek has been using TQ's HPR motors for a while now, so the integration is well-developed and seamless. There is no motor or battery rattle to report, and the remote/display functions were intuitive and easy to use. I've mentioned it before, but the charging port and associated hardware feels uniquely high quality on TQ's system, which bodes confidence for long-term durability and utility. Even the charge estimates are quite accurate, which is nice if you're tying to squeeze a little more life into the battery before heading out to ride.

Trek's choice to spec a 580Wh battery is one of the best assets of this bike, as it takes the range to impressive levels, without increasing the weight to a point where it feels unmanageable. Sure, a smaller battery would mimic the ride feel of a bike even better, but I haven't been impressed with the range those smaller cells offer. The 580Wh feels like a great balance, and gives this bike a lot of utility.

Battery Issue:

During testing, I had two occasions where the battery percentage misrepresented the actual range. While draining the bike from 100%, the battery would deplete as normal to about 10-13%, at which point it would die as if it had none left. Turning the bike off, on, yelling, playing hard to get, nothing seemed to alleviate the issue.

I warrantied the battery through the local Trek shop, and it turns out there was a software issue with the specific battery in this bike. I haven't replicated the issue with the replacement unit, so hopefully all is well.

Geometry

Bad news for those striving for simplicity - thanks to the rocker link and lower mount kits, there are three geometry tables to reference here. Luckily, the differences aren't too extreme, with only a little over a degree in headtube angle and about 12mm of reach separating the full span of configurations.

Let's take a size Large Fuel+ as an example.
The EX head angle is 64.5°, the MX is 64.2°, and the LX is 63.4°.
The EX seat angle is 77.3°, the MX is 77.0°, and the LX is 76.3°.
The reach and stack figures are as follows: EX, 485mm reach, 638mm stack. MX, 482mm reach, 641mm stack. LX, 473mm reach, 647mm stack.
The EX and LX chainstays are 443.5mm, while the MX is shorter at 440.4mm.


Is this confusing? Yes. Are there compromises in some of the geometries to get the link system to work? Also yes. But do you generally arrive at a geo table that makes sense for most of the bike's use case? Luckily, yes.


To me it feels like the EX model is the primary in the lineup, with the other builds following suit. All three scale nicely per size, with stack heights growing much more proportionally than before. Chainstay lengths don't scale as well as the other figures, but do grow by a few millimeters per size to keep up with the front center growth. The EX bike should suit most riders in most places, with neutral geometry figures that delivers easy handling and a capable enough stability.

Suspension Design

Trek is using their classic ABP layout here, implementing a co-rotating pivot around the rear axle to deliver simple curves and suspension performance that is relatively unaffected by braking forces. As there are three models on tap here, there are also three distinct suspensions to talk about. Instead of getting into the weeds with each possible iteration of the links and shock mounts, I'll touch on the three main offerings.

The Fuel+ uses a lower shock mount flip chip to adjust progression, with More or Less indicating the overall shock progression. All three builds use either a 205x60mm or 205x65mm shock to deliver the 145-163mm of rear wheel travel, with the final travel figures dependent on which setup you land on.

There are also some secret menu options - I've included the likeliest use cases below, with the stock options highlighted in bold.

Fuel+ EX: 145mm travel, 205x60mm shock, High progression 21.3%, Low progression 16.4%.

Fuel+ EX Long-Stroke: 156mm travel, 205x65mm shock, High progression 21.9%, Low progression 16.6%.

Fuel+ MX: 150mm travel, 205x60mm shock, High progression 22.1%, Low progression 17.9%.

Fuel+ MX Long-Stroke: 163mm travel, 205x65mm shock, High progression 23.0%, Low progression 18.3%.

Fuel+ LX: 160mm travel, 205x65mm shock, High progression 24.2%, Low progression 19.1%.

There are both air and coil shock build options available, so folks should be able to find a combination of factors that suits their use case best.

RIDING THE
Fuel+ EX

Test Bike Setup

I was initially meant to test the MX variant of the Fuel+ platform, but after just one ride on that bike it became apparent that there was a critical assembly issue that needed to be addressed. The problem ended up being an out of tolerance spacer in the rear linkage, causing quite a bit of play while riding. I only note this to say: if something seems wrong, it's always best to go back to the shop and ask.

Once I took delivery of the Fuel+ EX, things were smoother. The Shimano-centric build kit was fine, save for one critical detail. Trek chose to spec Organic pads with the XT brakes, which are quiet and functional in dry weather, but fail to function once things get wet. I swapped them out for metallic pads, and proceeded happily.

The suspension package was a very familiar baseline, which made setup very quick and easy.

Testing Info

I rode the Fuel+ EX everywhere. The shorter-travel platform is fun on mellower trails, but capable enough to tackle some proper terrain. Thanks to the excellent battery range, I was able to explore some zones I rarely poke into, just to see what the view looked like from the top of that hill.



Climbing

It should be a surprise to nobody that a 43lb mid-travel eMTB climbs well. The motor plays an obvious role in that character, but it's not the only factor at play. Trek did a good job of managing the build kit here, keeping the Fuel+ EX light without sacrificing much in the way of capability.

The EX variant's geometry is the best suited to climb-heavy riding, with the steepest seat angle of the bunch. This is one of the main compromises in the do-everything frame approach Trek took here, as the effect of increasing fork travel only slackens the seat angle further as you go to the MX and LX versions of the bike.

Luckily, the EX's 77.3° seat angle felt steep enough for all the riding you'd expect to do on a trail bike, even at a fairly high saddle height as I had to run it. The pedaling platform feels even-keeled enough to keep you from dipping off the back of the bike too much, and stays composed in technical sections of trail.


As far as the motor feel goes, the TQ HPR60 falls right between the timeless power of a human body and the ludicrous speed of modern full-power eMTBs. You're not oblivious to the boost provided by the bike, but it's far more normal feeling than the 100Nm+ alternatives. Those who like using the motor to scrape up heinously steep and tricky climbs will probably want to look elsewhere, but for folks who want to augment their experience with more power and speed, this could be it.

Realistically, I think this motor and battery combo makes the best case for eMTB use to date. It's nearly silent, the aid provided is substantial without getting extreme, and it facilitates more laps / easier climbs / quicker rides as effectively as just about anyone needs. I know people love full-power bikes, but there's something really compelling about this take on the lightweight eMTB, even if the segment isn't massively popular.


Descending

Trek tends to do a very good job of delivering on the prompt for a given bike's intended purpose, without letting it stray too far outside the lines. While the Fuel lineup does get a bit rangy as a whole, the EX itself is a tidy, fun, and capable trail bike. The fact that it's electrified feels like a secondary element in some ways, as the overall weight and handling errs more on the bicycle end of the spectrum.

You still benefit from the extra heft that comes with the integration of a motor and battery, but the Fuel+ has an ease of handling that full-power bikes rarely achieve. Despite a build kit that doesn't prioritize weight, you get a bike that feels lively and engaging even when you're not plowing. This 43lbs / 19kg mark feels like a sweet spot in balancing that calmed feel you get from the eMTB and the more familiar feel of a bike without a motor.


As mentioned before, it seems to me that the EX was the primary option in the new Fuel+ lineup, with the geometry shifting shorter and taller as you increase fork travel to achieve the MX and LX models. That shift should work for the steeper terrain you'd expect to ride those bikes on, but many users might end up benefiting from a different size in the lineup to hit their ideal figures. For instance, I opted for a Large EX, and was happy with the 485mm reach; if I was shopping an LX, I may opt for an Extra Large, as the reach figures shrink significantly with that modification, putting the Extra Large at 498mm. Obviously, reach isn't the only figure of note, but it exhibits the trickiness of sizing the Fuel+, particularly if you think you might be swapping between variants.

The only on-board adjustment the Fuel+ has is the progression adjustment at the lower shock mount, and I found myself preferring the more progressive of the two options in just about every situation. The feel isn't radically different between the two, but the more active feel of the higher leverage position was nice, without taking away too much from the platform the bike provided for pumping and jumping.

That said, I think the majority of riders will probably be best served by the Fuel+ EX, relative to the other options in the family. The 145mm travel is plenty for most trail systems, and the bike feels intuitive and capable on a wide variety of terrain. Those looking to exclusively ride gnarlier trails will be better suited by the LX or the Slash+, but I found the EX to be a solid platform on steeper trails - you're just relying more on the handling than the displacement and maximum stability.

How does it compare?

All four of these bikes are viable options for those looking for something mid-travel or light weight in the eMTB world. There are plenty more to choose from, but these are on the short list.

Slash+: If you're getting a lightweight eMTB to smash out laps on steep trails, this is the one to buy. This bike still sits at the top of my list for lightweight eMTBs, and remains one of the best descending bikes I've ridden to date.

Vala: If you're not sure about the 60Nm output, and feel like you'd prefer a full-power experience, the Vala would be my pick. The Vala aims at a similar segment, but does so with the Bosch CX motor and battery system. That 600Wh capacity and 100Nm/750W power output adds about 5lb to the overall weight, but the Vala is equipped with burlier parts to match. Generally, it feels like a much more capable option, despite being close on travel.

MTe: Call it an upgrade pick. The MTe has the same frame travel as the Fuel+, but benefits from Yeti's excellent Sixfinity suspension system, which is easily one of the most composed and capable feeling we've ridden to date. The two are close on weight, even with the 580Wh battery, so it's ultimately a choice on subtle geometry differences, price, and brand.

Fuel+ EX: The Fuel+ is probably the bike most people should ride, but the capability and specialization of other options here will probably draw folks away. The Fuel platform is highly adaptable, but you can't swap between the EX/MX/LX options without a fair bit of work. The EX will suit the broadest band of riders, with easy handling and a happy medium feel to it.

Which Model is the Best Value?

Trek's lineup has seven variations on the Fuel+ EX right now, all priced between $7,700 and $11,500 USD. Shimano or SRAM, Fox or RockShox, the options are all fairly similar, with the choices coming down to which tier of component you want. If it were me, I'd probably opt for the Fuel+ EX 9.7 ($8,700), which has a solid balance of budget parts that still work nicely, all mounted to the fancier carbon frame. Having not ridden the alloy version, I can't speak to the performance, but the weight increase is substantial - you'd lose out on some of the normal bike feel that the lighter builds achieve.


Technical Report

Shimano XT Brakes: I'm happy to report a consistent and dependable bite point on these new Shimano brakes, thanks to the revised design and lower-viscosity mineral oil they're using. One detail I wasn't able to get dialed in to my liking was the free stroke, which always felt unusually long. I was able to get used to it, but would have preferred less travel in the lever to get the pads to activate.

Maxxis Minion DHF/DHR: It's been a while since I've had a review bike come with a DHF up front. A funny shift, considering how ubiquitous they were not too long ago. Luckily, the tire is still great if you trust it, hooking up nicely at a strong lean angle and clearing mud well. I think the neutral feel of the Fuel might cater towards an easier to handle tire for some newer riders, but maybe the DHF will teach good technique.

Fox 36 Fork: I'm a big fan of the new 36. It offers a comfortable ride, plenty of support, and adjustment that allows for a reasonably wide range of preferences. It felt like a perfect pairing for this bike, matching the capability well, even at the bike's upper limit. Folks looking to turn the EX into the LX might want a bigger fork chassis, but only because the 170mm travel on that version of the bike narrows your choices down.



Pros

+ Silent motor, excellent battery range
+ Open-ended build options with link kits
+ Neutral handling is intuitive and fun


Cons

- Battery reliability could be a concern
- One-frame-for-all system has inherent compromises
- Limited size range


Pinkbike's Take

The mid-travel lightweight eMTB isn't necessarily the segment of the electric mountain bike market that gets people stoked, but something like the Fuel+ EX might be the best choice for a lot of riders out there. The power output is usable, the range is excellent, and the ride quality feels neutral and fun on a wide variety of terrain. There are more exciting options out there, but this one gets the job done well.—Dario DiGiulio
