Review: 1Up Super Duty Hitch Rack

Sep 11, 2024
by Mike Kazimer

1Up USA has developed something of a cult following over the years – look at the comments under any bike rack review and you'll inevitably find a superfan espousing the wonders of these US-made, aluminum racks. The Wisconsin-based company's latest addition to their lineup is the SD, which is designed to hold bikes that weigh up to 100 pounds each, and is rated for extreme off-road use.

The one bike version (those always look a little lonely to me out in the world) is priced at $600, and adding on another tray bumps that up to $900 USD. Other optional accessories include a wheel chock that helps keep the front wheel from rotating – that's recommended for bikes over 75 lb, and is $110 per bike.

Installation

Assembling the SD rack isn't particularly difficult, but it does take some time. The instructions are clear and easy to follow, and all of the bolts threaded in nicely. The aluminum construction does help keep the weight down, but with two trays the rack still tips the scales at a little over 60 pound. One person installation isn't overly difficult, but another set of hands certainly doesn't hurt.

The hitch bar has an anti-wobble feature, where a large ball bearing is pushed outward to eliminate any slop. A special hex key is required to tighten to the rack – that plus the lock for the hitch bar helps keep protect your investment.

A slot in the hitch bar allows for 3” of horizontal adjustment, which can come in handy if you need room to allow the rack to clear a spare tire or something similar. There are also two different positions for the rack when it's not in use – one stores it vertically as shown in this article, and the other puts it at a slight angle for more clearance. On my Izuzu Trooper the second position made it possible to use the rack and still clear the full size spare tire.

In Use

Loading / Unloading

For riders who are more accustomed to bike racks with just one rotating arm the 1Up design can take a little getting used to. It gets easier with time, but I'd still say that when it comes to convenience those one-arm racks are quicker. With the 1Up, you raise both arms partway, load the bike onto the tray, and then push each arm into its final position

There is a new feature that's not immediately apparent that helps facilitate the loading process – the ratchet system for each arm can now be disengaged by rotating it up slightly, which means you don't need to push in the red button at the same time you raise the arm. It’s sort of difficult to explain on paper, so hopefully the video illustrates it a little better. Once the arms are close to where you want them to be, rotate the system downward and the racheting function is re-enabled.

Depending on how zesty you got when loading a bike, unloading it can require some force to take enough pressure off of the racheting mechanism. That means pushing the wheel arm against the tire – put your shoulder into it – and then the mechanism can be opened and slid backwards to allow the wheel out.

Again, all of this becomes fairly straightforward with practice, and there are upsides to the slightly more convoluted loading and unloading procedures. The biggest benefit to this layout is the fact that the rack doesn't contact the bike's frame at all – there's no need to worry about scuffed or scratched paint jobs.


Off-Road

I haven’t gotten too crazy with the SD rack installed; most of my off-road usage was on bumpy logging roads - I’d rather be out riding than trying to crawl up some silly steep slope inside a vehicle. That said, the rack does do a good job of securely holding bikes in place. The rack itself is exceptionally solid, free from any disconcerting swaying from side to side.

The optional wheel chock feature does make a difference, especially if you're going off-road with heavier bikes – I'd recommend it if that's a regular occurrence. It's also easy to take on and off, so it's feasible to just install it for those rowdier excursions.

Most of the motion occurs when the bikes tip forward toward the vehicle and then away from it after going over a bump. It’s a good idea to make sure you have enough clearance between your handlebar and the back window to accommodate some back and forth motion - deleting that glass deep in the boonies with the end of a handlebar wouldn’t be ideal.




What Could Be Improved?


The only real complaint I have about the 1Up SD has to do with the tilt mechanism. It’s a two-hand operation to tilt the rack upwards, and it seems very rudimentary, especially considering the price of the rack. When compared to something like Kuat’s tilt lever, which can easily be operated by one hand, 1Up's system isn't nearly as convenient.

1Up has offered an EZ Pull handle in the past that improves the system slightly, but not completely. Unfortunately, that handle isn't currently available due to a patent dispute, but 1Up's engineers are working on a solution that should be available soon.


Durability

The SD rack has lived outside for the last 5 months, and it’s still not showing any signs of wear. One of the arms did get a little squeaky, but a couple drops of lube on the pivot point quickly solved that. If anything does wear out or break, 1Up has an extensive selection of replacement parts - this is far from a disposable item. Plus, there’s a lifetime warranty if anything breaks during normal usage.





Pros

+ All metal construction, very well made
+ No frame contact
+ Holds a wide range of bikes and wheel sizes
+ Doesn't stick out much when folded up

Cons

- Tilt function could use improvement
- Loading / unloading process takes some time to get used to


Pinkbike's Take

1Up’s new Super Duty lives up to its name - it’ll easily and safely transport the heaviest DH or e-bikes out there. The price does start to climb pretty high once you factor in the additional cost of accessories like the wheel chock or EZ Pull tilt mechanism, but ideally this is a rack that should last as long as the vehicle it’s installed on (or even longer depending on where your car falls on the reliability scale). — Mike Kazimer
