It’s been a wild last 12 months for Revel. Back in April 2025, the brand went through financial turbulence, leading the original founder, Adam Miller, to repurchase it and once again take the helm. Since then, Revel has been full gas, picking up right where they left off before the hard times, and recently relocated their headquarters to Golden, Colorado

A few months ago Revel took top honors in our Pinkbike Trail Bike Field Test with the Rascal SL, thanks to its snappy handling, dialled suspension feel, and well-thought-out geometry. Now it’s time to see how those attributes carry over to the Rascal’s bigger, burlier brother, and Revel’s biggest brawler in the lineup, the Ritual.

The Rascal’s bigger brother is exactly that: a 165mm travel, 170mm-forked dual-29er marketed as an enduro race bike. It debuted quietly at Sea Otter 2025, replacing the outgoing Rail, alongside two other models in the same launch. That meant the Ritual didn’t get quite as much time in the limelight as some of the others, including the brand’s first foray into e-bikes with the ReRun.

As with all Revel full-suspension bikes, the Ritual uses its CBF suspension layout and offers relatively limited consumer-level fine-tuning. With the Ritual, what you see is what you get, no flip chips, no angle sets, and one chainstay length per size. That also includes size-specific wheel configurations: Small and Medium frames run a mullet setup, while Large and XL are dual-29-only.

I picked up the Ritual at last year’s Crankworx Whistler, and my first ride aboard it was a 2,000-meter slog. Since then, it’s become my daily driver for all types of riding thanks to its impressive mix of capability, snappiness, and efficiency. After nearly eight months on the bike, it’s finally time for a long-term review of this hog. Let’s dive into the details.

Frame Details


The Ritual is available in carbon only, no alloy version at this time, and uses Revel’s proprietary UD carbon layup comprised of high-end Toray T700 and T800 carbon fibers. It offers 165mm of CBF travel (more on that shortly) and, again, very little adjustability. What you see is what you get, and there’s something refreshingly simple and satisfying about that approach.

The Ritual is the first Revel frame to feature in-frame storage, accessed through the downtube. A cleanly tucked-in lever opens the storage hatch in a simple, tool-free process. Each bike also comes with a branded in-frame storage bag to keep contents from rattling around. Capacity is generous; you can easily fit a solid stash of tools and snacks. The locking mechanism feels very secure, and while the bottle cage mounts directly to the hatch, the robust lever caused zero issues throughout testing. Speaking of water bottles, the space is fairly limited and will not fit full-sized 750ml bottles, but it does accommodate most stubby 650ml bottles. There are also two accessory mounting bolts beneath the top tube for additional frame-mounted solutions.

Internal cable routing is handled via carbon tube-in-tube guides, making cable swaps or full frame builds relatively painless. There’s ample protection around the bottom bracket area and chainstays to keep noise down, and seat tube insertion is reasonable across sizes. Our size Large offers 300mm of insertion, enough to accommodate most 210mm droppers.

The Ritual is dual-crown compatible, provided the axle-to-crown measurement stays under 596mm. There’s no on-frame shuttle protection, though, which is worth noting for riders planning to run that setup.

Revel also put extra effort into beefing up the pivots on the Ritual, citing increased stiffness, improved bearing life, and easier maintenance. In practice, the rear end is impressively stiff laterally, and aside from one main pivot bolt that needed tightening after the initial shakedown ride, the bearings and hardware have been completely trouble-free. Since then, there’s been zero noise and zero issues.

Geometry

The Ritual is available in sizes Small through XL. Small and Medium run a mixed wheel setup, while Large and XL are dual-29 only. Reach ranges from 433mm on the Small to 508mm on the XL, with a consistent 64-degree head angle across all sizes.

Rear-center length is size-specific but remains relatively short for the category, ranging from 434mm on the Small to 441mm on the XL. The stack is relatively low throughout the range, favouring a more forward, over-the-bars climbing position. Stack starts at 622mm on the Small (90mm head tube) and increases to 645mm on the XL (115mm head tube). Seat tube angles range from 76 degrees on the Small to 77 degrees on the XL.





Suspension Design

The Ritual uses Revel’s CBF suspension design, developed with legendary bike designer Chris Canfield. CBF, which stands for Canfield Balanced Formula, differs from many suspension layouts that focus on the instant centre created by linkage motion. Instead, CBF centres the suspension’s behaviour around the centre of curvature.

In layman’s terms, the drivetrain forces are centred around the top of the chainring throughout the entire suspension travel. This creates a system with high pedalling efficiency regardless of where you are in the travel, translating rider input directly into forward motion without excessive sag or wallow, even on rough, technical climbs. On most multi-link suspension designs, the rear wheel doesn’t move in a single smooth arc; instead, the point around which the suspension rotates shifts constantly throughout the travel.

In real-world terms, the Ritual is an exceptionally efficient platform. Pedalling input feels like it goes straight to the rear wheel, with no noticeable squish, even when climbing through rough terrain.

The rear triangle connects to the front via two CNC-machined aluminum links, with bearings pressed into the links rather than the carbon for improved durability and serviceability. Revel states 17.5% progression through the travel, which is moderately low for this category. Despite that, I never experienced harsh bottom-outs, and Revel officially supports coil shocks for riders who want to go that route.

RIDING THE
RITUAL

Test Bike Setup

Story time. I picked up the Ritual during Crankworx Whistler and immediately threw it into the deep end on a big droup ride the included roughly 2,000m of climbing over 53km. I managed two laps around the park the day before for a basic shakedown, naturally opting for A-Line and Dirt Merchant.

That strategy turned out to be slightly flawed once I started descending on Blackcomb. The first laps down Crazy Train and Top of the World were significantly rougher than the jump trails, and the bike felt harsh. My feet were getting bounced around, and I started to worry the Ritual might be a dud.

At the halfway point in Function Junction, I borrowed a shock pump from the good folks at Chromag, let out a substantial amount of air from both the suspension and the tires, and did a bolt check, which revealed an almost fully backed-out main pivot bolt.

User error aside, I haven’t touched the suspension settings since. Those second-try settings have been excellent, and all linkage bolts have remained rock-solid through eight months of riding.

I’m running just over 30% sag in the rear at 240 psi, with 4 clicks of low-speed compression, 3 clicks of high-speed compression, and 14 clicks of rebound. Up front, I’m running RockShox’s recommended settings (80 psi) with rebound slowed by 3 clicks.

On my 2025 model, I found the stock Race Face cockpit a bit low for my taste. I would have preferred a 35mm-plus rise bar and had the stem spacers set to max below the stem. I also swapped the BikeYoke 180mm dropper for a OneUp 210mm, which fit without issue and eliminated the dreaded double-drop. Since then, I’ve made no further changes.

Climbing

The first thing that stands out when climbing on the Ritual is just how snappy and responsive it feels. Despite the large travel numbers, it accelerates and transfers power like a much smaller trail bike. This is clearly a product of the suspension design; the efficient power transfer claims feel spot-on.

The Ritual offers excellent traction and power on technical climbs. I never once reached for the climb switch during testing. Weight also plays a role here. At 34.5 lbs, the Ritual punches well below its weight class, adding to its lively feel. It’s a funny bike in that way, enduro numbers on paper, but a trail-bike personality until you point it downhill.

There are a couple of caveats. The seat tube angle is relatively slack, and combined with the shorter rear center, it can put more weight over the back wheel on steep, technical climbs. These traits won’t bother everyone, and some riders may actually prefer them, but they’re worth noting.

Regardless, the Ritual is one of the best-pedalling bikes I’ve ridden in this travel category.



Descending

Pointed downhill, the Ritual is immediately more playful than its numbers suggest. The CBF suspension does an excellent job of managing big impacts while staying supportive and predictable through rough, technical sections. The bike’s lighter weight makes it easy to maneuver and change direction quickly.

This was my first full dual-29er in a few years, and I was initially concerned about losing some manoeuvrability compared to a mullet setup. The shorter chainstays do a good job counteracting that, keeping the bike lively through tight sections, rolls, and flowy trails where popping and playing come naturally.

There is more pedal feedback than on some systems, especially compared to high-pivot bikes I've ridden in recent years, but once sag was properly set around 30%, it became noticeable but manageable.

Travel management is a strong point. The bike balances mid-stroke support with a supple end stroke, feeling composed without becoming a full-blown plow bike. I never experienced harsh bottom-outs, even when using full travel.

The chassis feels stiff and composed, with minimal flex when pushing hard. It’s also impressively quiet, no creaks, rattles, or unwanted noise. That alone earns points.

The trade-off is that it doesn’t feel as confidence-inspiring at full-tilt race pace through the absolute roughest terrain. Instead, it leans toward being an incredibly capable, fun, all-day bike that still has enough travel to bail you out when needed.



Livability

After eight months of riding, the Ritual has proven to be rock-solid. Aside from that initial pivot bolt check, the hardware has been completely hassle-free. No noise, no looseness, no play.

For this review, I pulled the linkage apart and found all bearings running smoothly and cleanly despite months of regular use. Overall, long-term durability has been excellent.

The SRAM XO drivetrain was fuss-free throughout months of riding.

Technical Report

Rock Shox Ultimate Suspension: You really can’t go wrong with top-level suspension from any brand these days, and the fact that Revel chose to spec top-tier suspension on all build kits for the Ritual speaks to their focus on ride feel. With all the adjustability on tap and their highest-performing dampers, the suspension performance is about as good as it gets, delivering a smooth, buttery feel regardless of which build kit you choose.

Bike Yoke Dropper: I do like the Bike Yoke dropper. It performs well, and its reset feature allows riders to quickly restore full extension if it develops sag. That said, post sag seems largely a thing of the past for most droppers these days, and I would have preferred a simpler OneUp post, as well as a longer drop on the size large. As it turns out, this exact spec change has been implemented for 2026 models, so all is well.

Continental Tires I’ve been impressed with Continental tires in recent years. Although the grip isn’t quite as all-out as a Maxxis setup, which is more commonly spec’d on bikes these days, the traction is still high and predictable. The standout feature for me is longevity. In my experience, they last two to three times longer than other brands. I’ve been running the same set on the Ritual since last August, and although they’re starting to show wear, they’re still very much usable. The casing feels supported and robust, with no flats to speak of over months of riding.

Audio Levels This is a big one for me. A noisy bike, even if it’s not technically a performance issue, can really change the ride experience. A quiet, muted bike just feels better. The Revel Ritual is about as quiet as they come, even after months of riding. Even in the summer, when creaks tend to come out of the woodwork with dust flying everywhere, the Ritual remained impressively muted. A big plus in my opinion.




Which Model is the Best Value?

Given that all models feature top-tier Ultimate suspension from RockShox and Maven Silver brakes, except for the upgraded XX top-tier version, I’d say the best value here is the Eagle 90. They still get the same tires across the lineup as well, so a lot of the really important bits aren’t being skimped on. Where they differ, the drivetrain has proven reliable, simple, and cost-effective, and I know there are a lot of cable enthusiasts out there.

The wheels and hubs are still solid options with the Crankbrothers Synthesis alloy wheels, and beyond that, the finishing kit is completely ready to go out of the box. At $5,499, the Ritual Eagle 90 build is arguably up there with some of the most value-oriented options on the market, and most riders should be just fine hopping on that stock build and having absolutely no qualms.

How Does It Compare?

Okay, this might not be the closest comparison, but it paints a good picture, and they’re also the last two enduro bikes I’ve ridden, so I feel comfortable making some educated comparisons. Both bikes are advertised as enduro bikes and fall into a similar travel class, though the Forbidden actually has 5mm less rear travel. That’s basically where the similarities end.

Pricing-wise, I’m going to refer to the best value comparison: the Ritual Eagle 90 vs. the Dreadnought 3. The Ritual comes in at $5,499 USD with Ultimate suspension, Maven Silver brakes, a OneUp dropper and cockpit, and Crankbrothers Synthesis alloy wheels. The Dreadnought 3 retailed for $6,699 USD (and is currently on sale for $5,024), which gets you Select suspension, Maven Bronze brakes, the same wheels and rubber, and of course, both share the same drivetrain. Purely value-wise, the on-sale Forbidden is slightly more competitive, but at the regular price, the Ultimate suspension and upgraded brakes on the Ritual are a better value. Both are strong options depending on what riders are looking for.

Pricing aside, I think the big thing to highlight here is that despite similar marketing and categorization, these bikes are about as dissimilar in ride characteristics as you can get. The Ritual is snappy, lightweight, and responsive, and will be just as at home on a big, all-day mission as it will be on rowdy enduro tracks. The Dreadnought, on the other hand, is a much heavier bike thanks to the burlier chassis and the added complexity of the idler. It’s more of a slog to pedal compared to the nimble Ritual, but it certainly outguns it in all-out downhill capability.

A couple of other Dreadnought notes: it has more adjustability, with the ability to change wheel size as well as chainstay length. Regardless, the main point I’m trying to make is that travel numbers and marketing copy never tell the whole picture. The Dreadnought is the option for someone who wants maximum descending capability at the expense of pedalling efficiency and comfort, whereas the Ritual is more versatile, lighter on its feet, and geared toward fun across a wide range of scenarios, while still having enough travel to get you out of trouble in a pinch.

Pinkbike's Take

Following the Rascal SL’s success in our Field Test, the Ritual had a high bar to clear. While categorized as an enduro bike, that label doesn't quite capture its versatility. It manages to pair the climbing efficiency and low weight of a trail bike with enough travel to handle heavy terrain. By prioritizing a responsive feel and manageable weight over pure high-speed stability, it works across a much broader range of trails than most 160mm bikes. There is some pedal feedback to navigate, but for anyone not focused purely on race times, it’s a negligible trade-off for a bike that handles almost any terrain with ease. It’s a capable, all-day platform that just happens to be a blast to ride.— Stephane Pelletier

justinfoil (Mar 9, 2026 at 12:20)
 Same an any other dual/multi-link: enables decoupling of various kinematics. Usually most useful for separating the anti-squat and anti-rise, and often leverage, curves. Some dual-links layouts are also very good at helping to decouple anti-squat from chain-tension/pedal-kickback, or at least make it less dependent on gearing, by adding significant AS coming from the forces coming of rear wheel's acceleration instead of the force coming from the chain pull (specifically DW-link, Dave has stated it previously, as I think it's a significant part of the patents). Thus you can get decent AS with minimal PK, which is not something that single-pivots are amenable to.

You can't see it in any of the pics here, but there is a link and two pivots behind the chainring. However, you shouldn't need to be able to see that to know it's not just a single pivot. You can see the upper link and the solid rear triangle: obviously it's not a single pivot at the bottom because that just wouldn't work at all.

justinfoil (Mar 9, 2026 at 19:17)
 @Jaks5: It's pretty obvious from looking just at the upper link that the center of rotation is not the "exact same". The the whole rear triangle rises up (lower link rotates CW looking from DS) to follow the arc of the upper link. It also looks like that might reverse about 2/3rds of the way through the travel, but hard to tell just from a picture (I'm not counting pixels for this armchair analysis). That's definitely something different than a single pivot.

While you can't see much of the lower link in these pics, you can see that it's sloped towards to back (rear-most pivot is lower than front pivot, front pivot just slightly above chainring), and that's where some of that magic seperation of kinematics comes from. The force from the rear wheel under power is going to want to pull that link further into that downslope orientation from the top of the travel, adding AS, and is going to push it into the travel when deep in travel, reducing AS. Meanwhile, the pull from the chain will do the opposite through much of the travel, thus balancing AS. On the other side, when on the rear brakes, the backward force is going to want to pull that link straight back, and that competes with a torque trying to rotate the rear-triangle into the travel. That's the cool shit: internal forces between the links and frame parts "balancing" (pun intended) against external forces for get the desired kinematics.

dlazzati FL (Mar 11, 2026 at 9:38)
 Center of rotation (CC) is related to the motion of a point, the rear axle. Instant center (IC) is a property of the motion of an extended object, which for mtbs is typically the structure that supports the rear axle and rear brake mount. In a single pivot design both rear brake and axle do a circular motion around the pivot CC and IC coincide and are fixed. Instead, with multi-link like CBF you can maintain a circular or quasi-circular motion for the rear axle and let the rear break move independently (think of it as the break position rotating around the rear axle as the suspension compresses). Alternatively, think of your forearm as your chainstay, with the axle at your wrist and the break on your fingertips. If your elbow flexes but does not move, you get a constant CC at the elbow. But you can rotate your hand around your wrist (do a "ciao" motion) and that changes the instant center of your hand.
So with a dual link one can keep the CC steady but let the IC go as far as wanted, effectively allowing for an independent tuning of anti-squat and anti-rise. Hope this helps, really would need a video to be more clear...
