For those who enjoy long days out on the bike, the problem of how to carry food, water, and gear can have an outsized effect on the success and ease of a given objective. As a result, there always seem to be novel approaches to this prompt, from on-bike baggage to the infinite variety of backpack-type bags on the market. A new addition to the list of options is the Epic 14.4 from Arctos Packs, which features a massive capacity for both storage and water, with a lumbar-style weight carry and shoulder straps for stability.
Arctos Epic 14.4 Details

• 14 liter total storage
• 4 liter water storage
• Oversized YKK zippers
• Material: 210D ripstop nylon
• Weight: 1142g/2.5lb w/ bladder assembly
• Price: $175 USD
• arctospacks.com

The Epic 14.4 features 14 liters of gear capacity, with a dedicated 4 liter water bladder. Weight is kept as low as possible, with the shoulder carry only involved in stabilizing the pack and holding the water hose. The design is reminiscent of a wildland firefighter's pack, which employs a similar carry method to distribute weight and allow for easy upper-body movement.




Fit & Packing

I'm around 6'3", with what I'd call a narrow build. Typically hip packs end up being mid-torso packs if they're not cinched down tight enough, and they're just as liable to rotate around my waist if not loaded enough to keep the back planted. All that in mind, the fit of the Arctos pack was lower than I initially anticipated, sitting below the waist and directly above the hip joint. This is their recommended setup, as is a loose fit on the shoulder straps.

Finding a comfortable fit was easy for my 32" waist and taller torso, but I wanted to see how it fared on a different body. My girlfriend is about 5'5", and she found the pack to sit a bit too high on the hips, and took up more room than desired. Addressing that fit issue, Arctos already has an Epic 10.3 pack in the works, with 10 liters of storage and a 3 liter bladder. The smaller overall size should work better for smaller bodies, though the low-slung position might not suit folks with big butts.

Five zippered pockets, one slide-in stash pocket.
The easy-access hip pockets are huge.

Packing the Arctos Epic was easy, thanks to the relatively massive volume. As I'll get to in the comparisons, there are some larger items that won't fit in the pack, but standard tools, jackets, and food will easily fit in the main pockets and side stashers alike. Compared to other hip packs and backpacks I've ridden with, the Epic feels like a crammer's dream, with big, uninterrupted pockets you can stuff without too much thought. Those who like to dial in their internal organization might be bummed at the lack of options here, but there's room for bags-within-bags if you're wanting to keep em' separated.

Four liter bladder and mega pocket.
This fairly narrow opening hides another very large pocket.
Small soft pocket for glasses, crystals, lizards.
Foam standoffs and a rigid back panel keep things in place.

Ride Impressions

The 14 liter nugget that is the Arctos Epic can get pretty heavy, with 4 liters of water and whatever gear you might be packing, but you wouldn't know it once you've strapped it on. That was the most notable first impression I had of the pack, and it continued to impress through the test period. The large side pockets allow you to distribute the load well over your hips, and once dialed in the fit keeps the weight squarely settled down low. Wide webbing, thick materials, and well-placed compression straps keep things fairly well secured, with the shoulder harness handling the side-to-side.

I never found the weight to be too low or rearward on climbs, which was a pleasant surprise given how the pack feels when standing upright. Like any pack, you could put all the weight in the wrong place and screw up the balance, but I found the Arctos spreads the base water weight very well, thanks to the rectangular bladder.

Very steep trails are where hip packs can get the most out of sorts, sliding around and jumping up the body as you move and groove. I found the Arctos sat still better than most, though the pack did shift more than the vest-style packs I've come to use more. The shoulder straps keep the pack from jostling side to side, but the pack can still shimmy up when all your weight's on your hands and the catch berm at the bottom is your only goal. Of course, that's not the most typical use case out there, so it's key to mention that when you're ripping around upright, the Epic 14.4 feels stable and comfortable, with a secure fit that stays in place off jumps and drops alike.

Sweat seems to be a big reason people ditch backpacks and vests, but in my mind it's more relocation than elimination. Hip packs - especially those with large capacity - are just as warm, it's just lower on the body. The Arctos has a decent footprint on the lower back, so expect some perspiration there. The foam risers do a good job of keeping most of the shell fabric from making contact, but nothing quite compares to the packless feel.

The Mission Workshop Hauser 14L, with a full set of Trail Boss tools inside.
The Arctos Epic 14.4 in attack position.
Comparisons

My go-to pack for short and long rides alike is the Camelback Chase Vest. The size is ideal for my use cases, and I've never found the fit of it to be uncomfortable or cumbersome. It can require some creative packing to get a full day's worth of gear inside, but rarely am I left wanting for more room. That said, there are certainly circumstances where a bigger pack is warranted. On those days, I usually reach for the Mission Workshop Hauser, a 14 liter backpack with a more traditional silhouette. The Chase Vest weighs around 460 grams with a 3L bladder, and the Hauser weighs about 950 grams with the same bladder.

My trusty little Chase vest.
The Henty Enduro pack.

The Hauser is the closer proxy to the Arctos in terms of storage capacity and overall weight, so that'll be the primary comparison here. The most notable difference between the two is the orientation of the storage - vertical for the Hauser, horizontal for the Epic. The Epic keeps the weight low and away from your head, with no interference with your helmet on steep trails and features. The Hauser does push against the back of the helmet if you have the fit cinched down, and carries the weight in a generally higher spot on the back.

My primary use for larger packs is to haul trail tools, namely those from Trail Boss. The segmented handles and heads are perfect for toting in and out of dig days, and have been in heavy use this fall. This is where a traditional pack is hard to beat, as the Arctos simply can't fit long rigid items, despite the 14 liter capacity.

Though I haven't used it myself, the Henty Enduro pack came to mind when I first saw the Arctos design. RC reviewed one of these a while back, and was quite a fan of the water capacity and comfort. The key differences come down to storage capacity and weight, as the Henty's 5 liter capacity is a good deal smaller than the Arctos' 14, but the 550 gram weight also halves that of the Arctos.


Pros

+ Nothing else offers this capacity and carry position
+ Much more stable than hip pack
+ Carries weight well

Cons

- Could use more internal organization
- Pockets are cavernous, but won't fit large tools
- Looks are sure to be polarizing



Pinkbike's Take

For my purposes, the vest I use on most rides has become a reliable companion, with a fit that works well for me. That said, if I lived somewhere where water were more scarce, I can imagine the Arctos coming into steadier rotation, thanks to the more comfortable weight carry it provides. Three liters in the vest can get cumbersome, four isn't really an option, and gear storage is pretty minimalistic. Again, that program works for me, but if you're someone who likes to bring a ton of items on a given ride, then the Epic 14.4 is worth a closer look. — Dario DiGiulio
