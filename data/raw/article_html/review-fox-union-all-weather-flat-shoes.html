Review: Fox Union All Weather Flat Shoes - For the Wettest Days

These shoes aren’t messing about. Look at the size of that gaiter - that’s the first thing a waterproof shoe should have. Without a healthy overlap under your pant cuffs, your toes soon start to swim in foot juice, no matter how impervious the shoe itself is.

The Fox Union All Weather Flats are essentially a gumboot specifically designed for riding bikes. They share a similar construction and appearance to the other Union Boa shoes in Fox’s lineup, at least below the ankle. One small difference is that the All Weathers only features one dial to lock your foot in place.

Union All Weather Flat Shoes
• Boa Fit System closure
• extra tall gaiter
• Ultratac outsole
• D3O insole
• Sizes: 37-47 (41-46 in half sizes)
• Colors: black, adobe
• Weight: 550 grams (per shoe, size 41.5)
• MSRP: $329.95 USD
• foxracing.com
When it comes to grip on the pedal pins, the All Weathers call on the Ultratac rubber, like the standard Union Boa Flat shoes. If flat pedals aren’t your jam, there’s a clip version that looks identical, aside from the cleat bed on the sole.

Puddles are irrelevant to these shoes.

Ultratac rubber is decently grippy. The concave sole detracts from the otherwise tactile connection to the pedals.

Overlap is key. Thankfully, Fox figured that out and went sky high.

Price and Weight

For how much wet weather gear costs, we sure do treat it poorly. You wouldn’t dare jump in a bog with your white Jordans, but that’s exactly what the Union All Weather shoes are ready for. That being said, $325 USD is a lot of dough.

Waterproof socks are a decent alternative, but the name isn’t as accurate as one might expect - they will get wet and clammy. You’ll also lose the close connection to the pedals with those thick, rubbery socks.

These hydrophobic moon boots aren't tremendously light at 550g per shoe. However, much of that is owed to the mega-high and very functional gaiter. I don’t necessarily see that number as a hindrance while riding, but it may bother the weight weenies.

Pull tabs front and back - they'll come in handy. So far, the stitching hasn't missed a beat.

Bunching can occur in forward foot movements.

Fit

Pulling these puppies on takes practice and patience; getting them off was nearly impossible at first. I nearly separated the back of the sole trying to step on the opposite foot. Sitting down and wiggling out of them in stages is the best method I’ve found. They will break in, ever so slightly. Still, it’s a comically strenuous job to remove them.

Once they’re on, the gaiter has a comfortable level of snuggness once your foot is planted. I found the fit true to size, just like all of the other Union Boa shoes I’ve tried - typically, I go for a size 8.5 US in riding shoes.

Considering these will be worn in the winter with thicker socks, and the width of Fox shoes in general isn’t the most roomy, I could see bumping up a half size being necessary for riders with wide feet. Fox also provides a second set of taller arch supports. I found the lower option worked best for me.

With the pant cuffs rolled down or zipped up, it's hard to differentiate the Union All Weather shoes from summer kicks.

Performance

Like I said, wet weather gear is a hefty investment. Should the ride connection be given up for gaining waterproof qualities? First, it’s unfair to assume waterproof shoes ride like standard summer issue shoes, especially those with insulating properties. Two major elements are being added to resist the weather, so the extra material will take away from the dexterity. Those features are well taken care of. However, some level of tactile contact with the pedals is crucial for control and less binary for those who choose to clip in.

So, what about that all-important “feel”? Looking lengthwise down the sole underside, you can see that it has a concave shape. I’ve come across this conundrum before with a pair of Scott SHR-ALP BOA and wondered why they were so hard to find the perfect spot on the pedals with.

Once you do find the LZ, the grip is decent. The tread isn’t as tacky or deep as that found in Five Ten’s special Stealth Rubber formula, but it’s not far off.

I’m a fan of the Boa Fit System on pretty much any piece of riding kit. It may not be the Boa laces' fault entirely, but the gaiter material behind the highest piece can scrunch up and cause a pinch point in extreme downward heel flexion.

The upper materials of some waterproof shoes are fragile. These are tough and showing little signs of wearing out.

So far, so good - no holes from pedal pins.

Durability

I’ve given these a thrashing already through a drastic shift into fall here in Squamish, but I’m hoping they break in further and the sole flattens out. In that area, the rubber still looks brand new, despite my tendencies to chew through the flat pedal shoe soles at a specific point from the pedal pins.

Judging by the two years I’ve spent bashing on the Union BOA Clip shoes, I’m confident that Fox has nailed their TPU rubber joining methods and carried that forth on the All Weather model.

Two vulnerable areas I thought might wear quickly are behind the uppermost BOA lace crossing and the cuff’s pull tab stitching. The gaiter is made from an incredibly tough material, but neither area has shown any weakness.

How Do They Compare?


Fizik Nanuq GTX: $299 USD. The sole is too stiff and the cuff verges on being too low, but with the right pair of pants, you're feet will be dry and warm through the deepest slush.

Shimano GF800 GTX: $220 USD. If dexterity and grip are your main focus, these Shimano shoes are among the best. It's a shame they aren't warmer or legitimately waterproof.


Five Ten Trailcross GTX: $200 USD. This model from Five Ten is an interesting concept; they're light, flexible, and almost waterproof. Grip and control are high, as you'd imagine from anything bearing the Five Ten logo.

Endura MT500 Burner: $189.99 USD. Another strong contender in the warm and waterproof realm, however, the rubber tackiness sits lowest on the scoreboard against these other options listed.


Pros

+ Fully waterproof materials.
+ Water is less likely to splash under the pant cuff thanks to the extra-tall gaiter.
+ Sleek design - no velcro to wear out or laces to tangle.

Cons

- Extremely difficult to remove.
- Not as tactile on pedal surfaces as other less water-resistant options out there.
- Forefoot gaiter fold can bunch up when leaning far forward.
- Performance comes at a high cost.



Pinkbike's Take

This is the wet-weather riding shoe, or boot rather, that I've been waiting for. In the Union All Weathers, there's no longer a need to avoid puddles - just pick the best riding line and you'll stay dry. They're not perfect, but I'm willing to give up a fraction of grip and feel on the pedals for the rest of their hydrophobic properties.— Matt Beer
