“Don’t mess it up!” That was the brief from Pivot’s enduro team for the V5 Firebird. The new bike is more about evolution than revolution, with none of the suspension or drivetrain weirdness developed for their Phoenix downhill bike. It has tweaked geometry and suspension, plus impressive adjustability and storage features that Pivot hopes will take their race-winning enduro platform up a notch.

The Firebird is designed to be a capable enduro race bike, with 165 mm of rear travel and a 170mm fork. But it’s more versatile than those numbers would suggest thanks to impressive pedalling efficiency and balanced geometry that make it happy to cover ground and rewarding on flatter trails. It’s also got room to carry more snacks and tools than most of its rivals.

Frame Details

The most obvious change to the frame is the addition of storage under the bottle cage. The downtube is large enough to swallow a regular inner tube plus a pouch for tools and snacks. The door is particularly well-designed with a secure yet easy-to-use latch that doesn’t rattle even with a full-sized water bottle attached. There's also a magnet to hold a master link on the underside of the door.

There are also three bosses under the top tube that are intended to carry tools but, at least on the XL frame, can accommodate a Fidlock water bottle in addition to a full-size bottle on the downtube. There are two more bosses under the downtube plus a small door in front of the bottom bracket that is to assist with cable installation but could accommodate an emergency gel or two.


This is one of the most adjustable enduro frames on the market. A clever flip chip at the rear axle allows the chainstay length to be increased by 8 mm. It’s one of a small number of frames that offer this feature while retaining SRAM’s UDH and Transmission compatibility. Interestingly, Pivot made use of a patent they had for a similar swinging dropout used on their fat bike. Even though the drive side dropout rotates when the chainstay length is extended, Pivot say there’s no need to adjust the B-tension. However, they do recommend using a chain with one more link with the longer setting to stay within SRAM and Shimano’s chain length recommendations. That makes it more of a set-and-forget feature than something to chop and change.

The chainstay length is also size-specific, with 7 mm separating a small and XL. This difference is accommodated with the front triangle only – the pivot locations change slightly relative to the bottom bracket – so the rear triangle and links are common to all sizes. This is nowhere near enough to maintain the ratio of front-centre to rear-centre across the sizes. But combined with the adjustability, there is a 15 mm difference between the smallest and longest combination, which is enough to notice. More specifically, the chainstay length in the small sizes is particularly compact, a plus for short riders who struggle to fully unweight the front wheel. The long setting in the XL size (tested) is not especially long.


A second flip chip adjusts the bottom bracket height by 5 mm and the frame angles by 0.4 degrees. This isn’t enough to keep the geometry the same with 29” or 27.5” wheels, so it is lower and slacker with a mullet setup. Pivot say either setting can be used with either wheel size, but the high setting is strongly recommended with MX wheels.

Yet another flip chip at the lower shock mount alters the leverage curve for a more linear or progressive suspension feel. As if that weren't enough, the oversized ZS56/56 headset makes it possible to install an aftermarket reach adjust or head angle adjust headset.

While the rear triangle is the same for all sizes, the front triangle’s carbon layup is "tuned" so taller riders have a stiffer frame and smaller ones get more flex. Cables are clipped to the inside of the downtube rather than running through tubes within the tube; this makes it easier to change brake routing depending on which side of the handlebars you want the rear brake to go. As you would hope, the cables come out of ports beside the head tube and not through the headset.

It's fair to assume that the downtube storage and flip chips will increase the frame's weight compared to the outgoing model. The 2022 version weighed 1 kg less when we tested it, albeit with some lighter parts and a smaller frame size. Fair to say the new Firebird isn't the lightest, but it's hardly heavy by modern enduro standards.

Out back, Pivot are sticking with the SuperBoost 157 mm rear axle standard, which they say makes a 30% stiffer and more reliable wheel. An obvious downside of this is that there are fewer replacement wheel options than the 148 standard, but on the other hand, the SuperBoost wheel is generally interchangeable with those on DH bikes, which could come in handy for those who own a DH and enduro bike. The rear triangle is shaped to provide maximum heel clearance, and Pivot say this is actually easier to achieve with a SuperBoost rear end.

It also uses a press-fit bottom bracket. According to Pivot’s Chris Cocalis, “Press fit doesn’t creak; poor tolerances creak.” Pivot say their manufacturing process ensures reliably precise alignment.

There are three colors to choose from: White Gold, Yolo (yellow) and limited edition Purple Galaxy.

The frame is covered by a lifetime first-owner warranty and a lifetime bearing warranty – if the pivot bearings wear out they’ll send a new set for free (fitting not included).



Suspension Design

Pivot did consider using a six-bar suspension layout like their Phoenix downhill bike for the Firebird, but decided any marginal benefits on the descents were more than offset in terms of climbing and complexity.

The DW4 design offers consistent levels of anti-squat, which helps the bike to pedal with minimal unwanted bob or slouching across a wide sag window. It also provides consistent anti-rise levels, which could make braking behaviour more consistent. The overall result is a bike that resists pitching whether pedalling or braking, so the chassis remains stable and centred in its travel.

Anti-rise.

The flip chip at the lower shock mount offers two options for frame progressivity: 32% when the eyelet is mounted towards the rear and 27% towards the front, when measured from 0 to 100% of travel. The more progressive setting is similar to the old Firebird and is more progressive than most without being an outlier. This is the setting I used most and found the balance of support and bottom-out resistance to be about right with the single green volume spacer that comes installed with the shock.

Pivot say the suspension travel is barely affected by the progressivity fip chip or the high/low setting, but switching to the longer chainstay will increase travel by a few millimetres. I measured the real-world travel on my test bike at a shade over 170 mm in the long setting.



Geometry & Sizing


Compared to the previous Firebird, the new model is 5 mm lower, 0.2 degrees slacker and with a slightly steeper seat tube in the smaller sizes. The charts above show the geometry in the stock configuration: the low setting with 29” wheels and the high setting with MX wheels.

By my measurements, the seat angle at my pedalling height measures 76.5 degrees in the low setting. That's slightly slacker than claimed, but shorter riders will get a steeper position because of the offset seat tube.

Note the XS is available as a mullet only. Pivot's sizing guidelines are as follows:

X-Small: 4'11"–5'4" | 1,50–1,63 m
Small: 5'2"–5’8” | 1,57–1,73m
Medium: 5'9"–6' | 1,73–1,83m
Large: 5'11"–6’4” | 1,80–1,93m
X-Large: 6'1"+ | 1,85m+

At 6’3” / 191 cm, I found the XL to be a great fit, but, interestingly, Pivot's guidelines wouldn't rule out the large. Bear in mind that the reach can be adjusted by +/- 5 mm with an aftermarket headset.

Test Bike Setup

Testing kicked off with four full days of shuttle laps in Finale Ligure, followed by a handful of rides on familiar soggier trails in Scotland’s Tweed Valley.

Pivot provides a handy sag indicator, which makes initial setup much easier than measuring and calculating sag with a non-RockShox shock. This corresponds to 33% shock sag, but I soon added 10 psi to the shock, which left me with 19 mm (30%) sag. I also removed one of the two volume spacers from the fork to balance out the suspension feel and stuck with the single green spacer in the shock.

After playing around with most of the geometry adjustments, I settled on the longer chainstay, low flip-chip setting and more progressive suspension. At 190 cm / 6’3”, the XL size fit me well. I did swap the 50 mm stem for a 40 mm, but I was happy with either on this bike.

Tire pressures were 23 psi & 27 psi in Finale and 22 psi and 26 psi in Scotland.

I rode Little Champery in Finale Ligure seven times thanks to shuttling from Finale Bikers. This allowed me to compare most of the Firebird's features (BB height, chainstay length and stem length) back-to-back. It's also a trail where the bike felt particularly impressive, both in the sweeping turns up top and the rough rock gardens at the bottom.


Climbing

The Firebird is particularly stable and efficient when pedalling. The suspension is remarkably still whether spinning or sprinting, but it feels especially good when accelerating out of the saddle. It's not the supplest when pedalling over bumpy terrain, but it’s not terrible; the suspension does a decent job of conforming to the terrain while still staying on top of its travel.

My only criticism is the seat angle. It’s designed to avoid putting the rider too far over the front wheel for riding the rocky traverses around Pivot’s Phoenix HQ, but for me, the position feels a touch off the back for the brutally steep, but not especially complicated climbs we have in the Tweed Valley. With the saddle slammed all the way forward it’s not far off my ideal position, and the bike still climbs well because the suspension stays high in its travel, but I think the XL could do with having the seatpost a little more upright. After all, how many people have their saddle slammed all the way backwards?


Descending

The stable suspension platform continues on the descents. The bike feels sturdy and solid, perhaps helped by a stiff wheel and frame package, and the suspension is well-damped and measured. It doesn’t give away travel unduly and never wallows. There’s excellent cornering support and a responsive feel that belies the travel and works particularly well in supportive turns. At the same time, the suspension responds well to small bumps and tames the most hectic terrain. It feels like there's plenty of travel in reserve and gets more impressive at higher speeds and on harder hits. It just doesn’t cosset and cushion you as much as some enduro bikes (the Deviate Claymore comes to mind).

Having said that, there is a lot of scope to adjust the suspension feel with damping, volume spacers and the frame’s flip chip, so if you want a more pillowy feel that's what it's there for. In the stock setup with one volume spacer and 28-30% sag, I found the end of the travel only on the biggest hucks-to-flat and sometimes felt the bottom-out bumper doing its thing towards the end of the stroke. But it’s not a dramatic or harsh ramp: it’s more a soft catch for when you’re getting silly.

The handling is similarly well-judged. The XL’s wheelbase is stable without being overly stretched out, which retains some of the agility you want for enduro-style riding. But the generous stack height and 35 mm bar offer a roomy cockpit out of the box. I started with the shorter chainstay setting and found it to be easy to manoeuvre without feeling out of touch with the front tire in loose, open corners. Swapping to the longer setting isn’t the night and day difference some might imagine. In a back-to-back test, the difference is subtle, but it adds a little confidence in long-radius turns. The change to the suspension is probably more noticeable if the shock isn’t adjusted to compensate for the extra leverage - it felt slightly more supple. It takes a little more force to initiate a manual, but to me this isn’t a downside so much as a difference.

The high setting with 29" wheels offers extra clearance for tight awkward sections and doesn’t detract from cornering confidence when you commit to the turn, but to me it made the bike feel more nervous on fast, steep sections. Pedal clearance is ample even in the low setting. Again, it's a relatively subtle change.


The Firebird is a solid-feeling bike in rough and technical terrain yet remains responsive and efficient on flatter sections. It’s more on the sporty rather than squishy side, but it’s never harsh or nervous. It feels like an appropriate tool for a wide range of riding from DH shuttles to trail rides.



Don't say there aren't enough options.

What's the Best Value?

It's hard to justify spending $2,200 more than the starting price as the "best value" option, but I think if value-for-money is your primary concern you'd be better served elsewhere. The Firebird is an unashamedly bougie bike whichever model you choose. With that in mind, I think the Pro X0 Eagle Transmission build that I tested makes the most sense relative to its rivals. It has the top-spec suspension components that you want in a posh enduro bike without the Live Valve or carbon cranks this bike doesn't need.

As if there aren't enough options in the chart above (I had to shorten the model names from Transmission to TR to fit them all in), the Pro and Team models can be upgraded to Reynolds Blacklabel Enduro Pro carbon wheels for an additional $1,200. But I wouldn't bother with that.

All in all, there are 64 combinations of spec options, colour and wheel sizes available. Also, Europeans will have a choice of Newmen wheels instead of DT or Reynolds.

How Does It Compare?


The Firebird fits into an elusive category of big-hitting enduro bikes that are happy to climb and don’t feel out of place on mellower trails. Some other bikes on that list would be the Merida One-Sixty, Canyon Strive, Orbea Rallon and Scott Ransom. The Pivot wins practicality points for its non-headset cable routing, impressive warranty, superior storage options and helpful adjustability. It’s probably the best-pedalling bike on that list with the shock left open too, although the Merida’s steeper seat tube gives it an edge when the climb points skyward.

When descending, the Pivot feels most like the Scott, with a stout-feeling frame and well-controlled suspension. I would say the Pivot’s suspension is more consistent and predictable, perhaps thanks to the 2026 X2 shock. On descending performance alone I would put the Firebird a hair ahead of that particular pack, although the Merida and Canyon are considerably more affordable.



Technical Report

Fox Transfer Seatpost: My test bike came with a 210 mm seatpost which was a little shy about returning to full height. It works fine if the lever is pressed fully and the saddle is unobstructed but if the saddle catches on your shorts or the lever is half-pressed it gets stuck a few centimetres from top-out. This is a little disappointing for a brand-new high-end post.

SRAM Maven Silver brakes: While the power and lever feel are excellent, the rear brake on my bike had an inconsistent bite point out of the box that persisted even after multiple re-bleeds. Following SRAM's piston massage procedure has since solved this (at least for now).

Continental Enduro casing tires: I’ve spent a lot of time on the DH casing Kryptotals but the enduro casings impressed. They stood up to four days of shuttling in Finale and provided predictable grip on Italian rocks and Scottish mud alike. They do squirm noticeably more than the DH version, but they are appreciably lighter and faster-rolling. I would prefer the SuperSoft compound up front though.



Pros

+ Well-balanced overall ride and handling rarely feels out of place
+ Superb pedalling efficiency adds verstaility
+ Impressive frame storage and adjustability
+ Lifetime frame and bearing warranty

Cons

- Expensive
- Seat angle could be steeper, at least for the XL size
- Super Boost rear axle limits wheel options

Pinkbike's Take

Mountain bikes are all about fine-tuning and subtle tweaks these days. The new Firebird isn’t a game-changer that’s head and shoulders ahead of its rivals; nor is it radically different to its predecessor. But it does ride like an extremely well-oiled machine. Everything from the suspension to the geometry feels well-balanced and refined. It also offers exceptional storage and adjustability which give it a USP. Its impressive pedalling efficiency makes it a good choice for those who want a capable enduro machine that’s got your back on the toughest terrain but doesn't sap the life out of mellower trails or punchy climbs.
— Seb Stott

privateer-wheels (Apr 2, 2025 at 11:22)
 @sdurant12: It creates more asymmetry in the hub, and then lower spoke tension on the brake side as a result. Most 157mm DH are symmetrical (or close too), so you can do equal tension on both sides, which is better for spoke life.

Also, wider flanges create sharper angles where spoke exits the rim. In many cases drilling angles on rims are not adequate to compensate for this, and you get added stress on rim/nipple, and then sharp bends on the spoke right out of the nipple, which is hard on spokes.

I'd prefer more symmetry and then achieve the stiffness though more equal tension on both side of the wheels, and have spoke path be more agreeable without sharp bends and added stress on all components beyond the hub.
