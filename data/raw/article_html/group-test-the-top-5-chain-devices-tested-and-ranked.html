There are plenty of nerdy debates in the world of mountain biking, and this is certainly one of them. Whether you call it pedal kickback, chain slap, chain flail, or some other cryptically vague fourth option, some folks have taken issue withe the impact the drivetrain can have on suspension performance. Encapsulated by the rare but undeniably lovely chainless run, there's certainly something to the concept of trying to mitigate just how much effect the chain can have on the rest of your ride - and that sensation is exactly what these companies are trying to offer.

In an attempt to get down to exactly what each of these can offer on your next ride, we went out for a day of repeat laps in order to see which devices worked as intended, and which ones didn't quite make the grade. Pinkbike's tech editors have also been using these devices over the past six months in order to form longer term impressions. Dig into the video for all the nitty gritty hems and haws, or read on below to get a feel for which device shook out on top.

The influence of the chain on a given bike's suspension characteristics varies significantly, but noise reduction is a common denominator that all bikes can benefit from.


STFU Trail
• Fits 9-13spd drivetrains
• Trim to fit, no adhesives
• Dual compound rubber
• DH 7spd, 10spd, and singlespeed variants
• Weight: 62g
• Price: $40 USD
• stfubike.com

One of the first and arguably the simplest in the game, STFU's chain damper is a clever way to isolate the chain's movement from the rest of the bike. By capturing the chain's range of motion within its soft rubber hoops, the STFU prevents the chain from contacting other surfaces, and keeps the overall flail to a minimum. As it doesn't have any mechanical complexity or direct involvement with the drivetrain, the STFU falls into our passive category.


Rali Chain Mag
• 5 size options
• Trim to fit, sticky tape
• Neodymium magnet and rubber fins
• Holds chain when not pedaling
• Weight: 93g
• Price: $33 USD
• ralibike.com

Rali's Chain Mag is essentially a stick-on chainslap protector with a trick up its sleeve. That trick comes in the form of a strong magnet, housed within some soft plastic towards the front of the stay guard. The idea here is to hold your chain down while coasting, to prevent it from bouncing up and down on the chainstay. Once pedaling force is applied, the chain lifts up and does its thing, unaffected. Though the Rali does try to hold the chain down for part of the ride, it's still essentially a passive device.


Ochain R
• Active chainring spider
• 4 deadband options & lockout
• 7 crank interface options
• Chainring rotates backwards under chain load
• Weight: 218g w/ 34t chainring
• Price: $415 USD
• ochain.bike

Ochain's lineup of devices have become a common sight on the World Cup DH circuit, with a variety of offerings rounding out their lineup. On test we have the Ochain R, which is their most current offering, with 4 positions of externally-adjustable deadband, as well as a lockout to get a sense of the baseline. The external dial allows you to select between 4°, 6°, 9°, and 12° of deadband, with the active spider clocking backwards as the chain tugs on the chainring. When you push the pedals forward to accelerate or climb, you take up that 4-12° rotation and bottom out the springs, where things feel firm and normal.


Rimpact Chain Damper
• Active chainring
• 30-36t chainrings
• 5 crank interface options
• Chainring rotates backwards & forwards under chain load
• Weight: 242g, 32t variant
• Price: £280 / $371 USD
• rimpactmtb.com

Like the Ochain, Rimpact's Chain Damper is housed in the architecture of a chainring, but Rimpact has taken the integration one step further. Instead of packaging the active portion of the device into a spider onto which you mount a chainring, the Chain Damper is itself a chainring, with all the active bits housed inside. There are various size and fitment options, but the motion within is non-adjustable - you get 8.6° of rearward rotation, and 4° of forward rotation. When you put enough power into the pedals, the springs bottom out, and you're back to a firm pedaling rotation as with a standard chainring.


e*thirteen Sidekick
• Anti-kickback rear hub
• Boost, Superboost, DH spacing
• 3 adjustable deadband options
• Freehub rotates under chain load
• Available as hub or full wheel
• Weight: 424g, Boost XD variant
• Price: $500 USD
• ethirteen.com

e*thirteen's Sidekick is a hub-based system, integrating all the complexity and monkey-motion into an existing component, as opposed to introducing complexity elsewhere. The Sidekick has three positions of deadband adjustment, with 12°, 15°, and 18° to choose from. Adjustment requires the removal of the wheel, but is otherwise quite easy. In order to keep that deadband position consistent, the "pawl pusher" resets itself as you coast via its own single pawl, with all the drive-pawls disengaged. As you pedal forward, that pusher engages the drive system, and the bike kicks into motion.


Climbing

All five devices target bikes that go up and down hills, so it makes sense to discuss their climbing performance before digging into their descending characteristics. While the passive devices don't really make themselves known, there are quirks and compromises to the active devices that are worth touching on.

As the passive devices in the test, the Rali and STFU devices should have zero effect on climbing performance, so long as they're installed correctly. Your bike will pedal as you're used to, with the chain now traveling through a rubber hoop or near a magnet.

The Sidekick has a noticeably deadband before engaging the drive pawls, producing a feeling similar to that of a low-engagement hub. Unlike a typical hub, that deadband and slop in the pedaling characteristic is very consistent and therefore easier to intuit once you've gotten used to the feel. There is no soft catch on the pedal input, so once you've engaged the drive pawls, it's a firm application of power. I can imagine the 18° option being quite annoying in just about any technical climbing situation, but the lower figures are easy enough to live with.

Those who climb a lot will probably find the Ochain to be the easiest active device to abide by, as the springs suspending the spider are light enough to bottom out under fairly little power output, with elastomers to clutch the pedaling force as you engage the hard stop. Due to the external adjustability and short-throw deadband options, you could even tailor the Ochain to suit a more climbing-biased riding characteristic. I never chose to lock the device out for climbs, but in theory one could do so, just as you would a shock. In an even more touchy-feely sense, there are situations where the rubber bump stops can feel like a slip clutch - namely when you're really mashing on the pedals, and traction is limited.

The standout device on the climbs is the Rimpact, but sadly not in a positive sense. Their Chain Damper uses very heavy springs to suspend the chainring, requiring quite a bit of consistent force through the pedal stroke to keep them bottomed out. When you're not applying that force in a perfect cadence, the sensation is similar to having a rounded-out square taper bottom bracket, with the cadence feeling notchy and awkward. I was able to get used to the sensation on some bikes, on well-graded roads, but when riding with friends or wandering around it's harder to maintain the required pedaling form. After a healthy amount of feedback to this end, Rimpact iterated on the concept and released what they've dubbed the Trail Spring kit, a lower-force pair of coils that feel much easier to pedal. I found these fairly easy to get used to, but others still struggled to adapt when compared to the other active devices on test.


Descending

Each of the devices on test have some influence on the bike's noise level while descending, but they don't all play a role in changing the dynamic feel of the suspension. Again that's a realm primarily dealt with by the active devices, where drivetrain forces are met head-on.

Like the climbing performance, the Rali has essentially no effect on the dynamic feel of the bike when braking or hitting bumps. It's stuck to the chainstay, doing its thing, letting the chain move as it may. In our experience the magnet never held the chain through serious compressions, doing nothing to counter any flail or tug that occurs.

The STFU does more to manage chain flail, and can calm some of that sensation before reaching the feet, quieting the feel of the bike down through chatter and heavy compressions. On some bikes, the STFU loops might be the only intervention needed to optimize the descending feel. Ultimately, both of these are meant to tackle noise more than feel, so more on that later.

On all three active devices, the shared descending sensation can be summed up in a few points. When braking through rough terrain, the feet feel more even-keeled and the chassis seems to pitch less as a result. Compressions and pre-loading the bike moves you through more of the stroke before chain force pushes back against you, reducing some of the effect of anti-squat. In a sense it can feel like you're using more travel more often - whether that's a benefit or not ultimately depends on the use case.

Riding the Rimpact up hills may not be for everyone, but it does an admirable job of quieting chain forces on the descents. The stiff springs keep chain flail at bay, and the fixed deadband handles most small and medium compressions before you feel the drivetrain engage. There is a reduction in the pedal feedback when pumping the bike, but in a way that feels less free-floating than the other active devices. Where the Sidekick can simulate the feeling of your chain being gone entirely, the Rimpact is more of a quieting of the system, with a quiet tug still apparent. Even on the descents, I preferred the Chain Damper with the lighter trail springs installed, as the float felt a bit smoother, and the pedaling performance wasn't as noticeably effected when grabbing some cranks out of a corner or into a feature - with the heavy springs, I found myself shifting up one or two gears to push through the spring resistance and get a firm pedal feel.

As alluded to above, the Sidekick profoundly changes the feeling of the bike's rear suspension, providing a chainless sensation for however far you set the deadband's travel. The effect of this hub is so significant, I found myself taking a few runs to get used to the altered dynamic feel of the bike, adjusting weight bias and braking points to account for the different feel. When the deadband is set to the right adjustment point, you can effectively move through the entire stroke of travel without feeling any chain tug, so it can take some adaptation when pumping, cornering, and jumping the bike. Because the cassette can still clock forward quite freely, you occasionally feel the clunk of it reaching the end of the deadband, but that's more relevant to the noise section coming up.

The Ochain has a firmer feeling deadband than the Sidekick, but with reduced overall travel and a smoother end-stroke stop. Those looking to delete all chain feedback through the entire stroke will probably be better served by the Sidekick, but the Ochain's smaller deadband options pair nicely with shorter travel bikes and situations where you want to reduce chatter through the feet and still retain some of the normal anti-squat feel of the bike and be able to get on the gas fairly quickly. I found the Ochain easy to adapt to, predictable through all riding situations, and adjustable enough to suit almost all bikes.


Noise

Rapid fire here, because thankfully noise is a simpler problem.

The Rali does almost nothing to help chain slap in our experience. The magnet block is quite hard, and while the magnet can hold the chain down in some ideal scenarios, it lets go quickly in most real-world riding and allows the chain to slap around as normal. On some bikes, we found that it added noise, as the magnet block's fairly hard surface generates quite a bit of impact noise as the chain flails about.

STFU was one of the first devices to market to combat chain noise, and it remains one of the best. Almost all chain slap noise is eliminated, save for the harshest compressions where the chain impact the underside of the chainstay. When used in conjunction with one of the active devices, true nirvana can be achieved.

Though the stiff springs can be challenging to adapt to, the Rimpact does do an admirable job of quieting chain slap. That said, the lighter Trail springs are equally effective in almost all situations, save for the hardest impacts.

The long-travel of the Sidekick's deadband takes up a lot of slack when the chain is flailing about, but plenty of noise is still generated when slamming the rear end into holes. Because there isn't any soft internal catch in the hub, once the cassette bottoms out at the end of the deadband, the chain continues to clock and smacks around. Most smaller-impact noise is eliminated through, and combining the Sidekick with the STFU dampers manages to eliminate just about all noise.

Thanks to some rather soft elastomers and light spring force, the Ochain keeps almost all chain noise at bay, especially in the longer deadband settings. On all of our test bikes it ended up being the best standalone option for noise reduction.


Service & Livability

Folks looking to keep their bikes simple and maintenance-free will be best served by the Rali and STFU options, as installation is essentially the first and last thing you have to do with them.

The Rimpact is fairly simple to service, with internal markings and stops to keep the springs in check when putting the device back together. Luckily so, as it has a tendency to creak after fairly little use, requiring cleaning and lubrication more often than the other active devices. One other note is the overall width of the body, which had a tendency to trap rocks between the inner plate and bashguard or bike frame. This happened often enough on a couple bikes to warrant a mention, though it won't be an issue on all platforms.

Ochain also features guides and internal marking to try to ease service, but in our experience it's significantly less pleasant a job. The adjustability adds some complication to the task, and loading the four internal springs and ancillary parts can be quite a frustrating task until you've completed it a few times. Luckily the service interval is fairly long, with creaking cropping up only after many months of riding. A note on the device as it comes stock - the provided chainring bolts are flimsy at best, and should be replaced by a more robust option. This may change now that Ochain and SRAM are one in the same, but having had a few of the stock bolts crack it's worth a mention.

With the active element integrated directly into the rear hub, the Sidekick's maintenance intervals and ease follow suit. Cleaning and lubrication are ultimately the only tasks to complete, and luckily the assembly procedure is very easy. Concerns of durability were common upon release, and I've been putting a massive amount of mileage on my original hub to see how well it would hold up. For a full year of minimal maintenance, it survived and continued to work perfectly, then one day that abruptly ended. Pedaling uphill, I felt a sudden catch in the hub's freewheel motion, and when I went back to pushing on the cranks, the freehub spun forward and never engaged again. It seems that somehow the pusher pawl got crimped and was unable to extend, leaving the drive pawls unengaged. One warranty later and I'm back on the hub - I like to assume this was a fluke, but we'll see how the second one holds up.

After sending the Sidekick hub back to e*thirteen, they found that the issues was due to a pre-production steel ratchet ring, and they haven't seen any failures of production ratchet rings in the field. You can read the full statement from e*thirteen here.

Conclusion

Favored by all our testers, the Ochain felt like the best combination of predictability and performance, with the added bonus of doing a very good job of keeping the bike quiet.

The Sidekick offers the most dramatic feel difference, and the noise it allows can be addressed by adding some STFU loops to the equation.

The Rimpact is a solid option for park bikes and DH riding, but the stock stiff springs make pedaling too awkward to recommend to anyone. The lighter Trail Springs are far more usable, and have most of the same benefits. If adjustability doesn't appeal, and you're not sensitive to an altered feel while pedaling the bike, it could suit.

Sadly the Rali Chain Mag fell flat on all of our test bikes, despite even trying to optimize setup to make it work. The magnet fails to meaningfully retain the chain, and in many situations just added noise. Add to that the incompatibility with certain bikes that have too large a chainstay-to-chain distance, and it feels like a second pass is warranted.

The STFU might just be the people's champ in this test, with longevity, easy installation, and serious effect on the chain noise while riding. It's cheap, requires no maintenance, and makes a real difference.

If a friend asked - and many have - I'd recommend the Ochain or the STFU for most applications.
