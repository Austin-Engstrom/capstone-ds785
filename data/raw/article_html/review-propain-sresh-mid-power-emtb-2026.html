Mammoth-sized e-bikes can rip up hills with batteries that outlast most riders’ agendas, but there’s something more familiar about the handling of a lighter “SL” eMTB. That’s where the Propain Sresh SL fits in perfectly - it’s able to tag along with full-powered rigs, yet doesn’t ride like a tank.

With a lightweight carbon frame, 150mm of travel, and a choice of either rear wheel size, its user selling points position it as the perfect happy-medium e-bike. At the center of that equation is TQ’s motor, the HPR60. It embodies the quiet operation and natural power delivery that we’ve come to appreciate from the HPR50.

As a consumer-direct brand, Propain offers complete and semi-custom build kits. This includes picking out your own paint and logo colors, but also modifying suspension, drivetrain, and battery sizes. Their sales model is appealing with aggressive pricing, however, you can go wild by choosing boutique parts, like the Trickstuff Dirrettissima brakes on our $10,193 USD package.

Frame Details

Bearing the same base name, the Sresh SL has the same trail manners as the full-powered Sresh - each bike has 150mm travel, MX wheel capability, and is only available in carbon fiber, even the geometry numbers are only a shade apart - the motor and battery size are primarily what distinguish the two.

With those weight-reducing components, the Sresh SL positions itself as a competitive player in the mid-power e-bike category. Pound for watt-hours, it's on par with the Specialized Levo SL. Granted the Sresh SL can be built up lighter, the full-powered Amflow PL Carbon weighs a few hundred grams less than the bike we have in for review. It begs the question, what's the point of these so-called "SL" e-bikes? A few years ago I would have told you that SLs descend better, but since Avinox (DJI) debuted their industry-leading power-to-weight ratio motor on the Amflow PL Carbon, I'm not so sure.

Propain rates the Sresh SL as a category 4 frameset, greenlighting it for enduro riding and mid-size bike park features. The rest of the Sresh SL carries many of Propain bike’s features along for the ride, such as the Pro 10, dual-link suspension design, optional internal headset routing, and a flip-chip at the upper seatstay pivot for changing rear wheel diameters.

When writing up the First Ride report, I found that my position rode heavily on the front wheel. After chatting with Propain, they suggested installing a 170mm to raise the front end, an option that they approved in the frame specifications later on. Now, when you scroll through their online build configurator, you can select burlier enduro forks like the Rock Shox Zeb and Fox 38 in that burlier travel bracket.

Motor System

Building on the success of the TQ’s first E-bike motor, Propain chose the quiet and compact HPR60. The torque has been bumped up to 60 Nm and 350 W of power, while maintaining virtually identical packaging to the original HPR50 model (50 Nm, 300 W).

Molded into the top tube is TQ's own LED screen. A single button controls the power; a quick press turns the motor on or adjusts the assist mode, while a longer five-second hold shuts the bike down. The motor assist can also be adjusted from the wired handlebar remote. Although it’s slim in nature, the angle at which the wire exits the remote caused conflict with the Trickstuff brake lever.

One of the largest factors that dictates a e-bike’s weight is the battery. Propain offers the size MD, LG, and XL frames with two battery options, 360 and 580 Wh. Due to the length of the downtube, the 580 Wh battery won't fit in the size small frame. To make up the difference, you can attach the 160 Wh range extender. The custom mount replaces the water bottle cage, so you’ll have to find other ways to carry H2O. If you really want to minimize the weight on board, you could remove the downtube battery and use just the range extender.

Suspension Design

Pro 10 is the sole suspension design used across Propain’s mountain bikes (excluding their kid’s bikes). The solid rear triangle rotates on two short co-rotating links. The swingarm has one vertical brace on the non-driveside and allows for easy shock removal. A cable guide attached to the lower link keeps the brake line articulating smoothly.

The Sresh SL meshes with a variety of air or coil shocks from Fox, Ohlins, and RockShox. Some of Propain’s longer travel bikes use a trunnion mount, but the Sresh SL runs on a 210x55mm stroke shock. There are flip-chips to alter the travel or leverage rate on the frame, though.

Most suspension designs feature a leverage rate curve with an upwards-facing parabola, but the Propain bikes have a downwards-facing parabola. Those links move in such a way to produce a moderately progressive rate, but more recognizably, an increase in anti-squat partway into the travel.

Geometry & Sizing

Propain sticks to a certain identity across all its bikes. They’re following along with the trend of lengthening rear center numbers, pushing seat tube angles forward, and increasing stack heights. This promotes ample front wheel grip and an upright climbing position.

Typically, Propain builds four frame sizes, each separated by 30mm jumps in reach. The Sresh SL is available in SM, MD, LG, and XL, but the gaps between frame sizes are not evenly distributed. The LG comes in with a reach of 473mm (high MX position) and the MD at 448mm. Luckily, the small is still “small” at 423mm, but super-tall riders may be left in the cold as the XL tops out at 498mm. All chainstays measure in at 450mm, regardless of frame size.

At the top of the seatstay lies a flip-chip in the pivot to fit a 27.5 or 29” rear wheel, but be careful. The same geometry won’t be retained. With a 27.5” rear wheel installed in the high mode, the head tube angle sits at 63.8. The effective seat tube lands at 77.4 while the chainstays stretch to 450mm. Putting the Sresh SL in the low setting kicks the head angle back to 63.6 degrees. A 29” rear wheel in the low setting results in the steepest head angle possible at 64.5 degrees.

Test Bike Setup

Propain sent over this custom-built Sresh SL with a SRAM Transmission shiftset, Fox Factory suspension, DT Swiss EX 1700 mixed wheels, and Schwalbe Radial Gravity tires. Overall, it’s quite extravagant with the shiny Trickstuff brakes, however, I did run into a few issues outlined in the First Ride.

A 2026 Fox 36 resolved the air spring issues with the previous fork and allowed me to leave more steer tube for a higher bar height. A good chunk of time was spent on the 160mm fork before switching to a 170mm travel fork as advised by Propain. A Formula Selva V fork, which was also in for a review, fit the bill in terms of travel.

Pressures in the rear shock hovered around 190 psi. That number gave a dead even 17mm of sag, or 30%. The shock tune in the Float X provided was CMSSB024/RM002 with a 0.1 cubic inch volume spacer installed. The rebound was set five clicks from closed and the compression only four from closed.

I mainly rode the 180m post with 50mm of usable post exposed. I don’t need a 200mm dropper post, but I think an extra 20mm would have helped lower the saddle out of the way in some scenarios. Aside from that, the 35mm rise handlebar was cut to my usual 770mm width with 20mm of spacers under the stem.

I enjoyed riding the Sresh SL in the High motor assist setting the most, and found that it didn’t chew up the battery overly enthusiastically. 1.5-2 hour rides in that mode would get me home with 20-40% battery left. Most of the routes I picked off would be considered Squamish classics, without going too far into the deep end of the stunt pool.




Climbing

I was beginning to get worried there for a minute; we hadn’t seen much activity from TQ since they made a splash with their HPR50. Thankfully, they’ve stuck it out and are still setting the bar high. The HPR60 retains a compact size and silence under power. It glides up the hill, undetected by anyone not listening closely.

On paper, the boost in power and torque to the HPR60 doesn’t sound massive, but on the trail, it’s noticeable. Putting the HPR60 in the highest assist still won’t match the capabilities of most full-powered e-bikes, but that’s not the HPR60’s full intent. Instead, it’s all about smooth, natural power delivery. In that respect, it never puts out an overwhelming burst or cuts the power early. I’d give it a gold star for being top of its class.

A pesky groan somewhere in the motor’s bearings developed early on. It only creaked occasionally under extreme side loads or max pedal efforts, though. I inspected the motor mounting bolts and crank arm interface, but never got to the bottom of it. That experience hasn’t been consistent with my time previously spent on TQ equipment or other riders I have spoken with, so I’d still rate the overall experience high. TQ does stands behind their product with a two-year warranty on both the motor and battery systems.

As far as the Sresh SL geometry and suspension go, the ride is a familiar space after spending time on other Propain models. The seat tube angle is steep, and the chainstays are long enough to keep the front wheel from lifting on any climbs possible with the Sresh SL. I find the seated position and rear end length settle down the need to redistribute body weight when you point up a steep pitch. Another plus is that the front wheel doesn’t understeer when turning into switchbacks.

Sometimes a steep seat tube angle can put unwanted pressure on the rider's hands, but that’s not a major concern on the Sresh SL - the angle isn’t too extreme. A 120mm head tube length on the size large frame and 35mm bar option helps the case here.

Under power, the suspension holds itself up in the travel well. Like all of Propain’s bikes designed for pedalling uphill, the shock orientation places those handy lockout levers close to the lower link. Flipping that on the fly means pulling off a yoga stretch, or more safely, stopping and waiting for spinning components to come to a stop.

Range

When it comes to the overall experience of riding e-bikes, I believe we’re getting close to an ideal power-to-weight ratio. The 580 Wh battery matches the other efforts of the Sresh SL. It doesn’t ride like there is a bag of bricks strapped to the downtube, and there’s enough juice to clock 2000m of climbing, or more, if you can resist using the higher assist modes.

If you’re keen to ride for an extended time, there’s always the 740 Wh battery option (excluding the size small frame) or the range extender.





Descending

Overall, the Sresh SL checks a lot of boxes for what I look for in a mid-travel, mid-power eMTB. The motor is light, the weight distribution feels dialled, and it descends quietly. Between the battery and rear-wheel options, its adaptability is high. Propain made a wise choice to extend the fork limits. As expected, a longer 170mm fork and taller 50mm bars helped retract some of my initial impressions.

It’s hard for me to put pen to paper to describe why I didn’t totally get along with the descending characteristics of the Sresh SL. Mainly, I found the rear suspension to dive into the first half of the travel. Even after bumping up the air pressure to achieve 26% sag, and slowing down the compression, an underlying wallowy trait followed. Effectively, it can help absorb small bumps and give excellent grip when you aren’t forcibly weighting the bike. The downside is that the suspension suddenly pushes through the travel when trying to pump through rhythms on the trail or boost extra high off of jump faces.

To me, that “hinging effect” the Pro 10 suspension exhibits governs the dynamic balance of the bike. It’s similar to how I’d describe other FSR bikes with a top-tube-mounted swing link, a la Rocky Mountain or Ari, handle. The knock-on effect of that is when riding super steep trails, the pitch of the bike can change. Maybe it’s just a “me” problem, but I never felt 100% confident nosing into trails that you need the utmost confidence for.

With that said, while riding more casually or on less daunting trails, the Sresh SL levels out nicely and is pleasant to ride. The rear triangle is on the softer side and follows the contours without getting knocked offline too easily. I’d also like to point out that there’s minimal chainslap and the motor clutch doesn’t rattle whatsoever.

One smaller spec-check is the dropper post insertion length. The tube tapers near the bottom to give clearance in order for the shock to clear as the links compress towards each other. The Sresh SL arrived with a 180mm dropper and worked for me, just. Slamming that post leaves 40mm exposed. I’d wager that most riders choosing a size large would appreciate a 200mm dropper, which may pose limitations for riders seeking more drop.





What's the Best Value?

Consumer direct brands can sometimes offer better value. Where you live can influence the model availability and the landed cost, so watch for those added fees. More to the point, the Sresh SL only comes in two models in North America, versus the five options available in Germany.

On our side of “the pond”, building your own custom Sresh SL starts at $5,999 USD, then the Signature Spec 1 and 2 add another grand each time. I figured I could ditch the SRAM GX AXS shifting for a Shimano XT mechanical combo and use the cash to include a longer 170mm Zeb Ultimate fork. The savings earned on the Magura MT5 brakes allow for a Fox DHX2 coil shock to be added to the mix.

All in, the custom build sits at $7,759 USD, marginally cheaper than the Signature Spec 2.

My custom build: RockShox Zeb Ultimate 170mm, Fox DHX2 Factory coil shock, Crankbrothers alloy Enduro MX wheels, Magura MT5 brakes, Shimano XT mechanical drivetrain, and a 580 Wh battery.

How Does It Compare?

The carbon finish and travel numbers are about all these two lightweight e-bikes have in common. They’re intended to cover the same types of trails, but their ride dynamics are very drastically different.

The Sresh SL and most Propain bikes in general force the rider’s weight on the front wheel. It takes an assertive effort to ride further back on the bike and not let your weight influence the steering too much. Whereas a Levo SL takes the opposite approach: shorter chainstays require the rider to lean forward and push weight onto the bars wheel when riding low-angle trails, or else the front wheel can start to slide. The benefit is that the Levo SL is a whole lot easier to handle in tight corners and more relaxed on steep, surfy trails.

Even though they’re both light for eMTBs, Sresh SL is much harder to play on when the trail does provide defined jumps. Again, I’d point partly towards the balance of the bike, but also the suspension. I found there to be a shade of wallowyness which can sap some energy when trying to press into rhythms on the trail or gaining some extra boost on jump faces.

Comparing the weight-to-range is tricky because the Sresh SL’s stock battery is bigger (580 vs. 320 Wh). Ripping up to the top of the Diamondhead trail network gives me a solid reference point. On the Levo SL, I’d have to tone down the assist level to “Trail” in order to muster out 1000m, whereas the Sresh SL can almost double that in the “High” setting. A weight penalty of 2.3 kg (5.07 lb) has to be paid, but considering you can climb faster on in the “High” setting too, it makes the HPR60 motor a compelling player in the SL game.

Technical Report

DT Swiss EX 1700 Spline wheels: Ah, my dear old friend, the 36-tooth Star Ratchet hub, how I love and hate thou. It’s dead simple to work on, easy to find parts for, upgradeable and isn’t too buzzy. The rims have been stellar, but a stripped set of ratchets left me jaded, and stranded way out in the middle of a tech XC maze with a useless e-bike. I slapped in a 54-tooth ratchet ring after I limped my way out. If I were spec’ing a e-bike, I’d be looking for alternatives.

Trickstuff Direttissima brakes: These are bougie brakes. They add $700 USD to your custom-built Sresh SL versus a set of SRAM Maven Silver brakes, but I’m not convinced they’re worth the extra cost. My main concern is that they’re challenging to keep running consistently. Storing the bike vertically will likely introduce air into the system. They also lack adjustment beyond lever reach, so setting the left and right lever to feel the same is nearly impossible, given how difficult they are to bleed.

Fox 36 Factory Grip X2 fork: This is my first hoon on a 2026 36 fork, and boy does it ride swell. The level of grip is more than noticeable between the new and the old; it is drastic. The change from rebound to compressive is much less sticky, as is the breakaway into the travel - give the new Glide Core “floating” air spring a round of applause for that performance gain.

Sixpack Millenium 35 stem: On two occasions, the stem twisted on the steer tube. It overlapped the steer tube completely and was tightened to spec with a torque wrench. Was it bad luck with the steer tube and stem tolerances added up on the low side? Who knows. Luckily, I wasn't moving fast during both of those instances. I swapped it out for another stem after that second scare.




Pros

+ The TQ HPR60 is a standout performer in every way (quiet assistance, no rattles downhill, simple app).
+ System weight is impressive for its ride time.
+ Descends like an unassisted bike.


Cons

- Ride dynamics put a lot of weight on the front wheel.
- Not the most supportive rear suspension.



Pinkbike's Take

Propain has summed up all the options that riders could want in an SL e-bike and packaged it nicely. Their Pro 10 suspension characteristics that bring a defined sense follow the Sresh SL, where comfort overrides support. If that floats your boat, then the TQ HPR60 make the Sresh SL a hot contender in the mid-power eMTB space.
— Matt Beer
