Review: Lazer’s New Impala KinetiCore Helmet

Nov 18, 2025
by Dario DiGiulio

Shimano's acquisition of Lazer reinvigorated the brand, and broadened their catalog to include more mountain bike-specific models. The new Impala represents the pointy end of that development work, with new safety technologies and industrial design to improve on their current offerings.

I've been a fan of their helmets for a while now, with the Jackal and Coyote both spending plenty of time aboard my head. The Coyote in particular is a strong value lid, with features and safety that compete with the best of them. The Impala has a sleeker look and some nice features, so it felt worth spending some time in.

Impala Kineticore Details

• KinetiCore DualCore two-layer protection
• Magnetic buckle, detachable camera mount
• 3-position visor
• Weight: 415 grams (size L)
• Safety credentials unknown
• 5 colors
• MSRP: $199 USD / 199 EUR
• lazersport.us

Ventilation

The Impala is well ventilated, with ample holes through the shell and a hidden trick up its sleeve. That trick comes in the form of the KinetiCore foam protection, which allows for far more airflow than your average helmet. The fins/ribs/protrusions have deep channels in between each other, allowing for circulation at slower speeds than you'd expect. As a result, Lazer's helmets tend to feel cooler than the average, even despite their large liner pad.

That liner is a bit of a weak point to me, as the foam-filled fabric gets rank faster than more minimalist options out there. The Polygiene fabric feels like lip service more than anything, as the pads got stinky just as fast as anything else I've used recently. You can remove them to wash the horrors away, but that does reduce the lifespan of the liner.


Technology

Lazer continues to use their KinetiCore shear system to protect your brain and all it's memories, as opposed to an off-the-shelf system like Mips. KinetiCore consists of a bunch of small foam protrusions inside the helmet that are meant to give way in the event of an impact, allowing the head to move independently of the helmet's shell.

Beyond the performance in safety tests, KinetiCore's other benefit comes via the static nature of the design. As opposed to shear systems like Mips, KinetiCore doesn't wiggle or creak during use, and requires no setup or maintenance to continue to work. Additionally, if you do take a hit and deform the foam, you'll know to replace the helmet, leaving less of that decision up to chance and guesswork.

Unfortunately, the safety credentials aren't yet available for the Impala, but based on their numerous 4 and 5-star Virginia Tech helmets, I'd assume this will do fairly well in kind. That said, it's good to see any and all proof before putting a lot of faith in a plastic and foam bucket.


The Impala does include a plastic universal mount (aka the two-loop GoPro mount), for adding a light or camera to the top of the helmet. It slots cleanly into the apex vent, and stays put nicely. The one downside is the somewhat flimsy construction allows a light or camera to bob around if there's any weight to the object, so I'd stick to lights that don't have a built-in battery to avoid the jostle. For a closer look at the finer points here, head to the video review.

Technically a technology, this helmet also includes a Recco reflector. These passive reflectors are essentially body recovery devices for avalanche incidents, and always feel a bit superfluous on a piece of bike kit.

Fit & Eyewear

The cradle adjustment on the Impala has a very wide range, allowing you to alter how deep it feels without much effort. I found the rear dial easy to use, and sturdy while riding, without any loosening or jostling to speak of. Cradle fit systems like this can change position if you take the helmet on and off frequently, but it's easy to find your preferred position again if things to move around.

Lazer did a good job with the visor here - it's large enough to actually serve a purpose without looking too goofy or obstructing vision. Their visor adjustment mechanism is prone to wearing out, and isn't improved on here, so expect the detents to wear down after a few seasons of use. One other annoyance is the ear loops, which are completely nonadjustable. The stock setup might work for some, but any and all premium helmets should come with a way to fine-tune fit, as that small detail greatly changes how a helmet feels on-head.

The Impala has some texturing at the rear of the shell to help keep a goggle strap in place, but there's little in the way of on-board glasses storage. The best solution I found was just slotting the stems into the top vents, as you can do with many half shells. The brow of the helmet has enough clearance to avoid clacking against my XL Rx specs, but I can't speak for the ridiculously large riding glasses some of y'all are wearing now.

Meaningless, in my experience.

Price & Weight

The $199 price tag is a bit hard to swallow here, as the features don't exactly stack up when compared to Lazer's other helmets. The Coyote feels just as well-featured, and is $80-90 cheaper; the $199 Jackal is a bit funny looking, but does all the same things with the added bonus of adjustable ear loops.

Tipping the scales at 415g, the Impala is comparable to other enduro-coded helmets out there. 350-450 grams feels like the typical range of safe helmets, and felt comfortable on my large/extra-large head.


Pros

+ Well ventilated
+ Nice industrial design
+ Secure fit

Cons

- Nonadjustable ear loops
- Iffy value compared to other Lazer offerings
- Large liner



Pinkbike's Take
I've been a consistent fan of Lazer's no-nonsense helmets, thanks to their great ventilation and secure fit. The Impala may just be the best looking lid they've released yet, but it lacks some of the key fit elements that the others retain. Still, it's been a helmet I keep reaching for, even if the pads are a little gross and the straps don't fit quite right. — Dario DiGiulio
