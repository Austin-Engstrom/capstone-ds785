Review: Fair Bicycle's Drop Best Offset Saddle Clamp Looks Weird, Works Great

Jan 7, 2026
by Seb Stott  
Share

Sometimes, simple solutions are most effective. The Drop Best is an offset saddle clamp that allows the seat to be placed 27 mm forward or backward of the standard position on top of the seatpost. This results in an effective seat angle adjustment of approximately 2-degrees, depending on your saddle height.

That could be a major upgrade for bikes with a seat angle that's on the slacker side, or for riders who prefer an unusual position. At $134, it's a lot of money for some aluminum parts and a few bolts, but it can transform a bike's climbing performance and fit more dramatically than some pricier upgrades, like electronic suspension. It could be just the ticket for older bikes with slack seat tubes - and much cheaper than a new bike.
Drop Best Details

• Moves saddle position forward or back by 27 mm
• Around 2° of effective seat angle adjustment
• Compatible with most twin-bolt seatposts & all saddle rails
• ~65 g weight penalty
• Zero stack increase (in most cases)
• Independently tested to ISO standard
• Fair Trade policy
• MSRP: $134-158 USD
• fairbicycle.com


Performance

I tested it on a 2025 Pivot Firebird, which isn't exactly old-school in the seat angle department, but I wanted to try it with a more upright position. At my pedalling height (83 cm from the bottom bracket to the top of the saddle), the Firebird's effective seat angle measures 76.5-degrees in its low flip-chip setting. With the Drop Best installed, this increases to around 78.5 degrees.

Everyone has their own take on what the ideal seat angle is, but for me, this is a noticeable improvement, making for a more comfortable and efficient feel when tackling steep scrambles. The Pivot's supportive suspension covered for its slack-ish seat tube, but the Drop Best still unlocked a new level of composure.

The Drop Best allows the saddle position to be fine-tuned by sliding the rails in the clamp in the normal way. I chose to clamp it in the most forward position, giving the most extreme position possible. Conversely, by setting the saddle all the way backwards on its rails, you can get a position that's pretty similar to a conventional seapost with the saddle all the way forwards, so it offers a continuous range of adjustment.



The saddle tilt can be adjusted in the normal way with the two vertical bolts, although this is more fiddly, especially with a multi-tool, because the forward bolt is more recessed. For extreme saddle tilt setups, you may need to add spacers, which increases the stack height. On the plus side, adjusting the fore-aft position or swapping saddles is made easier by the two-part design, as the side-loading saddle clamp bolts don't need to be removed, so there's nothing to drop on the floor when swapping seats.

With the saddle mounted fully forward, there's no discernible difference in seatpost action with my 240 mm travel OneUp V3. I suspect that if you had the system set to the rearward position (a pretty odd thing to do in my opinion), the friction in the seatpost would be noticeably increased.

While the offset saddle looks weird in profile, it doesn't feel strange on the bike. There's no noticeable flex or creaking or seatpost binding - nothing that feels any different from a bike with a seat angle that always was two-degrees steeper.

It weighs 140 g (slightly more if you need spacers), which adds around 65 grams once you take into account the stock saddle clamp components that are removed. A negligible weight penalty if the climbing position suits you better.

Check the clearance with the post compressed.

Installation

The installation manual is pretty intimidating, but fitting the Drop Best is easier than it looks. Start with the compatibility chart, which gives specific instructions and parts required for 33 compatible dropper post models.

In the case of the OneUp V3, I used the post's stock bolts as the OneUp spherical nuts are compatible with the Drop Best. Clearance was tight between the bolts and the post seal when compressed, but not so tight as to be concerning. I needed no spacers to get the saddle tilt I was after, so the stack height remained unchanged. It's important to make sure the saddle rails are fully home in the grooves before tightening the rail clamp bolts.

Giggity

Will it damage your seatpost?

Mounting the saddle forwards puts minimal extra load on the bushings because it counteracts the slope of the seatpost, but mounting it backwards will add significant leverage to the seatpost. To what extent this is a problem remains up for debate.

Fair Bicycle claimed last spring that they have heard of zero seatpost failures (or Drop Best product failures) in the field resulting from use with their products, which they've been making since 2020.

I asked a few seatpost manufacturers their thoughts on whether this would accelerate wear or cause problems with dropper posts. In the end, two companies got back to me and asked not to be named:

Here is our perspective on this aftermarket accessory:

• [It] changes the bushing loads (increases side loading)
• Adds bushing wear (since bushing loads are increased)
• Changes drop force (since the force is offset from the line of action of the post). This will be noticeable when the rider does not first unweight the saddle when actuating the post.
• Could potentially change the top-out feel/noise (due to added weight of the device)

At some point, adding enough offset will impact reliability. We don’t know what this specific point is because we test the post as designed. If the device is used to move the saddle forward, that is minimally concerning but still not something we would condone or approve. If the device is used to move the saddle back, that would be considerably more concerning.—Anonymous seatpost manufacturer 1

Anecdotally, we've occasionally had riders encounter seatpost binding issues when force was applied behind the stanchion during dropper compression.
The problem is usually exacerbated if the frame features a particularly slack seat tube (actual, not just effective).

Applying force at the nose of the saddle normally helps the post compress smoothly, so I think running the Drop Best in the forward position would be fine; running it in the rearward position poses some issues in my eyes.—Anonymous seatpost manufacturer 2



Pros

+ Noticeably alters climbing geometry
+ No noticeable creaking, flex or binding
+ Compatable with most twin-bolt seatposts
Cons

- Expensive for what it is (though still good value if your bike's seat tube doesn't suit you)
- The jury's out on how it will affect seatpost reliability, and it won't help if set to the slacker position
- Looks ugly in profile



Pinkbike's Take

When it comes to climbing, a steeper seat angle makes a big difference. Not everyone needs this, and it is pricey for what it is, but if your bike's seat angle is slacker than you'd like, it's a simple and effective way to achieve a more commanding climbing position. It makes it possible to try a range of seat angles in one bike, or breathe new life into an older bike at a relatively affordable price.— Seb Stott
