Review: Kona Process 153 - Predictable, Rewarding, & Sharp

There aren't that many times that the entire Pinkbike comment section bands together in agreement. However, when Kona looked to be in real trouble as rumors of them going under swirled at Sea Otter in the spring, it felt like most people agreed that it would be sad to see them go.

Uncertainty seems to have stalked the brand for a few years since they were sold to Kent Outdoors in 2022. Eventually, founders Dan Gerhard and Jake Heilbron bought back the brand two years later, signaling what it's hopeful is a real fresh start.

As a brand, Kona has touched most things in gravity mountain biking and had some bikes that have been nearly synonymous with a movement, type of riding, or a certain philosophy.

When you think Stinky, you think freeride. When you think Stab, you think floating brake arm; when you think of the Process 111, you think of one of the first genuinely fun short travel bikes... and the Process 153? You think of a bike with a short rear, balanced geometry, and a platform that offered a huge amount of versatility when it was introduced a decade ago.

Folklore and business acumen to one side, what's up with the new 153, and more importantly, is it a fresh start or throwback?

On its trail, it's predictable, rewarding, and sharp. It's a set of carving skis or a hot hatchback. Yes, it has its limits, and in some instances, when pushing hard, you can feel the front begin to wash or the rear hang up, but all in all, the Process has made good on its potential, and is a fun bike, even if not the out-and-out most capable.—Henry Quinney


Four holes in the front allow for either-or cable routing.

Frame Details

The Process has an angular, chiseled look that I personally quite like. Some elements threaten to be almost brutalist, as flat surfaces combine with sharp edges. This is mainly apparent on the rear of the bike, where the swingarm looks overbuilt with a bridge sitting on the underside of the seat stay and the chainstay being asymmetric, with a twist as it nears the main pivot.

There are some smart features, too. My personal favorite is the tubing. There is a y-junction, where two ports at the headtube lead to one exit port. This means that whichever way you like your routing, Kona has you covered. This is what a sensible, functional, and well-executed routing feature looks like, and I would have it over internally routed headsets any time, any place.


The bike came as a full 29" but has a flip chip where the stay meets the rockers, which offers enough offset to go between wheel sizes. There are two bottle mounts for both a cage and tool, plus plenty of downtube protection for both trail-litter and tailgates.




Geometry

The geometry is largely the same as the alloy 2022 Process 153 DL that I reviewed a few years ago. There have been some small tweaks, though, and the alloy bike was absent of some of the features I've already mentioned, like the wheel-size flip chip or tool mount on the underside of the top tube.

The large size that I have on test uses the same 480mm reach, 64.5-degree head angle, 435mm stays, and 625mm stack. In fact, apart from a few millimeters on the headtube, the only significant change is the 30mm reduction in length on the seat tube, which is very welcome. If it's not broken, don't fix it, right? Well, I quite enjoyed the geometry of that last bike; the issue was a little more related to the shock.

The geometry of this bike is a true-trail bike, meant for going both up and down, without being so biased to steep climbs or descents that it undermines the balances on flatter sections or more gentle gradients.


Suspension Design

The bike uses a linkage-driven single pivot. The main pivot is intersected by the chainring in a bid to mute pedal feedback, all while giving something that is responsive and alive. This is something I often like about single pivots. They don't do everything well, but can be really rewarding to ride in certain instances, all while giving decent midstroke.

Anti-squat starts at between 110 and 120% depending on gearing and drops to around 100% around sag. The bike is progressive in a smooth, linear fashion.

The bike's spec was sensible in some regards and silly in others. I loved the improved shock, with far more damping available. I also enjoyed the WTB alloy rims on DT Swiss 350 hubs, the 200mm dropper, decent Maxxis tires, and, since the advent of DUB, I can even get on board with the press-fit bottom bracket. However, some pills weren't so easy to swallow.

The Guide G2s do not belong on a bike with a 160mm fork and a 64.5-degree head angle. Hell, I wouldn't want them on my XC bike. I get it. SRAM is a big brand, and makes huge OE plays. That said, if the Guide family gets demoted to hardtails and commuter bikes, I wouldn't bat an eyelid. Less power, more hand fatigue... and that's not even mentioning the underpowered soft organic pads that seemingly did their best impression of damp tissue paper. Please, product managers, please... please stop.


Testing Info

This bike was a great companion for late summer rides in Squamish. After a heady summer scaring myself silly in the bike park on downhill bikes, it was nice to come back to Squamish and get back to enjoy more natural mountain biking on the amazing trails we have here. Typically, this bike would see single-blacks and fast, dark blue trails. Ditch Pig is perfect for a bike like this. That said, there are so many choices and diversions into unmarked trails coming off it; I wouldn't blame you if you considered Ditch Pig to be more of an area than a trail in itself.


Climbing


The Process' suspension feel is consistent and muted, both going up as well as down. It's a bike that pedals well yet doesn't feel particularly spritely. However, thanks to the adequate damping on the shock, you can run a fairly middle-of-the-road setup, which means the bike is comfortable and offers decent grip. That said, it's not something that gives a great deal of initial sensitivity. However, once at sag and beyond, it is relatively supple.

The climbing position on the Kona is something some will love, and some will feel slightly alienated by it. The 76.7-degree is steep enough, but only just. Should you be somebody who runs a lot of exposed seatpost, you might begin to find it too slack. I'm not opposed to bikes like this, with a weight bias that isn't about winching to the top of the steepest fire roads but more about finding balance on undulating trails and not overloading your wrists on flatter terrain.

I would say that the setup of the Process is 1-degree slacker than where I would want it for a bike like this, though. The seat tube angle is countered somewhat by the 625mm stack. However, it does feel long and unwieldy in some spots, with the seat tube and low stack combining to give some that are balanced if only stretched. On technical climbs, it would be nice if it had just a little bit more weight on the front wheel. While the cockpit is in reach, it makes that long position feel slightly longer as you try and claw back grip on the front.


With the same angles as the previous model, I would say it largely reflects similar feelings that I had those years ago. However, I would say that my preferences and how I want a bike to be weighted have changed slightly, with me now preferring a bike with a slightly more forward weight bias on climbs. In the meantime, I've started running clips as opposed to flats, which means I run a higher effective saddle height. I wonder if this has just taken me over the edge of what I now deem a comfortable seat tube angle.


Descending

I still feel the same way about the geometry as I did in 2022, except now Kona has given a shock feel that actually lets you enjoy what the bike can do. In short, my liking for damped shocks has only grown, and it's a mark of how far the shock tune on the Process has come that, even with more compression than ever the Kona delivers. This bike rides far, far, far better with a shock that offers a decent level of compression damping.

The short rear end, much like the seat tube angle in the climbing section, is offset by the lower front. This is a balanced bike that absolutely rips on tighter, medium-paced trails. That said, the short geometry can begin to feel a little overwhelmed when things get particularly rowdy, but everything has a blind spot.


Some riders don't agree with my personal preference of longer rears and higher stacks. In some ways, this bike is for them. It's not the new frontier of geometry and ultimately isn't as capable as the fresh-faced new schoolers, but then again, what are you buying a trail bike for? This bike is a product of the old-school... but perhaps could be described as a better execution of a more limited concept.

It's smooth through turns, and the oversized bridges on the stays give something that is firm underfoot and resists twisting forces well, all while being compliant enough. The single pivot gives less chain feedback than you might think, and for a single pivot is almost quite a neutral feeling.

Through turns, the shorter wheelbase really rips and combines well with the taut feeling delivered by the single pivot. On its trail, it's predictable, rewarding, and sharp. It's a set of carving skis or a hot hatchback. Yes, it has its limits, and in some instances, when pushing hard, you can feel the front begin to wash, or the rear hang up, but all in all, the Process has made good on its potential, and is a fun bike, even if not the out-and-out most capable.

How Does It Compare?


The Trance and the Process are two heavy hitters of the category, and 10 years ago, the argument was between the alloy Trance X or the original 153. Both are fun bikes, that will suit somebody who has a wide remit of trails and types of riding they want to do, even if they'll be most suited to single-blacks and challenging but not heinously technical terrain.

The Trance seems to me to be the best of modern geometry, and it feels all that more capable for it. The Process just feels that bit sharper, for both good and bad. Through smooth turns or medium-speed tighter trails, I would prefer the Process. I would say the Trance is better rounded, though, and is ultimately more capable while being a better climber with a suspension system that offers more grip on both climbs and descents.



Prices in USD.

Which Model is the Best Value?


In a world of extreme prices, the higher end bikes aren't bad value by any stretch. On the other end, the Process 153 range is offering some real value too, with the alloy bikes starting at $2,499. I do feel there is a slight gap, though, in that, I would love to see a higher spec on one of the alloy bikes. I don't really care whether the frame is carbon or not, and I remember the alloy bike I tested in 2022 having a comfortable and amply direct feel.

With that in mind, I think the entry-level carbon bike would be the one I would go for. The Shimano build, which features a patchwork quilt of several different tiers, will be easy to live with, and the Fox suspension will do all you need it to and plenty more. I still think Shimano has a significant edge on SRAM when it comes to anything other than the highest-level electronic parts.



Technical Report


SRAM Makes Some Great Parts...: This bike has a very SRAM build. RockShox suspension and dropper work well, and even though I am something of a Luddite, I have to acknowledge that lots of people do enjoy running wireless drivetrains even if I don't. Everything worked well. The RockShox suspension was the star of the show. The Ultimate level Super Deluxe and Lyrik really are very, very good.

And Some Truly Terrible Brakes: I get somewhat irked by people bagging on the Codes. They aren't perfect, but they serve their purpose. If, however, all that ire and frustration was aimed at the G2, I would consider it entirely justified. Underpowered, soft with a mushy feel, and outgunned by every other performance brake I can think of. I really, really, really, really dislike these brakes.

Product Manager Kudos: Apart from a momentary suspension of reason with the choice of brake, the PMs for this build got everything else right at least for my tastes. I like the 200mm seatpost, the suspension and wheel, the tires, and the hub choices. If we take the brakes out of the equation, save for some very personal things such as bars and stems, this bike is ready to rip.

Pros

+ Well balanced on tighter, medium gradient trails
+ Great suspension feel at low-medium speed
+ Well executed single pivot
+ Some neat frame features

Cons

- Geometry and suspension begins to feel overwhelmed at higher speeds
- Slack seattube will punish taller riders
- Underpowered brakes



Pinkbike's Take

The Kona Process 153 G3 isn't a bike from the future, but it's a bike built on the lessons from the past. If you've checked out of new school geometry and shake an angry fist in the face of long rear ends and bikes that are most rewarding when ridden hard, the Process is a welcome tonic. It's balanced, fun, and will shine on the things you want a long-travel trail bike to excel on, and it isn't distracted by trying to be an enduro bike that just so happens to have less travel.
—Henry Quinney

KJP1230 (Dec 9, 2024 at 10:01)
 Very often, placing a pivot on the chainstay (four bar or horst design) allows for lower antirise values and a decoupling of the braking forces from the main frame (this, for example, is why Forbidden switched to an "inverted 4 bar" design in their newer models).

Some people prefer lower antirise as this will allow the bike's suspension to remain more active under braking - although it comes with the tradeoff that the bike's suspension will more readily extend and allow the mass of the rider to move forward under deceleration. Conversely, high antirise bikes have a tendency to "squat down" under braking and maintain a slacker stance, but the suspension can also give a somewhat locked up feeling.

Ultimately, it comes down to rider preference.
