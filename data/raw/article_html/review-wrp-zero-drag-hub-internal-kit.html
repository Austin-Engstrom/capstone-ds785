Mic Williams is one clever guy, a problem solver with the tools and know-how to improve great products even further. One of his greatest creations is the least visible: the Zero Drag kit for DT Swiss hubs.

The Zero Drag kit is a double-clutch system that retrofits into popular DT Swiss hubs to virtually eliminate any rolling friction from the freehub mechanism. In fact, it’s attractive enough for World Cup racers, such as Troy Brosnan, who took the win with one aboard his bike at Mont St. Anne in 2024.

Details

• Easy installation
• Decreases rolling resistance
• Fits DT Swiss Star Ratchet and EXP Ratchet hubs
• Weight: 9g
• Price $379 AUD ($429 for Star Ratchet hubs) / (Approximately $248 - $280 USD)
• williamsracingproducts.com



Design

The Zero Drag kit is made up of a series of springs, washers, and clutch plates to decrease rolling resistance by disengaging the forward-direction clutch. This shift occurs when the rider backpedals slightly, letting the wheel freecoast, where the wheel can spin in the reverse direction while the cassette “floats”. When enough force is put on the chain by the cranks, the forward drive will re-engage.

The Zero Drag kit is not intended to reduce pedal kickback; however, it can have a positive effect on chain feedback, as we’ll see later on. It’s also not intended to handle the forces of freestyle riding, where a dedicated freecoaster hub may be beneficial.

Another notable point is that the Zero Drag kit may not work as designed on an e-bike. The motor clutch will inhibit the rider from being able to backpedal the chain and disengage the hub clutch.

The Zero Drag kit comes in two configurations: one for DT Swiss Star Ratchet hubs and another for the newer Ratchet EXP-style. Both DT hubs have the option of a 36 or 54-tooth clutch, however, the Zero Drag kit only comes in a 36-tooth option at this time.

Despite the Zero Drag kit having a greater number of parts than the EXP clutch internals, it's actually lighter than the stock internals, but only by a marginal amount (9g vs 11g).

Installation

This may be the component with the highest ease-of-installation-to-performance ratio. First, the only tools needed are the necessary wrenches to remove your rear wheel. Next, you can pull the freehub body off with the cassette still in place and remove the existing clutch and spring (Star Ratchet internals have two of each).

WRP has also loaded all of the parts of the clutch onto a dummy axle, which allows you to transfer them in the proper order. Simply, remove the backing plug and while keeping tension on the Zero Drag components, use the freehub body to push the assembly onto the hub axle. The hub end cap should pop into place when all of the parts are correctly aligned.

WRP does state that excess amounts of grease on the clutch plates can prevent the system from engaging properly.

I exclusively rode this on my personal enduro bike, but there are plenty of applications for this kit, from downhill racing to road cycling. The hub of choice was a DT Swiss 240 Ratchet EXP.

The gear cluster arrives ready to install on this dummy axle. An extra O-ring and clutch plate are pictured.
The dummy axle fits into the hub axle and allows all of the pieces to slide on in order.


Price

Depending on which hub you choose to start with, there are two price points. Each kit achieves the same benefits. If you have an Ratchet EXP-style hub, the Zero Drag kit will cost $379 AUD, whereas the WRP internals for the more common Star Ratchet hubs are an extra $50 ($429 AUD). Considering that’s about the same price as a stock DT Swiss 350 (in Australian coin), free speed is a hefty investment.

Like WRP’s other components, the Zero Drag Kit is for the savvy tinkerer who loves to add niche aftermarket components (and can afford it), squeezing out the very last drops of performance from their bike.



Performance

“Zero drag” may be in quotes on the product page of this component because some amount of friction will be there. That force only comes from the hub bearings so the wheel rolls incredibly freely and silently. There’s something about a stealthy hub that makes you think you're riding smoother than you probably are, but you know what? I’ll take it.

As for reliability, the Zero Drag kit has withstood riding through sloppy winter conditions and enduro racing in the dust. The system never had any hesitancy to start freecoasting or re-engage from that state. On one or two rare occasions, I did experience the odd “pop” from the clutch plates. This never affected performance, however, it did set off an alarm bell in my head.

WRP explained that this misfire can happen when the tips of the opposing teeth happen to line up exactly and are then placed under load. Subsequently, they will slip ever so slightly into the next phase with an audible clunk, catching without issue. This is not particular to the WRP Zero Drag kit, but more the DT clutch design itself.



Rolling Resistance Timed Test

To find out the claimed benefits of the WRP Zero Drag kit over the stock DT Swiss Ratchet EXP clutch, we set up a simple test to measure how long it took a wheel to come to rest from 20mph.

An e-bike was used to measure the wheel speed. The wheel was spun above 20mph, the timer started when the wheel reached 20mph and then stopped when the wheel came to rest.

The same wheel was used for all ten trials and torqued to 12Nm each time it was installed. The brake pads were also removed to eliminate any possible friction.

On Trail Comparison

On the trail, it’s tough to perceive the exact gain in efficiency, but doing back-to-back laps with and without the kit, there is a difference in chain feedback. Although that's not the intention of the system, it was worth a deeper look.

What you can distinctly tell is when the clutch is disengaged, since it rolls silently. This happens nearly instantly when you pass over the first bump on the trail - there’s no need to initiate a backpedal, at least on the Santa Cruz Nomad we used to review the Zero Drag.

While it’s nearly impossible for even the most dedicated and clever riders to pinpoint pedal kickback, I can say that I do feel that the system calms down chain feedback. It’s not totally eliminated, but there’s less chaos happening at your feet.




Pros
+ Less resistance than stock DT Swiss internals
+ Fast and simple install
+ Silent on the trail



Cons
- Speed isn’t free - it’s an expensive upgrade
- Only 36-tooth clutches available at this time




Pinkbike's Take

Cyclists are always looking for incremental gains in efficiency, particularly racers, and especially those focused on uphill performance. After our time on the WRP Zero Drag kit, it's clear that it rolls faster than the stock DT Swiss Ratchet EXP-style clutch internals, which are already highly acclaimed. The workmanship of the tiny parts inside the kit have held up when smashing the pedals in an enduro race and bursting up technical climbs, too.

Although it's not totally clear how much the Zero Drag kit mitigates chain feedback (which it's not intended to do), it does "free" up the chain tension to a noticeable degree. With that said, I'd steer towards another source to damp the chain if that's what you're after.— Matt Beer

phops (Jun 11, 2025 at 23:00)
 Imagine your freehub, where you mount your cassette is made of rubber. When you apply pedaling force and keep applying it, the rubber will deform due to the torque. People figured out that you can make that torque control a mechanism, namely a clutch that slides in and out (through the use of ramps), which is the basis for limited slip diffs in cars, freewheels, coaster brakes, and this thing.

The only difference is that the system only engages one way - i.e when you pedal and cassette speed is faster than the hub. When you stop pedaling, a spring pulls the clutch back and fully disconnects the system. Notionally the question is then why doesnt it engage when you fakie, since the relative motion between cassette and wheel is the same, so there should be torque applied. Well - the torque is only sensed on the driving piece (i.e the cassette) to make the clutch move. Without the clutch active, the system is fully disconnected - so no torque transfer happens.
