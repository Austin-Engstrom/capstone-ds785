Review: Ride Concepts Accomplice BOA Flat Pedal Shoes

Aug 29, 2024
by Matt Beer  
Share

Last season I spent a solid chunk of time in Ride Concepts' Tallac Lace, a shoe that brought high performance and security on and off the pedals. For less rigorous riding, such as dirt jumping, pump track sessions, and intermediate mountain biking, Ride Concepts offers the leaner and lace-free Accomplice.

Available in neutral color (the women's version comes in grey), the Accomplice aligns with a more casual approach, featuring a slip-on entrance and cushioned fit. Contrasting that is the BOA lace system which closes up the fixed tongue with just one dial.
Accomplice BOA Details
• Max Grip rubber sole
• Dual-density EVA insole
• Single BOA L6 dial lacing system
• Four-way stretch slip tongue
• Colours: black
• Low volume fit
• Sizes: 7-15 (half sizes from 7-13)
• Weight: 368g (per shoe, size 42)
• MSRP: $140 USD / $180 CAD
• rideconcepts.com

Underneath, Ride Concepts uses their own "Rubber Kinetics" recipe for grip and durability along the outer sole. The toe is finished with a TPU cap and the sides are guarded by a heavy-duty nylon material.


Fit

The Accomplice is more than just a skate-style, slip-on casual shoe but, as stated by Ride Concepts, they do have a low volume fit. Although the heel cup and tongue are very cushioned and comfortable, they don't offer a lot of hold, especially with the single BOA closure. If you're not a fan of that low-rise style, then these won't be the shoes for you.

I found the size 9 to be true in length and generous enough across the front for my E-width foot. You can check Rice Concepts' EU sizing conversions on the size chart here.

The EVA mid-sole has a medium-low arch and isn't overly thick, giving a precise connection to the pedal, a crucial starting point for any good flat shoe. The width of the sole itself is also larger than the body of the shoe, so your foot is decently supported.


Performance

Besides the easy entry, comfort is another one of the Accomplice's main attractions. Even with one BOA lace, I rarely found an issue where the wire tightened unequally to cause any discomfort in in the front of the ankle. The upper portion of the shoe is on the soft side which does reduce pressure points or chafing.

On the pedals, though, there's a high amount of flex in the upper shoe, especially in the lateral direction. Based on the low-rise ankle cuff and single BOA lace, it doesn't give the Accomplice the best stability to stave off rolling an ankle. In the other direction, fore and aft through the shoe, the sole is fairly stiff. Ride Concepts rates them a 5/5 compared to their other models, including all of their clipless models. That may be a touch exaggerated - the Accomplice shoes are not that extreme.

As for traction on the pedals, the Accomplice strikes a balance for those riders that complain about their sticky soles wearing out too quickly while providing a moderate amount of grip. Only when combined with pedals like the Tectonic Altar V2 pedals and their savage pins does the Rubber Kinetics sole really latch on. When used on more basic pedals, the traction isn't significantly greater than something as basic as a multi-function shoe, like the Vans Sk8 Hi Pro.

Venting is one issue you won't have to worry about here. In fact, I'd recommend a thick pair of wool socks, and definitely waterproof toe sleeves for those splashy days because the airflow is high with the Accomplice shoes.


Durability

The topside materials may look fragile but the exposed edges are layered up with a durable canvas. Lower down, the nylon lower portion of the shoe and molded TPU have been holding up well after buckets of full-soaker rides. All of the glue has kept the seams together perfectly and the BOA hasn't skipped a beat. For riders looking to get high mileage out of the soles, the underside still looks brand new - that's even after spending half of their time sitting on very spikey pedals.

Ride Concepts Accomplice Boa - $140 USD
Specialized 2FO Roost - $150 USD

Pricing and Comparison

A buck and a half seems to be the going rate for a middle of the road, performance flat pedal shoe these days. A comparable option in terms of value would be the Specialized 2FO Roost which comes in at $10 more than the Accomplice. Their looks are more casual than the robust 2FO DH version and have a considerably higher degree of flex. A lot of that comes in the fore and aft direction, which helps the shoe wrap around the pedal, adding to the high level of traction. With that said, they offer a lot more lateral stability than the Accomplice.

Grip-wise, these shoes are drastically different, especially in cool, wet weather. The SlipNot ST rubber used by Specialized may be one of the closest rivals to Five Ten's Stealth S1.

Although the 2FO Roost shoes are flexible in the right ways, the tongue is curved in a specific shape. That could be great if that fits your foot, but I found that to cause a lot of pressure when laced up snuggly.

For the price of the 2FO Roost, it's hard to knock them, however, the toe seam between the canvas and TPU has let go after some months of wet riding. The Accomplice, on the other hand, has been resilient in that area.



Pros

+ Stiff sole that isn't overly heavy or thick.
+ Durable outer materials.
+ Tall, one-piece tongue makes for an easy-on, comfy and consistent fit.

Cons

- Low volume fit and hard rubber limit the riding and hiking capabilities.
- Not a lot of lateral ankle support.



Pinkbike's Take

It seems that the Accomplice would best align with the dirt jump and freeride crowd with their simplicity, casual looks, and mild-mannered grip, however, the low heel cup certainly won't accommodate the added bulk of an ankle brace.

For general trail riding, the Accomplice shoes paints themselves into a unique corner of a competitive market with specific functions. While some riders ask for less grip to move around the pedals, the majority will want tackier, grippier soles, even if that comes with a shorter lifespan. If you're looking for a comfy, high-mileage flat pedal shoe, though, these could be the ticket.

— Matt Beer

Saidrick (Aug 29, 2024 at 10:49)
 @htwo

I have been riding Boa’s since they came out on cycling shoes, all clipless. Here’s the reason for the placement:

If you put the boa on the top of the shoe, like Pearl Izumi sometimes does, the tongue has to be built much tougher and is more uncomfortable as a result. I currently have a pair those.

If you put the Boa on the inside of the shoe, you will hit it on the crank arms and damage it just by pedaling.

The Boa on the outside is unlikely to get hit, because, in my experience, trail rocks, bushes and trees will hit the front of your shoe before they hit the side of your shoe.

I have not tried out Ride Concepts yet, my current favorite boa shoes are the Fox Unions.

Your milage may vary…
