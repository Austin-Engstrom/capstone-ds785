The new Maxxis Dissector looks a lot like a dialled-down Assegai, a favorite amongst gravity riders, but its fast-paced intentions cover much more ground, quite literally.

Maxxis designed the Dissector to be a rear wheel specialist that excels in dry, hard-packed dirt. As it turns out, the tire performs well beyond those conditions. And, on the front wheel, it's a treat for shorter travel trail bikes too.

The Dissector isn't a new name in the Maxxis catalogue, and although there's no secondary suffix to separate it from the retiring model, it's distinctly different. Gone is the large void between the tightly spaced center knob trio and the shoulder lugs. The new pattern is spread out more evenly, and the siping has been scaled up. In short, this leads to a predictable tire that pushes its intended boundaries.
Dissector Details

• Faster-rolling tread than DHR II
• Rear wheel focused
• Rubber: Dual, 3C MaxxTerra, 3C MaxxGrip
• Casing: EXO, EXO+, DoubleDown
• Sizes: 29x2.4"
• Weight: 1,030 - 1,205g
• MSRP: $85-116 USD
• maxxis.com

An evenly dispersed 2-3-2 tread pattern is much more predictable compared to the previous version on the right.


Specifications

One specification that the Dissector falls short on is sizing. There’s only one option: 29x2.4” - you won’t find a 27.5” version in the catalogue. It’s possible that Maxxis could add that size later down the road.

Maxxis also narrowed the casing options to just three: EXO, EXO+, and Double Down. If you aren’t familiar with their casing names, those three choices (listed in increasing levels of protection and weight) pertain to anything in between cross-country race bikes and downhill sleds.

Deciding on the level of grip will hinge on the choice of casing. In the line are three options; the soft Maxx Terra, mega soft Maxx Grip 3C, and a hard-wearing dual compound for lesser performance-driven riding.


Price and Weight

Maxxis tires are known for their high-level performance, not affordability. A Dissector in the EXO+ casing and MaxxTerra compound will cost $104 USD. In the case of the Dissector, you’ll pay more for a triple compound rubber. The price also rises with casing security. Even the least grippy dual compound rubber is as expensive as Michelin, Continental, and Specialized competitors.

When it comes to weight, the Dissector ranges between 950 and 1,205 g. One of the most likely choices, the EXO+ casing in the MaxxTerra compound checks in at 1,030g. That’s on the heavier side of the five options shown above. In my experience, the EXO+ casing has proved to be more durable than equivalent Schwalbe and Specialized options.

Compared to other 2.4” wide EXO+ treads from Maxxis, the Dissector lands right in the middle of the milder Forekaster (999g) and blocky Minion DHR II (1,102g).


Ride Impressions

Setup and Conditions

I doubled up on Dissectors, using an EXO+, MaxxGrip up front and a Double Down, MaxxGrip out back, first on board a Chromag Reazon in Whistler across dry, rocky trails with plenty of square-edge hits. Later, I’d swap that front to the rear, choosing a Santa Cruz Tallboy as the test rig, taking that bike deep into the Chilcotins for some longer days in the saddle. Another pair of EXO+ Dissectors, this time with the harder-wearing MaxxTerra on the rear wheel, served well in our Trail Bike Field Test down in Bentonville, Arkansas.

Throughout the review period, the tires were installed on numerous rims and never caused any woes when inflating them. I ran the EXO+ at 23 psi front, 27 psi rear. For the heavier casing on the rear wheel, I left the pressure at the same number since it was mounted to a longer-travel bike and therefore was being ridden harder.

Climbing

Even the stickiest, heaviest Dissector option is a fast-rolling tire, and that’s apparent covering flat ground and uphill. Compared to a DHR II which arrived on the Chromag Reason test bike (in the same Double Down casing and MaxxGrip compound), the Dissector greatly decreased the rolling resistance at the same pressure.

The Dissector’s traction is plentiful and consistent on hard surfaces. Mid-height knobs scattered evenly across the tire latch onto rock, root, and dry duff. The distinct feature of the Dissector is its predictability in all directions, and that’s much more than can be said about the first version. It’s also versatile, despite being pegged as a dry condition tire.

The space between the center and shoulder knobs lays into turns smoothly.

Turning

Although Maxxis says the Dissector was designed primarily as a rear tire, it performs well on the front wheel, too. Don’t expect it to give you the same cheat code an Assegai will - one where you can tip the tire slightly and rip into dry, loose corners with supreme confidence. Reaching the side knobs takes more lean. On hardpark, that's easy to get a sense for. Move to softer conditions, though, and you'll need to exaggerate leaning the bike over to get those side knobs to bite.

Braking

The profile of the tread peaks slightly towards the middle with less side knob purchase compared to a DHR II. The Dissector also seems more "knifey", or skinny when braking in a straight line. That’s most noticeable when braking across cambers or long sweeping turns. I'd still reiterate that it's consistent, but if you'd prefer a more square rear tire for dry conditions, the Maxxis Aggressor or Continental Xynotal might do the trick.

To clarify, the Dissector isn’t meant to keep up with those gravity-oriented treads. Instead, the Dissector provides a versatile stepping stone between the Assegai or DHR II, and tinier treads like the Rekon or Forekaster.

Can you tell from the wear that most trails in the Chilcotins traverse to the left?

Durability

As mentioned, the Dissectors were put to every type of rock and dirt. The casings and compounds were also alternated between front and rear wheels. The MaxxTerra withstood the sharp, loose limestone pebbles of Bentonville with just the occasional sliver showing. Even the MaxxGrip held up well to freeride skids on shale slopes. All said and done, I’d choose the MaxxTerra for the rear wheel in the name of efficiency.

You’ll get a longer lifespan out of the 3C MaxxTerra over the 3C MaxxGrip, but sacrifice grip and rolling resistance for the sticker option that would be best reserved for the front wheel on longer-travel trail bikes.

The redesigned Maxxis Dissector.
And the Michelin Force AM - another versatile rear tire option.

How does it compare?

If you had to pick a tire from any other brand that looked as similar to a Maxxis Dissector, it would likely be the Michelin Force AM. I’ve spent enough time on that rear tire to confidently say that they don’t match the might or versatility of the Dissector. The casing is as robust as the EXO+, in my experience, with similar levels of support and grip in corners. The braking traction is decent in the dry, but the Dissector's ability to cope with moisture is the separating quality here.



Pros

+ Fast-rolling
+ Consistent and predictable
+ More versatile than expected



Cons

- No 27.5" options



Pinkbike's Take

Compared to the previous version of the Dissector, it's a night-and-day improvement. Now, the evenly spaced knobs provide less wheel spin while climbing and lean predictably into corners. The new tread pattern hasn't lost its speed either. This makes the Dissector a fitting middle-ground for a rear tire on a long-travel trail bike, or as a reasonable front option on downcountry builds. — Matt Beer
