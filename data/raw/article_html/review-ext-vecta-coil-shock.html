EXT are known for their exquisite suspension components, but also for their higher prices. The Vecta aims to retain EXT’s pedigree without skimping on the features found on their more premium shocks.

The Vecta is primarily aimed at the enduro and e-bike crowd, but downhill-bike sizes are also available (without a climb switch). At $775 USD, it costs roughly $300 less than the Storia V4.

In general, a lower-priced component is usually heavier, and that applies to the Vecta, which is about 100 grams heavier than a Storia in the 210x55mm size. Those tradeoffs don’t seem to hamper the Vecta’s ride quality.

Both EXT dampers offer high and low-speed compression, rebound, and use a hydraulic bottom-out control (HBC) system to soften blows. So what’s the catch?
Vecta Details

• Adjustments: low-speed compression, high-speed compression, rebound, lockout
• High turbulent flow valves
• Hydraulic bottom-out control
• 14 mm hardened chromoly shaft
• Thrust bearing seats for spring
• Bladder reservoir design
• Sizes: 210x47.5mm - 250x75 Metric, 185x47.5mm - 225x65 Trunnion
• Weight: 477 gr in 210×55 mm (without spring)
• Price: $775 USD / €749 EUR (w/o spring, VAT Included)
• extusa.bike



Vecta Details

The Vecta is a mono-tube shock that runs on a low-pressure, bladder reservoir design. The greatest factor separating the two dampers is the hydraulic bottom out control adjustment. On the Vecta, the position-sensitive circuit is set internally, whereas the Storia uses a dial to make trail-side tweaks.

Speaking of adjustments, EXT has revised the high-speed compression dial. In the past, an open-ended wrench or socket was needed to change that. Now, a 5mm allen key will do the trick. Likewise, a 4mm allen key turns the LSC adjuster. The single rebound adjuster is still rotated by hand. Each one of the damper dials offers a reasonable range of settings to work through.

Reducing the price further, the Vecta’s shock shaft doesn’t receive the extra-slippery coating found on the high-end Arma and Storia models.

Contrary to some cheaper damper models out there, the Vecta doesn’t forego a climb switch. It features the Lok 2.0, with a customizable shim stack.


Sizes and Weight
Standard sizes: 210x47.5mm-55mm, 230x57.5mm-65mm, 250x67.5mm-75mm
Trunnion sizes: 185x47.5mm-55mm, 205x57.5mm-65mm, 225x67.5mm-75mm

Compared to the Storia, you save about $300 by going with the Vecta, which is on par with a Fox DHX or a RockShox Vivid Coil Ultimate. The Vecta offers a hydraulic bottom-out control system, as well as separate high and low-speed compression over the DHX. The Vivid Coil Ultimate touts all of those features and offers adjustment to the bottom-out circuit.

Like most aftermarket shocks, the Vecta's price doesn’t include a spring. EXT does offer very light, high-quality steel springs, which cost $130 USD each. Those are available in weights from 250 lb all the way through to 800 lb, in 25 lb increments. The springs also include spacers, so the springs can fit any coil shock on the market.

On the scales, the Vecta weighs 80 grams more than the Storia, but it's robust. In fact, it’s actually one of the dampers, along with the E-Storia, that EXT recommends for bikes that use a yoke shock mount.


Setup

When I arranged for EXT to send out a Vecta damper for the review, they tuned it for a Santa Cruz Nomad, my current test sled. Originally, they supplied a 425 lb spring for my weight of 78 kg / 170 lb, without riding gear. The sag measured close to 35%, more than Santa Cruz recommends. I decided to give it a shot anyway.

As I imagined, the balance of the bike was upset, I spent far too much time sitting over the back of the bike, and crank strikes were frequent. A 450 lb spring with 1.5 turns of preload positioned me close to 30% sag and solved the issue.

Once a reasonable amount of sag was achieved, I started with the high and low-speed compression wide open, but eventually closed those in. Each compression dial offered 14 clicks of adjustment on the Vecta, while the rebound

EXT tuned the Vecta for the Nomad, however, I found the compression damping to be on the firmer side. After a solid amount of time on the Vecta, I settled for -11/14 clicks on the HSC, -7/14 on LSC, and -11/16 on the rebound end.

User Experience

Riding the Vecta has been enjoyable and hassle-free. Once I'd dialed in the spring rate, finding a happy place didn’t take long, and overall I’ve been impressed with the performance.

The stock compression tune is on the firm side. I’d wager that most riders will leave the HSC further open, rather than closed. Although the window is usable, the rear end of the bike could become harsh when turning the HSC in, even a few clicks from fully open.

On past EXT coil dampers, a plastic washer was situated on the shock shaft to clear debris and would rattle excessively. Thankfully, that’s been eliminated from the equation. As far as hydraulic noises go, the Vecta is a mouth-breather and is louder than most other shocks I’ve ridden.

Did the HBC circuit come in handy? You betcha. Bottom-outs aren’t totally eliminated - you want to use all of your travel anyway - but the way it touches down gently is much appreciated. It does so in a much different way than an air spring.



How Does It Compare?

I think it’s valuable to compare the Vecta against the Fox Float X2. Wait, the Float X2 is not a coil shock - what gives? Well, the Nomad is one enduro bike that can handle either type of shock, plus it’s the only other damper I’ve ridden on this bike.

Funny enough, the weight of the Vecta (758g) and the Float X2 (761g) is nearly identical, as well. The price of the Float X2 Factory is also comparable at $809 USD, just $40 more.

The Float X2 has more adjustment with high and low-speed rebound, and also has the ability to tap into small air spring changes as conditions or riding locations change. Often, riders on coil-sprung shocks will find themselves stuck between two spring rates; however, components like Springdex are a convenient solution to bridge the gap between coil weights.

Both shocks help alleviate hard bottom-out events, but in different ways. The Float X2’s progression can be increased by upping the pressure, but also by adding volume spacers. One possible downside is that the rebound force will also increase, sometimes requiring a revisit to the high-speed rebound setting or air pressure. As the Vecta nears full travel, the HBC piston displaces oil and slows the shock down. That doesn’t affect the rebound force.

Another reason I wanted to compare the two was the small bump performance. A general assumption is that a coil spring will always feel more supple, but the Float X2 challenges that. I’d even say it has a leg up, and when the shock shaft changes direction, it’s seamless - the Vecta has a tiny hiccup when it reaches that point.





Pros

+ EXT performance at a lower price.
+ Hydraulic bottom-out control buffs out the worst impacts.
Cons

- Suggested setup may not work for everyone.
- Loud hydraulic operation may be distracting to some riders.




Pinkbike's Take

EXT has stepped up to the plate with a high-quality damper packed with benefits, yet it doesn’t cost a fortune. Their suggested spring sat on the softer side, and the tune on the firmer side for my setup. I’d like to see that buttoned up, or perhaps two springs included with the price. Either way, the Vecta offers decent value for high-performance features.— Matt Beer
