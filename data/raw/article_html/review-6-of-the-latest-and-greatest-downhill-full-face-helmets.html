Whether you’re racing downhill, sending big features, or regularly riding a bike park, a downhill rated full-face is the best option for protecting your noggin.

Lightweight enduro full-face helmets are a decent alternative if you are just occasionally riding one of these disciplines, but a downhill full-face offers the most protection from the higher speeds with increased padding, stronger materials and a more secure fit system.

We’ve been bouncing downhill with six of the latest and greatest full-face helmets for the whole summer, putting them to test and seeing how they compare.

Fox RPC Details

The Fox RPC or Rampage Carbon Pro is a long-standing premier helmet in Fox's lineup. In fact, we reviewed this helmet and its current form three years ago. It’s unchanged except for the colors, yet it’s one of the most popular choices you’ll see in the lift line—and for good reason.

The safety features are abundant. Our RPC helmet has a carbon fiber, shell, MIPS liner and dual density Varizorb EPS protective foam. There’s also an Ionic liner with quick remove cheek pads and a breakaway visor in case of an accident. A D-ring system at the chin strap locks you in.

It comes in four sizes ranging from 55 to 62 cm in circumference, weighs 1250 g for the size large, and costs $499 USD.
RPC Details
• MIPS safety system
• Carbon fiber shell
• Breakaway visor design
• Dual-density Varizorb EPS foam liner
• X-Static liner
• Quick-remove cheek pads
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL (55-62 cm)
• Colors: various
• Weight: 1258 g (LG, actual)
• Safety certifications: CPSC, EN 1078, AS/NZS 2063, ASTM 1952-15 downhill
• Price: $499 USD
• foxracing.com

In terms of fit, the one caveat to that high volume shell is that you’ll likely want to size up with the RPC. Otherwise, there is a lot of cushion inside the helmet which can make the fit snug and secure. I never noticed any pressure points and compared to Fox's helmets, I find the fit to be rounder around the forehead.

Compared to the other helmets on test, the RPC does feel more substantial on your head for better or worse. More material generally means more energy dissipation, but it also makes it on the heavier side. The ventilation is adequate, but wearing it all day for an enduro race might be challenging.

All in all, it’s easy to see why the RPC is an attractive premium helmet. The price is on average with other high-end contenders - you’ll just want to make sure you’ve got the right fit for you.





Pros

+ Very secure yet comfortable fit
+ Well constructed, with high-end features
Cons

- Traditional size is small
- On the heavier side




Bell Full 10 Spherical

The Bell Full 9 seemed to have been around as long as the American company has been making helmets for. Its successor, the Full 10, looks drastically different and packs a lot into a very lightweight and vented helmet. The futuristic looking Full 10 seems to have all the bells and whistles, no pun intended, and it is better because it costs $650 USD.

What makes it so expensive? Well for starters it has a carbon shell that makes it super light. The Bell Full 10 is the lightest helmet we have here today. It weighs 1080 g for a size medium. It comes in four sizes to fit head from 51 to 63 cm.

There’s also a lot of technology packed into this extremely vented and angular helmet. It features the MIPS Spherical system, which is made up of two bowl-shaped layers of progressive EPS foam. This helps to dissipate energy from crashes of all sizes and angles.
Full 10 Spherical Details

• Carbon shell
• MIPS Spherical safety system
• Progressive layering EPS
• Adjustable, Flying Bridge visor
• Quick-release magnetic cheek pads
• Thermal Exchange Airflow System
• Camera mount
• Sizes: XS/S, M, L, XL/XXL (51-63 cm)
• Colors: various
• Weight: 1080 g (MD, actual)
• Safety certifications: NTA 8776 (e-bike), US CPSC, CE EN 1078:2012, ASTM F1952-15, DH ASTM F2032-15 BMX
• Price: $650 USD
• bellhelmets.com

Bell has also designed what they call the Flying Bridge visor that uses a bolt on each side, but no center post. It’s a method that they say increases protection by allowing for material in that area. The visor itself also incorporates a breakaway function.

The Full 10 is hands-down the most breathable helmet we have here. It looks like someone has taken a hacksaw to it. This promotes cool air in and exhausts hot air out the back - something Bell calls the Thermal Exchange Airflow System. On top of that the liner material is made from Ionic+ fabric which is soft but also top notch at wicking away sweat. There are also pull-away cheek pads and titanium D-rings for the chinstrap closure, plus a trick magnetic clasp for the end of the chinstrap.

The fit of the Full 10 is also spot on. There is a huge field of vision, and like the Sweet Fixer, it reduces the chance of hitting it on your stem or bars in extreme scenarios.

Don’t let the size or the shape of the Full 10 for you, this is a helmet that could be worn for high speeds, big stunts and all-day enduro racing. Yes, it is expensive, but it has some of the best helmet technology going right now and the workmanship to back it up.




Pros

+ Best venting on test
+ Lightweight
+ High degree of safety features

Cons

- Highlights are reflected by the pricetag
- Styling may not be for everyone




POC Coron Air MIPS

Being the safe Scandinavians they are, POC does things a little differently when it comes to helmets and protection. Round shapes and more elastic properties are part of their designs.

The Coron Air MIPS cover heads from 51 to 62 cm, however, POC uses just three shells across that range. Additional cheek pads are included, which helps bridge those but compared to the Troy Lee Design D4, it’s not as tailor-fitted. It’s available in black, white or silver and costs $330 USD.

Highlighting the safety features on the Coron are a MIPS evolve system, pull-away cheek pads and a multi-impact EPP liner.

What exactly does that mean? Well, the formula of EPP that POC uses is softer and malleable compared to other helmets. For example, when you push into the foam with your fingernail, it has the tendency to absorb and rebound to its original shape.
Coron Air MIPS Details

• Fiberglass shell
• MIPS safety system
• Multi-impact EPP liner
• Quick-release chin strap clasp
• Pull-away cheek pads
• Sizes: S, M, L (51-62 cm)
• Colors: black, white, silver
• Weight: 1165 g (MD, actual)
• Safety certifications: CPSC Safety Standard, EN1078:2012+A1:2012, ASTM F1952, NTA 8776,
• Price: $330 USD
• poc.com

The Coron is also very round and smooth which has been theorized to reduce impacts by glancing off of objects, but it doesn’t have as many vents as other competitors. POC has also integrated a breakaway visor and designed their own release, magnetic chin strap clasp.

The interior of the helmet is comfy for wearing on longer rides for two reasons. First it has decent airflow due to the air pockets throughout the liner and secondly you can hear very clearly due to the sizeable voids around the ears - something that POC specifically focused on.

One thing I did notice was that the depth of the helmet feels more shallow along the top and forehead areas in comparison to the other equally sized helmet here.

Overall, the Coron Air was a surprise performer in terms of ventilation in overall comfort. It’s definitely geared towards the side of gravity side of riding. If you're focused exclusively on enduro, you might be better off with their lighter Otocon option.





Pros

+ Quick-release chin strap is convenient
+ Unique safety features

Cons

- Three shell sizes means a less tailored fit
- Breathability could be better




Troy Lee Designs D4 Polyacrylite

If you haven’t seen a Troy Lee Designs D4 helmet before, then you must be living under a rock. This is their most recent evolution of the D-series helmets. It comes with the same styling and safety features as the more expensive carbon version, but in a more affordable package.

At 1150g (claimed), the D4 polyacrylite is only 150g heavier than the carbon version, but it costs $300 less. Inside, you’ll find the MIPS C2 rotational system and collarbone suspension system. The liner features antimicrobial padding with quick release cheek pads. For the strap closure, there’s the classic double D-ring loop for peace of mind

Fit has always been one of the highlights of TLD helmets and the D4 comes in a wide range of sizes from XS to XXL. That aligns with head circumferences from 53-63 cm. With six sizes, you’re more likely to find a snug, yet comfy fit versus other brands that may only have three or four.
D4 Polyacrylite Details

• MIPS C2 safety system
• Polyacrylite shell
• Front and side air mesh vents
• Breakaway visor screws
• Collarbone Suspension System
• Quick release cheek pads
• Sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL, 2XL (53-63 cm)
• Colors: Various
• Weight: 1198 g (MD, actual)
• Safety certifications: CPSC 1203, CE EN1078, ASTM F1952-DH certified
• Price: $299.99 USD
• troyleedesigns.com

Behind the aggressive styling of the D4 are numerous vents along the top, side and rear of the helmet for excellent air flow. I’d put it in second place, just behind the Bell Full 10 by a slim margin. This is definitely a helmet that won’t boil your brain on long descents in the heat of the summer.

The TLD D4 Polyacrylite may not have the pizzazz of the carbon version, however, you still receive the main features, fit and safety that make the D4 a crowd favorite.





Pros

+ Widest range of sizes
+ Excellent venting

Cons

- Padding can occasionally become folded when pulling the helmet on
- Longer jaw piece compared to competitors




Abus HiDrop

Abus is another new player in a full-face helmet game and the HiDrop is their most protective offering. At just $200 USD you’d think that they would have to cut some corners, but it still receives the ASTM rating for downhill racing.

The HiDrop weighs just 1053 g and comes in four sizes. Those run from 55 centimeters up to 62 cm in circumference. The shell comes in for neutral colours and the styling is a mix between the POC Coron and Sweet Fixer.

The outer shell is ABS and it is said to make the helmet impact resistant - as it should be - I think what they mean is the plastic is more resistant to cracking or denting on small impacts. Under the shell, there is EPS and EPP foam to handle the major forces.

In addition to those features, the chinstrap uses a double D-ring, and the visor has a breakaway safety feature to reduce neck trauma.
HiDrop Details

• Breathable cheek pad inserts
• Break-away visor
• Double D-ring buckle system
• ABS shell
• EPS & EPP foam core
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL (55-62 cm)
• Colors: black, white, blue, green
• Weight: 1053g (MD, actual)
• Safety certifications: EN1078, ASTM F1952, ASTM F2032, CPSC, AS/NZ
• Price: $200 USD
• abus.com

One thing absent from the HiDrop is a MIPS liner and that may deter some riders from the helmet. Opting around the technology does keep the price of the helmet down though.

Compared to some of the more luxurious helmets on test, the liner does feel a little rougher. With that said, it still keeps the air flowing well through the vents. The goggle port is plenty wide and the shell doesn’t have any pressure points.

I would add that it does fit slightly larger for a medium than most of the other helmets we have here. Other than that, Abus has hit the mark well with a breathable, comfortable helmet that doesn’t break the bank.





Pros

+ Lightweight
+ Superb value

Cons

- Padding material is reflected in the price
- Absence of MIPS liner may deter some riders




Sweet Protection Fixer 2Vi Details

Sweet Protection may not be the first name that comes to mind when you think of gravity-oriented full-faces. It’s been some time since they made one with a dedicated chin bar, but their new Fixer 2Vi looks to change that in all the right ways.

There are two versions, but this is the carbon which comes in at an impressive 1104 g for a size medium. It comes in two colors, white or black and costs $600 USD. There are five sizes, each which use their own individual shell mold. That’s not something you’ll find most helmet manufacturers do for such a wide size range - they might resort to inserting thicker or thinner padding to squeeze two sizes out of one shell.

Alongside that carbon shell is a two-layer MIPS system, which is where the “2Vi” name comes from. That liner uses EPS and EPP segments strategically placed to take any incoming impacts.
Fixer 2Vi Carbon Details

• 2-layer MIPS safety system
• Carbon fiber pre-preg shell
• Multi-density EPS impact shields
• Adjustable (two-position) break-away visor
• Emergency removal cheek pads
• 11 penetration resistant vents
• Titanium double D-ring buckle
• Sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL (53-62 cm)
• Colors: black/carbon, white/black
• Weight: 1104 g (MD, actual)
• Safety certifications: EN1078:2012m, A1:2012, CPSC 1203, ASTMF 1952-15, NTA 8776
• Price: $600 USD (available Spring 2025)
• sweetprotection.com

Large vents in the front of the helmet bring in a moderate amount of cool air. The big story is how the pads are placed that improves the acoustics inside. The cheek and neck pad also form space around your ears and hug the back of your head moreso than any other helmet I’ve tried.

If you have claustrophobic feelings about full-face helmets, this could be the answer for you. In addition to the space inside the helmet, the goggle port is very large and the chin bar is tucked in out of sight as well.





Pros

+ Snug but spacious fit
+ Wide field of vision

Cons

- Not the most sweat absorbent padding
- On the pricier side



Pinkbike's Take

Best Performer: Bell Full 10 Spherical
The looks may not be everyone's taste but the performance of this lid is impossible to ignore. The weight to protection ratio, as well as the venting to protection, is as good as it gets. A large size range, adjustable fit, and premium liner round out the inside. Plus, that MIPS Spherical and GoPro mount haven't made a peep.

Best Value: Troy Lee Designs D4 Polyacrylite
Yes, it's $100 more expensive than the Abus HiDrop, and 100g heavier, but hear me out. It's the fit and function that gives the D4 Polyacrylite the Best Value title. It has the same safety features as the D4 Carbon, including the MIPS C2 slip plane - no doubt a big miss for many riders considering the HiDrop. Add in the wide size range and incredible venting, only 2nd to the mega-expensive Bell Full 10.
