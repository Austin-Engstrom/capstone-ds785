Review: FSA's New KFX i28 XC Carbon Wheels are Compliant & Comfortable

Sep 19, 2024
by Henry Quinney  
Share
Tweet
You must login to Pinkbike.
Don't have an account? Sign up

Join Pinkbike  Login
52 Comments

FSA has released a new XC-focused wheel, the KFX range. The 1560-gram wheelset aims to find a home on cross-country bikes set up to handle the rigours of a modern course while also offering comfort and performance for more casual riders.

The look at the wheels shares a similar profile to other brands, most notably a set of carbon Reserve wheels, with a raised portion around each spoke nipple. The rim profile varies between 22 and 24mm. FSA say this was done to tune compliance and adaptability, as well as aid overall riding performance and feel. The rims are also asymmetric.

FSA KFX i28 Details
• Size: 29"
• Intended use: XC/Trail
• Rim dimension: 28mm width, 22-24mm profile
• Hubs: KFX i28 72T
• Straight pull spokes w/ six-bolt rotors
• Weight: 1576g total (actual)
• MSRP: €1,899 (EU) and $1,810 (USA)
• More info: fullspeedahead.com

While they have been developed for the rigours of XC-racing, and in conjunction with the Cannondale Factory team, they aren't as light as one might expect for a cutting-edge wheelset in 2024. At nearly 1600-grams, they're not portly, but one could go several hundred grams lighter should they be so inclined. That said, they have already won a world championship under Alan Hatherly.


Technical Features

The rims utilize a hookless bead with an internal width of 28mm, and use what FSA refer to as a turret around the spoke insertions. This enables them to use more material around the spoke hole, and less between spokes. Not only does this have claims of increased performance, but also the removal of the excess material reduces weight. The hubs use the brand's PRS 72-tooth ratchet system, which equates to 5-degrees between engagement points.


The freehub itself is interesting, and the ratchets are designed in an almost conical fashion (the outer and innner ratchets have angled interfaces) with one sitting inside another. This is not only efficient in terms of space along the width the the axle, but also lets FSA make the parts more substantial. The alloy hubs use direct pull spokes, and the rear features 5 sealed bearings.

The spokes are straight pull and feature a 2-cross lacing pattern. The wheels come with a lifetime warranty.


Ride Impressions

While FSA level this wheel as ready for the XC World Cups, I saw their middling weight as an excuse to try them out on something a little burlier. The bike I've ridden them most on has been a Kona Process 153.

Saying things about carbon wheels has become increasingly difficult, as so many brands offer something so good in the current landscape. Ultimately, these wheels aren't the lightest, but have held up well with a decent ride quality. The wheels are comfortable more than they are particularly stiff - which is no bad thing. I've ridden them across a decent amount of terrain, most of it beyond the remit of classic XC, and I've had no complaints. The ratchet sound is quite a loud, metallic-sounding click, and the engagement of 5 degrees is very adequate for those looking for a snappy feel. In the months that I've been riding them, the hubs have proven to be reliable.

I haven't gone out with the intention of breaking them, but I have hit them off rocks and square edges, and so far, so good. I even snuck them into Whistler Bike Park once (although that's a secret between you and me) and had no complaints.

Should you need to swap between freehub bodies, it's an easy task, thanks to the press-on end caps.


Pros

+ Compliant and comfortable
+ Clean looks
+ Fast engagement

Cons

- Not the lightest for a true XC wheel


Pinkbike's Take

The FSA wheels sit in the middle of other offerings in terms of price and, to a certain extent, weight too. The fast engagement and comfort will suit riders who want to thrash their XC bikes, both up and down, but true racers might find these have a better home on shorter travel trail bikes.—Henry Quinney
