Welcome to the great eMTB motor debate. Which motor is the best one out there? It’s not as simple as picking the one with the most power or torque. Noise, efficiency, and useable power on the trail are larger factors to consider.

As you know from our Field Tests, we can't test every option out there - before you ask, we tried to get our hands on the Pinion MGU, but it turns out those are a rarity in North America. The list was slimmed down to five popular motors:

• Bosch Performance Line CX (4th gen.)
• DJI Avinox
• Shimano EP801
• SRAM Powertrain
• Specialized Turbo Full Power 2.2

In order to test them head-to-head, we established some qualitative and quantitative tests to simulate regular riding scenarios that focus on trail performance and how quickly the motor drains the battery. The shootout tests include a Boost Mode Hill Climb Challenge, Perceived Noise Trial, and Motor Efficiency Test Loop.

We’ll also dive into the user experience of each motor system in terms of tuning, app functionality, and user experience in this shootout.

Test Setup

Now there are a few caveats with these tests. Since each bike has its own motor mount, it’s not possible to use a control frame and swap out motors (like we did with rear shocks for Shock Week).

For each test, the motor was placed in the highest assist level (we’ll call that boost mode throughout the test for simplicity’s sake) and set to its stock mapping. Each bike’s battery size also varied, so we calculated the amount of battery potential used over the vertical gain achieved on our set test loop. Over those five laps, the vertical averaged 484m.

This follows Car and Driver Magazine’s method for measuring fuel efficiency conducted in their reviews. It also allowed each bike to be ridden on the test loop on the same day for equal trail conditions, which were wet and cold.

During the motor efficiency test, the cadence was kept to what I would consider a normal climbing cadence (between 80-90 RPM) although some variance in crank length and gear selection was noted. Another control we used for all three tests was a set of MX wheels with soft compound, gravity casing tires.

Front tire: 29x2.4" Schwalbe Albert Radial Ultra Soft compound, Gravity casing, 25 PSI.
Rear tire: 27.5x2.4" WTB Judge High Grip compound, SG1 Tough Casing, 27 PSI.



Boost Mode Climb Challenge

In this test, we’re looking to rank the real world functionality of the boost mode on a technical climb. If you haven’t spent a lot of time on e-bikes, you might think climbing the steepest or most technical hill is all about selecting the strongest mode, pointing the bike straight up and going for it, but there’s a lot more finesse needed than that.

Again, all of the motors were set to the highest stock assist mode. It’s not necessarily how you’d want to tackle a technical hill, but it shows how useable, or unusable, those modes are on the trail. We recognize that all of these motors are highly tunable. Changing the parameters can be done through the corresponding apps. The purpose is to point out those characteristics like the overrun (how long the motor stays engaged), pick-up (re-engagement timing), and torque.

Ranking:


#5 - Specialized

The Turbo 2.2 has the lowest peak power amongst the group, at 565 W, but still has a good amount of torque (90 Nm). While it builds power quickly, the drawback on this technical climb was that the overrun is too high to slow the bike down when you need to pause. Then, reengaging took more rotation from the cranks than anticipated. Again, it’s possible to tweak this in the app. When you are on the pedals, the traction is very manageable.

#4 - DJI

Wow does the Avinox stand out from the rest here. There’s a tonof torque to manage here as it builds momentum incredibly quickly. That means, when you’re ready, it’s possible to get up some big moves. Speed is your friend if you’re thinking ahead.

This mode is quite hectic and not practical for tech climbing. It’s likely most riders will want to drop to “Trail” (which is more comparable to the rest of the motors' boost modes here) to navigate any sort of obstacles. Although the pickup is decent, there’s too much overrun.

#3 - Shimano

The EP801’s strength point is the natural pick-up on the pedals but it’s the how the torque is applied that makes managing grip from a standstill trickier. That makes engaging the motor again more manageable than the Specialized, but it may be worth toning down how quickly the power ramps up.

#2 - SRAM

I dig how smooth the manageable the power delivery is, but, as you’ll notice in the video, it takes more gusto to get the bike over the log and up the next rock. In those moments before each lunge move, the power doesn’t ramp up quickly. That does allow to find and manage traction in the highest boost, or “Rally” mode though, making the Powertrain ride more natural to an unassisted bike in it’s stock parameters.

#1 - Bosch

The Bosch motor might be the Goldilocks of power management. There’s a fast pick-up and the power rolls off smoothly. Bursting up the big moves applies a sensible amount of torque without carrying on out of control. This motor is one step above the Powertrain in terms of intuition.



Perceived Noise Trial

Now that we’ve got the hardest part out of the way, physically, it’s time to talk about the noise emitted from these motors while climbing. Overall volume isn’t the only aspect we’re judging here, but also the pitch or tone of the gears spinning inside there.

Here we’re using a scorecard system to rank them. We decided against using a decibel meter because that doesn’t account for the pitch or whine emitted. We’re listening for how annoying the noise is - microphone shoe installed and away we go. The least annoying wins.

For this test, all the bikes were placed in the highest stock boost settings and the 36-tooth cog on the cassette while keeping a relatively similar cadence. We also recognize that each frame can resonate differently. Without tearing apart each bike, this is how we perceived the noise level of each motor.

Ranking:


#5 - Shimano

The EP801 might have other strengths, but a stealth operation isn’t a strong point. Aside from being the loudest, it’s also the whiniest. The tone has a distinct cry that fluctuates quickly depending on cadence and speed.

#4 - DJI

The Avinox motor falls somewhere in the middle of a hum and a whine. The overall noise level is less than the Shimano, but the tone of the motor is sharper than the rest.

#3 - Bosch

The tone of the Performance CX Line is what I would call squeaky. The noise level emitted is quite low, and less erratic than the Shimano or DJI. Overall, it’s liveable, but not as easy to forget about compared to the Specialized or SRAM motors.

#2- Specialized

The electric motor manufacturer Brose makes both the Specialized Turbo 2.2 and SRAM Powertrain units so it’s no surprise to see them rank next to each other. While an exploded view of the Powertrain was hard to come by, the Turbo 2.2 uses a belt. That could the key to the least annoying sound.

Each one emits a quiet humming sound with a low tone. Neither sounds like they’re working as hard as the previous three motors mentioned.

#1 - SRAM

It was a close fought battle but the Powertrain edges ahead of the Turbo 2.2 in this pseudo-science challenge.



Motor Efficiency Test Loop

Now, there’s no perfect way to do this. This is the mountain bike industry after all, and standards are never quite “standards”.

As we mentioned, every bike uses a different motor and battery size, so instead of fully draining the battery, we measured the percentage used over the vertical - the lower the figure, the more efficient the motor is. This removed the variability by which a given bike might encounter a steep climb, changing the overall efficiency. It’s similar to how Car and Driver perform their fuel economy test.

As with the other tests, we used the same wheel sizes and tires across each bike and started with a fully charged battery. We took the same lines, kept the cadence and gearings as similar as possible and we only used the stock boost mode.

The loop totalled a vertical gain of 484m up the infamous Debeck’s Hill here in Squamish. The route consisted of a paved commute to a mellow dirt single-track climb, followed by a steep gravel road climb. Then the trail dropped back to the valley bottom and we returned back to home base.

Results:

#5 DJI - 0.645 Wh/m

There's a lot of buzz around this motor, and for good reason, but efficiency may not be at the top its list, at least not in the highest assist level. That may be because the Avinox has the highest peak power (800 W) and torque (105 Nm) out of these motors. It doesn’t stop there though, because remember, the Avinox can pump out 1000 W of power and 120 Nm of torque for a 30-second burst in the boost setting. You could draw a parallel between the Avinox and electric cars. Its acceleration is unmatched compared to what we’ve previously known for e-bikes.

While those are impressive numbers, all Class 1 E-bikes are limited to 32 km/h or 20mph, and that includes the Avinox. Yes, its acceleration is lightening fast, but it will never exceed 32 km/h.

It’s golden ticket is that it holds that peak power. When pedalling moderately hard, the motor churns out an intense amount of support right up to the speed limit. Zooming up steep road climbs is comical, however, it chews through the battery very quickly in Turbo.

Here’s another plot twist though: the other four bikes took about 45 minutes to complete the test loop. The Avinox knocked ten minutes off that time. That might explain why the Avinox had the lowest efficiency in this test. Although that’s the goal of this test, it does show how incredibly powerful the Avinox motor is.

In fact, the Avinox’s “Trail” mode is more comparable to other motor brands’ boost setting. The Trail mode also cuts off the wild Turbo burst, lowering the pace, but more importantly, conserving the battery. For what it's worth, we conducted a bonus lap in the lower Trail mode and result in efficiency score of 0.604 Wh/m. Remember, every bike was set to their stock motor parameters for this test.

We’ll have a full-length review of the Amflow PL, the only bike on the market currently built around the DJI Avinox motor, coming soon.

#4 Shimano - 0.621 Wh/m

Judging by the riding range during Propain Sresh review, I had a sneaking suspicion that there would be more efficient motors in the group than the Shimano unit. The EP801 placed 4th with a rating of 0.621 Wh/m.

#3 SRAM - 0.586 Wh/m

The Powertrain motor nudged ahead to 0.586 Wh/m, although it was surprising to see it lag behind the Specialized motor by so much, since these two motors are made by the same company, Brose

#2 Specialized - 0.492 Wh/m

The final two motors fought a tight battle for the top step. The Specialized Turbo 2.2 motor was pipped by a hair in this test.

#1 Bosch - 0.490 Wh/m
From dishwashers to drills, Bosch is well known name when it comes to motors. As it turns out, they know a thing or two about efficiency. Although it squeaked into first place, my money was on the Performance CX Line based on total ride times with other eMTBs. It was satisfying to confirm that theory to rest with back-to-back laps on each motor.



Liveability - Remotes and Displays

This section covers the user experience with various remotes and displays.

There's a balance between having just the basics and being overloaded with buttons and lights. Having functions visible in the display, like mode selection, speed, and battery percentage are worthwhile. After that, it gets distracting to look at while riding.

An ideal remote should integrate with the most common handlebar components and not be too vulnerable in the event of a crash. Wires can than be cleanly routed. You can't argue with the stealthy look of wireless remotes, but you’ll have to remember to check the battery life of the remote.

We’ve omitted charging times since this will depend on the size of the battery. The convenience of charging also depends on how the battery is integrated into the frame and where the charging port is located.

Bosch

Most of the bikes featuring the Performance CX Line motor tend to use the built-in top tube Controller unit and the wireless Mini handlebar remote. This is a slimmer package than the Kiox display and bulky remote we’ve tried in the past.

The Controller lets you turn the bike on, change power modes, and sync to the app with Bluetooth connectivity. One downside here, versus the Kiox remote, is that you can’t see the battery percentage or other useful stats, like speed and vertical ascent. In order to see that, you’ll have to connect the bike to the app.

Yes, it’s annoying to pull out your phone to see the battery percentage. We all get range anxiety once in a while.
Most Bosch e-bikes use the built-in Controller built into the top tube along with the wireless Mini Remote.

There's also the less common Kiox display and wired Kiox remote.


DJI

DJI has drawn on their expertise in building drones for the motor components, but also OLED touchscreens in cameras. This type of display is a first for eMTBs and enhances the user experience another notch. The 2” screen features automatic brightness adjustment and can still read fingertips if it’s raining. It’s a novel concept that I could see being developed even further, similar to what Stark motorcycles have done with their Varg device integration.

The Avinox features not one, but two wireless remotes on the handlebars. The right-hand side toggles through the display screens while the left changes the assist level and engages walk mode. The screen toggling remote could be removed altogether since a button on screen will turn the bike on and toggle through the windows.

The Avinox has one handlebar remote on either side: left toggles the assist, right shuffles through the screen display windows.
Specialized and SRAM use frame integrated displays like this, but they're not touchscreens.


Shimano

When internal cable routing became a headache, some riders rejoiced when wireless drivetrains came about. But then when they arrived on e-bikes, people wanted to go backwards. Well, not necessarily backwards, but they figured, if I have to charge my E-bike, it should charge all of the components at once. Shimano has been down the electronic shifting road before. Their e-bike remote controllers and displays are wired to one main battery.

The new SW-EN600-L switch has ergonomic, rubber-coated buttons that are easy to locate. On that switch is also a power button and other lights for the assist level and battery percentage. Although those are tiny, it makes the same stats display on the handlebar mounted SC-E8000 screen a bit redundant. Like the Bosch Controller unit, the Shimano display only shows the battery life remaining in bars. Surprisingly, the Shimano E-Tube Ride app doesn't take that any further.

The SW-EN600-L assist switch packs a lot into a small area. Finding the rubberized up and down assist buttons isn't an issue.
The SC-E8000 display tucks in next to the stem out of harm's way.

All of Shimano's remotes and shifters connect with wires.
If you want to utilize the Autoshift function of the EP8 motor, you'll need this shifter.


Specialized

Specialized can take credit for kicking off the built-in top tube displays, but now, DJI has surpassed them with their touchscreen display. Still, the Mastermind TCU is a clean attempt that doesn’t throw too many numbers at the rider.

Likewise, the handlebar remote is slim and ergonomic, although not wireless or a hinge-clamp design. There are four buttons two for assist modes, one to toggle through display windows and another to activate Walk Mode. That button can unfortunately overlap with some dropper post levers and be tricky to hold.

Compared to Bosch, DJI, and Shimano, Specialized offers just three assist levels. That should be more than enough for most riders, plus you can play with the tune of each mode. It's also possible to adjust the amount of assistance as a percentage in 10% increments, which can be helpful when trying to match your output with that of a rider on a different motor system.

Specialized's wired remote is discrete in size. Unfortunately, the walk mode button can be tricky to push depending on the dropper post remote/lever position.
The Mastermind TCU has just one button - hold the button to power up or down or press quickly to shuffle through windows.


SRAM

SRAM’s Powertrain is all about integration, from the built-in top tube display, to the full AXS ecosystem. The Powertrain system is wireless except for one optional piece of the puzzle - Automatic Shifting option. Oddly enough though, there are just two assist levels; Range or Rally.

Should you want to turn the motor off for descending, you need an AXS Ultimate pod on the lefthand side of the handlebars. That’s fine if you also have a RockShox Reverb AXS. Otherwise, you can use two remotes, which can get a bit cluttered. The other option is to run a non-SRAM dropper lever and toggle through the assist modes on the Bridge Display.

That's a clean look. All of the Powertrain controls are wireless and sit below the bar. Imagine wireless brakes.
The top tube display has two buttons; one to turn the power on or off, and the other to change the assist and pair with the AXS app.

There's a low chance you'll end up on an e-bike with a Powertrain motor without a RockShox AXS Reverb dropper post, but if you want a handlebar remote (AXS Ultimate Pod behind) to change the assist level, you'll have to get creative with how they integrate.
If that's the case, you can ditch the AXS Pod and resort to pressing the top tube button.



Liveability - Apps


Bosch

Bosch’s Flow app is fairly thorough but a bit overcrowded. Once you navigate to the area you’re looking for, making motor tune adjustments is simple.

Locking motor functions, although not something to solely rely on, are sweet if you’re an e-bike owner. Bosch offers a free security step that the owner can choose to turn on, only allowing the bike to assist once permission is granted through their phone. Additional security and tracking services are available with the premium paid version of the Flow+ app. Those features are available for free from DJI and Specialized.

Another minor quibble is that you can only connect one bike per app at a time, however, that’s less likely to be an issue for a consumer.




DJI

Connectivity is another area where the Avinox shines. Pairing the bike to a phone is done by capturing a QR code. The app covers everything from security, diagnostics, motor tuning, and an auto-assist mode. The Avinox also has the possibility to change the assist level from the motor automatically, based on the cadence and speed of the bike.

Navigating through the motor parameters is straightforward with little redundancy on each page. There are four modes (Eco, Auto, Trail, and Turbo) to tweak with inputs including the amount of assistance, motor overrun, pickup and torque. The user can also optimize the motor for either a 27.5 or 29” rear wheel - there’s no need to bring it to a dealer to make that change.

A highlight of the Avinox has to be its security, which includes a locking motor function, alarm and tracking device.




Shimano

For apps, Shimano has two; the E-Tube, which is more of a diagnostic tool, and the E-Tube Ride, to connect rider’s with the bike. The E-Tube Ride allows for the usual motor tuning and unit tracking. Included is a map function, however, there are no security features.

Overall, the E-Tube Ride app has more of a Windows feel to it than iOS, meaning it’s not the smoothest to navigate. One thing that Shimano does offer though is AutoShift. For that, you'll need a specific shifter and wired derailleur.



Specialized

When browsing the Specialized app, I realized that it’s oddly similar to the one that DJI built. They both balance simplicity with functionality. Everything you’re looking to modify or activate is easily accessible.

Like the DJI Avinox, the Turbo 2.2 motor and Specialized app includes a free flocking motor function, alarm and tracking device too.




SRAM

The AXS app is one of the more intuitive apps to use. Maybe too straightforward. It lacks the ability to change the motor overrun or pickup, granted, it worked well on our Boost Mode Challenge.

When using two AXS pods, you can change the controls around in a variety of ways, including engaging Automatic Shifting.





Verdict

Winner:

Each one of these motor systems is unique in its own way and neither one is perfect. Picking one winner wasn't easy, but if we'd choose just one, based on these tests, it would be the Bosch Performance Line CX. Why? Well, in our tests, the Bosch came out on top in efficiency and useable power. It also fared well for perceived noise. The only small downside to the Bosch unit is the rudimentary Controller.

It's not about the big numbers. While the DJI Avinox had us giggling over its immense peak power and torque, it's still a Class 1 E-bike, and that limits it to a top speed of 32 km/h. The motor is also impressively light, but proved to be less efficient and not that quiet.

Best power:

The Avinox motor also has four distinct modes, one of which taps into an immense amount of power and torque. Thankfully, you can turn this “overdrive” off because it blows the doors off of any other motor here. Granted, those two higher-assist modes use a considerable amount of battery power, it’s a rocketship.

It’s also worth highlighting that the most powerful motor, the Avinox, is also the smallest and lightest motor in the test. Should DJI liscence the Avinox to other E-bike brands, the slim size motor could allow for less constraints on suspension packaging.

Best integration:

This is a closely fought battle that I’d put in a two-way tie between the DJI Avinox and SRAM Powertrain.

The Avinox’s touchscreen makes life easier. You don’t need to pull out your phone as often to read stats. It also works in the rain and with gloves on - that’s better than most phones. Plus, the handlebar remotes are wireless.

SRAM’s marketing spiel might call it an “ecosystem” but when all of their parts are lumped together, it’s a very clean system. The lack of modes and tuning may be less appealing for some E-bike enthusiasts, but for a lot of riders, it simplifies the experience. A clutter-free app tops off the Powertrain drive unit - another motor that’s capable of shifting automatically.
