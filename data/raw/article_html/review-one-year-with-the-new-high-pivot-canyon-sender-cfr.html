Canyon’s Sender has been a fixture on the World Cup circuit since its debut in 2016. A lot has changed since then, but two things have remained true: legendary racer Fabien Barel has been instrumental in the bike’s development, and it’s always been engineered with the pinnacle of downhill racing in mind.

The latest CFR redesign is the most significant overhaul yet, marking the biggest visual and technical changes across the bike’s three generations. Developed in close collaboration with the Canyon CLLCTV team and tested over a full season of World Cup racing, the new Sender brings a high-pivot suspension platform, added adjustability, and a full-carbon chassis built for speed.

Sender CFR Details

• 200 mm travel, 200 mm fork
• Carbon frame
• MX wheels
• High Pivot MX Link suspension platform
• 62.7°- 63° head angle
• 438mm rear center (all sizes) = 455.5mm at sag
• Sizes: S, M , L(tested), XL
• 6-year frame warranty
• Weight: 17.92 kg / 39.4 lb
• Pricing: €4,999 - €5,999 / $5,110 - $7,779 USD
• More info: canyon.com

It’s already proven itself on the big stage with wins from Tahnee Seagrave, Aletha Ostgaard, and Troy Brosnan, but how does it stack up outside the tape for a rider who just loves downhill laps? After months of testing on everything from slick clay ruts to blown-out bike park lines, the Sender CFR has shown itself to be one of the most dialled DH bikes on the market, though not without a few quirks. The most obvious miss? No size-specific rear ends. More on that later.

Canyon offers two carbon-framed models: the Underdog build at C$6,349, and the CFR Team model, a no-expense-spared replica spec with SRAM suspension and DT Swiss wheels for C$8,399. That’s not the cheapest, but it’s still strong value considering the kit. Low-slung race geometry, smooth kinematics, and powerful brakes form the foundation of a bike built to go as fast as possible. And Canyon doesn’t mince words in their own marketing: “Our goal was to build the best and fastest DH bike on the planet, no excuses.”


A nice baby blue frame sure does pop in the lush woods. For the more low-key riders, a blacked-out version also exists.

Pinkbike's Take


Pinkbike's Take
The Sender CFR is the most refined Canyon DH bike to date, quiet, fast, and impressively adjustable. It’s not just a race bike for pros; privateers and park riders will appreciate its versatility and calm handling too. Whether you’re chasing seconds on the clock or stacking laps at the bike park, the new Sender feels like a bike built to win.

The only notable aspect holding the Sender back on faster-pace tracks would be the slightly shorter rear center. That of course will play in your favor on steeper tracks or if you're a freerider looking for responsive 200mm travel rig.— Stephane Pelletier






The High Pivot MX layout looks futuristic and tidy, with all the linkage tucked neatly inside the bottom bracket area.

Tube-in-tube management is nice and tidy for those running their rear brake on the right side.
One downside to a split pivot system is that it makes size-specific chainstays a real struggle. The Sender has one relatively short 438mm rear center length, though it grows to around 455mm at sag.

Construction & Features

The Sender’s frame is a tidy package of race-focused details. The carbon chassis uses Canyon’s polymer-reinforced HIT Technology for impact protection, paired with a full rubber armour kit that shields everything from the downtube to the rocker link. Internally routed cables, a skid plate, ISCG tabs, and integrated fender coverage ensure a quiet and user-friendly experience. Frame weight comes in at 4,680 grams.

The most noticeable update is the new high-pivot MX suspension layout, Canyon’s first foray into the high-pivot world. The frame has a futuristic, clean look, with a low-slung bottom bracket, long and slack geometry, and a linkage-driven single pivot that manages to pack plenty of complexity into a surprisingly tidy design.

Another standout feature is the K.I.S. steering stabilizer, which adds a subtle centering force at the bars for extra stability on high-speed straights. It’s easy to adjust, or remove entirely if it’s not your thing. Out back, Canyon opted for 148mm Boost spacing instead of the DH-standard 157mm, a different approach to most, but one that allows for wheel swaps with trail and enduro bikes in a pinch.

Since the goal was to make a bike that’s race-ready straight out of the box, Canyon built in plenty of adjustability. There’s ±8mm of reach adjustment via tool-free headset cups, flip chips that tweak BB height by ±5mm and progression between 32.7–37%, plus easy-access linkage hardware for quick pit servicing. Importantly, all adjustments are said to work independently, so you can fine-tune one aspect without throwing off another.

Overall, Canyon has clearly sweated the details, aiming to make this race-ready machine as straightforward to live with as possible, whether you’re a factory pro or a privateer.

Suspension Design

At the heart of the new Sender is the High Pivot MX Link layout. A concentric BB pivot and split pivot at the axle create a rearward axle path, with up to 23.5mm of rear-center growth that helps the wheel move up and back through square-edge hits.

If you’re wondering about the “MX” name, it doesn’t actually refer to wheel size, although the Sender is now mixed-wheel only. Instead, it comes from “motocross linkage,” an adaptation of suspension layouts used in motocross. This isn’t entirely new to Canyon either; the Sender has run a version of this linkage since its inception, as have several other DH bikes on the market.

Anti-rise sits high early in the travel (around 130%), which means the bike should stay composed under braking without pitching forward, a characteristic shaped by feedback from Fabien Barel and the Canyon CLLCTV team. Pedal kickback remains impressively low thanks to the idler placement, and the leverage curve can be fine-tuned via a flip chip to run either more supportive or more supple.

Canyon designed the frame around coil shocks, though it’ll run air without issue. The Sender uses a 250 x 75 mm coil shock, with stock spring rates tuned per frame size (S = 350lb, M = 400lb, L = 450lb, XL = 500lb). In practice, the kinematics strike a sweet spot between traction and support, one of the Sender’s most defining traits.

You can read a very in-depth article about anti-rise written by Jessie-May Morgan here.

Geometry & Sizing

With a 63° head angle and a low bottom bracket (347–352mm depending on setting), the Sender is every bit as aggressive as you’d expect from a race-bred DH bike. The 438mm chainstay length looks short on paper, but it extends to 455.5mm at sag, long enough to deliver stability without feeling like a bus.

Unlike the previous Sender, the new version uses a single rear-center length across all frame sizes. The old bike had an axle flip chip to fine-tune chainstay length, but with the move to a split pivot at the axle, that option disappeared. Manufacturing different rear triangles for each size would also add cost and complexity. Canyon says they tested multiple rear-center lengths with their World Cup riders, who agreed this was the best compromise, but I can’t help feeling that true proportional sizing would improve the ride for a broader range of riders.

There’s 5mm of BB height adjustment via a flip chip in the upper shock mount, and reach can be shifted ±8mm with tool-free headset cups, making it easy to tweak fit for different tracks. Four frame sizes cover most riders, with a low-slung top tube that provides plenty of clearance when muscling the bike through rough terrain.

Reach values range from 443mm on the Small up to 518mm on the XL. Interestingly, the jumps aren’t consistent: 25mm from Small to Medium, 27mm from Medium to Large, and 23mm from Large to XL. Stack numbers run from 626mm to 639mm. Overall, the Sender feels roomy across the range, and the ability to fine-tune reach and stack means riders can adjust between a shorter, more upright position for steeper technical tracks, or a longer, lower setup for flat-out speed.

In my case, at 6’3” (191cm), I went with a Large even though I sit between Large and XL. I usually size down for more nimble handling in the bike park. I ran the +8mm reach setting with 10mm of added stack, which put me in a comfortable, versatile position. It worked well for both fast technical DH tracks and Whistler Bike Park laps. For pure racing, though, I’d be curious to try the XL. That said, bigger riders may find the single-size rear end on the short side.

One note: if you want to run a 29” rear wheel, you’re out of luck; the frame is 27.5-only in the back. Personally, I’m a big fan of mixed-wheel DH bikes; they strike a great balance between plowing through rough terrain and keeping the back end maneuverable.

As a consumer-direct brand, Canyon has built its reputation on value-packed build kits, and the new Sender stays true to that formula. The lineup is simple with just two models: the budget-friendly Underdog at $6,349 CAD (not available in the U.S.), and the high-performance Team at $8,399 CAD / $7,779 USD—the version I’ve been riding.

The Underdog gets a well-rounded mix of proven parts: Fox Performance suspension front and rear, DT Swiss F1900 rims laced to 350 hubs, SRAM Maven Bronze brakes, and SRAM GX DH shifting paired with alloy cranks.

The Team spec takes things up a notch with components you’ll find on top racers’ bikes: RockShox Ultimate suspension, a SRAM X0 DH drivetrain with carbon cranks, Maven Silver brakes, and DT Swiss FR1500 wheels on 240 hubs.

Both builds roll on the same Maxxis tires and come with Canyon’s in-house G5 cockpit. The bar-and-stem combo has a quality finish and comfortable sweep, with 30mm rise bars. I do wish bar rise varied by frame size, but it’s easy enough to fine-tune stack height with spacers. Ergon grips and saddles round out both builds, adding a nice finishing touch across the range.

Test Bike Setup

I was fortunate to have Canyon’s Factory Race Team helping with the initial setup, which meant a lot of the fine-tuning most riders would do at home was guided by their mechanics and engineers. I started in a neutral setup: 0mm reach, linear progression, low BB setting, and the stock 450lb spring.

After a few laps on Windrock Bike Park’s Tennessee Nationals course, I made some key changes. I switched to the +8mm reach headset cup, went to the progressive shock mount, and bumped up to a 525lb spring. That progressive mount allowed a slightly more supple initial stroke, but also gave me more mid- and end-stroke support at speed and through big compressions. I also raised the BB setting; the low setting felt too close to the ground and sketchy over rough terrain with pedal strikes. Even in the higher setting, the bike still felt plenty low.

This setup put me at about 30% sag, right where I’d typically be on a DH bike. Shock settings landed at 2 clicks LSC, 2 clicks HSC, and 11 clicks rebound, while the fork was at 185 PSI, HSC fully open, and 7 clicks LSC.

Back home in Canada, riding Whistler Bike Park and Mount Washington, I tweaked things again, slightly stiffening the fork for jumps and adding 10mm of stack height with spacers for a more comfortable position.

As for the K.I.S. steering stabilizer, I experimented with different levels but ultimately wasn’t a fan. It felt unwieldy and unpredictable, so I kept it at the lowest setting for most of my testing.

I first picked up the Sender at Windrock Bike Park at the end of March. Since then, it’s been my go-to for park and shuttle laps—everything from greasy early-season tech at Windrock, to blown-out Dirt Merchant laps in Whistler, to rough bike park tracks at Mount Washington, plus a good dose of loamy, compression-filled trails closer to home. The bike has seen a lot of riding in a short time and, so far, hasn’t skipped a beat.




Performance

Right away, the Sender felt like it was asking me to push harder. The low-slung geometry and high-pivot design make it incredibly confident through rough terrain; it floated over chatter and square-edge hits like a magic carpet.

It did take some adjustment to feel balanced. At first, the relatively short rear end threw me off, but once I switched to the +8mm reach cups, things felt more natural. From there, the more I pushed, the more the Sender rewarded me: braking later, carrying more speed, and trusting the bike to smooth out the nastiest lines.

One thing that stood out immediately was just how composed the Sender feels when braking hard. The bike settles into a neutral position under load, which allowed me to stay calmer, brake later, and push harder than I usually would in steep or blown-out sections.

On high-speed, rough trails, the suspension platform shines. On flatter terrain, however, it takes a bit more effort to keep momentum, and the slack, low geometry can feel twitchy. Cornering was a highlight, the rear-center growth lengthens the bike mid-turn, giving me a really centred and stable position.

Surprisingly, the Sender handled jump trails in Whistler better than expected. It’s not the most playful park bike, but the mixed-wheel setup and raised stack put me in a stable, comfortable position in the air. Manuals were less intuitive; like many high-pivot bikes, the balance point felt vague, almost like balancing on a ball.

Mechanically, my test bike has been almost flawless. Aside from regular chain lube, I didn’t need any mid-season maintenance. Bearings stayed smooth, pivots tight, and the frame shrugged off some nasty rock strikes thanks to its HIT-reinforced armour. Issues were minor: the chain hopped out of the hardest gear twice, and the headset cups developed some creak during peak dust season, but both were easy fixes.

My only ongoing gripe is chainstay length; size-specific rear ends make sense on DH bikes, and the Sender’s single rear-center dimension feels like a compromise for larger riders.

Serviceability has been excellent. After a year of heavy use, the bearings are still solid, and disassembly was moderately straightforward, though not as easy as VPP or 4-bar layouts, since the linkage is all cocooned in the BB area. I was impressed to find Canyon utilized rubber gaskets around all pivot hardware, which seemed to do a remarkable job of keeping the grime out. After a year of riding as much as possible, the bearings were still smooth, and even still had cleean, factory direct grease packed in there. Canyon also offers a deep catalogue of replacement hardware, so keeping the bike running long-term should be painless.





What's the Best Value?

Both build levels are strong options. The Team spec is race-ready right out of the box, with high-end suspension, drivetrain, and wheels. That said, the Underdog punches well above its weight. Performance-level suspension has really stepped up in recent years, and the difference between Maven Bronze/Silver brakes or GX/X0 drivetrains isn’t as dramatic as the price gap suggests. Pair that with solid DT wheels, Maxxis rubber, Ergon touchpoints, and Canyon’s G5 cockpit, and the Underdog delivers a ton of performance for the money.



How Does It Compare?

Canyon Sender CFR Team: $7,799 USD
Commencal Supreme DH V5 RockShox: $7,800.00 USD.



The Commencal Supreme DH V5 is a natural comparison; both are consumer-direct, podium-proven, high-pivot race bikes. Geometry is nearly identical, though the Commencal runs a touch longer and lower. The biggest difference comes at the rear end: the Supreme offers size-specific chainstays plus an axle flip chip, whereas the Sender sticks to a one-size-fits-all solution. That adjustability makes the Commencal more versatile, especially for taller riders.

Spec-wise, Canyon edges out the Commencal. Both share RockShox Ultimate suspension, but the Sender upgrades to an X0 drivetrain, Maven Silver brakes, DT FR1500 wheels, and Maxxis tires versus the Supreme’s GX drivetrain, Maven Bronze brakes, DT F1900 wheels, and Schwalbe rubber. Ergon touchpoints are another nice detail on the Sender. At 39.5 lbs, it’s also lighter than the 41.2 lb Supreme.

Both bikes are clearly capable of winning World Cups. If value is the deciding factor, the Canyon’s build kit tips the scales in its favour.



Technical Report



Maxxis tires: Both builds come with sticky DH casing Maxxis rubber, a proven choice that’s race-ready out of the box.

SRAM Maven Brakes: The new benchmark for power and consistency. The Silvers offer full adjustability, while the Bronzes on the Underdog drop contact point adjustment but retain the same power. My arm pump noticeably decreased with Mavens; less death-grip braking, more energy for holding lines.

Ultimate Suspension: No surprises here. High-end suspension that’s buttery and controlled, exactly what you want on a DH race bike.

Carbon X0 Cranks: Although good on paper, thanks to their lighter weight, I find carbon cranks to be a long-term durability concern. Maybe it's a me thing as a flat pedal rider with some sort of an instep, but that level of wear in a year is concerning. I'd take the alloy version's weight penalty for durability any day.




Pros

+ Race level spec right out of the box
+ Extremely composed under heavy braking
+ Tons of adjustability to suit most riders


Cons

- Taller riders or racers may want a longer chainstay
- Headset creak developed during dusty bike park laps
- Carbon cranks can be a durability concern.
