Review: Formula Selva V – Are 35mm Stanchions Still Enough for Enduro?

When Formula set about designing a new Selva V single-crown enduro fork, they never bought into the 38mm stanchion trend that Fox, RockShox, DVO, and X-Fusion all rushed toward. Formula held their position on 35mm stanchions with the theory that forgiveness in a fork chassis is desired.

The Selva V’s travel can be set to 160, 170, or 180mm, making it ideal for enduro or eMTBs. $1,298 gets you all the tools and auxiliary pieces to keep you busy tinkering on the internal adjustments. Externally, there are just three dials: a singular rebound and compression adjuster, and the seldom-seen fork lockout lever.

The focus of this fork, besides the narrow tubes, is the highly adjustable Compression Tuning System (CTS) damper design. This is a truly unique way of tuning the damping with plug-and-play compression circuits. With eight possible tunes, the Selva V can yield drastically different ride characteristics, but is the CTS an over-engineered solution to a problem that doesn’t exist?

Selva V Details

• Travel: 160, 170 (tested), 180 mm
• Adjustments: rebound, compression, lock-out gate
• Redesigned air spring
• 35 mm stanchions
• Designed for 29" wheels
• Offset: 43 mm
• Maximum rotor size: 220 mm
• CTS damper w/interchangeable shim stacks
• Integrated fender
• Weight: 2,125 g (w/ axle, lever, fender, uncut steer tube)
• Price: $1,298 USD / €1,250 EUR
• More info: rideformula.com

Is bigger always better? Formula doesn't think so - the Selva V only has 35mm stanchions.

Air Spring and Damper

Fox’s new GlideCore air spring moves radially on elastic mounts to reduce friction for a more sensitive ride. Formula does the same on the Selva V, except they use it on both the air spring and the damper. It’s something they call Internal Floating Technology.

Externally, the Selva V doesn’t appear to have a ton of adjustment; there’s just one rebound and compression dial, but don’t let that fool you. Major changes are accomplished by changing the CTS valve or pre-assembled shim stack. There are eight options in total, which are divided into two series of compression force curves: the Traction (progressive) and Support (digressive). It’s enough to keep a full-time suspension tuner busy, but changing the CTS valves is not as daunting as one might think.

A proprietary tool unthreads the colour-coded CTS valves without the need to rebleed the damper. It’s not something you’d likely do on the trail, but they can be swapped faster than an air volume spacer.

On that note, the air spring holds another intriguing component, part of any Formula air fork: the NeoPos air volume reducer. Unlike plastic spacers used in some other forks to adjust air spring progression, the foam-like NeoPos reducers compress as the fork moves through the travel to produce a shallower end-stroke force curve.


A svelte crown for the Selva V.

Compression in blue, lockout "gate" in black. A lockout lever isn't that useful on an enduro bike - you want the fork to compress and steepen the seat tube angle while climbing. Rebound is on the bottom of the lower leg in red.

A short fender is included; bleed buttons are not.

Chassis

35 millimeter stanchions may seem small by today's standards, but it wasn't that long ago that it was the norm on forks like the RockShox Lyrik B-series. Formula says they kept the stanchions narrower to achieve the balance of steering precision and compliance that they believe to be ideal for enduro bikes - they also make a dual-crown version, dubbed the Belva.

Despite its slim looks, the Selva can handle abusive bike park riding. A new lower casting sets the bushings further apart to increase stiffness, a hot topic in the inverted world. There's no pinch bolt on the non-threaded side to grab onto the 15mm axle, though.



There are eight CTS valves split into two series: Support and Traction. The gold and red are included with the fork. Individually, they cost $67 USD, or you can buy the whole series for $280, including the spare tool.

Damper curves: The Support Series uses digressive compression tunes, whereas the Traction Series give progressive variations.

Air spring curves: notice the sharp curve that a solid volume spacer (green) inflicts versus the shallower NeoPos spacer (orange).

Spring on the left. Damper on the right.



Formula includes everything you need to dive into the tuning setup, from the proprietary top cap tool, Neopos volume reducers, two CTS valves, and fork oil. Changing either the CTS valves or the number of NeoPos volume reducers can transform the ride characteristics of the fork.

Price and Weight

There is no base model Selva V, which means it comes at a premium price of €1,250 EUR, the same cost as a RockShox Zeb Ultimate. At that price point, I’d prefer a fork with a traditional setup process, one with a wider degree of external adjustment that I can change on the trail. All of Formula’s products do include a two-year owner-transferrable warranty and are backed by a 10-year support program - nice!

Aimed squarely at enduro and eMTB customers, the 2,125g Selva V sits on the scale right in between the Fox 36 (1,920g) and 38 (2,381g) models.




Setup and Adjustments
The Selva V ships with the gold CTS valve installed, but Formula advised starting by moving up a step to the orange valve for a more aggressive rider. They also proposed leaving the compression and rebound fully open with the ride height set to 20% sag.

That’s more sag than I would typically choose. The trails around Squamish are slower, steeper, and generally have a mix of soft organic soil and mineral dirt. For this, I prefer a higher ride height for the gradient, support for larger compressions that want to throw you over the bars, but still need top-end sensitivity to deal with the slippery rocks and roots that can pop up. On faster, firmer, flatter trails, I saw how Formula’s starting point worked well.

Through talks with Formula and my own experimentation, I tried numerous setups on both the orange and red CTS valves. Each of those valves has a drastically different feel, keeping all else equal.

Formula has an online guide for how to choose the right CTS valve for your style of riding. The language used to describe each valve has a lot of overlap, which makes eight options seem redundant and leads to confusion. Plus, there’s no guidance as to why a rider might close the compression dial or add NeoPos.

With more tuning options comes a greater chance of ending up in a suboptimal setting. Even knowing which direction to go in can take a considerable amount of time.

Each adjustment made to the fork was done one step at a time, starting with less air pressure and open compression.


Performance

Either way you chalk it up, the Selva V is a svelte fork (for the travel) and heavy on hydraulics. The orange digressive CTS valve has that feeling of jumping into a puddle. There’s a firm to overcome in the initial stroke. It offers control and comfort when the ride height favors a lower air pressure.

I found the orange valve too slow to absorb small bumps effectively, even with the compression and rebound fully open. At the same time, I was searching for more support further into the travel and bottom-out resistance. Increasing the air pressure and later adding a second NeoPos firmed up the fork considerably, but obviously did not solve the sensitivity issues.

From there, I switched to the red CTS valve, starting back at 20% sag, 1 NeoPos, and open adjusters. Instantly, the fork was transformed, and sensitivity took shape. A higher ride height and bottom-out support were still needed, so the journey to increase air pressure and add volume reducers started again. I ended up preferring the red CTS valve, 78 PSI (18% sag), 1 NeoPos, and the dials open.

The NeoPos volume spacers offer a unique approach. They change the mid-stroke support in a way that no other air fork using plastic spacers can achieve. Through large compressions or steps in the trail, the fork remained higher in the travel and didn’t run into a harsh wall of progression. It wouldn’t be out of the question to cut them in half and install them in smaller increments, though - adding a second is a big change. I’d also be tempted to fit these to other air forks that typically use plastic volume reducers and see how they react.

I’ve dragged you through tons of damper and spring talk, but the main thing you’re probably wondering is, are those 35mm stanchions flexible? Unless we put the fork in a jig and measure that, it’s a subjective statement. The front tire, wheel, handlebars, and even the frame can play a role in perceived fork flex.

That being said, I didn’t find that to hold me back in any capacity, nor did it noticeably reduce any body fatigue. I never experienced any unusual flex from heavy compressions or through loaded corners. Any comfort claims on Formula’s behalf will be outweighed by air spring and damper harshness found on some air spring or CTS valve combinations.



The Formula Selva V, 35mm stanchions, 2,125 g (w/ axle, lever, fender, uncut steer tube): $1,298 USD / €1,250 EUR/
Fox 38 Factory Grip X2, 38mm stanchions, 2,381 grams (29", 170mm travel w/ 190mm steerer + starnut): $1,249 USD / €1,589 EUR.

Comparison

The Selva V was installed on a Rossignol Heretic enduro bike, which came with a Fox 38 and a Propain Sresh SL eMTB, replacing a Fox 36. If you are focusing on fore and aft flex, you can notice a difference between larger diameter forks, especially under braking and flat landings.

The Formula fork stands out from those two in many ways. First, it has narrower 35mm stanchions than either of the Fox forks, and then comes the damper adjustments. As easy as it may be to twist more knobs in the wrong direction on the Fox, I think the external, on-board adjustments offer a more approachable user experience.



Pros

+ A light chassis option in the long-travel bracket.
+ CTS valves offer quick and drastic damper changes.
+ NeoPos volume spacers add more gradual progression to the air spring.

Cons

- With so many tuning options, it can be overwhelming and time-consuming to find the optimal setup.
- Not the plushest ride.
- Rebound range is limiting the return speed.
- Clunky user experience given the cost.




Pinkbike's Take

Through all the trials and tribulations with the Selva V, I never did arrive at an ideal setup. A muted, hydraulic sense overrides the fork, giving it a glued-to-the-ground response. Traction and support can be sought out, but if you’re looking for a lively, reactive fork, I’d stick to the usual suspects - they're generally less expensive, too. I believe those damping characteristics match the planted ride of a portly e-bike perfectly, which is somewhat ironic based on the smaller stanchion diameter. — Matt Beer
