PINKBIKE FIELD TEST REVIEW

Spot Mayhem 140


Words by Matt Beer; photography by Tom Richards



The Spot brand of bikes may be best recognized for introducing a leaf spring to a few of their full-suspension bikes. One of those is the new Mayhem 140, which was designed to be both stronger and lighter than their previous offerings. The unique suspension design still remains, with a small titanium leaf spring that runs from the downtube to the swingarm.

Lying at the shorter end of the trail bikes here on test, the Mayhem 140 has, you guessed it, 140mm of rear wheel travel. Our bike was built with a 150mm fork, but those focused on descending will be happy to know that the Mayhem can handle a 160mm fork as well.

Spot Mayhem Details

• Carbon frame
• Dual 29" wheels
• 65.2° head angle
• 77° seat angle
• Reach: 415, 445, 475, 505mm
• Chainstay: 435mm
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL
• Weight: 14.1 kg / 31 lb
• Price: $9,499 USD
• More info: spotbikes.com
The carbon frame rides on 29” wheels and is the only bike in the bunch that doesn’t offer any geometry adjustments. Also absent is an In-frame storage compartment - another box not ticked for the Mayhem. Aside from that, the cables are internally routed, the chainslap protection is looked after, and a standard water bottle fits inside the front triangle on its unique mounting bracket.

Geometry figures like a 65.2-degree head tube angle (with a 150mm fork, as tested) and 435mm chainstays don’t exactly paint this bike as a downhill brawler, however, numbers don’t always translate as how we expect them to on the trail. The Mayhem surprised us in two of our introductory challenges; one up and one downhill.

Our “6-Star” build from Spot spares no expense and makes the Mayhem the most expensive bike on test at $9,499 USD. This includes RockShox Ultimate suspension, a SRAM XX1 Transmission drivetrain, and carbon wheels from Industry Nine.





FIELD NOTES



Climbing

Whistler’s valley trails (those outside the bike park) will swiftly point out a bike’s flaws due to their steep and tricky climbs. The Mayhem proved to be a brilliant climber in areas such as riding position, traction, and efficiency. Being the lightest bike on test probably didn’t hold it back either.

The head angle is in the steeper zone, the stack is slightly higher, and the chainstay is short, but you know what? When linked up with supportive suspension like the Mayhem’s, it all equates to an exceptional climber.

On paper, those 435mm chainstays might lead you to believe the Mayhem would be prone to looping out on steep climbs. That’s not the case though. Whatever is happening underneath you with that funky leaf spring, well, there’s some magic to it. That may be due to how the anti-squat rises towards the sag point, similar to Propain’s Pro 10 suspension design, and hence, where Spot’s “Inversion“ moniker comes from.

That doesn’t diminish from the traction on tap either. The wheel hugs the ground impeccably well over small bumps without detracting from the forward momentum you put into the cranks, making the Mayhem light on its feet.

Another bonus is how the system holds the rider forward and upright on the bike. The Mayhem doesn’t lag around tight switchbacks or stumble in those stop-start instances. Pedal strikes aren’t an issue and that allows the Mayhem to scoot through tight cruxes with ease too.



Descending

Surely then, the best climber must be the worst descending bike? That’s not always the case, nor is it true of the Mayhem. This is a bike that wants to pump and jump, rather than tip in and hang on, though. As we saw in our “Perceived Time” test, it’s a bit of a live wire. The Mayhem nabbed the fastest time but took a good amount of focus to keep it pointed straight.

You get the sense of riding on top of the bike, rather than settled down in between those big wheels. It’s also snappy and reactive, helping it navigate tight, slow trails in a flash. The downside is that the front wheel can tend to tuck or twist when dropping into steppy corners. Once the grade of the trail really falls away, the front wheel can have the effect of riding underneath your hands. Reaching the limit of the Mayhem happens sooner than the longer, lower, slacker bikes, such as the Canyon Spectral CF.

And what of that Living Link leaf spring? It is simply another spring, meaning, it resists and restores energy in combination with air shock. You can read more in depth about the influence it has on the leverage curve here.

Coincidentally or not, it provides a springy, light reaction to the suspension. The initial break away isn’t as smooth as the Specialized Stumpjumper, but then again, nothing else on test came close. There is, however, a lot more support earlier on in the travel that keeps the Mayhem from floundering in the middle of its travel.

Frame stiffness has become an increasingly popular topic of discussion amongst us tech editors. Spot tout the improved stiffness on the new Mayhem 140 over the outgoing model and that can certainly be felt on the trail, although it’s not over the top. The front end has a solid feeling, similar to the Santa Cruz Bronson. Out back, it's toned down a degree. The Mayhem offers a solid ride without any harshness when going down rough trails.

While the Mayhem won’t encourage you to chase friends on enduro bikes down fall line descents, it will leave them feeling deflated on fast-paced trails where a dynamic rider is rewarded with bursts of speed. Nothing is lost in the suspension, for better or worse.



Technical Report

Tire Clearance: Whether it’s a constraint of the linkage design or the robust appearance of the chainstay yoke, the gap between the frame and tire is incredibly close. Our 29 x 2.5 Schwalbe Alberts don't have the most aggressive tread out there either (they measure exactly 2.4" in width, although they are on the taller side when measured from the rim sidewall to the top of the tire). Periodically, we experienced instances while climbing where the rear wheel would lock up as the tire wedged debris against the rubber protector. The swingarm also crunched a few rocks against the bottom bracket area too. According to Spot, part of the reason for that tight clearance is the thickness of the rubber frame protection. From the sounds of things, they're working on an update, but for now there's not a ton of room around the tire.

RockShox Super Deluxe Ultimate: This shock hasn’t let me down on any bike I’ve swung a leg over. That holds true for the Mayhem as well. There’s a wide berth of adjustment given by each turn of a clicker. On the ending stroke, the built-in hydraulic bottom out control is handy to have as well since the Mayhem leverage rate isn’t overly progressive.

Industry Nine EN325 Carbon: These carbon rim-alloy spoke wheels are as unique as the bike they’re mounted to. The alloy straight pull spokes have a count of 24 per rim. At the center, the Hydra rear hub doesn’t wait around. With just 0.52-degrees of engagement, they're as close to instantaneous as you’ll get for a pawl system.


Ease of Ownership

Despite different design methods, there’s not much to critique the Mayhem on. The layout of the frame is clean, aside from the interruption of the reinforcing Slaybar. The frame doesn’t require any proprietary components or have strange standards. There’s tidy internal cable routing, full seat post insertion, and a water bottle mount inside the front triangle, and on the lower link.

The leaf spring also reduces the number of bearings used in total on the frame, at least, compared to a dual-link bike. Not all cages will hold the bottle tight enough to keep it from tapping against the shock though, as we found out with our Elite Prism model.

A small issue also arose earlier on in the First Ride when the linkage started to creak, although that escalated into a secondary issue. Torx 30 bolt heads aren’t commonly found on a multi-tool either, so you’ll have to be prepared to tighten loose hardware trail-side, should that occur (we didn’t have any issues during the Field Test). Once extracted and greased, the Mayhem withstood our thrashing.


Pros

+ Great suspension feel
+ Excellent climbing performance
+ A trail bike through and through

Cons

- Very limited tire clearance
- T30 frame hardware is annoying
- No in-frame storage (although there's plenty of room for a frame bag)



Note: In the time since this review aired, Spot has taken steps to increase the amount of tire clearance. Here's the update from Spot:

What we know:
• With the current rubber protection and stock tire (Maxxis Dissector 2.4WT EXO+), we measure 6.5mm of clearance. The Aggressor 2.5WT shows the same clearance.
• With the protection modification we’re implementing, clearance increases to 9mm, providing significantly more room to accommodate a wider range of tires.
• The new Schwalbe Albert used as the control tire is 2.4” wide but is taller than other 2.4” tires, highlighting the reduced clearance.

What we’re doing:
• We’ve updated the chainstay protector to maximize clearance. The rubber still extends across the chainstay bridge but doesn’t duck into the wheel well.
• This new part will be included on all future bikes and sent to existing customers who want the additional clearance. We'll also stock the updated protector as a replacement part.

When we’re doing it:
Now. We’re implementing this change starting immediately. Existing owners can reach out for the new protector.

SpotBikes (Nov 22, 2024 at 13:19)
 Hi everyone, we wanted to hop on to address the tire clearance issue. First though, we want to give huge thanks to Pinkbike for including the Mayhem 140 and mention how stoked we are that the editors dug it!

We appreciate the feedback and have already taken steps to address it directly. The great news is that the reduced clearance is related to a soft part that’s easy to tweak, not a design flaw in the carbon structure. In addition, we’re confident that many riders will not experience a clearance issue with the tire we spec or many of their favorite rubber options. But, with an easy change, we can open things up and provide more room for a wider range of tires, which we’re all about. Here’s the deal:

Basically, we overprotected. The original design of the rubber protection wraps all the way around the chainstay yoke, and additionally, extends down onto the vertical surface inside the wheel well. While this was meant to increase scratch protection, the manufactured part and its adhesive layer came in significantly thicker than what the design called for, resulting in a tolerance tighter than anticipated.

What we know:
• With the current rubber protection and stock tire (Maxxis Dissector 2.4WT EXO+) we measure 6.5mm of clearance. The Aggressor 2.5WT shows the same clearance.
• With the protection modification we’re implementing, clearance increases to 9mm, providing significantly more room to accommodate a wider range of tires.
• The new Schwalbe Albert that was used as the control tire is 2.4” wide but is taller than other 2.4” tires on the market, and highlighted the reduced clearance.

What we’re doing:
• We’ve updated the chainstay protector to maximize clearance. The rubber still extends across the chainstay bridge but doesn’t duck into the wheel well. This is more in line with the protection seen on other bikes.
• This new part will be included on all future bikes and sent to existing customers who would like the additional clearance, and we’ll be stocking the updated protector as a replacement part.

When we’re doing it:
• Now. We’re implementing this change starting immediately. Existing owners can reach out for the new protector.

We are genuinely proud about the performance of the M140 and are so stoked to see it validated by the PB test crew. We think you’ll love it too, and as always, offer our 30-day return policy. Please reach out to us if you have more questions.
