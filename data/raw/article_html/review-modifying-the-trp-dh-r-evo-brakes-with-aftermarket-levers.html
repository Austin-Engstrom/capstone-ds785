Brake choice is a rather personal matter, and while there are objective measures that play into the decision, much of it comes down to little details and subtle differences. Ergonomics are a big part of this calculus, and are generally the hardest part of a system to tune - you're typically stuck with whatever the initial designer laid out, and left to work around those constraints. That element is changing though, as we enter another era of aftermarket brake parts to tune your ride.

I've been a big fan of the TRP DH-R Evo brakes for a while now. They offer excellent power that feels easy to control thanks to a light lever action, and they're impressively consistent over long descents and in foul weather. I don't have too many gripes with the stock levers and ergonomics, but a common complaint revolves around the limited reach adjustment offered by TRP's design. Folks with smaller hands and those who prefer a close bite point often comment on the relatively far distance from grip to lever, even at the extreme of the adjustment range.

As a response to this, more and more aftermarket levers are popping up to offer different ergonomics to the stock offering. Freedom Coast and Oak Components have both come to market with hooked designs that bring the bite point closer to the bar, while maintaining the same adjustment and leverage as the stock brakes. I've been riding both of those as well as the stock levers to get a sense of which best complements these excellent brakes.


Oak TR Lever Details

• 10-35mm adjustment range
• Compatible with TRP DHR, Slate Evo, Slate T4, Trail Evo, and Quadiem master cylinders
• Four color options
• Manufactured in Bavaria
• 20g per lever blade
• $119 USD per set
• oakcomponents.de

The Oak levers have a square and sturdy body with a tapered and rounded hook at the finger, with dimpling machined into the catch for increased grip. They've been extensively machined to remove excess material, in an attempt to reduce weight and visual presence while remaining stiff under hard braking. All cut edges have been chamfered for looks and comfort, and there are 4 finish options available to match your brakes.

Their adjustment range sits 10 to 35mm away from the grip edge.

Freedom Coast Lever Details

• 7-32mm adjustment range
• Compatible with all TRP EVO models
• Ten color options
• Manufactured in California
• 24g per lever blade
• $119 USD per set
• freedomcoast.net

Freedom Coast's levers feature a very aggressively hooked shape, topographical machining marks for grip, and a wild array of color options. They too have some material reduction for weight savings, but not quite to the level of the Oaks. The blade is much flatter and wider, making for a larger feel and form factor in use. The outer edges have been rounded, and the blade tapers outwards towards the finger contact, where it is at its widest.

Their adjustment range sits 7 to 32mm away from the grip edge.

TRP Stock Lever Details

• 21-46mm adjustment range
• Compatible with the brakes it comes on
• One color option
• Manufactured in Taiwan
• 19g per lever blade
• $Free / embedded cost
• trpcycling.com

TRP's stock levers have a long flat section with a hooked end, with cut holes at the tip for grip. There is also dimpling the length of the blade, which might come in handy should you want to want to brake with more than one finger for some reason, or find your self choking up on the lever in some circumstances. They're rounded, smooth, and have a nice pebbly texture to them.

Their adjustment range sits 21 to 46mm away from the grip edge.

Conclusion

If you don't currently have any issues with the fit and function of your TRP brakes, then I wouldn't spring to pay for either of the aftermarket options here. Both are quite expensive, and though their manufacturing quality and bling factor may warrant the price for some, they don't offer quite the same performance upgrade as something like a new set of tires or a fork service. Barring reach issues, I never found the stock levers disappointing when swapping back from the aftermarket options.

That said, if you find that the stock TRP brakes don't work for your hand size, or simply feel wrong per how you tend to set up brakes, then both the Oak and Freedom Coast levers offer some benefits to optimize these already excellent brakes. For me, the choice comes down to two differentiating factors: ergonomics and reach. For me, the Oaks win out on ergonomics, with a much more comfortable blade and finer attention to detail throughout. The rounded grip portion makes a difference when you're reefing on the levers, and feels like an upgrade over the stock option.

For those that want the absolute minimum reach on their brakes (think bite point in the meat of the grip), then the Freedom Coast levers do get a little bit closer at minimum reach. That isn't my metric for success, but some folks love that to-the-bar brake feel and will appreciate the extra few millimeters. It's worth noting that the lever blade is quite wide and flat, so those that like a rounded feel might want to look elsewhere.

My ultimate choice for the TRP DH-R Evos on my personal bike are going to be the Oak TR levers, thanks to their excellent ergonomics and stiff feel. The hooked end mates up perfectly with my preferred bite point (±30mm from the grip), and the finish quality and shaping keep them feeling comfortable and precise.
