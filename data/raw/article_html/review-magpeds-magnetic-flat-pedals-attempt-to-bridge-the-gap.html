Review: Magped's Magnetic Flat Pedals Attempt to Bridge the Gap

Flat pedals or clipless, possibly one of the most fraught and regularly rehashed arguments in the mountain bike world. Clipping in has long been the preferred method for the more pedal-focused disciplines, but the gravity fed side of mountain biking sees more mixed use. This year has been one of the more successful in recent memory for those racing on flats, with numerous EDR wins and podiums already racked up at this point in the season.

There are many folks who have learned to ride exclusively on either flats or clips, and now might want to change systems to reap the benefits of the other option. For some, that change can be tricky to adapt to, especially when transitioning from flats to clipless pedals.

Magped Details

• Gravity & Enduro2 models tested
• Strong magnet on each side
• Multiple pin layout options
• Shoe plate mounts like 2-bolt cleat
• Weight: 558g (Gravity), 475g (Enduro2)
• Price: $209 USD (Gravity), $189 (Enduro2)
• magped.us
As something of a middle ground, Magped's magnetic flat pedals aim to add some foot retention to the standard flat pedal, while remaining easier to disengage than most clipless systems. I've been riding with them off and on for the past few months to get a sense of how viable the third option really is - dig in below.

Gravity on the left, Enduro2 on the right.

Magped makes numerous options for different riding applications, but the core function is similar across the board: it's a flat pedal, but a strong magnet holds onto your foot. There are three magnet strength options available, 100N, 150N, and 200N - Magped suggests the 150N strength for riders up to 80kg, and the 200N for riders 75kg and over, or who would like maximum grip. The 100N option isn't listed in the typical ordering process, as they only recommend it for riders under 45kg.
Both pedal designs come multiple spare pins, so you can customize the layout to suit your use case.

You can modify the holding power of the Magped's magnets via different shoe plates, with their aftermarket "STRONG plates" offering 30% more retention power. Both options bolt into your clipless shoes like an SPD cleat would, with the plate oriented to the fore or aft of the shoe depending on your fit preference.

Sticky!
There's also a stronger attraction plate available.

One unusual detail that you'll need to get honed in before the first ride is the relative height of the magnet relative to the pedal and pins. This is controlled via a through-bolt in the center of the magnet that you can use to compress the rubber backing plate behind the little magic disc. In my experience, this is a slow process of guess and check; once you've honed it in things to tend to stay in place.


Beyond all the magnetic details, they're still essentially just a pair of flat pedals. The Enduro2 and the Gravity differ in their details, but share a similar design and internal architecture. The major differentiator between the two is where the magnet lives; the Gravity's lives in the front half of the pedal, optimizing for a mid-foot pedal position, while the Enduro2's magnet sits behind the axle for a traditional forefoot pedal position.


The Gravity's platform is 112mm long, 110mm wide, and has a 17mm thick body. Forged out of 7075 aluminum and featuring a reinforced 12mm axle, these are the sturdiest option in the lineup. That's reflected in their weight, clocking in at 558g on my scale.


The Enduro2 has a smaller platform, 108mm long and 100mm wide, with a similar 16mm thickness. The body is CNC cut from aluminum, and while there is less information on the axle, it has held up as expected through testing. The 150N pedal weighs 475g, dropping a significant amount off the Gravity option.


Ride Impressions

First, some context on my existing pedal preferences. If I'm clipping in, as I tend to do on rides where I want to go fast or far, I use Crankbrothers Mallet DH or Enduro pedals. I like the ease of entry and release, and the shoe support offered by those pedals remains unparalleled. For chill riding and solo fun, I opt for flats, with a rotating cast of options on deck currently. I like softer-soled shoes for flats riding, as the pedal feel and perceived grip makes a huge difference to me.

Now, the Magpeds. Because you have to use a clipless shoe to mount the metal plate, and because these are essentially flats with a bit of extra stick sprinkled in, you're going to have to be picky. Plenty of clipless shoe options will work, but I think most will be far from ideal. The sole should be fairly flexible, the rubber sticky, and the tread as close to flat and knob-free as possible. I've settled on the Shimano GE9, but any of the options in that lineup should work similarly.


For the most part, I've preferred the Enduro2's layout to the Gravity. That comes down to where the magnet is located, and where it ends up placing your foot. I tend to slam the cleats all the way to the back of my shoe, and like a fairly mid-foot position on most clipless systems out there. With the metal plate set up similarly, I found the Enduro2 to be the more natural foot placement. Even with the plate slid all the way forward in the shoe, the Gravity still felt awkward, with the axle squarely in the middle of my foot. That might appeal to some, and definitely feels more appropriate on a bike where pedaling is a secondary focus - bike park, DH, etc.

Once the magnet height was set and balanced to a nice level of retention, smearability, and release, I just pretended I was on a ride with a typical pair of flat pedals. If you ride in that manner, the Magpeds rarely surprise you, save for the moments where you need to dab a foot faster than your brain can think, and when they really do hold your stompers down tight on the pedal through rough bits of trail.


Those dabs and unclips (de-magnetization moments?) take a bit more planning than expected, and involve what I think of as sort of a sideways peeling motion to get away cleanly. You can't pivot like you would when clipped in, as the pins of the pedal prevent that motion. If you happen to be in the 6-and-12 position with the cranks, you can simply lift the upper foot straight up, but that's an awkward position to find yourself in. Hence, the peel.

In their best case scenario, the Magpeds offer a feel similar to flat pedals, but with no chatter or pedal roll in the roughest sections of trail. I think that same effect can be achieved if you get your shoe and pedal combination right with typical flats, but the magnet is a fast track to the sensation. They don't really simulate the clipless experience for me, but if you were to pair the Strong plates with the strongest magnet option available, you'd be a shade closer to that sensation. Ultimately, that's not really what they're for, so I'd sooner err on keeping them in their place as a third and separate alternative.


Pros

+ Third option, crossover of flats and clipless
+ Solid pedals, magnets aside
+ Tunable system

Cons

- Fussy adjustment
- Not actually the best of both worlds
- Magnet could be more robust



Pinkbike's Take

If you find yourself caught between the freedom of flat pedals and the security of clipless, then this could be a viable third platform to try. I found the setup and learning curve a bit more frustrating than I'd expected, and often found myself wishing I was either standing on flats or clipped in with a cleat. That said, I think some riders will dig the semi-secure flats feel, and the adjustability that this system offers. — Dario DiGiulio

 @fabwizard: from this and other reviews, it think it’s more a case of “different to unclip”.
With a clip less pedal you rotate your foot. (Ankle out or toe out). With these you “roll” you foot, lifting first the inside (medial) edge of your foot.

Also, with magnets, the range of motion required will be tiny, while clips need a few degrees.

So, for adaptive use, I think it comes down to individual riders’ range of motion, and strength in particular directions, and then choosing clips vs magnets based on that.

whittemoregulch (Jun 13, 2025 at 15:26)
 I've been riding with these for a couple of years and really do like the hybrid aspect of the the pedals. They act like flats but you engage more muscles and power in pedaling sections. If you disengage the magnet you still have ways to use the pedal flexibly like a flat with the shoe. The main thing you have to get used to is the float-less feel of the shoe when you are snapped in, though there are ways to tune this to give you more float tolerance and there is a full float option as well (see below). A few things to add:

1. If you brought flats or regular shoes by accident the pedal works just like a flat pedal. This is also useful if other members of your family or friends use your bike but don’t like to go clip-less. You don’t need to swap out the pedals all the time in these situations.
2. Similarly, if you jump a friends bike with flat pedals your clip-less pedals with the recessed metal plates work fine on flat pedals.
3. Your Magped clip-less shoes won’t mar your hardwood floors for the same reason since the plat is well recessed in the cleat space area of the shoe.
4. There is a cleat option were you can treat the pedal more like frog clip-less pedal. It’s basically a plastic plate with a hole you can add the cleat plate the covers over the magnet when you clip it and get float on the pedal instead of using the pins to keep your feet planted. It completely changes the characteristic to a floating the pedal connection, but you lose the recessed advantages of the cleated shoe per above (Namely 2. and 3.)
5. Get the strongest magnets and strongest plates for MTB use. You can bunny hoe with them on medium weight MTBs, but for ebikes bunny hoping is not reliable.
