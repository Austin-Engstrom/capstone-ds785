Review: The New Transition Spur is Lightly Revised and Still Just as Fun

Mar 3, 2026
by Mike Kazimer

Somehow nearly six years have gone by since Transition first let the Spur out into the world. The 120mm 29er deserves at least partial credit for ushering in the downcountry trend, the funny blip on the mountain bike history timeline when more riders started to realize that relatively lightweight, shorter travel bikes don’t necessarily need to have sketchy handling. While the term has had its time to shine (and it was pretty much a joke to begin with), there are now more bikes than ever that could be placed in this made-up category.

These days, I’m inclined to call the Spur a light trail bike. With 29" wheels, 120mm of rear travel, and a 130mm fork you could certainly roll up to an XC race on it, but it’s best suited to quickly covering a wide range of terrain; it’s a cross-country bike in the original sense of the term, back when there wasn’t a category for every 10mm change in rear travel.

What's New With the Spur?

In-frame storage: Just like the Sentinel, the Spur now has in-frame storage. Transition calls it a B.O.O.M. Box, which stands for Burritos Or Other Munchies. Ha. I give that a 3 out of 5 on my marketing acronym ranking scale. The nice thing about this storage design is that it’s located below, rather than directly under, the water bottle cage. That means the door is less likely to rattle or wear out, since there isn’t any weight pulling on it from above. That’s a good thing, since it's kind of flimsy feeling.

Fully internal cable routing: All of the housing is internally routed on the new Spur, a departure from the original and its externally routed brake line. Sometimes aesthetics win out over practicality, and tucking everything inside does give the frame a nice clean look. There are new cable entry ports at the headtube, and full tubes on the inside of the bike to make brake, dropper, or derailleur housing installation a relatively simple procedure.

Hidden linkage hardware: In another move that was done to polish the Spur’s looks, the bolts for the upper shock link thread in from the back side, leaving a smooth surface at the upper end of the seat stay. That does mean you’ll need to unbolt the shock to access those bolts - I feel like there’s a saying about beauty requiring pain, or something along those lines that would apply here.

Adjustable geometry: The lower shock mount can be unbolted and flipped over, which slackens the head angle by .5-degree head angle lowers the bottom bracket by 7mm.

Geometry

The Spur’s geometry was slightly revised compared to the original, but it’s not drastic - riders that enjoyed the first generation will feel right at home on the new one. The fork travel has been bumped up to 130mm on all models, but the head angle stays the same at 66-degrees (with the option to slacken it to 65.5-degrees via those aforementioned flip chips).

The seat angle has steepened slightly, and now measures 76.4-degrees, and the chainstay lengths now vary depending on the frame size. On the small and medium frames the chainstays measure 434mm, and on the L and XL they’re 439mm.

The final geometry alteration to note is the seat tube length - that’s been chopped down substantially, which should make it easier for riders to run longer travel dropper posts.





Suspension Design

The Spur is still rocking a flexstay suspension layout, which has become the norm for the vast majority of bikes with 120mm of travel or less. The small rocker link drives a 190 x 45mm shock. According to Transition, their goal with the Spur's kinematic was to create a supple initial portion of the travel, while maintaining control and bottom-out resistance later on in the stroke.

SPUR


Test Bike Setup

The new Spur comes with a cockpit setup that matches my preferences perfectly - it’s almost identical to how I set up the Spur that I owned four years ago, with a 40mm stem and a 40mm rise bar. It even comes with a 210mm dropper post, an indication that it’s meant for more than just cruising around on mellow, rolling terrain.

I settled on running 27% sag for most of the test period. Transition suggests setting up the Float shock between 22% - 33% sag, which is a pretty wide range, but it makes sense given the different way this bike can be ridden. Go with less sag for a snappier, more responsive feel, and increase the sag if you want more grip at the expense of some pedaling efficiency.

The Forekaster tires were a good match to the trails where this bike shines, and they stayed in place and full of air for the duration of testing, inflated to 21 psi for the front and 23 psi in the rear.

Climbing

The Spur’s seat tube angle is a little steeper than before, but the reach also increased, which means the climbing position feels nearly identical to the original. I wouldn't have minded if it got even steeper, but realistically it sits in a good place, one that allows for a range of setups. Plus, seats have rails for a reason. Riders who prefer a more hunched-over, XCish position will be able to achieve that by installing a longer stem and lower rise bars, and riders who’d rather be little more relaxed and upright will feel at home with the stock setup.

The Float SL shock on the Spur has three different compression options (Open, Medium, Firm), but I was totally content keeping it in the middle setting and never touching it again. The Spur maintains its geometry well while climbing - it doesn’t have the extra-firm, brutally efficient feel of some XC bikes, and it’s all the better for it. I’d call its overall climbing feel comfortably calm - there’s plenty of grip to be had, but there’s also enough support for putting down the extra watts required to beat your buddies to the top of that seemingly endless fireroad climb.

The Spur goes into its travel and settles at the sag point easily, and then hovers nicely around that spot while climbing. Even bigger weight shifts, like the ones that occur when standing out of the saddle to get the bike up and over a taller obstacle, don’t do much to upset the Spur. With all of the flexstay XC bikes out there these days, I understand the desire to pigeonhole them into one category and say they all ride the same, but that's simply not true - different leverage ratios, shock tunes, and frame geometry numbers can make for very different ride characteristics. With the Spur, Transition have given it a compliant yet lively feel, one that works really well on more technical climbs (and descents).

On tighter, slower speed climbs the Spur’s wheelbase length will be noticeable to riders who are used to shorter, and possibly steeper XC machines, but for riders coming to the Spur after an extended stint on longer travel trail or enduro bikes its handling will feel refreshingly quick. The same sentiment applies to the 27.4-pound weight of my test bike. That’s not crazy light compared to purebred XC race machines, but it’ll be a welcome change to anyone who’s grown accustomed to slogging around on a 37-lb enduro bike, and it certainly doesn’t feel heavy in the grand scheme of things.


Descending

It’s the Spur’s descending capabilities that helped put it on the map when it was first launched back in 2020. At the time, many XC bikes were still shedding their ‘steep and sketchy’ geometry numbers, and there weren’t nearly as many options for riders looking for a lightweight bike that could still hang on the descents. Thankfully, things have changed for the better, but the Spur is still up towards the front of the pack when it comes to just how much fun it is to ride. This is a goof-off bike if there ever was one - something about its handling and overall feel encourages slashing turns, tossing in a little manual here, double up some trail features over there.

It’s possible to make the Spur lower and slacker than its stock configuration, but I was completely content with how the stock 66-degree head angle and 40mm bottom bracket drop felt. It’s quick and easy to live with on rolling terrain, but it’s also impressively surefooted on steepr trails for a bike in this travel bracket. When speeds ratchet upwards, the longer reach helps add stability, and the bottom bracket height gives it that oft-cited ‘in-the-bike’ feel, which makes for some very satisfying cornering moments.

The comfortable nature I mentioned on the climbs carries over to the descents as well - the Spur does a good job of managing its travel, doling out just the right number of millimeters to take the edge off rough sections of trail without blowing through it too quickly. Yes, if you send it too far you’ll bottom out the shock with a little ‘thwunk’, but there's plenty of progression to keep that from happening too often - running out of travel wasn't usually on my list of concerns when tipping it into something steep and strange.

The last time I was on a Spur it had a RockShox SidLuxe shock, which I didn't really have any complaints about, but with the Float SL and the 34 SL this updated version has a slightly deeper suspension feel; there's a little more margin for error, and the consequences of a poor line choice (or three) aren't as substantial.

How Does It Compare?

The Norco Revolver and the Spur were developed within an hour’s drive of each other (Transition’s in Bellingham, WA, and Norco is in Port Coquitlam, BC), so it's pretty interesting to see the similarities (and differences) out on the trail.

I’ll cut right to the chase in this matchup: for my riding style, I prefer the Spur. Its geometry addresses the small gripes I had about the Revolver; the lower bottom bracket and longer chainstays give it a rounder, more carvey feel in corners (which I prefer), and the frame itself does a better job of muting chattery bits of trail. On the flip side, the Revolver's frame is lighter and stiffer, making it better suited for riders with more of a focus on XC racing.

The Spur’s build kit also caters more towards the way I’d set up a bike like this, with a 210mm dropper post, and 40mm rise bars. That’s how I ended up running the Revolver too, but it wasn’t how it came out of the box.

For riders that are more focused on the climbs than descents (yes, those riders do exist), the Spur may not quite scratch that itch. It is a very good climber for a light trail bike, but it doesn’t blast uphill the same way as something like the Yeti ASR, which weighs 3 pounds less and has a 30mm shorter wheelbase. You could certainly knock some weight off the Spur with lighter wheels and tires, but I personally wouldn’t spend too much time or money trying to morph it into an XC race machine.

The Eagle 90 build is the one that stands out to me. Sure, you don’t get Shimano’s fancy Di2 wireless drivetrain, and the fork and shock don’t have a Kashima coating, but it’s also $1,000 less expensive than the XT Di2 model. I’ve had good luck with the Eagle 90 drivetrain, and while it doesn’t shift as quickly as the XT Di2, it’s quieter and doesn’t have any batteries to charge.

That $6,499 price tag still seems pretty high, though; that’s the same as a Specialized Epic 8 Evo, which has a GX AXS wireless drivetrain and carbon wheels.

Honestly, the best value would be to purchase the previous generation Spur - some of those are still available on closeout, and they're no less enjoyable to ride than the new one. Sure, there's no in-frame snack storage, but that's not really a huge selling point, at least not to me.

Technical Report

165mm cranks: I was skeptical of the whole short crank movement when it started gathering steam a few years ago. 170mm cranks are perfectly fine, damnit. But then I switched to 165mm cranks and the world didn’t end, and my pedals didn’t hit as many rocks. It felt a little strange at first, but now I don’t give it a second thought. Even the roadies have hopped on the short crank bandwagon.

Maxxis Forekaster tires: The Forekasters are a great option for a bike like the Spur, especially in the middle of a Pacific Northwest winter. They’re grippier than a Rekon, and much faster rolling than an Assegai / DHR II combo. They strike a nice middle ground between XC and enduro tires, which makes them the ideal choice for the Spur.

Shimano XT Di2 drivetrain: Another day, another noisey Di2 drivetrain. I’ve said it before, so I won’t dwell too much on this topic - Shimano’s Di2 derailleurs suffer from excessive chainslap due to the lack of an actual clutch. Other than that, the shifting speed is lightning fast, the lever ergonomics are great, and the battery life is commendable. If only it were quieter.

Finishing touches: Historically, the paint quality on Transition’s frames hasn’t been particularly noteworthy. If the condition of my test Spur is any indication, things have taken a turn for the better. Maybe the glossy finish helps, but so far there aren’t any premature wear marks or chips to be seen, and this bike has been on plenty of greasy, grimy rides.

Fox 34 SL: The previous generation of the 34 was an excellent fork, and the 34 SL maintains that legacy. More aggressive riders may want to consider a 36 SL, due to the extra stiffness, but I was able to find a good setup with the 34 SL that complemented the feel of the Float SL shock.

DT Swiss 350 Ratchet hub: I had to do the scoot of shame, half pedaling and half pushing my way back home after several of the teeth on the 54-tooth ratchet ring in the 350 hub deformed and would no longer engage properly. It was a colder day, with temps just above freezing, which could have affected the special grease that's supposed to keep things operating smoothly, but still, hub failures are never any fun.



Pros

+ Hits the mark when it comes to overall handling.
+ Short stems, higher rise bars, and longer dropper posts are great to see on a 120mm bike.

Cons

- The frame weight's reasonable, but gram counters may want something even lighter.
- Even the base model is on the pricier side of things.





Pinkbike's Take

The Spur is new and improved, but luckily it’s not all that different from the previous version. It’s still an absolute riot to ride on all sorts of terrain, a short travel trail bike that feels right at home on those big, all-day missions, or just ripping around on familiar trails during an extended lunch break.— Mike Kazimer
