Review: The Yeti SB165 Is Still a Pedalable Park Bike

Oct 7, 2024
by Mike Kazimer

REVIEW
Yeti SB165

WORDS: Mike Kazimer
ACTION PHOTOS: Eric Mickelson

The SB165 is the longest travel option in Yeti's lineup (excluding the protoype DH bike that Richie Rude has been racing on this season... fingers crossed that makes it to production someday). There's no reason it couldn't be used in an enduro race, although at its core it's really more of a freeride / big mountain machine, a bike with more of a focus on navigating gnarly terrain than going as fast as possible between the tape.

As the name indicates, the SB165 has 165mm of rear travel, which is paired to a 170mm fork. It'll accept up to a 190mm fork, and its approved for use with a dual crown fork, just as long as the axle to crown measurement is at or below 607mm.

Yeti SB165 Details

There are four different complete models, with prices starting at $6,300 USD for the C2, and going up to $9,200 for the T3 version shown here. Highlights of the T3 build include a Fox 38 Factory for with a Grip2 damper (the bike was launched before Grip X2 came out), a DHX2 coil shock, SRAM Code RSC brakes, an X0 T-type wireless drivetrain, and DT Swiss EX1700 aluminum wheels.

Frame Details

Over the last few years an increasing number of brands have implemented various levels of adjustment into their frames, everything from a simple flip chip for different rear wheel sizes, to frames that allow almost every geometry figure and the amount of shock progression to be adjusted. You won't find any of that with the SB165 – other than the ability to run a longer travel fork, which would slacken the head angle even further, there aren't any adjustments to been seen. There's not any frame storage either, for that matter.

I actually don't mind the what-you-see-is-what-you-get approach – it's kind of nice being able to hop and a bike and not have those "Would this be better in X mode" thoughts rattling around your head. That said, it's still worth noting that the constant tinkerers out there may find themselves wishing for more chips to flip.

Yet offers the SB165 in two flavors of carbon – Turq and C. It's similar to what Santa Cruz does with their CC and C frames, where one is lighter and more expensive, and the other is slightly heavier and slightly less expensive. The concept of having two different layups for bikes other than full-on XC race rigs is still a little strange to me – I'd be curious what the sales split ends up being between the two options.

Other details included tube-in-tube internal routing (even for a cable actuated drivetrain), a threaded bottom bracket, generous chainslap and down tube protection, plus plenty of room for a water bottle inside the front triangle.

The 165 was designed with coil shocks in mind.
The SB165's Switch Infinity system.



Geometry


The SB165's geometry isn't all that different from the previous model, with the exception of that 29” front wheel. The head angle still sits at 63.5-degrees, although now that's with a 170mm fork vs. the 180mm fork on the prior model, and reach numbers range from 430mm for a size small and go up to 505mm for a size extra-large. The chainstay length varies depending on the frame size, but there's only a six millimeter difference from the smallest to largest size – that's closer to the size difference you'd expect to see between each size in order to think about claiming they're size specific. No matter what, the chainstays are on the shorter end of the spectrum.

The head tube length is on the shorter side too, which means the stack heights aren't all that high, although Yeti does spec' the bike with a 35mm rise bar to keep the front end from feeling too low.


Suspension Design

The SB165 uses Yeti's Switch Infinity suspension layout, which uses a translating pivot that moves upwards in the beginning of the travel, and then downwards deeper in the travel (that's what the two Kashima-coated rails above the bottom bracket are doing). The SB165 has a 22% leverage rate change, with a linearly progressive change for the first two-thirds of the travel, and then a more pronounced ramp up for the last third in order to increase the bottom out resitance for bigger hits. That leverage rate change should allow the bike to work well with both air and coil shocks.

A grease fitting is in place to help keep things moving smoothly, and Yeti recommends a lubrication interval of every 40 hours.

RIDING THE
YETI SB165

Test Bike Setup

I only made a few small changes to the SB165 to get it to better suit my needs. The first was swapping the 50mm stem for a 40mm one. That's mainly down to personal preference, but for my 5'11” height and overall dimensions I'm typically more comfortable with the handling of a bike with a 40mm stem vs. something longer (or shorter), at least if we're talking about enduro / freeride bikes. With that swap made, I trimmed the bars to 770mm and was ready to roll.

I've spent a lot of time on the Fox 38 Grip 2, so getting that setup only took a matter of minutes – I ran 82 psi, and the rebound and compression set to my personal preference. For the DHX2 I ran a 450 lb spring, which put me just under 30% sag.

Testing Info

Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, and the surrounding area over the course of the spring and summer, with conditions tending to be on the drier and sunnier side of things, although there were more than a few soggy rides. The loop above would be a good way to get acquainted with the 165's handling before venturing off in search of even steeper, rougher tracks.


Climbing

At its core, the SB165 is a bike that's best suited for grinding up a logging road and then bombing down something steep and rowdy. Many of my rides on it involved exactly that – I'd flip the climb switch on the DHX2 to firm things up and then comfortably cruise to the top of whatever exciting trail was on the menu that day. The climb switch is easy to reach, and since it doesn't fully lock out the shock it's usable even on rougher climbs, since the shock can still move enough to provide traction. The movement that occurs when the shock is open has to do more with rider weight shifts and the sensitivity of the coil shock more than anything else – it stays fairly calm if you're good a staying seated and spinning circles, but there is more movement when you start to muscle it around.

The seat angle is steep without being extreme, which means that the climbing positions remains comfortable even when you're not trying to scale a vertical wall. I am glad Yeti spec'd a higher rise bar and left enough room for a decent stack of spacers under the stem – the 107mm head tube length is a little stubby, and with the stem slammed and lower rise bars the fit of the bike wouldn't have been as comfortable.

On more technical singletrack climbs, the SB165 does feel like a big bike, but it's not so slack and sprawling that it feels totally out of its element during those mellower moments. The short chainstays help with maneuverability, at least to a certain extent, but the also make it a bit harder to keep the front end down on really steep ascents. Once your weight gets back past a certain point the wheel wants to lift, and it takes more body movement to maintain balance compared to a bike with longer chainstays and a steeper seat tube angle.

Compared to other bikes in this category, the SB165 isn't as energetic as the Ibis HD6 or the Propain Tyee, but it has more support for those out-of-the-saddle efforts than the Rocky Mountain Slayer. On really steep climbs I prefer the geometry of the Raaw Madonna, but for all-round usage the SB165 gets the nod.


Descending

The SB165 falls into the no-fuss category when it comes time to descend. It's one of those bikes that doesn't ask that much from its rider; you don't need to try to break the sound barrier in order to have a good time. Of course, it does feel best blasting down steep trails, but it doesn't feel overly lethargic when things mellow out a little bit.

The slack head angle and overall wheelbase keep it stable at the higher speeds encountered in a bike park or similar setting, although the shorter chainstays prevent it from having the same unflappable level of calm that a bike like the Raaw Madonna possesses. I'll get into the handling differences between those two specific bikes in a bit, but the main takeaway is that it's easier to let the back end break traction on the SB165 compared to the Raaw.

The SB165 doesn't feel quite as balanced front to back as the Madonna, or Commencal's Meta V5 for that matter. Yeti says that the short rear end was chosen to complement the smaller rear wheel, but more and more I'm convinced that going slighly longer with the chainstays on a mixed wheel bike is the way to go. That said, I try not to be too dogmatic with my geometry ideals – my job would be pretty boring if all bikes ended up with the exact same geometry.

For some riders, the ease with which the SB165 can slap through a corner or loft off the lip of a jump will be strong selling points, and I get it. After all, if this is a freeride bike then it might as well make those freeride flicks nice and easy, right? (Just kidding - I'm actually very glad the trend of randomly sliding out the rear wheel on straight sections of trail to get 'style' points seems to have died off). The short back end can also be a benefit when navigating through tight, awkward turns, since there's less bike behind you to worry about.

When it comes to the overall suspension performance, the SB165 does a great job of predictably handling impacts of all sizes. The small chatter is nicely muted, and there's plenty of ramp up for those bigger hits. I was happy with the coil shock, although I do wonder how the SB165 would feel with a larger volume air shock – I feel like that would help it lean into its bike park / big jump tendencies.

How Does It Compare?


As that old, strange saying goes, “There's more than one way to skin a cat,” and putting the SB165 up against the Raaw Madonna V3 is a perfect example. Both bikes are focused on descending, and both have a 170mm fork with 160+ millimeters of rear travel, but there are some significant geometry differences.

The front end height is a big one – the stack on the Madonna is 664mm vs 630mm on the SB165. That's due to the Madonna's tall head tube, and its low bottom bracket. The Madonna also has a longer wheelbase, at 1284mm vs the Yeti 1266 thanks to its 450mm chainstay length.

Those differences are very clear on the trail – cornering on the Madonna reminds me of carving on skis – it's more about tipping the bike as one unit. It felt like that I was steering more with my feet on the Madonna, and with my hips on the the SB165. I really enjoy the calm nature of the Madonna, but that may dull the edges a bit to much for some riders. The SB165 is more eager to get off the ground, and its nature is a little more drifty / slappy, compared to planted and composed.

As for price, the Madonna's aluminum frame and shock is $3,258 USD, and the carbon Yeti goes for $4,800. The Yeti frame is going to be lighter, but whether that's worth the extra $1,600 is between you and your wallet.



Which Model is the Best Value?

Realistically, if you're on a budget, Yeti isn't the best company to gravitate towards. $9,200 is a lot of money for a freeride bike, and you're not even getting carbon wheels for that price. A Nomad with carbon wheels and a similar / better spec is almost the same price, and Santa Cruz isn't exactly known for focusing on affordability.

That said, if I had to choose, I'd probably go with the $6,300 C2 model, and consider upgrading the fork damper once my savings account recovered from that sizeable hit. Or, there's also the option of going with the previous version, the full 27.5” SB165. Those bikes are on sale at a significant discount – you can save thousands of dollars if you're not fussed by the smaller front wheel.


Technical Report

I mentioned most of the quibbles I had with the build kit in the First Ride article on the SB165, but I'll briefly go over them again here.

Centerline rotors: Running a 220mm front rotor helps maximize the power available, but SRAM's newer, thicker HS2 rotors would have been a better choice – those provide a noticeable bump up in power and consistency. Maven brakes wouldn't have been out of place either, but those weren't available when this bike was launched.

Silverado saddle: There's a new WTB Silverado on the market, but the one on the SB165 isn't it. The dimension almost look comical, especially compared to the shorter and slightly wider options that are becoming more common. I swapped it out for a Nukeproof Horizon saddle (RIP) and was much happier.

Tire spec: The SB165 was initially spec'd with an EXO+ tire front and rear. That's since been updated, and there's now a DoubleDown casing rear tire. I'm a fan of that setup for most conditions here in the Pacific Northwest, and riders that want the most flat protection can always swap out the front for something beefier.

Livability

The SB165 is an easy bike to keep up and running, as long as you don't totally neglect that Switch Infinity system. All of the hardware has remained secure, and there haven't been any mystery creaks to chase down. The cable routing is well thought out, and I didn't have any excessive rattling to deal with. Overall, a pretty pleasant bike to live with – it's the mountain bike equivalent to a roommate that cleans up after themselves almost all of the time and doesn't hold multi-day ragers, at least not without asking first.





Pros

+ Easy handling, especially when it comes to berms and jumps
+ Smooth, predictable suspension performance
+ More versatile than numbers might suggest

Cons

- Some of the spec isn't the latest & greatest
- Expensive, even compared to direct competition
- No geometry adjustments or frame storage



Pinkbike's Take

Yeti tout the SB165 as being a do-it-all big bike, and that's exactly what it is. For riders that want to ride lifts and slash berms one day and then go for a big pedal to access some steep and spicy lines the next, it certainly fits the bill.

It's not going to be a bike for everyone – the price is one factor, and riders that need maximum adjustability, or who prefer longer chainstays will want to look elsewhere, but there's certainly room in the world for a long travel machine that doesn't require reaching ridiculously high speeds to come alive— Mike Kazimer
