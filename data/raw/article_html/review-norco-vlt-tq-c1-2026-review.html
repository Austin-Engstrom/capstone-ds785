Review: Norco's New Sight VLT TQ - High-Pivot Meets Mid-Power

Feb 18, 2026
by Mike Kazimer

The new Norco Sight VLT TQ brings the total number of models bearing the Sight name up to three. On one side of the lineup you have the non-motorized Sight, the all-mountain machine that came out in 2024, and on the other side there’s the Sight VLT CX, the full-powered eMTB that uses Bosch’s Performance CX motor. The new VLT TQ splits the difference, putting out 60 Nm of torque and a maximum of 350 watts of assistance thanks to TQ’s HPR60 motor.

The Sight rolls on mixed wheels, and has 150mm of rear travel paired with a 160mm fork. There are three complete models, with prices starting at $6,599 for the C3, and going up to $10,599 USD for the C1 reviewed here. Build kit highlights of the C1 include a 160mm Fox Factory 36 fork, Crankbrothers Synthesis carbon wheels, Shimano's wireless XT Di2 drivetrain, and 4-piston XT brakes.

Frame Details

The VLT TQ (every time I write those initials it feels like I’m just typing random letters) has a similar but slimmer frame profile compared to the full-power VLT. All three TQ models have full carbon frames, and they’re all equipped with mixed wheels. 150mm of rear travel is delivered by Norco’s VPShp design (hooray, more initials), which stands for Virtual Pivot Suspension high pivot.

There’s a small display situated in the top tube, and a two-button remote to switch between motor modes on the left side of the handle bar. The charging port is located partway down the top of the downtube, which helps keep it from getting absolutely blasted by mud and grit - I prefer e-bikes with charging ports in this general area, rather than down low in the splash zone by the bottom bracket.

There’s room for a water bottle inside the front triangle, and there are two bolts on the underside of the top tube for storing a tube or tool.




As for the motor itself, the TQ HPR60 puts out - you guessed it - 60 Nm of torque, and up to 350 watts of peak power. For comparison, the full-powered Sight VLT with Bosch's CX motor has 100 Nm of torque, and 750 W max power. It's essentially the difference between jogging up a hill versus sprinting up it as fast as possible.

There are three main motor modes that can be customized in the TQ app. The Sight ships with custom modes that Norco selected, which means there's room to make the bike even peppier by opening the app and bumping up the output. I was happy with the stock settings, but just know that it's possible to give the bike a little more juice by making some adjustments in the app. Of course, that'll also reduce the range, which is why I kept things close to stock.








Geometry

The Sight TQ’s geometry numbers are identical to those of the Sight VLT CX - Norco call it all-mountain geometry, and I’d say that’s accurate. Key numbers include a 64-degree head tube angle, a 77-degree seat tube angle, and a 436mm chainstay length for a size S3. That chainstay length may appear to be on the shorter side, but don’t forget that it increases as the bike goes through its travel - at sag it measures 445mm.

Norco was one of the first companies with different rear center lengths for different sized bikes, and that's still the case here. It's not really a massive difference, but it is more than zero - there's an 8mm difference in rear center length between the S1 and the S4. Reach numbers range from 422mm for the S1 up to 522 for the S5.



Suspension Design

The Sight TQ VLT uses the same high pivot Horst link layout found on the non-motorized Sight and on the more powerful Bosch-equipped Sight VLT. This design has the idler pulley located on the chainstay, separate from the main pivot shaft, which allowed Norco’s engineers to fine-tune the amount of anti-squat. The design also requires paying a licensing fee to I-Track, who hold the patent for the design.

For riders looking for a little more squish, running a 230 x 65mm shock instead of the stock 230 x 60 will increase the travel to 162mm. The bike is spec’d with a 160mm fork, but running a 170mm fork is totally acceptable.

RIDING THE
Sight VLT TQ

Test Bike Setup

Norco's Ride Aligned online guide serves as a good place to start when it comes to suspension settings - just plug in your height, weight, and select your riding position and it'll spit out fork, shock, and even tire pressures. Norco was still fine-tuning the outputs when I received the bike, but by the time you're reading this everything should be dialed in and ready to go.

I've spent a ton of time on the latest version of the Fox 36 fork, so it was a quick process to get it set up to my liking. My settings were as follows: 83 psi, 6 HSR, 8 LSR, 6 HSC, 10 LSC.

I never totally got along with the Float X shock, as you'll read later on in this review. By the end of the test period I was running it with 205 psi, which equated to 25% sag, and the low-speed compression dial fully closed. I also added the a slightly larger volume reducer to increase the bottom out resistance.

As mentioned, testing took place during some seriously wet conditions, and I ended up running a Maxxis High Roller III / DHR II tire combo for a good portion of the test - the stock Continental tires are fine for most conditions, but aren't the stickiest when it's really wet and slimy - they both use Continental’s Soft rubber comping with the enduro casing, rather than the SuperSoft compound. I also ran a dual Magic Mary Radial combo, but the more compliant casing combined with the softer suspension feel made things feel too vague for my liking.


Test Locations

My first lap on the Sight TQ took place on Burke Mountain, located just up the road from Norco's BC headquarters. After that, I put the remaining miles in on the trails surrounding Bellingham, Washington. Conditions tended to be on the wetter side of things, but January did deliver a solid two weeks of perfect riding conditions.

Climbing

There’s a meme floating around poking fun at the inclusion of a climbing section in eMTB reviews, and it’s not totally wrong - after all, we are talking about a bike with a motor. Still, not all e-bikes are equal, and in the case of the TQ-powered Sight it makes its way uphill in a more moderate manner compared to the full power rocketships on the market. I really enjoy the way that the HPR60 delivers its power - it’s very natural feeling, and almost completely silent.

The amount of assistance is enough to make most climbs much easier, but it’s not so dramatic that you’ll be spit off the trail if you don’t pay attention. It strikes a nice balance, where it still clearly feels like you’re on an eMTB due to the average speed, but the handling and overall ride sensation is closer to that of a regular mountain bike. I mainly rode with the motor in the full power mode, only dropping down to the two lower power settings when I was trying to maximize my range.


As far as range goes (no pun intended), 3,500 vertical feet of descending and 20 miles of distance felt like the sweet spot - that usually equated to about two hours of ride time, and I’d end up back home with 10% or less battery remaining. Below 10% battery the amount of assistance is reduced, a feature I found myself annoyed with more than once - I’d rather be allowed to completely drain the battery as fast as I wanted rather than need to finish a ride at a reduced pace. I did find that the battery life didn’t quite match that of the Yeti MTe that I reviewed earlier this year. I wasn’t able to standardize for all variables, but on several rides with a fellow tester with a similar weight to myself on the MTe they were finishing with more battery remaining, and we both stayed in the same mode for the entire ride.



Descending

These days it seems like most full-powered eMTBs weigh around 47-50 pounds, and the mid-powered options end up hovering around 42 - 45 pounds. That’s a broad generalization, of course, and depending on the battery size and parts spec those numbers can change by a significant amount. That said, the handling difference between a 50-pound bike and a 44-pound bike is very noticeable - the handling of the Sight TQ is much closer to that of a non-motorized bike in the way that it feels while cornering or maneuvering through tighter sections of trail. You’ll still feel the extra heft when bunnyhopping at slower speeds, but other than that it’s a very intuitive bike to ride, one that doesn’t require extra time in the gym to handle.

The geometry gives the bike a very neutral feel - it’s not super slack, or super long, at least in the S3 size that I was on; I’d likely be singing a slightly different tune if I was trying to pilot the S4 and its 497mm reach. The rearward axle path from the high pivot design isn’t extreme, and I didn't notice any odd handlinq quirks from it while cornering or landing off drops and jumps, moments where the wheelbase of the bike extends.

I did try the bike in a longer travel configuration, removing the 5mm travel reducer from the shock and swapping the fork for a 170mm Fox 38. After trying both setups, I ended up preferring the stock configuration - the bike is a little more nimble, and the bottom bracket’s a little lower, two traits that keep it engaging in mixed terrain. Even in that longer travel configuration the Sight TQ doesn’t really feel like an enduro smasher - its handling is more on the all-mountain side of things; it’s a bike for exploring, for riding a wider range of terrain, as opposed to something that shines on the steepest, nastiest trails around.

Other than a couple gripes about the XT brakes lacking power compared to SRAM’s Mavens, and the not-that-sticky front tire, it was the rear shock that left me slightly frustrated. Despite plenty of experimentation, I wasn’t able to get the Float X to feel exactly the way I wanted. In some ways, its behavior reminded me of the 2020 Sight - that bike tended to move through its travel quickly, and the same thing was happening with this one.

Even at 25% sag and with the low-speed compression dialed all the way in I still found myself wanting more support - the bike is very eager to go through its travel, and while the off-the-top sensitivity is commendable, the lack of anything to push against in the mid-stroke was frustrating at times. That lack of support makes the bike ride deeper in its travel, and it’s more active over small to medium hits than it needs to be. Norco went with a CS40 tune, and I’d say they could have gone firmer, or spec’d a Float X2 for a wider range of compression adjustment.




How Does It Compare?

The Yeti MTe is a good point of comparison for the Sight. Yeti’s comparable build kit is more expensive than the Sight due to the carbon wheel spec, but even without those it’s still pricier, so for value-oriented riders the Sight gets the point.

As far as ride characteristics go, the point goes to the MTe - even though it has 5mm less travel than the Sight it doesn’t feel like it, and it does a better job of managing its travel. It’s calmer while climbing without sacrificing traction, and on the descents it feels like it’s always in the sweet spot of the stroke.

The on-trail differences between the two was a good illustration that just because two bikes share the same suspension components doesn't mean they'll behave the same way. On the MTe, I never worried about using all the travel, and it remained supportive and responsive even on chewed up sections of trail. With the Sight TQ, that lighter compression tune didn't give much to push against, causing the bike to feel less responsive than the MTe.

The geometry numbers between the Norco and the Yeti aren't worlds apart, but I do prefer the slightly longer reach of the MTe - the 480mm reach with 449mm chainstays felt nicely balanced to me.

Which Model is the Best Value?

Out of the three bikes in the Sight VLT TQ lineup, it's the C2 model that stands out. It has a nearly identical build kit to the C1, but the aluminum wheels instead of carbon and the Fox Performance Elite suspension instead of Factory lowers the price by $1,500. Yes, nine grand is still a boatload of money - I'm not about to try and justify e-bike prices - but the C2 does have a solid built kit for the price, one that doesn't need any immediate upgrades (other than maybe a stickier front tire).

The Kryptotal / Xynotal tire combo works for most conditions, but a softer compound front tire would have been appreciated for those wet winter rides.

Shimano's XT Di2 derailleur was a quieter operator with the Sight's high-pivot, elevated chainstay design.

Technical Report

Shimano XT Di2 drivetrain: I’ve complained a fair bit about the chainslap on the new XT and XTR Di2 derailleurs, but thankfully that wasn’t an issue on the Sight TQ. The elevated chainstay and idler pulley helped keep things nice and quiet, and I don’t have any noise-related gripes about this bike.

Shimano XT brakes: I ended up swapping out the rear 180mm rotor for a 200, but there were still several times when I found myself wishing for more braking power. I’ve been spoiled by all the e-bikes that have been coming with SRAM’s Maven brakes over the last year or so - the Mavens have much more power, and feel more appropriate for heavy or even slightly heavy e-bikes.

Dropper post: At 5'11" I’m right in between frame sizes, at least according to Norco's chart, and going with the S3 meant that the bike arrived with a 170mm dropper post. A 200 or 210mm post that could have its travel reduced would have been nice to see; as it is, riders that are in a similar boat as myself may need to budget for a longer dropper. Thankfully the uninterrupted and fairly short seat tube means the frame isn’t limiting the options.




Pros

+ Motor is nearly silent
+ Easy handling on a wide variety of terrain
+ Lower weight improves maneuverability

Cons

- Goes through travel very easily - a firmer shock tune would help
- Doesn't really stand out in any one specific area




Pinkbike's Take

The mid-power eMTB category is an interesting one. For a time it seemed like it was going to go extinct, erased by bikes that were powerful and light, but we really haven’t seen that happen yet. For now, TQ’s HPR60 motor is one of the best options in this category, allowing brands to create bikes that are nearly silent and a decent amount lighter than their full-powered compatriots.

The Sight VLT TQ doesn’t really break any new ground, but it does slot nicely into Norco’s lineup, where it’s a good option for riders looking for a true all-mountain e-bike that’s versatile and easy to handle; just keep in mind that the stock shock tune may not be ideal for some riders.— Mike Kazimer
