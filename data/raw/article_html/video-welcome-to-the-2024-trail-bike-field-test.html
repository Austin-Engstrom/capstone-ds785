Welcome to the 2024 Trail Bike Field Test

What exactly is a trail bike? Honestly, there's not one perfect definition, but at its core a modern trail bike should be a capable all-rounder, the type of bike that can handle most trail situations without much fuss. For riders on the hunt for that elusive one-bike-to-do-it-all, this is the category that makes the most sense.

For the sake of this year's Field Test we brought in eight new bikes with between 140 – 150mm of rear travel, bikes that sit on the slightly more aggressive side of the spectrum. All of these models are versatile enough to take out on bigger days of pedaling on a mix of terrain, and still handle the occasional trip to a bike park.

On paper, there aren't really any major outliers as far as geometry goes – the head angles all sit between 64-65 degrees, reach numbers are around 475 – 480mm on the size large(ish) bikes, and chainstay lengths range from 435 to 448mm. There were two mixed-wheel contenders and three full 29” bikes, although many of the models have flip chips that allow for either rear wheel size.

The numbers may look similar on paper, but it's a different story out on the trail – every single bike had its own unique personality, whether that was the light and lively Spot Mayhem 140 or the plow-through-everything Devinci Troy. Stay tuned for all of the individual review videos, as well as more behind-the-scenes looks at what went into testing this latest crop of trail bikes.

Control tires were provided by Schwalbe, and all the bikes were set up with the new Albert Radial tires front and rear with Trail Pro casings. Impressively, there was only one flat tire over the entire test period, and that was caused by a sharp pointy rock that would have destroyed any tire, no matter the casing.

Where Did We Test?

Testing took place in Whistler, BC, in and out of the bike park. The Creekside zone is full of both flowy and technical trails that are ideal for the style of bikes we were testing, and on the other side of the valley, away from the lifts, there's no shortage of tricky, janky climbs and slower speed, chunky descents.

Our test lap outside of the park consisted of climbing up Piece of Cake to A La Mode, then descending Three Birds to Danimal Middle and then Butterflies and Flowers. It's a short loop, with about 500 vertical feet of elevation gain, but it packs in a lot of terrain features, and the length was ideal for getting in multiple laps. Plus, there are some even sportier descents in close proximity for those moments when we wanted to push the bikes even further.

In the park, Miss Fire, Spingnar, and Call Me Maybe were all part of the regular rotation.


It wasn't all chairlift laps - there were plenty of pedal-powered miles put on each bike.
