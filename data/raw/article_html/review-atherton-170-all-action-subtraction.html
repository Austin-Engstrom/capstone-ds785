REVIEW
2024 Atherton S.170
All Action Subtraction
—
WORDS: Henry Quinney
PHOTOS: Tom Richards


The Athertons aren't strangers to making a stir. However, at the end of 2018, when they departed from Trek, there was an element of going into the unknown. Rumours swirled, and the paddock was a hotbed of gossip, but what came next was very different from what anyone had expected. The birth of their own bike brand and downhill team has seen World Cup wins, rainbow stripes, and seemed to foreshadow a trend within the wider industry of rapid prototyping and a granular approach to sizing by using carbon lugs bonded into 3D-printed titanium joints.

The DW6 platform used on those carbon bikes also lent itself to their shorter travel options, and aesthetic similarities run through the whole range. Simply put, any model that starts with an A (A200, 170 and 150) is made via additive manufacturing and uses the six-bar suspension layout.

The S Range, which includes the 170mm travel version reviewed here, uses subtractive manufacturing. CNC-machined blocks of aluminum are then joined by aluminum tubes cut to length. The two methods have some key similarities, most notably in the small gaps in sizing; however, there are some key differences.


The S170 has a frame that is both interesting, with several easter eggs of smart design, and interesting looking. I never could make up my mind about how I felt about the look of it. One day, it looked like a Pagani Zonda; the next, it looked like the same car had fallen off a multi-story car park. There is something about the angular lines that almost seem to contradict and disrupt one another. One thing is for sure: in Canada, if you ride this bike, you will be asked a lot about it. There seems to be a real mystique to these bikes, which people can't get enough of. I don't think there was a single time I rode it in Whistler where I didn't have somebody ask my opinion of it. The trouble was, as I'll get into later, it wasn't the easiest bike to form an opinion about.

Our test bike had 170mm travel coupled with a 180mm fork, uses mixed-wheels, and is equipped with the S.170.1 build kit.

Frame Details

The front triangle is 7075 series aluminum, with the tubes bonded using a double lap shear joint into lugs. The swingarm is two machined segments that is bolted together. You can see how hard Atherton Bikes have worked to de-tune the stiffness of the rear triangle, and they've taken a huge amount of material away from the inside, which can be seen when looking underneath. The hollow, slender profile of the rear triangle also allows the cable and hose to be stowed away neatly.

There does seem to be some excess resin around the connections, but it's better to be safe than sorry, I suppose. During testing, the plastic shim inside the large seat tube came loose. This shim sits beneath the collar, and it was very frustrating to change the seatpost height when using the stock standard dropper. Things would bind, and it meant changing the saddle height would become a 10-minute job.

The bike uses internal routing, including a gear cable that diverts through the swingarm. The routing might not please all right-hand front-brakers, but I'm sure there is a way to make a good fist of it. One thing many riders will enjoy, though, is the removable cover to access the cables. It's simple, effective and doesn't really have any drawbacks. The storage goes all the way into the open downtube. If you want to sneak some treats or a tube in there, then there isn't anything stopping you; you just need to anchor it somehow. While there aren't any tool mounts on the top tube, there is a mount on the downtube that has plenty of room for bigger bottles.

The seat tube allows for long droppers. During testing, I ran 210 OneUp dropper without issue. Since we received this bike, and as we were readying to publish, Atherton informed us that they were changing the seat tube junction slightly. The revision will be both bonded and then bolted with a custom-made threaded fixing.

I love the mixture of flat and round surfaces.




Geometry

If you want choices or find yourself looking for an extra-medium, then you are in luck. There are 12, yes 12, sizes. As the reach grows, so too does the rear end. However, when combined with relatively high stack values across the board and slack headtubes, it could potentially give a bike with a more rearward weight bias and one that shines on steeper trails but feels slightly less positive on flatter sections.

The reach begins at 405mm and increases to 515mm in 10mm increments. The smallest frame has a 430mm chainstay length and a 76.6-degree seat tube angle, whereas the largest frame has a 440mm chainstay length and a 78.6-degree seat tube angle.


Suspension Design

The frame, unlike the carbon bike, uses the DW4 linkage, as opposed to the DW6. Even at a glance, they do look very different. The S.170 uses a swing arm and two co-rotating links, whereas the DW6 is a bit more complicated. Although DW6 might look like a standard four-bar, hidden behind the chainring and bottom bracket are two links that the chainstay floats on.

The DW4 this bike uses is the classic DW. It's two co-rotating links that guide a swingarm through its axle path. As DW-links go, there is sometimes a consideration that they're most often deployed on pedal-efficient, sportier bikes. While that may be true, I wouldn't say this is the case for the Atherton. Although requested, Atherton didn't provide any graphs, but it feels to me that anti-squat is a little lower, and this bike is very happy to go into its stroke, even if at the cost of all-out pedalling efficiency.

The bike's progression is very smooth and genuinely has a bottomless feel. I might not be able to tell you the exact values, but it feels to me to have middling to high progression and it works very well with the coil shock provided.

RIDING THE
Atherton S.170


Test Bike Setup

The test bike featured a RockShox Ultimate level Zeb and Vivid coil, with a cable-operated SRAM X01-GX mixed drivetrain and Hayes brakes. There were big gaps in the spec, and while it might be the cheapest Atherton you're able to purchase, it would be great if it was specced more sensibly. I photographed the bike in the spec I rode and enjoyed most, not the stock parts, which seemed wholly unsuitable for a bike billed as having downhill credentials. This normally isn't how we like to do things in these tests, but I wanted to highlight the differences I felt I needed to make to make this bike good at what they sell it to do.

The stock 170mm Fox Transfer dropper was too short. The Continental tires were too hard, thin and lacked decent grip. The 180mm brake rotor was too small, and the cockpit was unsuitable for a bike with this much of a rearward weight bias. The short 35mm stem gave a front that lacked tracking and consistency and left your weight lurching onto the unsettled front wheel under compressions. I didn't enjoy the FSA Gradient bars for their shape, either.

When first getting on the bike, having to run such high pressures in the tires to stop them from deforming too much (around 30 - 32 psi on bike park trails) made the bike feel very harsh and uncomfortable. Tires are meant to suspend us to a degree, and if you spec tires so incapable of handling what you designed the bike to do, you've flatly made a mistake. This was very disappointing. Initially, I questioned whether this was a frame feel and a consequence of having essentially stems connected by large alloy tubes. However, while this bike isn't ever going to be the most comfortable, its feeling was wholly enhanced in all the wrong ways by the tire spec.

With downhill tires, a weighted front end, and a dropper with travel better proportioned to the bike's size, the whole thing rode drastically better and was far more enjoyable.


Testing Info

This test bike was well-traveled. I started riding it in Leogang, before coming back to Squamish to ride it all over the Sea to Sky and then taking it on a BC road trip. It proved reliable and predictable and was well suited to the gnarly climb-and-plunge trails of the interior. Castlegar has some of my favourite riding ever. Captain Kangaroo is a very good place to put a bike through its paces.

The seated position ensures a well-weighted front.

Climbing


The S.170 is truly a very versatile bike. Any bike that has a 180mm fork that offers this balance of grip and efficiency deserves that accolade. The seated position is by no means slack, but it does feel slightly less sharp. However, thanks to its high stack and the ability to choose reach in small increments, riders will get a really comfortable position that suits them. I loved the 475mm reach of the test bike and the 617mm effective top tube it produced. The next size up, which sees a 485mm reach combined with a whopping 657mm stack, uses a degree steeper effective seat tube angle but gives an effective top tube of only 1mm longer. This paints a good picture of how key dimensions such as stack and seat tube angle can either maximise or reduce the knock-on effects of reach for our seated climbing position.

I ran the shock with the stock 400lb spring with lots of compression dialled in. The setting was very impressive in climbing and offered excellent tracking without any noticeable bob. Some riders take advantage of the lockout switch, and if they want to maximise the bottomless feeling with a comparatively soft spring without adding the compression, they will need to. However, I didn't feel the need and was always happy to just sit, spin and let the suspension work as needed. That said, it's not a bike that is ever going to be ultra-efficient on smooth roads.

Part of that is also how this bike handles square edges without janking at your feet. Seated pedalling while hunting for grip comes very naturally to this bike, even if you do have to focus on keeping the front wheel weighted - at times, it can feel like your weight is more over the rear axle than some other enduro bikes that feature longer stays. The rear wheel has a great ability to just get up and out the way and is a very strong technical climber.


Descending

The Atherton S.170 is ultimately a bike built for descending, so how does it fare? Well, firstly, the spec choice seems doesn't enhance its good points or offset its weaknesses but rather doubles down on the things it's bad at. You can get this bike to be really, very, very good, but to do so, you need to take it back a few layers and concentrate on the fundamentals of how the bike balances and what you like.

The slack, high front and comparatively short rear will give a bike that feels unsettled with the shorter 35mm stem. To each rider, there will be a threshold of weight exercised on the front wheel, where it will begin to feel settled. This will vary from rider to rider, and you only need to look at Dakotah Norton's handlebars to understand that people do enjoy different weight biases. To me, and my preference for a settled front, the stock build was dramatically enhanced by going to a 50mm stem. I'm not averse to 50mm stems, although they can mean you do have to do more body language as you move around the steering axis. Truthfully, I was very happy where I got it, but I think I'm settling on bikes that work with things in the 40-45mm range. A small difference, but it does add up.

In some ways, this bike is a bit of a throwback, both in terms of its spec and geometry. Its tall stack aside, it has lots of the traits of a very good bike from five or so years ago. It's safe, and you're certainly not going out the front door, but it also feels far too light on the front. The new school is even newer now, and I really believe that the new approach of higher stacks, longer rear ends and slightly steeper head angles is the way things will go. One and a half out of three ain't bad, though... or something like that.


Once you do get the right balance, there is a lot of performance to unlock from the bike, especially from the DW4 rear end. I haven't yet had the chance to ride their A-range, but if that platform is better, it must be something incredibly special. I was very impressed by the suspension on this bike. It struck an incredible balance of small bump, mid-stroke and end-stroke. The suspension was so effective at tracking and suspending my mass that I hardly ever hit the rim.


The bike's stiffness is interesting. Despite my initial concerns of harshness, when fitted with DH tires, it really came into its own. It's a certainly direct feeling bike, and providing you can really commit to the front, you can really carve and pump through turns. In some ways, the rearward weight bias can make this bike feel stiffer than it actually is. As we drive a bike through turns, the bottom bracket can swing with our weight and follow the front axle, helping to create a bike that can either feel easy to change direction on or something that loads up like a spring and wants to spit us out. This swinging action is not only resisted by a stiffer bike but is simply harder to do with more weight closer to the rear axle. The S.170, with your weight so rearward, can give a bike that feels a little unrewarding to turn unless you can get the front really settled. The two things combine to give a bike that can really carve turns, but will be most rewarding to riders that really drive the bike with their feet and commit to the front.

I loved where the spring rate took me in the travel, but I would be curious to try a more damped shock. I ended up closed on both high and low-speed compression adjustments.

How Does It Compare?


Two bikes steeped in racing pedigree, suspension layouts that rely upon a swingarm and not a bit of carbon in sight. How do they compare on the trail?

Simply put, the geometry of the Commencal is superior in almost every regard to the Atherton. The position it puts riders in just wills you to push while making you feel safe and effective. The Atherton was a little more nuanced.

I really enjoyed the Meta SX's supportive yet active stroke. The S.170 is a little easier to go into its stroke and feels lighter in terms of its action. It also doesn't have the hiccup that Meta suffers from in the early part of the stroke. In some ways they feel similar, but the Atherton seems to just swallow bigger holes easier.

The Commencal is an easy bike to ring through turns, and I would say it offers a shade more grip in off-camber sections. That said, it also flexes more and can threaten to spit you out when really committing. The Atherton is the opposite in this regard. It is far torsionally stiffer, for good and for bad, and is best at keeping composed no matter how rough it gets, even if that means it perhaps doesn't offer the same all-out grip when riding on angled terrain with repeated hits.

Which Model is the Best Value?


All three bikes aren't too far apart in terms of cost. I loved the Zeb on this bike, and it really suits the character of the bike. That, and a few other nice touches such as the X0 shifter and high-end coil shock, would convince me that it would be the best model to get.


The bike has a great spec, save for some key dimensions

Technical Report



Tires and Dropper: This was one of the bikes that recently inspired an op-ed where I couldn't understand or justify the poor spec choices. Short droppers and thin tires have no place on bikes this capable or gravity focused.

RockShox Suspension: This S.170 had a Zeb Ultimate fork with a Charger 3 damper, which has since been superseded by the 3.1 damper. At longer travels, the things they tried to correct with the 3.1 don't seem so obvious, and the 3 seems wholly more suited to a longer travel application (the threshold for LSC adjustment is lower, meaning you have a usable range). It also suits the gentle, ground-hugging sensitivity of the DW4 layout to give a bike that has a very consistent feeling between both axles.

Cockpit: Stem length and handlebars are very subjective, but they should also build on the foundation of the geometry. The 25mm rise was about right on the FSA Gradient bars but there was something about the shape I just didn't gel with. As also previously mentioned, the 35mm stem gave a light feeling on the front.

Hayes Brakes: The Hayes Dominion brakes are taking it to the bigger players and offer lots of power and a light touch. That said, when sticking it to the man, I wish they would stick it a bit more quietly. The pad rattle was very loud.


Pros

+ Incredible tracking and grip on rough trails
+ A strong climber, and focused descender
+ Makes the arguement for more travel
+ Will be most rewarding to ride for committed riders

Cons

- Weight bias is quite rearward
- Strange spec dimensions and choices
- Will be most rewarding to ride for committed riders



Pinkbike's Take

The Atherton S.170 is a bike that offers a lot of performance at a reasonable price. It's exotic, it's got a great suspension platform, and it will turn plenty of heads. It covers off a lot of bases and, when set up correctly, can really shine. That said, the geometry definitely suits riders who like a very rearward weight bias. It's a true mini-DH bike for both good and for bad.
—Henry Quinney
