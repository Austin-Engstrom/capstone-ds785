The new Orbea Wild launched a few months ago, and as it turns out, it’s whole lot like the previous version. That’s not a bad thing, especially since that iteration snagged our eMTB of the Year award for 2023.

While the geometry and the overall look of the bike remain the same, the new Wild did receive a 10mm increase in travel, putting it at 170mm front and rear, and it's equipped with the latest version of Bosch’s Performance CX motor. It’s also mixed-wheel compatible, although that does require a different rocker link. Riders can choose their preferred wheel size at the time of purchase, or buy the link as an aftermarket upgrade.

Orbea Wild M-LTD Details

• Wheel size: 29" (27.5" link available)
• Travel: 170mm
• Carbon frame
• 63.5° head angle
• Chainstay length: 448mm

• 600 Wh (range extender compatible)
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL
• Weight: 49.3 lb / 22.4 kg (size L, MX wheels, DH tires)
• Price: $11,999
• More info: orbea.com

There are two aluminum and four carbon options in the lineup, with prices starting at $6,499 USD for the Wild H20, and going all the way up to $11,999 for the Wild M-LTD that’s reviewed here. Orbea’s My-O program allows riders to customize their bikes by choosing the paint scheme, along with things like tires, cockpit components, and tires. For instance, my test bike had DH casing tires with Maxxis’ sticky MaxxGrip rubber installed front and rear, a fitting combo for the slippery conditions that prevailed throughout the test period.

Frame Details

As I mentioned, the differences between the incoming and outgoing Wild models are fairly minor, so I’ll keep this section fairly short.

The Wild is equipped with the newest version of Bosch's Performance Line CX motor, which puts out 85 Nm of torque with 600 watts of max power output. That's connected to either Bosch’s new 600 Wh battery or the older 750 Wh battery, and there’s also the option to add on a 250 Wh range extender that takes the place of a bottle cage. It may seem like a no-brainer to go with the biggest battery possible, but I’ve come to appreciate the 600 Wh battery + range extender combo - I can leave the auxiliary battery at home for quicker laps, and add it on for those times when I don’t want to have any range anxiety.

The top tube display has five lights indicating the battery level, and a colored light at the top of the display to indicate the ride mode. There is some unused room around the display that wasn’t there before - I wouldn’t be surprised if Bosch has a larger, more detailed display in the works.

The ride modes themselves can be customized (or swapped out altogether) in Bosch’s Flow app. The app still has a few quirks , including the fact that it can only work with one bike at a time, but it is easy to quickly customize the characteristics of each mode. I typically bump up the assistance level of the modes by +2 or +3, and leave the eMTB mode alone. That makes Eco a more tolerable mode to maximize range, and the other modes get you up to the top of the hill that much faster.

I spent most of my time in the eMTB and Tour+ modes depending on my goals for the day. Both of those modes adjust the level of assistance dynamically depending on rider output. eMTB adjusts the power between Tour to Turbo, and Tour+ adjusts the power between Eco to Turbo. Basically, those modes mean you don’t need to switch modes during a ride; they’re adjusted automatically.






Geometry

The Wild's geometry is nearly identical to the previous version, other than the slacker, 63.5-degree head angle thanks to the 170mm fork. The chainstays measure 448mm across all sizes, and longer head tube lengths give all the sizes a healthy amount of stack, creating a taller front end and a more upright riding position.

It's possible to run the Wild with either a 29" or 27.5" rear wheel, but you'll need to purchase a different rocker link to make the switch, as opposed to using a flip chip on the frame.


Suspension Design

The Wild uses a concentric pivot around the rear axle, with a seat tube mounted rocker link driving the 205x65mm trunnion shock. Along with receiving a 10mm bump in rear travel, the starting leverage rate is slightly higher, and the overall amount of progression has increased slightly. The amount of anti-squat decreases as the bike goes through its travel, starting around 115% at sag and finishing at 55%. In theory, that should give more support for pedaling earlier in the stroke, and then the reduction should reduce the amount of pedal feedback deeper in the travel.

Test Bike Setup

I didn't need to do anything out of the ordinary to get the Wild set up to my liking, which you'd hope would be the case for a $12,000 bike. Orbea's customization program makes it possible to get all of the little details sorted out at the time of purchase, including things like stem and dropper post length, battery capacity, wheel size, and even the frame color. I didn't personally choose the hues on this one, but I don't mind the pink and black color scheme. I went with a 40mm stem and a 35mm rise bar, and thankfully didn't need to do any cockpit component swapping once the bike arrived.

As for suspension setup, I've spent a lot of time on the Fox 38 and the X2 shock, so I started with my usual base settings and didn't need to adjust much from there. That equated to 83 psi in the fork, and the shock set to 30% sag. Fox's suspension guide is a good place to start, and then adjust accordingly depending on personal preference and conditions.

Testing Info

Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, during the transition from fall into full-on winter. That meant the Wild was ridden in everything from hero dirt to slippery, sloppy, and sometimes frozen conditions.


Climbing

The overall position of the Wild is upright and comfortable, thanks to the fairly steep seat angle and tall stack, which means I’d typically stay seated until things got steep, and then I’d lower the saddle slightly to get into the partial-squat stance that’s key for getting up extra-tricky climbs on an eMTB. The overall handling is fairly neutral, although we’re still talking about a slack, full-powered bike here - it takes more effort to maneuver compared to something like the Rise, Orbea’s lighter weight, more trail oriented offering. The Rise is approximately 5 pound lighter, with less travel, shorter chainstays, and a steeper head angle, all factors that make it feel quicker on tighter sections of trail. Of course, its max power output doesn’t match that of the Wild, which means that the Wild has the edge when it comes to just how steep and sustained of a climb it can get up.

The Wild is a descent-focused eMTB, but the Bosch Performance CX motor does help make climbing much more entertaining, with a very well controlled output that makes it easy to deliver just the right amount of power to the rear wheel. When it comes to wheel size, I preferred the 29” rear wheel for climbing - that increased diameter made it easier to roll up and over obstacles, and it was a little less likely to get hung up on awkward, steppy climbs. On the other hand, I prefer the mixed wheel setup’s performance when descending, so it’s really a matter of figuring out where your priorities lie. If climbing performance and outright speed top the list, 29” front and rear is probably the way to go. If downhill manuverability and easier handling as slower speeds are more important, than I’d suggest going with mixed wheels.

One way to further increase the climbing performance of the Wild would be to install some shorter cranks. I didn’t have too many rock strikes with the stock 165mm cranks (I live in a region where you’re more likely to hit a root than a rock in the middle of a trail), but dropping down to 155 or even 150mm cranks would create more ground clearance and make it easier to half pedal and keep the motorized momentum going. For the record, I’m not as convinced by super short cranks on non-motorized bikes, since at a certain point it can feel pretty strange spinning those little circles, but on an eMTB it’s a different story, and going short makes a noticeable improvement.




Descending

The differences between the new Wild and the version I previously reviewed are fairly subtle when it comes to descending performance. The extra 10 millimeters of rear travel is nice to have for those not-quite-perfect line choices, and it still has that punching bag-like quality I enjoyed with the last one - it does a great job of absorbing hits without getting rattled.

Speaking of rattling, the new motor is nearly silent while descending, and overall the Wild is an extremely quiet bike, motorized or not. Some riders aren’t bothered by extra clacks and rattles, but I’m not one of them - I want the bike I’m on to be as quiet as possible.

I took the Wild off some sizeable drops (at least for me), and it took them all in stride, free of any harsh bottom outs or nerve wracking noises. The suspension does feel a little more eager to initiate travel, and that improved small bump sensitivity was nice to have for finding grip on wet, slippery trails. It doesn’t have quite the same bump-erasing ability as the Trek Slash+, but I’m not sure that trait would be possible to replicate without a high pivot suspension layout.

The overall handling is in line with what I’ve come to expect from a modern enduro bike, which makes sense when you look at the geometry numbers. Its handling is very neutral, which is great news if you’re looking for a bike that’ll get up to speed and do what you want it to without any quirks, but it also makes it a little trickier to hone in on one standout ride characteristic. That’s a problem for me, the reviewer, rather than the rider, though; there’s something to be said about a bike that just does exactly what it’s supposed to.

It is good to see the mixed wheel option added to the menu; that was one of the requests I had after testing the last one. Of course, it would be nice if a different rocker link wasn’t required to make the change, but at least it’s possible. I found the smaller rear wheel improved the downhill cornering performance, at least when it comes to tighter, steeper turns - it was a little easier to tip the bike in and out of corners with the mixed wheel setup.

What’s It Like to Live With?

Overall, the Wild has held up reasonably well to the elements, but there are a few issues that have cropped up. The first has to do with the design of the bash guard / motor protector. That plastic cover has holes in it that allow for air flow to keep the motor from overheating, but those holes also let mud and other debris makes its way in. That wouldn’t be a big deal if the dirt could also escape the same way it came in, except that there are raised plastic portions inside the guard that do their best to hang onto as much soil as possible. If you’d like to take home soil samples from every area you ride in, this design is perfect. Otherwise, be prepared to remove four bolts (they’re not all the same size either - you’ll need two different hex keys) to take off the guard and clean things up.

The charging port is another area that wants to suck in dirt - it didn’t always fully seal, and over time it became more difficult to pull it out and rotate the cover out of the way. Plus, if you do use the range extender the area around the port is more exposed, which gives dirt even better access.

How Does It Compare?

The competition is heating up, but the Wild still holds its own against other contenders in the full power enduro eMTB category. Compared to the Norco Range VLT, it’s significantly lighter, and the geometry works better for my 5’11” height. On the Range I fall in between sizes, while with the Wild the size L is a good fit. The Range is bike that prefers to smash and plow its way down the trail, while the Wild doesn’t require the same level of aggression to get the most out of it.


Which Model is the Best Value?

For riders on a budget that’s big enough to afford an e-bike, but not big enough to drop $12,000 on one, the aluminum H10 is the one I’d go with. It’s still not cheap, at $7,300, but you do get the same Bosch motor found on the carbon models, and a 750 Wh battery. It’s also possible to upgrade select components at check out; personally, I’d go with the 600 wH battery and range extender, and maybe consider the X2 shock over the Float X for the increased level of adjustability.



Technical Report

Range extender: I used the range extender for, duh, extending the range of my rides. It’s easy to install, although as I mentioned it does leave the charging port more exposed to the elements. I also wish it was possible to charge the main and the auxiliary battery at the same time from one charger. As far as I can tell, there isn’t any sort of split cord available to make this possible.

SRAM XX Transmission derailleur: The Wild’s XX Transmission is wired into the main battery, which should mean there’s one less thing to remember to charge. Except that I ended up on a ride where the wire stopped working, leaving me with a really expensive derailleur that could only be shifted by pushing on it and ignoring the loud crunching noises. Once I got home I removed the wire and installed an AXS battery, and everything worked like it was supposed to. It turns out the extension cord retails for $190, so hopefully if that issue cropped up it would be covered under warranty.

Shimano XTR brakes: The XTR brakes with Galfer rotors provided enough stopping power to keep the Wild from running away with me, that is, after I swapped out the organic pads for metallic ones. Organic pads don’t last very long in the rain, especially not on heavy e-bike.




Pros

+ Calm and composed handling
+ Very quiet while descending
+ Excellent power delivery while climbing
Cons

- Still has thru-headset cable routing
- Even shorter cranks would be nice to see
- Motor cover is a mud magnet.




Pinkbike's Take

The previous Wild was (and still is) a great bike, so it makes sense that the new one received only a slight revision rather than a dramatic overhaul. Gripes about headset cable routing aside, the Wild strikes a very good balance when it comes its handling and suspension performance on the climbs and descents. It's a bigger, full power ebike that hides its weight and wheelbase length relatively well, which means you don't need to have the absolute rowdiest trails to still have a good time on it.— Mike Kazimer
