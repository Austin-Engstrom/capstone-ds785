PINKBIKE FIELD TEST REVIEW

Rocky Mountain Instinct


Words by Henry Quinney; photography by Tom Richards & Max Barron


The Rocky Mountain Instinct was bred for the very hills that we got to test it on, or near enough. Based in Vancouver, the Canadian brand certainly isn't short of the terrain where a bike like the Instinct shines. BC, in fairness, by its sheer variety, has something for most bike categories. However, when it comes to backcountry riding and pedal loops, riding the mid-travel aggressive bikes here can be the perfect companion for some of the lesser-known gems, as well as some of the modern classics.

The Rocky has a lot going for it and hits all the high notes on the spec sheet one might expect from a mid-travel aggressive bike. The adjustment, the geometry, and the low weight will all prove popular for somebody who wants their 140mm bike to do just about anything.

Instinct C70 Details

• Travel: 140mm rear, 150mm fork
• Carbon Frame
• 29" wheels (27.5" for XS & S)
• Head angle: 63.5 - 64.3°
• 76.4° seat-tube angle
• Reach: 480mm (L neutral)
• Chainstay length: 437 or 447mm
• Weight: 31.3 lb / 14.2 kg
• $6,499 USD
• More info: bikes.com


The Instinct pairs a 150mm fork to that rear travel. The bike uses 29" wheels in all but its smallest sizes and three different ways to adjust its geometry. It now also includes a storage compartment. The internally routed cables can be run in either orientation, and are clamped on the entry to try and reduce rattling.

In terms of geometry, our size large bike was built around a 480mm reach. That can be increased or decreased by 5mm thanks to the supplied headset cups. A nice touch is that the cups can be pressed into the frame by hand, so you could theoretically do it trailside. The bike also has a chainstay adjustment of 10mm. That said, that's not with a UDH hanger. If you do want to run a T-Type mech, you would be forced to run the bike in the 447mm rear-centre option. Since this is a Rocky, it of course has a multi-directional flip chip at the lower eyelet. In past years, there could be as many as nine different settings. Now, riders must make use of just four. Thankfully, that's ample.

All of the adjustments mean that there are many, many different options in regard to geometry. The head tube angle can be as slack as 63.5 degrees or as steep as 64.3 degrees; 64 degrees is the middle, neutral position. In that same neutral position, the seat angle is 77 degrees.


FIELD NOTES



Climbing

The Instinct was the second lightest bike on test, and that alone makes it feel sharper on the climbs than some others. That said, the low weight isn't the overriding characteristic when riding this bike. Off the geometry sheet alone, the Instinct has a lot going for it. The front wheel feels planted and secure when climbing, and the bike has the feeling of being in range and maneuverable despite the longer rear center. The beauty of the adjustment is, of course, that with that light frame, it could just be a canvas to paint your ideas of what a trail bike should be onto.

The bike is very progressive, meaning it tends to sit quite deep and feel quite soft under power. It's certainly not a spritely feeling when putting the power down. It's not often I make use of climb switches, but with the Instinct it's something I was reaching for. When using it, you did get a bike that tracked worse, even if it was more efficient, and it subsequently struggled to find grip over edges or bumps. In the open mode, it can deliver a lot of grip, but you are presented with two very opposing choices with the Instinct should you reach for the switch. When open, the low ride height combined with a low bottom bracket height makes pedal strikes something you have to be cognizant of.


Descending

Reviewing the Rocky is tricky. I'm going to break it down into two parts: the suspension and the geometry.

"I need a bazillion more PSI in here, maybe a bazillion and two." While I was shouting this over shakey GoPro footage, it should be worth noting that I still only used 75% of the stroke. Early in the test, we removed all the volume spacers of the shock to try and give a bike that offered more support, but even then, the bike just felt far too progressive. The compression tune on this bike is simply too light. While I do have a track record with running more compression damping than most, in this instance, I think Rocky has got it wrong. All of the testers found it to be underdamped.

The Instinct uses the start of its stroke very easily and was incredibly reluctant to use the end of the stroke. If you dropped the spring rate, you might get closer to bottom out, but would have something that felt very unstable to ride. If you found something that was more supportive, you would bounce off the ramp in progression. All in all, it was a very difficult bike to really get to grips with. Various testers found it better in the shorter chainstay position to reduce leverage over the shock. I personally ran it with a slightly softer spring rate and with the climb switch on. Is this the desired, best, or an adequate long-term solution? Absolutely not, but it did showcase how well the Rocky could ride if only it offered more compression damping.

In some ways, the Instinct is its own worst enemy because it gives riders the geometry that will encourage them to thrash and charge, which directly undermines the shock's abilities. The geometry is balanced and suitable, and it wants to let the rider descend with confidence. The adjustment gives riders a lot of options, but at its core, in the neutral setting, the Instinct is fundamentally a well-proportioned bike. 636mm stack, 480mm reach, and a 77-degree seat tube angle are exactly where I would want those key dimensions, and when combined with the 480mm reach and 448mm rear end, it has the bones to be right at the sharp end of what a trail bike is in 2024. It's just a shame the shock tune can't cash the cheque.

Overall, the bike does have decent angles and a sharp feeling underneath you that is direct without feeling harsh. If this were a long-term review, I would have experimented with a firmer compression tune and maybe even a coil shock, but sadly, our time frame during the test was slightly too condensed to do it in a meaningful way where I could then come back to you with a tune that I thought really let the bike shine.





Technical Report

Fox Suspension: The Fox Performance Elite 36 is as high-end as you could ever really need from the brand. Yes, it might not be shimmering gold, but it has all the key parts you would want. Fox does make some really great shocks; the tune on the Float X was to blame in this instance for the bike not delivering the performance it should have.

Adjustment Galore: If a rider knows what they want their bike to feel like, Rocky has you covered. If you don't, then through simple experimentation you can quickly learn what works for you thanks to the adjustments on offer. They're not completely hassle-free but represent as near to a seamless process as you get on a mountain bike in 2024 for changing key dimensions.



What's it Like to Live With?

The frame storage on the bike is well sorted, with one of the largest openings. It also has an AirTag fitting on the underside of the door, which is a nice touch. The adjustments of the bike will make it a fan favorite with die-hard riders, but it should also hopefully make it fit everyone better. The +/- 5mm adjustment on the headtube is simple yet effective. Plus, bikes using the standard hanger will also benefit from the easy chainstay adjustment, which can not only affect the whole balance of the bike but can even be a way to fine-tune the shock feeling.

The Ride4 adjustment is good, but it does feel somewhat ironic that even with four different settings, three of which are there to provide meaningful changes to both the shock and geometry, there isn't any setting that makes the shock feel good, and the most meaningful and useful geometry adjustments happen elsewhere on the bike.

 


The bike happened to be quite noisy and was one of the loudest bikes on the test - it seems to be the housing rattling against the inside of the frame, something that some foam or heat shrink tubing could potentially fix.


Pros

+ Balanced geometry
+ Very adjustable
+ Light
+ Well designed storage


Cons

- A shock tune that undermines the geometry
- T-type compatibility issues in short chainstay setting
- Noisy
