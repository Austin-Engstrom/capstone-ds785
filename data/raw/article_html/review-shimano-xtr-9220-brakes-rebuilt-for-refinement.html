Shimano's brakes have become one of the more passionately divisive components in the bike industry - a fair share of riders view them as the pinnacle of what's available, while others don't have quite as much praise to dole out. Well aware of the chief frustrations out there, the team at Shimano has been hard at work refining their brake platform to match modern expectations.

Though the architecture, ergonomics, and finer details may be brand new, the overall feel and function is still distinctly Shimano. Whether that's good news or bad depends a bit on how you ride and what feel you desire.

XTR 9220 Details
• 4-piston caliper
• Tool-free lever reach adjust
• Free stroke adjust
• New mineral oil
• New caliper seals
• Weight: 295g (actual, front caliper w/pads, hose, and lever)
• MSRP: $335 USD / $428 CAD / $484 AUS per wheel
• www.bike.shimano.com

A complete front brake with pads weighs in at 295g, which is about 18g more than the current XTR 9120 brakes. Pricing is quite high for these silver stoppers, clocking in at $335 USD per wheel.



Details

Though the XTR 9220 brake has been completely redesigned, the goal wasn't to drastically change how they felt or performed on trail. Ergonomics and consistency were the primary focal points here, with many small changes made to improve both fronts.

I'll start with consistency, as that's the more hotly contested issue. The engineers, product managers, and athletes at Shimano were well aware of the wandering bite point issue that has plagued their previous 4-piston brake models, and wanted to be certain that they eliminated that issue in the updated series. By moving to a lower viscosity mineral oil, the brakes perform more consistently at operating temperature, and should experience little to no pump or fade as the system heats. I put the question to Shimano's North American Mountain Bike Product Manager Nick Murdick, so here's the scoop in his own words.

What was addressed to fix the wandering bite point?


It’s both the low viscosity fluid and new caliper seals. The key attribute of both is their consistent performance of their jobs. The fluid flows quickly and returns to the reservoir right away when the rider lets go of the lever, but more importantly it doesn’t speed up or slow down as conditions change. The seals rebound consistently to retract the pistons and push the fluid back towards the lever at the same speed every time too. Just changing one or the other doesn’t work, and can even make things worse.—Nick Murdick

Unfortunately, Shimano is recommending that riders avoid using the new oil in the previous generation 9100/9120 brakes, as the seals are not compatible with the new fluid.


The updated pad shape is backwards compatible.
The oval pin hole and slight wedge shape both help to prevent rattling.
No, the lever's not bent.
Shimano gave the blades a 5° upsweep to better match hand angle in an aggressive position.

Another detail that should be immediately apparent if you're giving the 9220 brakes a good ol' shop floor squeeze test is the slightly upwards angle of the lever blade. By angling the lever 5° up from flat, Shimano found that their ride testers were able to get a setup that better matched the ergonomics of their overall cockpit. Those who are picky about brake angle might find it a bit tricky to settle in on their preferred tilt with these, but it only takes a couple of attempts to end up at a happy spot - just bring a 4mm on your ride.

Lever reach is adjusted in the same manner as the previous model, but the knob has been integrated into the lever blade to give the brake a slightly cleaner silhouette. That reach knob is a little trickier to fiddle with, but the tucked away position should keep it safer from damage. The knob has very tightly spaced click detents to make it easier to count clicks of adjustment.

You may find that reach point different than past Shimano models, and that's due to the fact that they've moved the lever's pivot point 5mm closer to the bar, allowing the flatter lever blade to bite parallel to the bar in a more 'typical' reach position.

Dimpled blade and a textured reach knob.
The 2mm inside the lever body is for replacing the lever blade.
The offset hose keeps things close to the bar.

The somewhat unusual offset hose at the lever is meant to bring the hydraulic lines closer to the handlebar for tidiness, regardless of how you're routing your brakes through/on/around your frame. In some circumstances, the offset actually brings the hose too close to the bar, causing it to rub or tap while riding - being fussy about hose length is going to be critical here, if you want to maintain a silent and scuff-free bike.

One upside to the new design is the reduced protrusion of the brake from the bar - hopefully this will keep them safer in the event of a crash.

Little links housed between the brace and the clamp.

This revised architecture was achieved thanks to a very interesting pull-link that actuates the master cylinder. Where most brakes use a simple linkage to translate lever pull to the pushrod inside the brake, the new XTRs use a longer linkage to pull the pushrod through its travel. This isn't critical knowledge for the daily use of the brake, but might come into play over long-term ownership, should you need to service the inner-workings of the system.

Tightly spaced click detents make reach adjust a bit more measurable.
Alleged free stroke adjustment, now done with a 2mm hex.

Though the reach adjuster has a very wide range of adjustment, the Free Stroke's impact on the system is still vanishingly small. Yes, when adjusted correctly as part of a larger bleed, you can get a couple millimeters of movement out of the lever throw, but it's hardly up to par with how much adjustment you can achieve with other brake systems on the market.

Performance

I've been riding a set of the M9220 brakes for the past few months, through freezing weather and toasty temps alike, and can happily report that my chief complaints about the previous brakes have been addressed. No more wandering bite point, no more rattling pads*, and no pump or fade over long runs - as you'd hope. Like the previous brakes, the 9220 model is quite sensitive to the bleed you give them, so getting that perfect should be a chief concern. Yes, all brakes benefit from the best bleed possible, but given the very progressive nature of Shimano's leverage, you notice changes in that feel over the course of a run in ways you might not with a softer-biting brake.

*In a cruel turn of fate, the pads started to exhibit some rattle only a couple days after I wrote this. I'm chalking it up to the braking surface quite worn down on the pads themselves, plus some very dry weather that does nothing to mask the little pings and pops that only come out of a bike in summer dust. Hopefully a fresh set is all that's required to alleviate this, but at least you can always run a non-finned alternative for guaranteed silence.

That bite point feel is one of those intangible preference items that can be hard to translate, and might not even matter to some riders out there. For those that are picky about brake feel, rest assured that the unique power band Shimano offers hasn't really changed that much, but feels a bit easier to control than before. There's a slight nose of resistance as you break the lever into travel, but it then swings freely to the bite point, where things ramp up pretty quickly to max power.

Photo: Lear Miller

The Northwest can be a uniquely taxing place on brakes, with plentiful grip when the conditions are good, and plenty of long steep descents to push them to the limit. Even under extreme use cases, I've found the XTRs to hold up well to heat and resist fading, even if they do start howling towards the bottom of a massive run. I'd call this a significant improvement over the previous generation, which experienced a noticeable change in feel and power in similar use.

Though Shimano says the power has been increased in the new XTR brakes, I don't find it to be a drastic difference over the previous model. You can still lock the wheels up whenever you want, but compared to the deep and accessible power in something like a Maven, they're really not that extreme. I personally prefer the feel and power curve of brakes like the SRAM Maven or TRP EVO Pro, but have been getting along with the XTRs well - it just takes a few rides to acclimate.

Photo: Sterling Lorence

Ultimately, I think my feel on the new XTR 4-piston brakes is this: if they were to come on a bike - especially a shorter travel bike - I'd be more than happy to run them, but I wouldn't necessarily go out of my way to get a set to replace many of the excellent aftermarket options that exist now. Brands like TRP, Hayes, Magura, and SRAM are all currently producing excellent brakes, so the competition is fierce. Nothing feels quite the same as a Shimano though, so if that's your jam then these will please.

Matt Beer's Ride Impressions

There’s nothing overly radical about the changes in performance of the new XTR brakes on the trail. The lever and caliper have been redesigned, but the way they operate still has that familiar Shimano sense - if you liked Shimano brakes in the past, then you’ll also find these will suit your taste too.

The most noticeable changes come at the lever, both in ergonomics and actuation. The lever pivot itself is now closer to the bar and makes the XTR, dare I say it, feel somewhat “SRAM-ish” in your grasp. In terms of the actuation, the power ramps up more gradually than the past model, keeping that double-swing action, but without turning on and off like a light switch. A redesigned bite point adjuster has been introduced, however, the amount it influences the level is marginal.


Pros

+ More power in classic Shimano feel
+ Consistent bite point
+ Novel and comfortable ergonomics

Cons

- Not as powerful as many competing options
- Touchy feel won't suit all riders
- Fussy bleed and minimal adjustment



Pinkbike's Take

Shimano did a great job of refining their system to eliminate their most significant problems, without changing the fundamental feel of the brakes. If you like the precise and sharp character of previous Shimano models, you'll love these. — Dario DiGiulio
