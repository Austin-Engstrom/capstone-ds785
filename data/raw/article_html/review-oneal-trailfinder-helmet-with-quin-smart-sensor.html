Review: The O'Neal Trailfinder Helmet With Quin Sensor Can Call For Help If You Hit Your Head

Oct 8, 2025
by Seb Stott  
Share

The O'Neal Trailfinder may look like a generic trail helmet, but it houses some pretty promising tech. Alongside the O'Neal Blade Polycarbonate full-face helmet, it's compatible with the latest Quin Pod sensor, which uses gyroscopes, accelerometers and algorithms to assess head impacts in real-time. If the algorithm thinks you've hit your head badly and you don't respond to a prompt on your smartphone, it can alert your emergency contacts, and even the emergency services, letting them know your status and location.

The concept is potentially life-saving, and could offer peace of mind to those of us who ride alone, but it's an idea that's been tried before and has so far remained niche. Quin says their latest sensor developed with O'Neal adds a gyroscope and updated algorithms, helping it to distinguish between a serious crash and a helmet drop, thereby virtually eliminating the annoying false-positive alerts which may put people off such systems. So the question is - does it work?
O'Neal Trailfinder With Quin Details

• Quin sensor is integrated into the helmet, detects impacts and communicates via an IOS or Android app
• Includes 2 accelerometers and a gyroscope
• IP67 dust and waterproof rating
• Helmet with adjustable visor
• Weight: 445 g (L/XL, including sensor)
• Sizes: S/M (54cm - 58cm) or L/XL (59cm - 63cm)
• Colour options: Black, White/grey, White/purple
• MSRP: £81/€89,99 (helmet) + £116/€129,99 (sensor) = £197/€219,98
• oneal.eu


The Quin System

The Quin Pod is the sensor unit that clips into the structure of the helmet behind the liner. It contains two accelerometers and a gyroscope to detect acceleration and rotation of the helmet up to 6,600 times per second, and communicates with your phone via Bluetooth and an app (Android or IOS). If the algorithm determines that an event could be serious, an alert is sent to the phone. You then have ten seconds to cancel the alert if you're okay. Otherwise, it will send a text message and email to your chosen emergency contacts (you can register up to three), letting them know your location with a Google Maps link, and prompting them to call you.

The app can also contact emergency services in thirty-two countries if you sign up for the Plus ResQ membership for $7.99 per month. With this service, Quin will also try to contact you to find out if you're okay. To be clear, you don't have to pay anything for the app to message your own emergency contacts.

Quin seems to have had the story of the boy who cried wolf in mind when designing their latest sensor. Compared to the system they released in 2021 with Abus, it has 2.5 times the sampling rate and adds a gyroscope to more accurately measure what's happening to your head, helping to prevent false alarms while detecting real events.

For example, if you drop your helmet onto a hard surface, the linear acceleration (G-force) can be similar to a real crash. But an empty helmet will tend to keep rotating after impact, while a helmet that contains a rider's head can't do that (unless you're an owl), so the algorithm looks at how the rotational velocity changes over time to distinguish an empty-helmet drop from a crash. If the helmet hits the deck hard, the app will register this as a helmet drop and prompt you to check for damage.

The sensor is designed specifically for each model and size of helmet, taking into account the shape, structure, and weight to more accurately determine whether an impact is deemed serious. To be clear, you can't mount the Quin sensor to any helmet.

The app also stores data on the severity of the impact (rotation and linear acceleration), which Quin says could be helpful for rehab in the event of a concussion. Quin claims this has helped in the recovery of one sponsored motocross athlete, but they acknowledge that head injuries need to be treated based on symptoms rather than impact forces.

The sensor is easy to take in and out of the helmet. It charges via USB-C in about two hours, and the battery lasts for 40+ hours of ride time and months of standby time. You don't have ot plug it in after every ride. It's IP67 rated, meaning you don't have to worry about it getting wet or dusty, unless maybe you drop it in the sea.

The app is easy enough to use. It offers ride tracking, but this is less useful than other apps like Strava. I just use the Quin app to make sure the sensor is connected and charged.


Does it work?

It's hard to say for sure how the system would respond if the proverbial hit the fan (I haven't been unlucky enough to "need" the system yet), but I can say it does a good job of eliminating false positives. Maybe too good.

Simply dropping the helmet on the ground either doesn't register at all or is logged as a helmet drop. Not wanting to put my own head at risk in the name of science, the best I could do was to ride with the helmet in one hand and hit it into a tree. But even this doesn't usually trigger a response, or it registers as a helmet drop.

I tried this literally dozens of times with different configurations of impact speed, angle and surface, but I couldn't "trick" the system into registering a crash and messaging my emergency contact (don't worry, Quin had disabled the emergency services feature on my device).

After talking to Quin, and making many more attempts, I managed to trigger an alert by filling the helmet with a bag of gravel to simulate the inertia of a head, which reduces the rotational acceleration a bit like a real head, then running with the helmet into the base of a tree.

This triggered a loud alarm in the app almost immediately, and after ignoring the notification, it sent a message to my emergency contact (whom I had informed of what I was up to), showing her my correct location on a map. Success!

So, yes, it can work. But it's hard to know how reliably it would work for real, as I only triggered an alert once despite many attempts. The Bluetooth connection between the helmet and the phone would drop out occasionally, too, despite having all app permissions switched on.

To be useful, the sensor has to correctly recognise a real impact, then send the signal to the phone. This requires the phone to remain functional after the crash, at least for a little while, and the Bluetooth connection to remain uninterrupted. You'll also need a phone signal for it to be able to reach your contacts or the emergency services if you've paid for that service.

I'm not saying that chain of events is unlikely, but it's far from guaranteed. Quin's efforts to eliminate false positives make it hard to trigger the system without hitting the deck for real, and that makes it hard to know if it would work when it counts.

I spoke to Quin about this at length, and they claim that they have detected thousands of crashes between their motorbike and cycling helmets, with "less than a handful of false positives and false negatives" being registered. Quin also say that while they've worked hard to avoid false alarms, the algorithm is tuned to trigger a crash alert if there's ambiguity.

I'm not going to put my own head on the line to test that out, and there is a lack of testimonials or reviews saying that it does work when it counts. Needless to say, don't think of it as an excuse to do stupid solo stunts, but as a last line of defense in case things don't go to plan.


O'Neal Trailfinder Helmet

My main issue with this implementation is not the Quin sensor - it's lightweight, unobtrusive, and relatively inexpensive in the grand scheme of things, so why not have it? My concern is with the O'Neal Trailfinder helmet it's fitted to.

It doesn't offer the best passive safety credentials, so there is a tradeoff in this case. While it complies with European EN1078 and American CPSC safety standards for bicycle helmets as you'd expect, it's not listed on Virginia Tech's independent and comparative safety rankings, and it doesn't have MIPS or any other device designed to create a slip plane to prevent rotational forces from affecting the rider's head. All the top-scoring helmets on Virginia Tech's list have technology like this.

It's only available in two sizes, which may not suit everybody. Personally, I found the fit a little narrow and tight above the ears.

The Trailfinder is otherwise a perfectly acceptable budget helmet (it costs just £81/€89,99 without the sensor), but if the appeal of Quin is to maximise safety, it's a shame the helmet isn't already one of the safest on the market. As it is, it's hard to know if the Quin sensor makes up for the Trailfinder's unproven safety credentials. For the combined price of the helmet and sensor (€220), you could have your pick of many of the safest helmets according to Virginia Tech, including the top-ranked Canyon Deflectr RLS ($160).

Personally, I use WhatsApp live location when I'm riding alone, along with Garmin's Incident Detection feature. This is far more basic than Quin's system, and Garmin is awful for false alerts (it's never going to be as sensitive when it's not connected to your head), but at least they're easy to cancel without pulling out your phone with the Garmin on your handlebars. More importantly, the Garmin can be used with any helmet, and I'm not sure the Quin sensor offers an edge over the Garmin system that justifies being tied to a relatively basic helmet.



Pros

+ Quin sensor can automatically alert emergency contacts if your head takes a hit
+ Doesn't cry wolf

Cons

- No guarantee the sensor will work when it's needed most - requires phone signal to be effective
- Trailfinder helmet lacks a Virginia Tech safety ranking and MIPS liner or similar




Pinkbike's Take
The idea behind the Quin sensor is appealing for those who ride alone. If I were to end up unconscious at the side of the trail, I'd want people to know where I was as soon as possible. But it's hard to know if the device would alert your emergency contacts when it came to the crunch, as a few things have to line up for that to happen: impact detection, Bluetooth connection & phone signal. It's a small and unobtrusive device, and the combined cost of the helmet and sensor is reasonable, so why not have it just in case? Unfortunately, the O'Neal Trailfinder helmet that the sensor is made for isn't the most recommendable if safety is paramount. It doesn't have a Virginia Tech ranking, nor does it have a slip-plane system like all the top-ranked helmets do. It's a budget-priced helmet with fairly basic passive safety features. Personally, I'd feel safer wearing a top-rated helmet without a sensor, especially when combined with more basic means of sharing your location.— Seb Stott
