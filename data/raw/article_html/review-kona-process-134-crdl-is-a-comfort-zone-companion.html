Cut from the same cloth as its longer-travel sibling, the Process 134 is Kona's answer to the needs of the modern day trail rider. Now in its 3rd generation, it rolls up with a new-found versatility with capacity to run a 29" or MX wheelset, with a subtly modernized set of geometry figures that don't oversell its intentions. Lo and behold the bike possesses 134mm of rear wheel delivered via the same linkage-driven single-pivot platform seen on the Process 153, and the Process X enduro bike.

The range-topping 134 CR DL reviewed here is a carbon-framed affair priced at $4,499, with one more affordable carbon option, and two budget-oriented aluminum options, too. They share the same wheel size versatility as the carbon frame.

Pinkbike's Take


It's not hard work to enjoy riding the Process 134, but it will reward an aggressive and precise riding style. Its firm ride feel and quick handling through turns can make you feel pinned on smooth bike park terrain, but it's easy to come undone when carrying that speed into looser, steeper terrain where a little more suppleness on- and off the brakes would make things a little more comfortable and predictable.—Jessie-May Morgan


Four holes in the front allow for either-or cable routing

Frame Details

The 134 CRDL has a carbon frame, and weighs in at 14 kg (30.8 lbs) in a size small. That's a little portly for a carbon bike in this travel category, but that has to be considered alongside the component specification and asking price before a fair judgement can be passed. The bike has a respectable build spec (though it is a little outdated), and its most expensive variant is clear of a thousand dollars cheaper than the low-end build options from competitors like Pivot.

Personally, I am a huge fan of the frame shape and the contouring of its carbon tubes. It almost looks as though it has been sculpted by an artist at the seat tube and along the stays. Those lovely shapes can't possibly be born purely born out of function, can they?

Love letter penned, let's turn to what matters.

A trough, for when you run out water on a rainy ride
The dropper cable makes a small appearance before entering the seat tube

All sizes can accept a water bottle in the front triangle. On the small, I had to make use of an offset mount from Apidura to shift the bosses up the downtube such that the shock piggyback would clear the bottle. Accommodation made, I can fit a 600 ml bottle in there with a side-entry bottle cage - if I wrestle it in. Such a palaver might not be experienced by anyone riding the entry-level alloy Process 134 as it runs an in-line shock.

Cable routing is internal and guided throughout by carbon tubes for easy cable replacements. The CR/DL model I tested has a SRAM Transmission AXS drivetrain, but there are ports for routing a cable should you ever want to go back to a mechanical drivetrain. Close to where the cable would exit the front triangle, there is a clamp for securing it on the main pivot so the cable shouldn't move and rub the carbon as the suspension articulates. Only the routing of the brake hose is external along the chainstay.

Tube-in-tube cable routing, a dropped stay and comprehensive chain slap protection make for a very quiet bike. Is there anything more joyful?

Some final important frame details: an ISCG 05 mount is there for the mounting of a chain guide and bash guard, and the maximum chainring size is 36T. The rear brake mount is sized for a 180mm rotor direct mount, though most models get a 20mm post mount adaptor for the 200mm rotor. Last but not least, the Process 134 runs a press fit bottom bracket.



Geometry

The Process 134 goes 5mm longer than the predecessor, matching the burlier 153 with its reach numbers. The four sizes span a generous range with reaches of 430mm, 455mm, 480mm and 515mm, paired with short seat tubes. Kona do not afford each front triangle its own appropriately sized chainstay length; all four sizes share the same rear triangle with a 435mm chainstay.

Though insertion depth of the seat tube isn't quite to the center of the earth, it is respectable. The 175mm Reverb spec'd on the S is wholly sufficient for that frame's intended rider height. Drop length increases to 200mm on the M-XL sizes, and many a rider will want to swap it out for something longer. To that end, these maximum seat post insertion depths should be kept in mind: S ; 270mm, M; 270mm, L; 300mm, XL; 320mm.

The head angle sits at 65.5°, ball-park for a bike of this travel bracket. The seat tube angles are steeper than those of the predecessor, but still a little off-the-back in regard to where the rider is positioned, and relative to some of its competitors. In a size large, it's an effective 76.7°. Given the all-round trail riding intentions, I can see why Kona are a little conservative on this front. I certainly wouldn't expect it to have the extra-steep, wrist-loading seat tube angles we see on longer-travel bikes, but a touch steeper wouldn't hurt, in my opinion.

Head tube length increases in 11mm increments as we look up through the size range, so taller riders may not have to make too many accommodations to bring the bars up to a comfortable ride height.


The flip-chip at the rocker-stay interface is a geometry correction tool to be used when switching out the rear wheel size. It corrects for the otherwise reduced bottom bracket height, and in doing so increases the rear axle's mechanical advantage over the shock, increasing the average leverage ratio. As such, you'll need to run a little more air in the rear shock when switching to the 27.5" rear wheel if you want to run the same sag.



Suspension Design

The Process 134 delivers its rear wheel travel via a linkage-driven single pivot, with a 185mm x 50mm trunnion mount shock. Its leverage curve is consistently progressive. In its stock configuration with a complete 29" wheelset, travel is its namesake 134mm, and the leverage curve starts at around 3.0, finishing up at just over 2.4. The leverage ratio is slightly increased with the MX wheelset, starting out just north of 3.1, and travel increasing to 140mm. All models ship with an air shock, though Kona state the kinematic works well with coil, too.


Anti-squat is relatively high, sitting just north of 100% around sag. That indicates the bike's suspension should feel agnostic of pedaling inputs at this portion in the travel when winching up inclines in the easier gears.

Test Bike Setup

I'm 163 cm, and I tested the Process 134 CRDL in a size small with a reach of 430mm paired with a 50mm stem. Sizes M-XL are shipped with a complete 29" wheelset, but the small arrives as a mullet. It can still accommodate a 29" rear wheel, however I didn't try it myself. I cut the 20mm rise bar to my preferred 740mm, and rode the bike with the shock sitting at 25% sag (128 psi).

I set the fork to the pressure recommendation from RockShox. I have spent a lot of time on the Charger 3 damper. As a rider who sits on the lighter end of the spectrum, I am familiar with the need to run its compression and rebound damping dials open, the latter to get the fork to recover its travel sufficiently from repeated hits. To those interested, the Charger 3.1 is a marked improvement on that front, allowing for much higher rebound speeds at low spring pressures and better traction as a result.

Testing Info

I rode the Process 134 on a variety of terrain; bike park and more natural, granite rock singletrack, mostly in dry conditions. It was the preferred option over my go-to enduro bike for rides on sculpted bike park-esque trails, and rides that involved very long stretches of climbing on punishingly steep fire road to access some of the more iconic mountain-top descents on this eastern edge of the Cairngorms. I also took a liking to grabbing it for quick lunchtime laps of the local hill that is riddled with several easily-lapped hand-cut descents of mellow gradient. I rode it both within, and outwith its intended comfort zone to find where it excels, and where it leaves you wanting.



Climbing

The Process 134 isn't as spritely a climber as its travel numbers and fast-rolling Maxxis Dissector tire might have you think. The suspension is relatively neutral while pedaling on smooth fire roads. The bike doesn't feel particularly inefficient in that regard and I didn't feel the need for a lock-out. It's not so supple on bumpy singletrack climbs though, and I found it a challenge to prevent the rear tire from breaking traction over rocks, roots, as well as softer ground.


The seated pedaling position is a little too far off the back for my liking. I have come to appreciate the upright position afforded by steeper seat tube angles, and the Kona is a little slack in that regard. I chose to push the saddle to its extreme forward position to alleviate some of the discomfort I felt in seated pedaling, but it didn't solve the problem entirely. On very steep sections of singletrack, the slack angle had me inching my way up the nose of the saddle in a bid to keep weight down through the front tire. Those riding flatter or more undulating terrain are less likely to experience an issue here.


Descending


The Process 134 is reasonably adept in descending most terrain. It got on well with most challenges I sent its way, though with varying degrees of finesse. With just 134mm of rear wheel travel, it would be naïve of anyone to expect the bike to slay on the most demanding technical singletrack there is, but also wrong to assume it isn't at least capable of managing without drama. I would generally steer clear of judging a bike too hard on its travel numbers alone, as it can often be rather misleading.

I was very comfortable riding the Process 134 on technical, flowing singletrack from the get-go. Stood up on the pedals, the cockpit is a little on the compact end of the spectrum for me, but I quite like that in a trail bike. It maximizes the bike's capacity to feel playful, and can be helpful in preventing its rider from getting too over-confident in steeper sections of trail. The 65.5° head tube angle is a long way off from feeling twitchy, but when pointing the bike down the fall line, one can't help but feel a little vulnerable. I did benefit from raising the bar by around 20mm (relative to the position shown). The bike felt better to climb with the bar in the lower position in which it arrived, but for descending I preferred the more carefree riding style that was afforded by the higher bar.


The suspension feels relatively firm off the top. I wouldn't go so far as to call it harsh, but on the whole the bike does have a certain firmness to it. For context, I have ridden more supple-feeling 120mm travel bikes. To begin with, I ran the rear suspension at 25% sag with high and low-speed compression wound to their open positions. On some bigger compressions and small hucks I was blowing through the travel and coming up against a hard bottom-out. I remedied that with an extra volume spacer, and a few clicks of HSC. This made the rear considerably more resistant to bottom-out, and considerably more supportive through the mid-stroke.

With these settings, the bike came alive. It was a joy to ride on fast, flowing singletrack, and felt utterly wonderful to push and pump through berms and rollers.

The rear end tracks very well in most situations, but under braking the suspension noticeably stiffens up. It caught me off guard on the first few rides, but I soon adapted, taking care to pick my braking zones effectively. It's not always possible of course, but when the opportunities arose, I felt it beneficial to brake before an array of braking bumps, and to roll through them off the brakes.

It's not hard work to enjoy riding the Process 134, but it will reward an aggressive and precise riding style. Its firm ride feel and quick handling through turns can make you feel pinned on smooth bike park terrain, but it's easy to come undone when carrying that speed into looser, steeper terrain where a little more suppleness on- and off the brakes would make things a little more comfortable and predictable.

How Does It Compare?

The recently-launched Pivot Trailcat LT has many similarities with the Kona Process 134, being MX- and 29" capable with a carbon frame and the same quantity of rear wheel travel. They deliver that travel through very different suspension layouts, but otherwise the two platforms share similar intentions. While the Pivot is considerably lighter (claimed weight of 13 kg in a medium, XTR), the $4,499 USD asking price for the Kona comes in well under the price of Pivot's most affordable Trailcat LT model. I won't do an in-depth analysis here, but know that you are getting a lot more for your money with the components on the Kona, as compared to the Trailcat LT. There are two sides to every coin, though; while the Pivot is sold with a lifetime warranty, Kona sell the Process 134 CRDL with a three-year warranty.

The Process 134 is considerably more capable in my hands, up and down the hill, than the Pivot Shadowcat, a 27.5" wheel 140mm trail bike, despite that bike being equipped with a 160mm travel fork - versus the Kona's 140mm affair. On the flip-side, I recently rode a 120mm travel Santa Cruz Tallboy which, despite its XC bracketed travel numbers and 29" wheelset, takes the top spot in my mind as the most capable, playful bike of those mentioned here. The most relevant performance comparison comes with the Pivot Trailcat LT that I have in for review just now; it is the more capable of the two bikes, especially on steeper trickier trails and would therefore be my top choice if money were no object.




Which Model is the Best Value?


Of the four Kona Process 134 models, the most expensive $4,499 USD CR DL one that I tested will be the lightest build. However, dependent on exactly what you value, the one may not be the best option for you. One rung down the ladder, you can pay $3,199 USD for the 134 CR. It has the same carbon frame as the CR DL, but a subtly different build spec. It switches out the Rockshox Reverb for a TranzX RAD+ dropper (the more adjustable and reliable post, in my experience).

Process 134 CR - $3,199 USD

It also switches out the SRAM G2 RSC brakes for the G2 R brakes. They have the same 4-piston caliper and lever architecture, and thus the same capacity for stopping power, but they lack the bite point adjustment. Sure, it is a nice to have feature, and can help reduce intervals between bleeding.

The other major differences lie in the suspension components and drivetrain. While the CRDL runs the previous generation upper-end suspension components from Rockshox, the CR model gets mid-range options from Fox. The CRDL has an electronic T-Type derailleur, while the CR model runs the SRAM GX mechanical option. The two bikes roll on the same Maxxis tires on the same WTB KOM Team i30 rims, but the CR model uses DT 370 hubs, as compared to the CRDL model's faster-engagement DT 350 hubs. If I was committed to one of the carbon models, I'd have to go for the more affordable CR model given it is a whopping $1,300 USD price difference.

Process 134 DL - $2,399
Process 134 - $1,599

If carbon gives you the boke and you don't mind a weight penalty then you have two aluminum models to choose from, though both are equipped with more budget-oriented componentry. Generally, you have to look to more boutique brands if you want to find top-level componentry on an aluminum frame. Both models are going to be considerably heavier than the 14 kg CRDL model tested here, and the cable routing is completely external, but importantly they are sold with a lifetime warranty. It has to be said, $1,599 USD for a complete bike, sold with a lifetime warranty from a well-established brand is undeniably excellent value in 2025 - cheaper than some frame only options from other brands. You'll want to swap out its SRAM Level two-piston brakes almost immediately, but you'll have plenty of cash to spare for a quality upgrade.



Technical Report


SRAM G2 RSC Brakes: These brakes are reasonably powerful. With the 200mm Centerline rotors I was never left wanting, but my heavier colleagues think these should be thrown in the trash and replaced with the more powerful SRAM Code, or something with a similar amount of power.

RockShox Reverb Dropper Seat Post: Early on the post developed undesirable sag. In its top-out position, its sags by around 20mm under my weight. Similarly, in its low position, it extends up by around 20mm, reducing clearance. This is a common issue with the mechanical Reverb, but is very quickly rectified with the Rockshox Vent Valve Tool that is supplied with the bike.

Maxxis Dissector: On the few wetter rides, I felt a notable lack of bite from the Maxxis Dissector rear tire on soft climbs, with a similar notable lack of braking traction. Its tread pattern is relatively shallow and the centre tread blocks don't provide much modulation in that regard, tending to lock up with little encouragement.

Kona Key Grip: The rubber compound is very firm and uncomfortable on long descents. I also disliked the raised bump at the end of the grip, as it prevents me from comfortably running my hands at the end of the bar.

FSA Headset: All sizes of the Process 134 get this Orbit 1.5 E ZS headset with a 20mm top cap that raises the minimum stack height unnecessarily.



Pros

+ Loves a flow trail
+ Quiet
+ More affordable than many of its direct competitors

Cons

- Seat tube likely to feel very slack for taller riders
- On the heavier end of the spectrum for a carbon trail bike
- Not the most proficient climber
- Spec is a little outdated, likely owing to Kona's sale and inventory issues



Pinkbike's Take

The Kona Process 134 didn't blow me away in any department, but that's not to say I didn't have a good time riding it. Its slack seat tube angle and spec from a bygone era hold it back from greatness, and it left me wanting more predictability and comfort on rougher descents, but it was a fine companion for flattering, flowing singletrack. The value is great though, making this a solid option for those watching their pennies.
—Jessie-May Morgan
