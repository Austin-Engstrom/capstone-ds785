Review: Vorsprung Telum Coil Shock - Canadian Made & Custom Tuned

Mar 6, 2025
by Mike Kazimer

After years of offering shock tuning services, along with manufacturing their own line of fork upgrades, Vorsprung Suspension decided to create a shock of their own. A three year development process resulted in the Telum, a Canadian-made, custom tuned, twin tube shock.

The overall design and resulting list of features aren't wildly different from what's currently offered from other manufacturers, but the smart details and the ability to revalve the shock without pulling it entirely apart help it stand out from the crowd. There's also the fact that each shock is custom tuned for the bike it will be installed on, and for the rider.
Vorsprung Telum Details
• Adjustments: low speed compression, high speed compression, rebound
• Adjustable hydraulic bottom out
• Climb switch
• Weight: 971 g (with spring, 205 x 65mm)
• Made in Canada
• MSRP: $1,200 USD
• vorsprungsuspension.com

That level of customization does come at a price – the Telum retails for $1,200 USD, making it one of the more expensive options on the market.

Details

Mentioning that the Telum's design isn't a radical departure from the norm isn't meant as a slight – after all, there's no need to start from scratch when there are decades of previous developments to pull from in the moto and mountain bike worlds. The Telum uses a twin tube design with a solid, 22mm diameter main piston. As the shock compresses, the piston pushes oil through the damping circuits in the reservoir bridge, and then it recirculates between the inner tube and the outer tube, back behind the main piston.

One of the reasons Vorsprung went with the solid main piston design was to allow for the shock to be revalved without being disassembled. With their Rapid Revalve design it's possible to externally adjust the force required to flex the internal shims, which enables significant changes to the level of damping without a full overhaul. It's worth noting that a revalve isn't something that most riders will need to do unless they're switching the shock to a different bike or had a significant change in weight or riding style.

The shock has 18 clicks of low-speed compression, 12 clicks of high-speed compression, 18 clicks of rebound adjustment, and 18 clicks of hydraulic bottom-out, along with a Sprindex coil; that provides a wide range of possible configurations. All of the adjustments are done with either a 3 or 6mm allen key – there aren't any tool-free adjustments, other than the lockout lever. The hydraulic bottom out kicks in for the last 15mm of the shock's stroke.

Steve Mathews, Vorsprung's founder, has put together four videos that do an excellent job of explaining each and every detail, free of any unnecessary marking fluff. Those videos are worth a watch if you want to take an even deeper dive into the inner workings of the Telum.


Ordering

Filling out a detailed questionnaire is part of the purchasing procedure for the Telum. Along with the typical rider weight and bike model information, riders are asked about their riding style, aggression level (on the bike, not in life), strength to weight ratio, plus typical jump size and terrain steepness.

It may seem a little excessive, but it's similar to getting fitted for a custom suit (or at least I assume so – I've never actually owned a suit, bespoke or otherswise). Just like you wouldn't expect a tailor to measure one arm and call it good, the more information Vorspung can gather the greater the chance that the shock they ship out will need as little setup time as possible. Ideally, it can be installed and ridden upon arrival without needing any adjustments. The information you provided is saved in Vorsprung's Tuning Hub database, as is the exact tune that the shock is shipped with. All of that information is accessible by the rider, which can come in handy if you decide to try a different tune.

In my case, I noted my preference a balanced setup optimized for steep terrain at a variety of speeds, and rated myself advanced / expert with a 'very athletic' strength to weight ratio. For my 160 lb weight I ended up with a 475 lb spring, although the beauty of the Sprindex design is that that can easily be changed by turning the collar.



Performance

When the shock arrived I did exactly what Vorsprung suggested – I installed it, checked the sag (27%), and did a quick bounce to make sure the settings didn't feel too wacky before heading out for a shakedown lap. That initial ride went well, free of anything that would cause me to pull out my multi-tool and start turning dials. I've been able to try a wide range of excellent shocks on my Raaw, so it wasn't a mindblowingly different ride experience compared to what I was used to, but it was very quiet, very calm, and free of any annoying top-outs. Given the choice, and if it doesn't negatively affect the performance, I'll take a silent shock over a noisy one every single time.

Three months later the shock is still as smooth as when it arrived, and I haven't had to make any drastic setup changes – I'm still within a click or two of the original recommended setup.

The best shocks fade into the background, and the Telum sits firmly in that category. It enters its travel smoothly, and then remains nicely composed when dealing with repeated sharp impacts. The hydraulic bottom out feature works well, and there isn't any harshness to speak of at the end of the stroke.

Many of my favorite rides involve an hour or so of grinding up a steep fire road, which gave me plenty of time to think about the shock's lockout. The Raaw is a pretty calm pedaling bike even with a shock fully open, but the level of support that the Telum provides without being jarring is commendable. It's the style of lockout I prefer – the shock can still move to take the edge of rougher sections of road or trail, but it's also firm enough to provide a more supportive pedaling platform. If you do forget to flip the lever to open before descending it's not the end of the world – a blowoff feature prevents any damage to the shock, and since it's not a full, super firm lockout it's not an unbearably harsh ride.

What about the Revalve feature? I haven't had a need to go down that route, but it's nice to know it's possible. It does require one additional tool, but the steps are simple to follow, and it doesn't require getting covered in oil or having access to a vacuum bleeder.


How Does It Compare?

I've had a wide range of coil shocks mounted to my Raaw Madonna over the last 12 months, including an Ohlins TTX22, RockShox Vivid Coil Ultimate, and the Push SV Eight. Each shock has their own slightly different feel out on the trail, but with a little experimentation I was able to find a good setup with each option.

If I had to rank them, the Vorsprung would be one step from the top, the spot currently occupied by the SV Eight. There's just something about that SV Eight that keeps me coming back to it – it feels slightly more sensitive than the Telum, and it's a little livelier overall, a sensation I prefer. I can achieve a feeling that's similar with the Telum, but it's not identical. At the end of the day, both shocks are excellent, and I'm certainly not being held back by either one.

Pinkbike's Take

Imagine if a band came out with a greatest hits collection as their first album. That's essentially what Vorsprung has done with the Telum – this isn't a tentative step on the long road to perfection. Instead, it's a fully realized, extremely well executed option, one that can go toe-to-toe (or eyelet to eyelet) with anything else on the market. It's quiet, consistent, and the custom tuning helps ensure that it's fully dialed right out of the box.— Mike Kazimer

VorsprungSuspension (Mar 7, 2025 at 10:46)
 The advantages of bladders are that they're cheaper and have zero stiction, but they have a large rubber surface area through which the gas charge diffuses and eventually aerates the oil, faster than with an IFP. IFPs are also much easier to consistently set the oil volume correctly. The real determining factor in whether you should use a bladder or an IFP comes down to the ratio of shaft diameter to IFP/reservoir cross sectional areas, because that determines the hydraulic advantage over the stiction force of the IFP seals. In the case of the Telum (10mm shaft, 27mm IFP), that gives a ratio of 0.137, meaning 1 unit of friction force at the IFP generates 0.137 units of force at the shaft. The actual measured friction of the IFP seal is around 2lbs, meaning the effect of that friction when measured at the shaft is 0.274lbs (about 2.7N). However, the average leverage ratio of most frames is around 2.6, so that force gets reduced by that factor, to around 0.1lbs (0.47N, or about 48 grams) of force at the wheel - this is really negligible.

For context, most air-sprung forks have a total of around 20lbs of frictional force (200x the difference between a bladder/IFP in this shock). That is not a gain worth we felt was chasing in this instance, since taking a hit on durability/serviceability for negligible performance benefit isn't a good move in our estimation.

With that said, bladders have their place - if the shock uses larger shaft diameters (or smaller reservoir diameters), or a reservoir body that's not replaceable if it sees any wear (eg in the Ohlins TTX22 and E-Storia) they make more sense.

VorsprungSuspension (Mar 27, 2025 at 12:57)
 @joni0001984: without knowing exactly what tune the SV8 had, hard to say with certainty, but I suspect it's just a lighter tune in compression and/or rebound. It's a bit of a shame that Mike didn't test the Rapid Revalve function, as it's just a couple of minutes to adjust the tune.

Lower IFP pressure in the SV8 acts on a larger shaft area than the Telum, and the only place where that makes any difference is on initial touchdown when the wheel leaves the ground and you have some gas charge preload to overcome. Adding ONE millimetre of spring preload has a larger effect than the difference between the two shocks there (500lbs/in spring, 1/25th of an inch = 20lbs difference in preload to overcome to get the shock moving). That's four times bigger than the difference between the SV8's and Telum's gas charge forces, and the SV8 has a lower hydraulic ratio (IFP to shaft area ratio) than the Telum, meaning it's actually MORE affected by IFP friction. So again, it's just not that.

S4's own sales copy highlights the advantages of the IFP (and implicitly, the disadvantages of a bladder). Realistically, a damper is literally and a device that converts kinetic energy to thermal energy (heat), that's its job - the additional heat that comes from the IFP friction is negligible in the context of the amount generated by the actual damping! Again, there are a few good scenarios where it does make more sense to use bladders, like in the original Kitsuma that did not have a replaceable reservoir (same with some versions of the X2 series), but there are also many suspension "upgrades" out there that make a negligible difference in any performance sense. The aftermarket bladder kits for the DB series are one of those things, but if you're going to sell them you have to market them somehow.

It's not that bladders are some unknown magical technology for suspension designers that we don't use just to cut corners or because we don't know they exist - they've been around longer than I've been alive, they're cheaper to make than an IFP, if they were the right choice for the Telum we'd simply use them in there.

Funny though that nobody is worrying about their fork damper cartridge, almost all of which have moved away from bladders (Fox 2004-2018, Rockshox 2013-22) to IFPs (pretty well all Fox/RS currently).
