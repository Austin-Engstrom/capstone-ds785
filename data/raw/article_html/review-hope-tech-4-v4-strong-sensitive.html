Hope Tech 4 V4


The heavily machined, often colorful look of Hope brakes always stand out on a bike, with a cult following devoted to the British brand's high-end stoppers. The Tech 4 V4 has been their benchmark gravity brake for some time now, earning a spot on DH, freeride, and enduro bikes alike.

Though they've since introduced an additional heavy hitter in the gravity lineup - namely, the EVO GR4 - the Tech 4 V4 remains one of the best brakes money can buy. Read on for our long-term impressions, as well as some comparisons to the new GR4.

V4 Details

• DOT fluid
• Adjustments: Reach, bite point
• 5 pad options
• Tested: Green Race pads, 2.3mm thick 200mm rotor
• 2x18mm, 2x16mm pistons
• Price: $628 USD (pair)
• Brake weight: 285g (front)
• Rotor weight: 222g (203mm)
• hopetech.com

BUY AT:
Competitive Cyclist




Setup

Hope's bleed process is a bit less refined than the other options on test, requiring some additional faff to install the bleed cup atop the master cylinder. That said, it's a repeatable and time-tested method, with solid results and only some cleanup to worry about. The Hope-branded DOT fluid is easy enough to come by, and we found that they never needed a re-bleed after initial setup.

There are five pad options and a few different rotors to choose from, opening up a wide array of tuning opportunities to the end user. As with all of the brakes on test, we asked the brand to send us the most powerful combination of both - in this case, that was the green Race pads, and their 2.3mm thick 200mm rotor, without the fancy floating aluminum carrier.

The Tech 4 lever features both reach and bite point adjustment, which work somewhat dependently with each other, meaning you'll likely be trimming in one setting after altering the other. No complaints here, as the desired setup position is still easy to achieve, and a fairly short deadband can be achieved at any reach adjustment.

One setup annoyance plagues the Tech 4 V4, and that has to do with the width of the caliper body. Hope's own website calls this out, and it's reflected in real life, where some bikes and forks struggle to accommodate the caliper's width. If your frame has an unusually tight rear brake area, or you're hoping to run the V4 with a 180mm rotor up front, it's best to check with Hope or do a test fit beforehand.

Livability:
The Hopes are some of the easiest to live with on test, thanks to a fuss-free hydraulic system, easy alignment, and solid adjustments. The caliper fitment could be an issue for some, but we experience no problems.


Feel

Hope achieved a very light lever action with the Tech 4 V4, but preserved a nice level of feedback, allowing the user to sense where they are in the stroke without much of a learning curve. The long lever was comfortable and easy to setup for both Matt and I, but we both noticed a bit of flex within the lever when you're really reefing on them.

That long lever gives you a smooth stroke, with no break-in force required to get the action moving. Once you hit the bite point, power comes on with a progressive ramp that feels aggressive enough to achieve max power quickly, but still feather things lightly when desired. The bite point is perfectly consistent, and doesn't shift with heat buildup.

One complaint that feels adjacent to feel is the noise the Tech 4 V4 caliper makes when braking. Best described as a screech, this high-pitched noise isn't alleviated with caliper alignment or any bed-in procedure, and is just something to get used to. Likely to bug some and be a non-issue for others, it's ultimately just the sound of them working.

Feel Ranking:
The light lever action feels intuitive and very easy to control, giving the Hope's an accurate and precise feel. With a quick ramp up past the bite point, you can brake efficiently, but still feather the lighter end of that power when needed.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 493 g pull force (3rd overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.


Power

The Tech 4 V4 balances power and sensitivity in a way that should appeal to many riders. With a fairly progressive feel, you can get up to max power quickly, but still control that initial bite point.

Compared to brakes like the Maven or Saint, the Hope feels less grabby, but still offers similar power in sustained pull moments when you're just working to slow the bike down. Fans of a very aggressive ramp in power might prefer those other options, but the Tech 4 V4 is not to be ignored here.

The Race pads offer a the best initial bite of their lineup, and are impressively resistant to fade. The screeching noise is an unfortunate byproduct, but worth it for maximizing the brake's performance.

Power Ranking:
Strangely, the Hopes didn't fare as well in our perfectly scientific Honda Fit Stop test as expected, slotting in 7th place. That said, their feel on trail is impressive, with accessible power that feels adequate for any gravity use case.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 30.2 m / 99 ft (7th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.

The new GR4.

Updates

Hope's newest gravity brake takes many cues from the Tech 4 V4 and introduces some refinements for user experience and ease of service. The EVO GR4 features 2x17mm and 2x18mm pistons, but still uses the same pad shape. The lever is compatible with three different lever blades, the longest and straightest of which (dubbed: Power) best mimics the feel of the V4. All of the lever options have a bit more of a hook to them, which some users will likely prefer.

There is an increase in power, and less flex in the system, yielding a firmer feel through the lever blade. The lever action feels slightly less light and free into the stroke than the V4, but still has a consistent and easy motion to it.

Both remain excellent options, but the implementation of some additional aftermarket tunes and the addition of a threaded bleed port on the lever will probably lead some users to the GR4. The new model seems to be less screechy, but the noise from pad friction hasn't totally been eliminated.



Pros

+ Sensitive, accurate lever feel
+ Massive power
+ Easy to modulate


Cons

- Lever flex
- Screeching pads
- Wide caliper won't fit all setups
