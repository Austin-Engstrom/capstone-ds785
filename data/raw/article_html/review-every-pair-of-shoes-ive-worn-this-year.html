Review: Every Pair of MTB Shoes I've Worn This Year

by Dario DiGiulio

Shoes can be an annoying investment to make, as they're one of the bits of kit that sees the most abuse on a regular basis. I've been guilty of forcing shoes well past their point of retirement, as once you've found a good pair it can be hard to move on. My collection ballooned this year, and left me with a bit of a pile to pick from on a daily basis.

I ride just about every day, so these all got a fair bit of use, though the favorites ended up seeing more time on the pedals than others. Read on for a breakdown of each pair, who I think they'd be good for, and finally which ones came out on top.

Some notes about me before we get into it, just for a frame of reference. All of these are a size 45 or 45.5, which is my standard street shoe size. I have a fairly wide forefoot, a narrow heel, and a high arch - that said I think arch support is a hoax to keep your feet soft.

I've spent a lot of time pedaling in all of these, and each pair has seen a fair bit of hiking around in the woods. My preferred cleat position is about as far rearward as a typical channel allows, with the cleat as far as it can go outboard to keep my feet close to the cranks. Crank Brothers pedals unless otherwise noted.

Crankbrothers Mallet Trail

Mallet Trail Details

• Boa dial, hook & loop strap
• TPU-reinforced toebox
• Ripstop upper
• Fiber-reinforced shank
• Sizing: 35-49
• Price: $240 USD
• Weight: 490g / shoe
• crankbrothers.com

The Mallet DH sold me on Crankbrothers shoes, and while the Mallet Trail is quite different, they uphold a positive legacy. The upper construction on these is lighter than you'd expect, but feels like it offers plenty of protection where it counts - the toe, heel, and side of foot are all well guarded. The gaiter is well executed, and keeps larger debris from working its way in.

Crankbrothers touts their Match concept as being an ideal soul-bonding of shoe and pedal - while I do use Crankbrothers pedals, and like how these shoes interface, I wouldn't say it's drastically different than any of the other options here. You'll have to use some shims to space the cleat out, but once that's done it does lock into the pedal nicely. I've found the soles of these get fairly chewed up, but they remain useable even after plenty of time on the pins.

High quality materials.

Gumshoe gets a bit torn up.

The stiffness is fairly neutral on these, suiting heavy-duty usage without sacrificing walkability. My only real complaint is that they feel a bit tall underfoot, as if you're further separated from the pedal you're standing on than with some of the other options here. Not a big issue, but a detail I noticed when switching back and forth.

Pros

+ Durable, high-quality construction
+ Comfortable balance of stiffness and give

Cons

- Simple fit adjustments
- Tall-feeling sole


First Degree Fuse AC

Fuse AC Details

• Lace up, hook & loop strap
• Agressive outsole
• Seamless welded upper
• Foam-reinforced tongue
• Sizing: 36-47
• Price: 175 €
• Weight: 492g / shoe
• firstdegree-mtb.com

I was initially quite excited about these, as the details, aesthetics, and feel all added up to a shoe that seemed pretty ideal for my use case. Sadly, one detail kept them from working for me: the cleat channel. For some reason, the cleat channel on these is very far forward in the shoe, rendering even the rearmost position a bit awkward feeling to me. I could deal with it and get used to the feel, but the rearward cleat position that all of the other shoes on test deliver was sorely missed here. The other annoyance with the channel is how deep it is relative to the sole - you'll have to run quite a few spacers to match these up with Crankbrothers shoes, and even Shimano pedals required some tall standoffs.

Besides that detail, the shoes are quite nice. The stiffness is similar to the Crankbrothers, and the uppers are robust thanks to a smooth welded construction. The recessed laces feel safe from snags, and stay in place nicely once you've snugged up your fit. Speaking of fit, the forefoot on these is nice and wide, without adding too much bulk - points for comfort.

Bungee for lace storage.

Bummer about the cleat position.

Thanks to the fairly aggressive outsole on these, their pedal contact is very strong. Perhaps too much if you're someone who likes to break free easily, in which case you'll have to space the cleat out even further. I liked the locked in feel, and found these to be some of the grippiest shoes for hiking and scrambling around, should you find yourself in those situations.

Pros

+ Nice details, great fit
+ Grippy sole

Cons

- Cleat channel needs redesign
- Many cleat spacers required


Ride Concepts Hellion Clip Boa

Hellion Details

• Boa dial
• D3O padding throughout
• Cordura upper
• Nylon shank
• Sizing: 39.5-47, women's available
• Price: $220 USD
• Weight: 586g / shoe
• rideconcepts.com

Ride Concepts overhauled their lineup earlier this year, introducing a few models under the Hellion banner. I've been using the more gravity-focused Hellion Elites, and these: the Hellions. As the more trail-focused version of the two, these shave down on some features in pursuit of simplicity and lighter weight, but ultimately they just feel like the lesser of the two options.

The sole, cleat positioning, and rubber compound are all great, offering solid connection to the pedal and great support for the foot. The main upper material is very robust, and offers great protection for the foot. Where these fall short for me is in the smaller details, such as with the Boa loops that started fraying almost immediately. It seems like brushing through undergrowth has been enough to cause these to fray quite a bit, and though they're holding strong so far, the design feels a bit flawed to me.

Not quite as burly as their Elite counterpart.

Same great sole and shank though.

Luckily the general fit of these is quite comfortable, with a generous forefoot and tight heel cup. I've found that the single Boa dial isn't adequate for tightening the whole shoe down in one go, requiring you to either pull the lacing through or flex your foot around to distribute tension as you crank things down. Compared to the Elites, which use two Boas, these feel a bit less secure.

A note: these and the Elites have the furthest back cleat channel of any of the shoes on test, so far rearward that I actually moved my cleat up a bit in the channel - a first in quite a while. Those who like a cleat at the forward tip of the shoe (why?) might not love this feature, through I really do.

Pros

+ Comfortable fit, good pedal connection
+ Protective and generally durable

Cons

- Weak point details
- Boa tension is hard to distribute


Shimano GE9

GE9 Details

• Boa dial, hook & loop strap
• Torbal flexible midsole
• Debris collar at ankle
• Fiber-reinforced shank
• Sizing: 38-48
• Price: $250 USD
• Weight: 507g / shoe
• ride.shimano.com

I've put this pair through the wringer, partially because they were the only shoes I had on hand for a little while, partially because they are very comfortable. If you have wide feet, these are plenty accommodating, with a big toebox and a fairly loose fit. One the flip side, if you like a locked-in ski boot feel this probably won't be your ideal shoe.

The material feels soft and pliable, with a fair bit of extra padding throughout. While that helps with protection and some comfort, it makes these feel a bit warmer and more prone to getting soggy. I also found that I had to alter my cleat position to keep the inner portion of the shoe from rubbing on the crank too much, due to how wide they are.

Lotta' rubbin'.

Nice hiking tread.

Due to that rubbing, I ended up sawing through the buckle that retains the hook-and-loop strap in very little time, necessitating the addition of a little DIY slot in the upper material. That did the trick, and they've continued to work despite. The plastic Boa lacing loops are great, and assure that the laces tension up the shoe every time, despite grit, grime, and wear.

Overall, the feel of the upper and sole of these is softer and more malleable than most of the other shoes on test, which makes them very walkable but takes away some of the racy stiffness some folks want. If you do want more stiffness, Shimano released the GE900HS, which is stiffer and a little slimmer than these shoes.

Pros

+ Wide and comfy
+ Nice lacing, good tread traction

Cons

- Soft and baggy feel isn't for everyone
- Strap durability


Ride Concepts Hellion Elite Clip Boa

Hellion Elite Details

• Two Boa dials
• D3O padding throughout
• Cordura upper
• Nylon shank
• Sizing: 39.5-47, women's available
• Price: $262 USD
• Weight: 596g / shoe
• rideconcepts.com

As mentioned earlier, these are essentially the bigger, better version of the Ride Concept Hellion - hence the name. The fit felt a bit potatoey at first, but after a short break-in period they started cinching in to my foot shape and feeling like home. The sole is plenty stiff without being ridiculous, and the overall fit feels comfortable enough for all-day use.

The heavy weight on the scale luckily translates to impressive durability in real world use, as these have held up remarkably well to my abuses. The laces are pulling smoothly, the upper fabric is intact, and the sole is still functioning well despite getting chewed up by the pedals. In my book, a bit more weight feels like a reasonable tradeoff for a shoe that lasts longer than just one season.

Boa bumpers.

Holding strong despite a tone of use.

That weight also manifests in a very protective shoe, one that has certainly saved my toes on a handful of occasions. The D3O padding seemed a bit gimmicky initially, but after having some talus rocket into the top of my foot I became convinced of its utility. Same goes for the plastic toecap, which deflects nicely off of trailside obstacles.

The Hellion Elites are warm and lack much breathability, but they're not unbearable even through the summer months. These have proven to be a pair that I reach for first, thanks to the well-rounded design and functional details.

A note: as I mentioned about the Hellions earlier, these have the furthest back cleat channel of any of the shoes on test, so far rearward that I actually moved my cleat up a bit in the channel - a first in quite a while.

Pros

+ Very protective
+ Good balance of comfort and utility

Cons

- Warm, not very breathable
- Heavy


Fox Union Boa

Union Details

• Two Boa dials
• Welded and reinforced toebox
• Ripstop upper
• D3O insert, two arch support options
• Sizing: 37-47
• Price: $275 USD
• Weight: 506g / shoe
• foxracing.com

The Unions are one of the stiffer pairs in this lineup, with a sole that gives very little even under massive loads. They feel precise, with good pedal contact and sticky enough rubber to lock in. The rigid last doesn't hike very well, but does the job when need be.

For me, the Unions have one critical flaw: the material of the upper comes way too far up the ankle, pushing into the tendon at the front of the ankle (tibialis anterior). The rubbing was at its worst on climbs, where the repetitive motion of pedaling really ground things down. After trying to break them in naturally, I resorted to a bit of user modification, opening up that corner with a pair of snips. This helped quite a bit, but didn't completely alleviate the issue.

Comfort modifications.

Rigid.

Fit-wise, the Unions are on the narrower side of things, but they don't feel too uncomfortable for me. The rubberized toe cap keeps things from opening up with use, but my feet had enough room for long rides. The dual Boa dials keep things tight and well-adjusted, and while they lack some of the dial protection that other shoes feature, I haven't had any damage occur.

Pros

+ Stiff and precise
+ Nice fit

Cons

- Annoying cuff detail
- Not great for hike-a-bike


Canyon Scampr

Scampr Details

• Boa dial
• Knit upper
• Tri-panel tension fit
• Solestar insole, Vibram sole
• Sizing: 36-48
• Price: $160 USD
• Weight: 451g / shoe
• canyon.com

Canyon first introduced these shoes to the gravel / adventure cycling market, but from the jump they struck me as a potentially ideal trail bike shoe. The defining feature here is the knit upper, which feels like a durable sock with some structure to it. The single Boa dial cinches the whole thing together, and you're off to the races - or the graveling or adventuring, whatever.

Thanks to that upper construction, the Scampr is breathable and light, with a somewhat disconcertingly airy feel. Great on hot days, but not so great when it comes to impact protection. That's entirely not the point here, so it feels like an unfair complaint, but don't expect these to protect your upper foot from much. There is a solid toe cap, and the sole offers plenty of support for real descending. These are my favorite shoe for short-travel bikes where I know I won't be pushing the limits, and for longer days in hot temps.

Extreme sports sock.

Stiff enough where it matters.

The stretchy fit of these should accommodate a wide variety of foot shapes, and felt very comfortable to me. The fit system Canyon implemented here is excellent, hugging your foot tight without any faff. Even if the Boa were to break, the shoe's cuff is sturdy enough to keep them on your foot, in a pinch. My one complaint comfort-wise comes down to the Solestar insole, which has an odd bump right in the middle of your forefoot, and never felt quite right to me - I swapped it out for a random insole that fit, and I've been happy.

Pros

+ Very comfortable and breathable
+ Excellent fit system

Cons

- Very little protection in upper
- Solestar insole


Giro Chamber III

Chamber Details

• Lace up, Velcro strap
• Padded microfiber upper
• Low-profile tongue
• DH-focused shank
• Sizing: 36-48
• Price: $200 USD
• Weight: 538g / shoe
• giro.com

The Chamber III is on par with the Fox Unions as the stiffest two pair of shoes on test here, but the Giros take things a step further with a tighter and more aggressive fit and feel. The last of these is quite narrow, and might not work with those with wider feet as a result. If that doesn't disqualify them for you, then they provide a very locked-in feel, with plenty of fit adjustment thanks to the traditional lacing system and a Velcro strap to cinch things down.

Giro reduced the heft and thickness of these quite a bit compared to the last iteration of the Chamber, and we're left with a shoe that feels nicely pared down, while still offering enough protection and durability. The protection here feels more like padding than a shell, especially when compared to shoes like the Hellion Elite - both work, but the shell method seems to withstand a bit more abuse. As you'd expect with a very stiff shoe, the hiking performance isn't amazing, but Giro did give these sticky enough rubber to keep you upright on all sorts of dumb scrambles.

Padded faux leather upper.

Real stiff, quite narrow.

I found the narrow fit required some lace adjustment here and there over long days, opening up slightly with enough time. The lacing feels a bit fiddly compared to the Boa dials featured on most of the other shoes, but you can get a very secure and stay up on your bunny ear technique. As a package, these are a pretty ideal choice for those looking to put a ton of power into the pedals, and get little to no feedback from those efforts or impacts.

Pros

+ Stiff, locked-in feel
+ Precise fit system

Cons

- Narrow fit
- Fussy to adjust


Shimano GF8 GTX

GF8 Details

• Boa dial
• Gore-Tex liner
• Tall elastic cuff
• Low-temperature sticky rubber
• Sizing: 38-48
• Price: $220 USD
• Weight: 513g / shoe
• ride.shimano.com

Shimano's GF8 is the only dedicated mountain bike flat shoe I'm including here, because it's my favorite that I've used this year. They are far from perfect, but the important things are close to my ideal. They used a softer, low-temperature sticky rubber for the sole, which hooks to pedals remarkably well, no matter how foul the weather. The extra traction is welcome, and luckily hasn't some at the expense of a sole that lasts - these still look good despite plenty of use. The stiffness also feels well-suited to my preference, with a bit of give to allow for good pedal feel, without sacrificing impact protection.

Yes, they're also waterproof, but that feels far less critical for me, despite living in a notoriously wet place. The upside is a dry foot through splashes and light rain, the downside is a permanently wet foot as soon as any water gets in past the cuff. Waterproof means it keeps just as much in as it lets out. I'm willing to live with that tradeoff, as the boot fit is nice and secure, albeit annoying to get on and off.

Weak materials and a very tight cuff.

Excellent rubber.

The biggest complaint I've had about the GF8 GTX comes down to durability - the liner material has proven to be far too light-duty for the job. Because the cuff is so tight, you have to really yank on these to get them on (or get a shoehorn), and the very thin heel area is prone to tearing as a result. I've continued to use these well past that patch giving up the ghost, but I'd like to see better design on a pair of shoes that otherwise ticks all the boxes.

Pros

+ Great pedal feel
+ Very sticky rubber, even in cold

Cons

- Poor durability
- Frustrating to put on


Vans SK8-Hi BMX

SK8-Hi Details

• Lace up
• Duracap high-wear reinforcement
• PopCush footbed
• Vulcanized rubber
• Sizing: 34.5-47
• Price: $90 USD
• Weight: 519g / shoe
• vans.com

These are just a good old pair of Vans. There's nothing special and mountain bike specific about them, they just happen to be one of my favorite shoes for the job. If protection is important to you, look elsewhere, but if grip and pedal feel are your main goals, then the classic high top is a pretty compelling option.

I picked these up to see if the BMX moniker actually improved anything in terms of durability and usefulness for the bike, and so far they've impressed. It's no mystery that Vans durability has been pretty horrible of late, but the rubber and uppers has held up on these so far. Though they lack any sort of upper foot protection, the UltraCush insoles do take the sting off any bottom-of-foot strikes.

Crankarm protection.

Classic.

I don't love the pointed-toe fit of Vans, but after a long break in they do start feeling like home. A simple lace-up fit keeps you locked in if you want it, though these are anything but stiff. The beauty of Vans is how much pedal feel you get, and how well the sole holds onto pedals. They're really not optimized for mountain biking, but that hasn't stopped them from being a great option for the right use cases.

Pros

+ All the pedal feel
+ Great grip

Cons

- Lacking protection
- Pointed fit


Final Choices

For those looking for a great all-rounder shoe, I'd recommend the following:

1. Ride Concepts Hellion Elite Boa Clip
2. Crank Brothers Mallet Trail
3. Giro Chamber III

For shorter travel bikes and lighter use, the Canyon Scampr is my top choice. For pure downhill use, the stiff and precise feel of the Giro Chamber III would be my pick, with a nod to the Fox Union despite some issues with the design of the upper.
