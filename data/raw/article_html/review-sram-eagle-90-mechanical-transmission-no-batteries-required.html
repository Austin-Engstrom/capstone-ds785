A couple of years ago the rumor that SRAM was going to fully eliminate cable-actuated derailleurs from their catalog started to spread. It seemed far-fetched at the time, but then several frames were released without any ports for derailleur housing and it all started to seem more believable. Thankfully, those rumors weren’t true, as the launch of the new Eagle 90 and Eagle 70 drivetrains confirms - both are cable-actuated, with no batteries to be seen.

The 12-speed drivetrains are based on the design of the existing wireless Transmission drivetrains, including the use of the Full Mount interface, where the derailleur clamps around both sides of the frame rather than using a traditional hanger. If a frame is universal derailleur hanger compatible it should also work with the new mechanical Transmission derailleurs.

Eagle 90 Details

• 12-speed, 10-52 tooth cassette
• Cable actuated
• Full Mount design
• Compatible with other T-Type components
• Groupset weight: 2085 grams
• Price: $670 USD (cassette, derailleur, cranks, shifter, chain)
• sram.com


The Eagle 90 drivetrain is the main focus of this article, but the Eagle 70 components are worth a mention, since those will likely be showing up on more entry-level mountain bikes in the near future. The Eagle 70 cassette has the same 10-52 tooth range as Eagle 90, but it’s designed to work with an HG driver body rather than the XD body used on the higher end options. That’s great to see, since it means riders won’t need to upgrade freehub bodies or potentially buy a new wheel to get a wider gear range.

The Eagle 90 drivetrain uses the same cassette and chain used by wireless GX Transmission; it’s the derailleur, shifter, cranks, and chainring that are new. The entire Eagle 90 groupset (including cranks) is priced at $670 USD, and weighs in at 2085 grams.



Derailleur
Price: $190 USD
395 grams

You can buy two Eagle 90 derailleurs for the price of one wireless GX Transmission derailleur and still have enough money left over for a burrito, which seems like a great reason to skip the batteries to me. The weights of the two groups are about the same too - the weight of the cable and housing balances out the weight of the battery and tiny motor on the wireless groupset.

SRAM claims that the Eagle 90 derailleur is the strongest mechanical derailleur they’ve ever produced, but if something does break it’s fully rebuildable. And by fully rebuildable I don’t just mean the pulley wheels and cages - nearly every single component can easily be replaced, and SRAM will offer different kits depending on which part needs to be swapped out. Taking the derailleur apart is quick and easy - with minimal practice I could fully disassemble and reassemble it in under 10 minutes.


Shifter
Price: $50
135 grams

The Eagle 90 shifter body's profile is slightly longer than what we’ve seen in the past, with a look and housing path that’s designed to help it blend in with SRAM’s newest brakes. The forward thumb trigger is nice and large, and provides a positive perch for shifting to a harder gear, while the larger lever can be used to shift up the cassette two gears at a time.

SRAM’s previous mechanical systems allowed for a larger jump with one push of the lever, but they also weren’t really designed for shifting under load, where with Transmission you can mashing down on the pedals as hard as you want and still shift. The cassette design also comes into play here - there’s a chance that shifting up the cassette more than two cogs at a time with one push of the lever could create less-than-smooth shifting due to the locations of the shift ramps.


Cranks
Price: $155
825 grams

The Eagle 90 cranks are made from forged aluminum and use SRAM’s 8-bolt chainring mounting pattern. There’s not a whole lot to comment on, but the steel direct mount chainring is nice to see - that should last longer than an aluminum ring at the cost of some extra weight. The cranks are offered in five lengths - 155, 160, 165, 170, and 175mm, a range that should cover riders who have hopped on the ‘shorter is better’ bandwagon, and those who prefer the more traditional lengths. There are also e-bike specific crank arms for Bosch and Brose motors that go down to 150mm in length.



Chain & Cassette
Cassette price: $250
445 grams

Chain price: $50
285 grams

Eagle 90 uses the same Flattop chain and 10-52 tooth cassette that are already used for the wireless GX Transmission. Of course, higher end T-Type chains and cassettes are also compatible with Eagle 90, for those riders who want to shave some grams and potentially improve longevity while still sticking with a cable-actuated system.



Installation

The setup steps for mechanical Transmission are quite simple, and follow a four word order of operation: prepare, hang, tighten, route.

Prepare involves finding the correct chain length and setup position for the derailleur based on SRAM’s online calculator.

Hang is just like you’d expect - the derailleur is mounted to the frame, and then it’s put into the correct position to continue that setup. On the wireless version, that position is achieved by clicking the shifter to move the derailleur, but it’s a little different with the cable-actuated version, since at this point the cable hasn’t been installed. In order to move the derailleur into the right position either a 5 or 6mm allen key is slotted into a hole on the underside of the parallelogram. The size of the allen key determines which cog on the cassette the derailleur will line up with.

After that, it’s simply a matter of tightening it down and then installing the cable like you would with a typical derailleur, except that there’s no B-tension or limit screws to worry about. It’s a fairly simple procedure, and SRAM has videos and instruction manuals that clearly illustrate the required steps.



On the Trail

Solid and stable are the two words that best describe the Eagle 90’s shifting - each click results in a defined ‘ca-chunk’ as the chain moves up or down the cassette, and other than some small cable tension adjustments (done with the barrel adjuster on the shifter) on the first two rides I haven’t had to touch anything.

The speed of those shifts is quick, but it’s nothing earth-shattering - I’d still give Shimano the edge when it comes to how fast the chain can go from one end of the cassette to the other. Some of that comes down to the shifter design - the Eagle 90 shifter can move the chain two cogs up the cassette with one lever push, and down the cassette one cog at a time, while Shimano’s shifters allow the chain to go up four cogs at a time, and then down two with one lever or paddle push.

SRAM seems to be placing the priority on consistency rather than outright speed, which makes sense if you’re advertising a drivetrain’s ability to shift under heavy loads, but I still feel like there’s a way to make a super fast and stable drivetrain - maybe someday we'll see an Eagle 100 option added to the catalog. The cassette's shift ramp design is also part of the overall shift speed equation - those ramps help keep each shift precise and well defined, but they also have an affect on just how fast the chain can move from one cog to another.

As far as the ergonomics of the shifter itself, the thumb lever and trigger are easy to reach and intuitively positioned, although I think there's room for improvement on the shape of the lever. I was spoiled over the summer when I tested Vivo's F3 shifter - that super expensive option and its array of thumb paddle options made me realize how much potential there is when it comes to the shaping of shift paddles.

There are dimples to help with traction on the Eagle 90's sweep lever, but those don't match the textured rubber pad that Shimano uses on their XT shifters, or even the ridges on the SLX shifters. Eagle 90 feels a little more basic than XT - granted, it's also less expensive, but it'd be nice to have the option to select a higher end shifter and stick with the same derailleur. Historically, that's been a great way to improve the feel of a drivetrain without needing to spend a ton of money upgrading. Unfortunately, the cable pull ratio of the Eagle 90 derailleur isn't the same as SRAM's previous drivetrains, so it's not as easy as digging a nice older X0 shifter out of the parts bin and returning it to service.

I’ve been very pleased with the strength of the derailleur’s clutch - it’s smooth while also providing enough resistance to keep chainslap to a minimum. Compared to the wireless Transmission derailleurs, the Eagle 90 seems even quieter - so far I’ve been enjoying the sounds of silence as far as chain related noises go.

The Eagle 90 drivetrain has been on my Raaw Madonna for a little over a month, which is enough time to discuss the shifting performance, but still too soon to go over long term durability. It’s held up very well so far, but I’ll report back with an update and more comparisons after it’s been dragged through more mud and smacked into some stumps.




Pros
+ Solid, stable shifting
+ Easily rebuildable derailleur
+ Strong clutch mechanism (so far)

Cons
- Not the absolute fastest shifting performance
- Shifter ergonomics are okay, but not outstanding.






Pinkbike's Take

I'm glad SRAM didn't fully drop cables from their lineup, especially since the new Eagle 90 drivetrain is a shaping up to be a solid, workhorse drivetrain, one that you can install and pretty much forget about for miles and miles. I would like to see a higher end shifter added into the mix in the future, one with a little more texture on the paddles and maybe a lighter action, but as the beginning of what's hopefully a return to a full line of battery-free drivetrains, Eagle 90 is a solid step. — Mike Kazimer
