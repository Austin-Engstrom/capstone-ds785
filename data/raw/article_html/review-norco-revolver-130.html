Review: The 2026 Norco Revolver 130 is Ready for Modern XC Riding

No, the new Norco Revolver doesn’t have 32” wheels - we’ll have to see what the next version looks like a few years down the line. What it does have is fully updated geometry, a lightweight carbon frame, and two different versions to choose from - the Revolver 130 or the Revolver 120. The two options share the same frame, but, not surprisingly, the 130 comes with a 130mm fork and has 120mm of travel, while the 120 has a 120mm fork and 115mm travel.

The 120 is aimed more at riding between the race tape, while the Revolver 130 is aimed at a broader swathe of riding conditions and styles. That’s why you’ll find a Maxxis Forekaster tire up front, a Fox 34 SL fork, and comfortable wheels that aren’t the absolute lightest, parts that help make it more surefooted on technical climbs (and descents) compared to its even shorter travel sibling. There’s also no lockout on this model, although the frame does have that as a possibility.

Frame Details

Norco absolutely nailed it when it comes to the overall look of the Revolver’s carbon frame. The lines are sharp and clean, free of any strange humps or lumps. It’s one of those bikes that looks fast standing still, which is exactly what you want from an XC speed machine.

Claimed weight for an S3 frame and a RockShox SIDLuxe shock is 1900 grams, which is right in the mix for a modern XC bike. For comparison, the Specialized Epic 8 has a claimed weight of 1795 grams for the S-Works version, and 1965 for the standard layup. It’s a similar story with the Orbea Oiz - the OMX frame is 1740 grams, and the OMR is 1940 grams.

There’s room for two water bottle inside the front triangle, although there isn’t any in-frame storage to be seen. That’s not too surprising, since it’s not found on any of Norco’s other models, and it’s also not as common in the XC world. The Specialized Epic has it, but most companies have decided to save a few grams by skipping the addition of gummy bear terrarium.

Other frame details include two ISCG tabs for running a little chain device if desired, a threaded bottom bracket, and titanium suspension hardware.







Geometry

There are five sizes of the Revolver, with reach numbers ranging from 422mm all the way up to 522mm. The head angle sits at 66.5 degrees with a 120mm fork, and 66-degrees with a 130mm fork; that’s around .5-degrees steeper than a Specialized Epic with the same fork length, and the same head tube angle as a Yeti ASR.

Chainstay lengths change by 4mm per size in order to help preserve the balance of the bike, at least partially, starting at 422mm and going to 438mm. The seat angle sits at 75-degrees, which is .5-degrees slacker than the Revolver 120 due to the additional fork length.





Suspension Design

Flexstay suspension layouts have become the norm in the XC world - it’s a good way to save some weight, and there are still ways to tune the design to give a bike different ride characteristics. In Norco’s case, they preload the seatstays slightly, which makes it easier to get into the first 30% of suspension travel, since the shock isn’t fighting against as much friction / resistance.

Additionally, Norco says that the leverage curve is designed to work best with the smaller volume air can of an XC shock, with enough ramp up later in the stroke to keep it from using all of its travel too often.

Test Bike Setup

We’ve said it dozens of times over the years, but it continues to be true - Norco’s Ride Aligned suspension setup app is one of the best around. Plug in your weight and height and it’ll spit out numbers that more often than not are right on the money.

I ran a 40mm rise bar for a portion of the test period, and that’s what I’d run if this was my personal bike due to the more upright riding position. The 50mm stem and 760mm, 20mm rise bars are totally appropriate, and I put in a bunch of miles with the bike in its fully stock configuration, I just happen to prefer a little less stretched out riding position.

I also ran a 210mm Fox Transfer dropper post for a good chunk of the test period. In this case too, the 170mm post that comes stock on the S3 isn’t out of the ordinary, and for racing I’d stick with the shorter post. For more all-around trail riding, and getting into some not-quite-XC terrain, the extra drop of the 210mm post was a benefit. The nice thing about the frame design is that there’s plenty of insertion room thanks to the uninterrupted seat tube.

Testing Info

Testing began in Whistler, BC, and continued in Bellingham, Washington, with conditions ranging from dry and dusty all the way to slightly frozen and a lot sloppy as winter set in.



Climbing

Modern XC bikes are converging on very similar frame shapes and suspension layouts, which isn’t exactly surprising - there are only so many ways to accomplish the goal of making a very light frame that also holds a couple water bottles.

Despite all the visual similarities, there are distinct differences in the way the rear suspension doles out its travel. On one side of the spectrum you have bikes like the Orbea Oiz, which is very firm initially, even in the fully open setting, prioritizing snappy acceleration over just about everything else. On the other end you have a bike like the Yeti ASR, which leans heavily on its lockout lever for climbing support, and then delivers tons of traction when the shock is fully open.

The Revolver falls in the middle of those two possibilities, with what I’d call the Goldilocks position of being ‘just right’ for most instances. That meant I could leave the Float SL shock in the middle of the three compression settings and not give it a second thought for the entire ride. That setting gives the bike plenty of support for out-of-the-saddle sprinting, while still remaining comfortable for bigger hits and choppy sections of trail.


At 5’11” I went with the size S3 Revolver, which has numbers that are pretty close to a size large from most brands. The 468mm reach, 66-degree head angle, and 75-degree seat tube angle are all pretty similar to those of the Specialized Chisel I’ve put a ton of miles in over the past two seasons, so choosing the S3 rather than the S4 made the most sense, and I know the 493mm reach of the S4 and the longer top tube would feel too stretched out for me.

I wouldn’t have minded if the seat tube angle was just a touch steeper (the 130mm fork slackens it even further compared to the Revolver 120), but with the stock 50mm stem and the seat slid forward a bit on the rails I didn’t feel overly stretched out.

The other geometry request would be for slightly longer chainstays. It’s great to see size-specific chainstays (something Norco has been doing longer than most), but there were a few instances when the front end felt a little light during steep, seated climbs, and I had to either stand up or make a conscious effort to really get my weight forward. The S3 Revolver’s 430mm chainstays are on the shorter side of things; for comparison, the Yeti ASR has 439mm chainstays, and the Cannondale Scalpel has 442mm chainstays.

Now, I should stress that these are fairly small geometry gripes - at the end of the day the Norco is a good, quick climber, and it’s in line with many modern XC bikes.



Descending

My first ride on the Revolver 130 took place on some not-exactly-XC trails in Whistler, which was a great introduction to its capabilities - the brakes are strong enough and the suspension is dialed enough that it can hold its own on spicier sections of trail without making you question your bike (or trail) choice. Keep in mind, it’s still very much an XC(ish) bike - sticking a 130mm fork on a race machine doesn’t magically transform it into an enduro bike built for the steepest trails around.

That said, the Revolver’s 120mm of travel is very well managed. Like I mentioned in the climbing section, I kept it in the middle compression setting for most rides, since that gave it enough support for generating speed on flatter sections of trail without feeling harsh. On the instances that I did flip it to the open setting the difference was noticeable, although it’s not a night and day change - it’s just a little softer off the top, and a little easier to initiate travel.

The short chainstays do make navigating tight corners a very enjoyable experience - the Revolver eats up those back-to-back turns, snapping from the exit of one to the entrance of another with a satisfying quickness.

At higher speeds and on longer, swoopier corners there were a few occasions when the Revolver’s back end felt a little chattery - the stiffer frame and short rear center means that it doesn’t carve through corners the same way as the Yeti ASR, for example. The sweet spot feels slightly harder to find; by that I mean that the Revolver isn’t quite as forgiving if you get your weight a little too far forward or back. Longer chainstays aren’t the answer to all the world’s problems the way some riders seem to believe, but in this case I do think a slightly longer back end would help on the climbs and the descents without taking away any overall speed.

How Does It Compare?

I get to ride a huge variety of bikes over the course of a year (that’s definitely the best part of my job), but I try to always have a short travel option on hand. They’re great palate cleansers, a way to pedal away the sins of riding a full-power e-bike, or to feel like you’re getting places way, way faster than you would on a 40-pound enduro sled.

Compared to my personal Specialized Chisel, the Revolver is a couple pounds lighter thanks to its carbon frame, and it has a slightly snappier feel under power. It also has 10mm more rear travel, travel that’s easier to access than the Chisel’s, which makes it a bit more forgiving in rougher terrain. The geometry numbers are pretty similar between the two, but I prefer the Chisel’s higher stack and slightly longer chainstays.

How about compared to the Yeti ASR? Realistically, it's the Revolver 120 that would be the more direct comparison, since it too has 115mm of rear travel and a 120mm fork, but I was really impressed with how usable the ASR’s 115mm of travel was. The ASR is a very comfortable bike in the fully open setting, although it is a bike that benefits from the use of a lockout in order to gain climbing efficiency. Ready for some more made-up categories? I’d call the ASR a comfortable-country bike, while the Revolver has a stiffer, more robust feel that pushes it more towards the light trail bike side of things.

And finally, the Transition Spur. That bike has had an excellent run - it’s crazy that it’s been over 5 years since its debut, and it still holds its own - for many riders it’s what they think of when the term “downcountry” gets mentioned. It has a longer reach, slacker head angle, and steeper seat tube angle than the Revolver, and while none of the differences are drastic, they do add up to give each bike its own flavor. The Revolver has a racier feel - it’s a little quicker out of the gate, and its frame is around a pound lighter than the Spur’s, while also being noticeably stiffer. For the rider that has more than a couple XC races on their calendar, the Revolver would be the better choice, but the Spur is still a very fun, very capable short travel XC/DC machine.



Which Model is the Best Value?

Norco has done a good job with the parts spec of the Revolver, and even $3,999 C3 model doesn’t make too many concessions, with a Shimano Deore cable-actuated drivetrain, Deore two-piston brakes, a RockShox Reba fork with a Motion Control damper, and a SIDLuxe Select+ shock. The one downside with that model is the weight - it's 30 pounds, which is a whole 3 pounds heavier than the C2 model.

That $5,399 C2 model is the one I’d gravitate towards if I was shopping with my own money. It has a Shimano XT Di2 wireless derailleur and shifter paired with a Deore crankset and cassette, Deore 4-piston brakes, and a Fox 34SL with a Grip damper. The claimed weight is a reasonable 27 pounds, a number that would be easy to reduce by upgrading to a lighter cassette, cranks, or wheels when the time comes. Out of the box, though, there’s really nothing to change right away; I’d take a good look at this one if you’re trying to find the best blend of price vs performance.

Technical Report

Zipp HiTop S wheels: At 1535 grams, Zipp’s HiTop S carbon wheels certainly aren’t the lightest option out there; this would be an easy area to save a couple hundred grams, at least if you’re willing to cough up the cash required to do so. On the trail, the HiTop S wheels fall on the softer side of the spectrum by design, although they feel much more “normal” compared to the extra-compliant 3Zero Moto trail / enduro wheels.

Other than the occasional twang from the spokes when the wheels were really loaded up the Moto S wheels faded nicely into the background - they stayed true, and I didn’t have any flats while running them.

Frame Rattle: Thankfully the Revolver doesn’t have thru-headset cable routing, something that’s still all-too common on XC bikes, but there’s still room for improvement. The dropper and brake lines run through hard plastic sleeves, and those sleeves can rattle against the inside of the down tube. I was able to quiet down the racket by taking the fork off and shoving a big piece of foam just behind the head tube junction, but I’d rather not spend my time pulling apart a fancy new bike while hunting for silence.

The more typical foam tubing seems like it would have been a simpler solution, or maybe an updated version of the down tube ports that the older Optic had, where zip ties could be used to snug the housing down against the inside of the downtube.

Shimano XT Di2: I really like the speed of Shimano’s new Di2 drivetrains, along with the shifter ergonomics and the excellent battery life. The chainslap is really the only downside - on bigger compressions the cage returns to its starting position with a loud “thwack.” It was a little less pronounced on this bike, perhaps due to the shorter travel, but it’s quite noticeable in certain situations.

Fox 34 SL: The 34 SL worked well for the entire test period, free of any strange squelches, creaks, or squeaks. I didn’t need to do much adjusting to it either, thanks to Norco’s Ride Aligned settings - those were pretty much identical to my final setup. I could see some riders contemplating going with a 36 SL, which is a great option, although that 265 gram weight penalty is something to keep in mind. It’d make sense if you were trying to make the Revolver more of a short travel trail machine, but stick with the 34 if you don’t want to deviate too far from the XC realm. The 34 SL is still stiff enough for the vast majority of trails where a bike like this makes sense.

+ Quick and responsive handling
+ Very well balanced suspension feel
+ Parts spec helps it shine on more technical trails without losing its XC nature.

Pinkbike's Take

The Revolver ticks almost all the boxes when it comes to how a modern cross-country bike should perform. Norco did a great job with the overall suspension feel, creating a bike with a very good blend of efficiency and grip. There’s some room for improvement with the in-frame housing design, and I wouldn’t say no to slightly longer chainstays, but those are fairly small gripes - on the whole, the Revolver 130 hits the mark, a cross-country bike that can still hold its own on more technical trails.— Mike Kazimer

 @mikekazimer: Steel has virtually infinite fatigue life. Find automotive shock coil spring from the 80s on a car that has seen >500k miles and it will still act like a spring.

The carbon itself also has a long fatigue life, but the plastic that holds carbon together doesn't. Thats why you can get delaminations and voids in the composite that weaken the structure. Bike industry by far does not use the same standard, which is why carbon frame failure is seen as a common thing, chalked up to "manufacturing defects"
