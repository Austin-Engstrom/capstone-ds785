The Good, The Bad & The Ugly: Pinkbike Tech Editors' Preferred Handlebars

The DMR O'Dub Bar in 31.8 mm and a 50 mm rise is the most compliant handlebar we have tested to date.

I've had a bit of an obsession with handlebar design over the last few years, investigating claims of vibration damping and compliance, and trying to tease apart any relationship between the two.

The vast majority of the bars we tested offer a typical 8° backsweep and 5° upsweep, with very few deviating from those numbers. This makes good sense, given that most trail and enduro bikes come with a bar of those dimensions. It's funny then that the handlebar I now run is a dramatic departure from what is considered standard fare. I have my reasons, spelled out below.

Despite key geometry features changing over the last decade (reach has grown and effective top tube length has shortened), handlebar geometry geometry hasn't changed much - at least in terms of sweep.

Should it have?

Probably not. I'm not sure there's justification for any changes on trail and enduro bikes, given that we spec cockpits with downhill handling at the top of the list of priorities. Bar rise is increasing, though. Many riders are appreciating the benefits of a higher stack, and what's more, high-rise bars tend to be more compliant than flatter ones.

In the end, it comes down to personal preference. Here's what us tech editors are choosing to ride just now.



My contribution to this piece ticks all of the boxes. It's good (in a small way), bad in most ways and, incredibly ugly.

Geoff is a 31.8 mm aluminum piece from budget brand Planet-X; he boasts a 45-degree backsweep, a plethora of hand positions, and a generous amount of real estate for accoutrements - ultra endurance bike-packers, rejoice. Alas, I cannot be accurately described as such.

Jessie-May Morgan
Position: Tech Editor, Keeper of Small Zoo
Height: 5'4" / 162.6 cm
Wingspan: 164 cm
Current Handlebar: Geoff
Clamp diameter: 31.8 mm
Material: 6061 aluminum
Width: 720 mm
Rise: 13 mm
Backsweep: 45-degrees

My reason for running this de-reacher handlebar on a trail bike arises out of misfortune. I have had a chronic shoulder issue for almost two years, resulting from a partial thickness rotator cuff tear. The following months and years have seen my shoulder reposition itself to a more internally rotated position. From this position, it is sore to move in a few directions.


I am in the process of retraining it to sit more rearward, in a more externally rotated position, from which point it feels strong and, importantly, pain-free.

My theory is this: by positioning my hands splayed out, my elbows naturally tuck inwards, and my shoulders naturally rotate outwards. It is solving a very niche problem while being horrible in most other ways, including:

• There is no good position for the brake levers
• Handling is terrible
• It's heavy

The silver linings? It's immeasurably better than not riding at all, and it is yours for just £29.99.


Like the Jones bars it mimics - which are considerably pricier, and also available in carbon and titanium, if you care - Geoff was made with the intention of offering improved comfort to those who are riding very long distances - like, Silk Road Race distances. The logic is that the severe backsweep allows the angle at the wrist to be straighter, which is meant to be more comfortable. That works well for some people, and not for others - I think I fall into the second camp, as my wrists start to complain after only 5 km. That said, they are conditioned for a mere 8-degrees of backsweep, which is typical for trail and enduro bikes.

For technical trail riding, they are not - not that I've done much of that lately. Steering is wildly sluggish, the only benefit of which is that it really forces you to be more mindful about anticipating the trail ahead, and accentuates that feeling of needing to direct the bike through the hips. It's quite the education.

As my shoulder rehab progresses, I'll be looking to switch to something a little more handling-friendly, and a little less jarring to look at - 16-degree backsweep, for example.



I've been experimenting with higher-rise bars recently. In my new position, I’ve had the pleasure of being able to test multiple bikes, which has been an absolute joy, but one thing became very apparent: cockpit matters a lot. I’d almost say cockpit setup can create one of the most distinctive ride characteristic changes of anything on the bike. Try hopping on a friend’s bike for part of a lap, and you’ll see. I personally can really feel when the sweep, brake lever position, and rise aren’t what I’m used to.

Stephane Pelletier

Position: Content Manager
Height: 6'3" / 191 cm
Current Handlebar: Deity Highside
Clamp diameter: 35 mm
Material: 6061 aluminum
Width: 780 mm
Rise: 50 mm
Backsweep: 9-degrees
Upsweep: 5-degree

As such, I’ve got a few sets of these Deity Highside bars kicking around that I typically swap from bike to bike, especially if I’m not getting along with the stock bars too much. They’re marketed as a dirt jumping and slopestyle handlebar, but I’ve found them to work just fine across other disciplines, too.

So here it is: 50mm rise, 35mm bar clamp diameter, and cut down to 780mm. I’ve also played around with the same bar in a 31.8mm equivalent, and I may actually prefer the slight compliance gains from the narrower diameter. Other than the higher-than-normal rise, it’s a pretty standard shape most riders would be familiar with, although it does feature slightly more backsweep at 9 degrees, alongside the typical 5-degree upsweep.

Readers who follow some of my ramblings will know I typically like to size down on bikes, or at least lean toward the smaller end of my frame size spectrum. This stems from my dirt jump background and a general penchant for fun over outright speed. I like a bike to be a bit more manoeuvrable and playful, rather than soled focused on all-out stability and speed. One unfortunate byproduct of this strategy is that smaller bikes can sometimes feel cramped, and they also typically don’t come specced with especially tall bars.



Going with the higher rise, although slightly reducing effective reach, does give me a higher stack and longer distance between my feet and hands. For me, it puts me in a really comfortable and confident riding position. I’ve swapped to these trusty Highsides on a few bikes and felt a noticeable improvement in confidence and stability almost immediately.

Other notable setup details include that I typically have the bars rolled slightly forward, partly to keep the reach as long as possible and partly to line up the flat part of the bar parallel to the ground. I’m not sure what angle I run my brake levers at; I’ve never measured, but I basically line up my arms in a neutral riding position and set them up so I have minimal bend at the wrist. I’d say around 45 degrees if I had to guess.

Also worth noting, I’ve still been running the funny little silicone brake lever covers on this particular ride. I picked them up at Sea Otter, and, partly because I didn't want to take them off and partly because they actually feel quite good, they’ve stayed on. Although I do wish they had given me a black set instead. You can learn more about those in my recent Check Out article.





I've gone all over the map when it comes to handlebar options, trying a wide array of sweep, rise, and width options before settling on what feels like home for me now. Both of my favorites have one distinct feature to them: as little backsweep as possible. I like the handling feel, the ergonomics, and the ease of finding a good roll that this facilitates.

Aluminum is a perk, the shot-peened finish keeps controls and grips in place without too much torque, and the 31.8mm bore wins core points with bar-heads everywhere.

Renthal is the tried and true when it comes to combining all of these factors into one consistent product, but Pembree is a welcomed newcomer hitting all the same targets. For my generally preferred rise of 30-40mm there are a few options between both brands, and I'd call the feel quite similar between the two.

It's hard to capture bar roll in a way that portrays the feel, but I aim to have the face plane of the handlebar in parallel with the head angle of the bike.

Relative to the norm, my bars tend to be rolled back a little bit.

I've run 770mm width for as long as I can remember, and would rather run narrower than wider if I had to settle elsewhere. If ever I ride a set of 800mm bars, I'm surprised at how slow and awkward the handling can feel. Like anything, you adapt pretty quickly, but I'm happy sticking with my tried and true setup.

With a 40-45mm stem, the flatter profile of these bars seems to hit a sweet spot for the handling characteristic I want out of most bikes. More backsweep is something I can adjust to, but if I want a bike to feel like home quickly, popping a set of Renthals on is a quick way to settle in.

Bikepacking side note: I've tried a few alt bars for extremely long days / months in the saddle, and I think I'm still happy with normal sweep figures even for that use case. I rode a set of Jones bars across the country, and my wrists were unhappy the whole time. I ran some SQ Labs 12° bars on the Colorado Trail, and found them comfortable and normal enough handling-wise. At this point, a set of the bars you see here with a set of inner-barends provides enough variety in hand position to keep the hands and wrists happy. Maybe my body has just morphed to fit these shapes over time, but I find them perfectly ergonomic.






Handlebars, like all touch points on the bike, are a personal preference. Heck, I even find that they can vary from “perfect” to “just okay” depending on the bike. Bar roll also plays a massive, well, role in how the bike handles.

On enduro bikes, I tend to like higher-rise bars with less backsweep. Usually, I roll them to a neutral (grips appear level with the ground) or a slight positive position. It likely has to do with the interactions between the head tube angle and fork offset.

Higher average speeds on downhill bikes equate to smaller bar movements. In this case, I might prefer a bar with more sweep or a mild negative roll, likely due to the longer crown offsets specified on those bikes, and more subtle steering inputs needed to turn a downhill bike.

Some of my favorites over the last few seasons have been the OneUp V2 Carbon and Race Face Era handlebars. Both bars are made from carbon, which doesn’t inherently make them stiff. In fact, Jessie-May Morgan’s article on handlebar compliance, these two options ranked in the upper half of the sample pool, in terms of measured deflection.


The cool thing about each one of these bars is that both OneUp and Race Face change the stiffness, depending on the chosen width. OneUp has two stock width options (760 and 800mm) while Race Face has three (760, 780, and 800mm). I trim both the 780mm Eras and the 800mm OneUp’s down to 770mm for the Nomad. On some shorter travel bikes, I might go as narrow as 760mm, depending on the fit of the bike.

Each bar is designed around a 5-degree upsweep and 8-degree backsweep, which I find to be the most comfortable and controlled for my riding technique. Height-wise, I’ve been rocking the 35mm rise from OneUp and the 40mm rise from Race Face on my Nomad.


On the downhill bike, I’ve been enjoying the Burgtec Ride Wide Enduro 30mm rise bar, which has a 31.8mm bar clamp. They’re on the softer side, but I can’t say it’s ever put me off kilter or caused any concerns.

My go-to DH width is 780mm, but again, that can change plus or minus 5mm based on the overall fit of the bike. The Ride Wides have a 9-degree backsweep, which balances out 52mm fork offsets well for me. When a 48mm offset is spec'd, I find that an 8-degree backsweep reacts best.




I wouldn’t consider myself overly picky when it comes to handlebars. The right amount of picky, sure. Carbon or aluminum is fine with me, and the same goes with 35mm vs 31.8mm diameters as long as they're not bone-jarringly stiff. I ran 780mm width bars for years, but after a couple of Field Tests where the other test riders were running 770mm I decided to switch, purely to make swapping bikes between easier during those group tests.

For me, 770mm works well for most bikes, and then I’ll go down to 760mm for XCish bikes. Side note: I'm a big fan of higher rise bars and shorter stems on short travel bikes - it's an easy way to turn a serious XC race machine into something that encourages goofing off.

I have gone higher with my typical rise over the last few years, and nowadays I’d pick something around 40mm if I had to choose. That’s the rise amount I’m running even on bikes with a relatively tall stack like my Raaw Madonna - I just run fewer spacers than usual under the stem to keep the front end from feeling too high.


On the Raaw I’m currently running a BikeYoke BarMate with a 42mm rise, 9° backsweep, and 6° upsweep. BikeYoke recently decided to take a slightly different route when it comes to handlebar measurements, which is worth a read (here). It's an interesting concept, which entails measuring "stack" rather than "rise." Stack is measured differently from the way that most brands measure rise - it’s from the center of the bar to the center of the out edge of the bar, as opposed to rise, which usually gets measured from the center of the bar to where the flatter part of the bar begins.

I do think it makes things more confusing for the end consumer. Take the bars I’m running - they have a stack of 65mm, which makes people think that they’re extra-high-rise bars, at least when browsing through pages of options online, when they’re not - they have a more typical 42mm of rise. I like BikeYoke’s concept, but it’s going to be tricky to convince other manufacturers and riders to adopt it as the default measuring method.

A 42mm Burgtec Enduro stem holds the Barmate Carbon bars in place. That pattern in the center of the bar is a titanium mesh that BikeYoke says helps distribute the clamping forces evenly.
Side profile. Not a French-flat brake lever angle, but not pointing directly at the ground either.

As for the bars themselves, they’re nice and comfy, and devoid of any flashy logos, which I like. I run them close to vertical or maybe slightly rolled back, but not by much. Other bars that I’ve been getting along with recently include Race Face’s 40mm rise Era carbon bars, and PNW Components Loam Carbon bar in a 38mm rise.
