FlexStab Review: A Handlebar With Ball-Jointed, Floating Grips

This isn't the first handlebar to promise reduced fatigue and hand pain, but it goes about it in an entirely new way. The central part of the FlexStab Bar (terrible name, I know) is nothing out of the ordinary - a round aluminium tube. It's what's happening underneath the grips that sets it apart.

The end portions of the bar underneath the grips are separate from the central part, and there's a ball joint between the two pieces about halfway along these end pieces. Elastomers allow the two parts to move independently of one another by a few degrees in any direction, with some (adjustable) resistance.

The idea is to allow your hands a degree of float, somewhat like the cleat mechanisms of many clipless pedals. According to the team behind it, which includes a Yeti enduro team physio, this makes it more comfortable to hold on during rough terrain and puts less strain on your hands, wrists, and even shoulders.
Details

• Elastomers allow "float" at the end of the bar in all directions
• Two stiffness settings
• Available in 780 mm by 45 mm rise (35 or 31.8 mm clamp) or 750 mm by 0 mm rise (31.8 mm clamp only)
• Can be cut down by up to 20 mm
• Weight: 444 g
• Price: € 250
• Availability expected in May 2026
• leveloffuture.com


Design

To be clear, it's not that the bar is particularly flexible in the vertical direction, like most handlebars with compliance claims aim to do; it's that the angle of the rider's wrists can move and twist a little, independently of the handlebar. Because the pivot point is right underneath the center of your hands, it doesn't create any more vertical separation (compliance) between the grips and the stem than any other bar. So rider input and steering precision are unaffected.

More like this...
... than this.

It's more like the Revolution Suspension Grips than the Fasst Flexx MTB handlebars. But unlike those grips, the FlexStab Bar allows the grips to float in all directions, not just about the handlebar axis like a motorcycle throttle - although they can do that too.

The bolt and washer hold the outer part and elastomers against the inner part of the bar. The smaller washer gives less resistance against the elastomer.

The stiffness of the elastomers can be adjusted by swapping out the washers that hold them in place inside the handlebars. There are two washers included - the smaller one gives noticeably less resistance to movement.


Safety


In previous articles about this bar, many commenters were concerned that the ends of the handlebars could fail or come off in a crash, potentially exposing the pointier inner part of the bar. The fact that the product name contains the word "stab" probably doesn't help allay these fears.

The outer parts of the handlebars are held onto the inner part with a 4 mm hex bolt. If this came loose, it would be possible for the grip and end portion to pull off. However, after riding these bars for four months, both bolts remained tight.

I reached out to Level Of Future about these comments, and they had this to say. "Regarding concerns about safety, I’d like to emphasise that our handlebar has passed the most rigorous EFBE durability test, the so-called Tri-Test. I regularly share this in our posts to dispel any doubts."

Level Of Future's product line in development: 750 mm width / 0 mm rise; 780 mm width / 45 mm rise; and a prototype moto bar.

Installation

It's important to get the pivot point the right distance from the end of the bar, so it sits underneath the middle of your palm. That means you can't go chopping these bars down too much.

We initially thought the bars would be custom-made for your preferred width and rise (as my 770 mm test sample was). However, Level Of Future now plans to offer bars in two sizes: 780 mm width and 45 mm rise or 750 mm width and 0 mm rise. They also say these can be cut down by up to 20 mm (10 mm per side) without affecting the function too much. So essentially, 730-750 mm or 760 - 780 mm are the options.

Mind the gap.

There is a gap between the inner and outer parts, 140 - 145 mm from the bar end (assuming you haven't cut them down). It's important to check that the controls you want to use don't clamp across that gap, or the mechanism won't work.


With my brake lever position, a SRAM pod clamp just fits, but a Matchmaker would be neater.

The support strut on modern Shimano levers may get in the way of the gap.

Personally, I find that SRAM Maven brakes clear that gap with room to spare, although those with smaller hands may have issues, and you may not be able to fit other controls (shifters, e-bike remotes etc.) between the grip and the lever clamp. Modern Shimano levers with short lever blades and the extra support arm near the grip probably won't work.

Price & weight

The Bar is slated to cost 250 euros when it goes on sale in May. The weight of my 770 mm wide, 40 mm rise, 35 mm clamp sample version is 444 g. Compared to an aluminium gravity bar with the same proportions (e.g. Renthal Fatbar alloy), that's about a 140 gram weight penalty. Compared to something emphasising compliance like the OneUp Carbon bar (35 mm rise), there's a 220-gram difference.

The OneUp carbon bar is €80 less expensive.


Performance

This is one of those products that fades into the background - and not necessarily in a bad way. The float is noticeable when deliberately twisting the grip, but it's surprisingly normal and easy to get used to while focused on riding. I fitted these to my main bike in October and soon forgot about them. I had no complaints, but without comparing it directly to a regular handlebar, it was hard to say if the comfort claims are real.

To find that out, you need a back-to-back test. I chose to compare against an aluminium handlebar from Race Face with identical width and rise, similar upsweep & backsweep, and conventional compliance claims Race Face call their "Goldilocks Tune".

I used a fast, hardpack, bike-park style trail called Flat White in Innerleithen for the test. It's not the roughest trail in the world, but it's littered with braking bumps, fast turns, and high-load berms. I did two runs on the Race Face bar to get used to it before swapping back to the Level Of Future, measuring my control positions to make sure they were in the same place.

The result?

Subjectively, my hands felt more relaxed with the Level Of Future bar. It's subtle, but it feels like I don't need to grip the bar as tightly, and I had less fatigue at the end of the run. As mentioned, it's not a game-changing difference, but I'd say it's more noticeable in terms of comfort and fatigue than the CounterSycle Tuned Mass Damper we tested recently.

It's not like having a softer tire or suppler fork - it's more like going from cheap plastic grips to comfy tacky ones. When things get hectic, my hands are more able to move as they want, and in particular, under harsh impacts the outside of my hands tend to push away slightly. It's an extra degree of freedom that seems to make it slightly less taxing to hold on.

And while it is a little heavier and pricier than a conventional bar, it doesn't throw up any discernible downsides or handling quirks while riding.


Pros
+ Subtle but noticeable improvement in hand/wrist fatigue in back-to-back tests
+ No obvoius downsides or strange sensations on the trail

Cons
- Around 140 to 220 grams heavier than comparable alloy or carbon bars, respectively
- Expensive, limited width options
- May not work with all control clamp positions, especially newer Shimano brakes




Pinkbike's Take

If you suffer with sore hands, wrists or arms when riding rough terrain and want to leave no stone unturned in pursuit of a more comfortable ride, these are worth a shot. They won't change your world, but they offer a small but noticeable reduction in fatigue, which I interpret as having more relaxed hands. If you can live with the weight, cost, width and control fitment constraints, there is no obvious downside on the trail.

— Seb Stott

I have never changed bars because of a concern about failure, but I am a chronic bar swapper, trying to find a perfect fit.

I'm also a mechanical engineer, and do a lot of work with aluminum fatigue. I can't guess about how aluminum handlebars are engineered, but aluminum does not have a fatigue limit, so it will eventually fail under very low cyclic loads (e.g. normal riding). That said - I would guess anyone designing bars is running FEA on them and has a life criteria (number of stress cycles to failure). Any reputable bars are also going to pass ISO 4210-5, which is the ISO spec for "bicycle steering equipment." Part of that standard is a fatigue life test on a bar, which is usually to 10 million stress cycles (unloaded -> loaded -> unloaded under the force generated by a "severe user").

Short story: yes, aluminum bars will eventually fail, but it will probably take a long time unless you are riding pro-level DH all day every day. If you want to be safe, replace them once a decade; 3 years seems very short given that there are a lot of older bars out there and they do not fail if you look at them wrong. If you damage the bar in a crash (visible gouges, which are stress risers and will decrease the fatigue life in that spot) or get hit by a car (forces much much higher than the bar is designed for) then I would replace them.

bmcghie (Mar 5, 2026 at 21:05)
 @pmhobson: the term is better described as "endurance". Steel can be said to have infinite life against a bending load, or cyclic compressive loading, provided the stress stays below the fatigue (endurance) limit. If a steel bar could take 800 MPa when new, maybe after 100000 cycles of a certain load, it will fail at only 150 MPa. But if you do another 1M cycles... It still fails at nearly 150 MPa. The endurance strength of the material is said to be 150 MPa in that case.

Aluminum doesn't do this. It just keeps getting weaker as you apply the same load over and over again. Why? Mainly to with the crystal structure of the alloys, and how microscopic crack propagation progresses. Steels will round off and blunt a crack, if the stress is low enough and the crack is small enough. Aluminum alloys tend to be unable to do this to the same extent.

Look up S-N curves for various materials for more exact rates and loads.
