Review: POC's Cularis Helmet Takes Virginia Tech's Top Spot

Aug 19, 2025
by Seb Stott  
Share

When it comes to picking a helmet, safety is paramount. Fortunately, Virginia Tech provide industry-leading and publicly available safety tests for bicycle helmets. I wrote a whole article about their test procedure and results, but the gist is that they try to replicate real-world crashes in the lab, impacting each helmet at 6 different locations and 2 impact speeds most associated with concussions in the real world. Their scoring system is designed to reflect the chance of suffering a concussion; the lower the score, the safer the helmet.
POC Cularis Details

• Currently #1 helmet tested by Virginia Tech
• Mips Air Node liner
• Breakaway visor, sunglasses storage
• Weight: 405 g (medium); 433 g (large)
• Sizes: S (51-54 cm); M (55-58 cm); L (59-62 cm)
• Nine colour options
• MSRP: USD 260 / £200 / 230 EUR
• poc.com


Top of the list for now, with the best score of all 279 cycling helmets so far tested, is the POC Cularis. Based on the best information available, this is the safest on the market today.

But safety is not everything. What about fit, ventilation, convenience, weight and price?



FEATURES, FIT & FUNCTION

The Cularis is designed with computational fluid dynamics to optimise ventilation. I can't say whether this approach is best, but the helmet does feel particularly cool and airy. There's very little material in contact with the head, and large channels provide continuous airflow from front to back. There's not a lot of fabric to absorb sweat, so if you suffer from sweat dripping into your eyes, that may be a consideration. But for me, it's a good choice for muggy rides.

Long hair approved.

The Mips Air Node liner doesn't intrude at all on that ventilation, and according to my better half, it doesn't catch long hair between the liner and the shell like many Mips liners do. And it still seems to do the business at reducing rotational forces, at least in the lab.

POC also boast an adjustable peak that breaks off on impact to absorb energy. I can't say if this feature contributed to the outstanding safety score, but the adjustability is a bit unnecessary, as the peak is barely visible even in its lowest setting, which is where I left it.

Similarly, POC promotes the "eyewear garage" where glasses can be inserted into the two outer vents above the peak, but I found this to be underwhelming as it's fiddly to install the glasses, and they can shake out.

In terms of fit, I found the Cularis to sit a little low at the front, with a little too much pressure on the crown of my head. I have a strange head shape, and have found this to be the case with a few other helmets, but most don't give me any bother. My partner's fitted her just fine, so as always, best to try before you buy.


WEIGHT & PRICE

POC advertise the Cularis as a lightweight trail helmet, but at 405 grams in medium, it's nothing special on the scales considering the $260 asking price. Here's how it stacks up on the price/weight spectrum against some other size medium crash hats we have weighed:

Pinkbike's Take
If you want a comfortable and well-ventilated trail helmet that's going to keep you as safe as possible, this is it. It's expensive, and like any helmet, the fit may not work for everyone, so try it on before buying. If the price is still too much to swallow, consider the Specialized Tactic, which is ranked 4th for MTB by Virginia Tech and costs just $110.— Seb Stott

Snfoilhat (Aug 19, 2025 at 10:08)
 On it's face, 7.1 is less than 7.2 and the difference is 0.1

But when you ask what the meaning of the difference is, it opens up new interesting lines of questions. We don't need to answer them in the comments and it's not really PB's responsibility to answer them, but it is important not to bury them in the rush to present new safety data as a straightforward blessing.

Q1. What is the basis for our confidence that (pick any helmet) the 2025 POC Cularis in the size tested (let's say medium) would score exactly 7.1 if it was retested 10 times. Would it? And if it was a little different each time, how different?

Q2. What is the basis for our confidence that (pick any helmet) the 2025 POC Cularis in the size tested (let's say medium) would score exactly 7.1 if the XS, S, L, XL, and XXL were tested? And if they a little different, how much?

Followup Q. If there was a difference between randomly chosen individuals of the same helmet as in Q1, or between different sizes of the same model as in Q2, then what is the size of that difference in score compared to the size of the difference in score between two totally different rated helmets (i.e. 0.1)?

Once you start thinking about variation, it calls into question the framing used in this article and the VT Helmet Ratings site like 'Top Spot' and '#1'. There are situations in which there simply isn't enough information available to break a tie. And it would be healthy to grow one's awareness of how often ambiguity gets flattened down into something we can put into a clear ranking even when there is no adequate basis for believing it. It's not a race; it's not spoiled if it doesn't end in a definitive podium.

And if you're about to answer the questions I posed above something like "well surely VT studied that, it's common sense that they would" kindly include a screenshot or paste the text for everyone to see where you learned that, please.

litany (Aug 19, 2025 at 10:34)
 @Snfoilhat: I don't think anyone at VT thinks that their scoring system is the be all and end all of helmet safety. But if you go back a few years, people on here were arguing that all helmets are the same because they all pass DOT, etc. which is obviously absurd. They test multiple copies of each helmet to come up with the ranking, so there already is sampling in the score. I don't know what you think they are doing, honestly.

If you read their published papers they talk about the limitations, the test procedure, how they calculate the score. They have almost an entire page discussing some of the limitations. It's right there on their website. You just have to click the link.

They talk about the variability between helmets, because again, they test multiple copies of the same helmet.

"Kinematic variance across trials one and two was assessed per impact configuration for each helmet as the range in PLA or PRV divided by the mean. Ten additional third trials were conducted spanning a range of impact configurations and helmets. While these trials decreased standard error by increasing the sample size, they had minimal effects on average PLA and PRV (£ 2.9 g and £ 0.6 rad/s on average, respectively). It was thus deemed that two trials provided sufficient data for the present analysis while minimizing practicality burdens.

"There are several limitations associated with the present study. First, although the impact configurations are comprehensive and based in real-world data, a finite number of configurations can be evaluated practically. There likely exist other scenarios in which helmets offer varying levels of protection. Helmets do not reduce kinematics in non-impact scenarios, which can produce rotational-related injuries (including concussion) in extreme cases. Second, the concussion risk function was derived from head impacts to American football players, who experience differing impacts from cyclists (although similar in duration and
magnitude) and have skewed physical characteristics compared to the general population...."

"Other limitations pertain to testing boundary conditions. First, although simulating road surfaces using a sandpaper-coated steel anvil represents the industry standard,11,26,27,40 this setup is likely overly stiff and may have inflated results.4 Second, testing was conducted without an ATD neck, as cyclist oblique impacts may subject the neck to considerable axial compression, a scenario under which the HIII neck is overly stiff..."

"A further limitation is that helmets were tested without extraneous attachments such as visors, which could affect resulting kinematics.6 Helmet fit was not directly quantified but rather replicated as a consumer might fit their own helmet. This may have increased variance in the results, although previous work has suggested that fit does not systematically alter cyclist head impact kinematics.43 Additionally, categorizing helmets by style and MIPS resulted in small sample sizes for some categories (i.e., only two urban MIPS helmets), limiting the generalizability of results. Some helmets marketed for multi-sport or mountain biking were categorized as road helmets due to similar structural designs; however, these helmets often possess some design distinctions (e.g., increased coverage and stiffer visors for mountain biking helmets) that
may affect impact attenuation capabilities. As consumers often choose helmets based on intended activities, these effects should be investigated as more helmets are evaluated using the STAR protocol."

For instance they are saying right there that individual helmet fit might matter, or might not, but they aren't sure. If you go read the study they reference, they will have even MORE limitations.

They are fully aware that it's difficult to quantify concussion probability. Because you can't just take a bunch of people and hit them over the head and see if they get a concussion or not. There's a lot going on, and a lot of things you can't measure. But the basic premise is that less brain acceleration = lower risk. Which is pretty hard to argue with, even without the most amazing data to back it up.

They even talk about how it's possible to get a concussion without your head or helmet hitting anything! What do you think that implies?
