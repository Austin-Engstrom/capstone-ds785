Review: FSA NS SC i30 Carbon Wheels

Mar 18, 2026
by Mike Kazimer  
Share

39 Comments

When it comes to catchy model names, FSA NS SC i30 doesn’t really qualify. I’m no marketeer, but it seems like there’s potential to come up with something slightly more attention-grabbing. Incredibly dull name aside, there’s one key detail that helps FSA’s new carbon wheels stand out from the crowd - the price. When they first launched, they were priced at $1,250 USD, positioning them on the lower side of the price scale. Unfortunately, a recent price increase puts the SC i30 wheels at $1,438, which is still decent for carbon wheels, but not earth shattering.

The wheels use hookless carbon rims with an internal width of 30mm and a depth of 22mm, pretty standard numbers in the modern wheel world. 28 straight pull spokes are laced up to FSA’s own hub, which uses a 4-pawl design that engages with 32 teeth inside the hub shell. The landing page for these wheels says they have a 6-pawl design, but that's not the case - I counted twice, just be be sure.
FSA NS SC i30 Details

• Wheel size: 29"
• Carbon rims, 30mm internal width, 22mm depth
• Intended use: trail / all-mountain
• 28 straight-pull spokes
• ASTM 4 certified
• Weight: 1655 grams (900 g rear / 755 g front)
• Price: $1,438 USD
• 2-year warranty
• fullspeedahead.com

When these wheels first came out they seem a little too good to be true, with an XC-friendly weight and an ASTM Category 4 rating that meant they could be used on trail and enduro bikes, or even light e-bikes. As it turns out, that 1495 claimed gram weight wasn’t accurate - on my scale the wheels weigh 1655 grams (900 for the rear and 755 for the front). That’s still a reasonable weight for a set of carbon trail wheels, but it’s not going to have the XC riders rushing for their wallets.


A four-pawl freehub...

...and 32 teeth inside the hub shell.


PERFORMANCE

The SC i30 wheels spent most of the test period on my Specialized Chisel and on a Norco Revolver. Those are both XC bikes that I don't always ride like XC bikes, so they felt like appropriate test sleds for these hoops. I ran WTB’s 2.4” Peacekeeper tires, a setup that strikes a good balance for winter trail riding, with enough grip for slimier conditions while still retaining enough rolling speed. Getting the wheels set up and ready to ride didn’t require anything out of the ordinary - the tires seated and sealed easily, and installation was as straightforwards as it gets.

It’s rare that a set of carbon wheels comes through these days that’s brutally stiff, and for the most part the average amount of compliance seems to have settled at a good middle ground. That’s where I’d place the SC i30’s - they’re not bone-rattling stiff, but they’re also not as soft feeling as something like Zipp’s 1Zero Hitop S wheels.

I went back and forth between the Zipp and FSA wheelsets during my time on the Norco Revolver, and the stiffness difference was noticeable. The Zipps are a more XC-oriented / light trail oriented option, while the FSA wheels are aimed at a broader range of bikes (they’re even approved for lightweight eMTB usage), and that’s how it feels on the trail. When pushed hard, the Zipps can get a little ‘twangy’ as the spokes load and unload in corners, while the SC i30 wheels stayed nice and quiet. When coasting, the freehub has a fairly loud racheting sound, but I didn't find it to be overly distracting, and I'm a rider that prefers quieter hubs.

The SC i30s have what I'd call a neutrally solid feel to them - there wasn't any undue squirming or flexing while cornering or touching back down from jumps or drops; once they were installed I didn't need to give them a second thought.

As far as overall durability, the SC i30 wheels have seen their fair share of rough terrain and are still spinning true. The hubs themselves are fairly basic, but they’ve held up well to the elements, and don’t have any play or crunchiness.


PRICE AND WEIGHT COMPARISON

DT Swiss XMC 1200: 1502 grams / $2,911 USD
Zipp 1Zero HiTop S: 1535 / $1,400 (designed for bikes with 130mm travel or less)
FSA NS SC i30: 1655 grams / $1,438 USD
Hunt Proven Trail Cabon: 1732 grams / $1,599 (currently $1,120)
Reserve SL: 1793 grams / $1,599 USD
Race Face Era: 1750 grams / $1,763 USD
We Are One Sector: 1762 grams / $1,595 USD (currently $1,197)





Pros
+ Moderate price for carbon wheels
+ Solid, neutral ride feel

Cons
- Advertised specs don’t match reality
- Only a 2-year warranty



Pinkbike's Take

It didn’t take that long for carbon wheels to become commonplace, and nowadays there’s no shortage of strong and relatively light options out there. FSA’s SC i30 wheels are a perfectly fine, solid set of wheels that would be a good match for a do-it-all trail bike, but they don’t stand head and shoulders (or rim and spokes) above the rest. The price-to-weight ratio is very reasonable for a trail / all-mountain wheelset, but the warranty isn’t as generous as what other companies have started to offer.

They’re closer to a wheelset that you’d expect to see spec’d on a complete bike, which isn’t necessarily a bad thing, it’s just that riders who are looking to jazz up their ride with something extra-flashy aren’t going to find it here. There aren't any string spokes, no colorfully anodized, instant engaging hubs; instead you have a straightforward, no nonsense set of carbon wheels. For some riders that will be enough, while others may need to look elsewhere to satisfy their carbon wheel needs. — Mike Kazimer
