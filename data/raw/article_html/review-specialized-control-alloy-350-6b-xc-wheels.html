Review: Roval Control Alloy 350 6B XC Wheels

Last winter, I decided to build up an all-alloy XC bike. I don't have any particular allegiance to aluminum parts, nor am I against carbon for some ecological reason. Quite simply, I had just bought a Commencal Tempo frame, and most of the parts I had or wanted weren't carbon, so when filling in the gaps, it seemed like a fun quirk to pursue. For me, I don't like carbon cranks or bars, and while partial to carbon wheels, I ultimately am open to either as long it rides well.

Roval Control Alloy 350 6B Details
• Size: 29"
• Intended use: XC/Trail
• 19mm depth, 31 external, 27mm internal width
• Hubs: DT Swiss 350 36T
• Sapim D-Light Straightpull Spokes
• Weight: 1650 grams
• MSRP: $800
• More info: specialized.com

Roval say these wheels were born on the World Cup, with the development and engineering of their race-ready carbon rims bleeding into these metal ones. They say, however, that this wheelset is a "trail-ripping, best-of-the-best, aluminum wheel," which is no mean feat. That said, many downhill riders still prefer alloy, and it's great to see Roval cater to riders of a similar disposition on the other end of the gravity spectrum.


Technical Features

The wheels used individually customized sleeves to bond the rim join, which they claim is not only "wildly over-engineered" but also helps reduce weight and eliminate creaking. Specialized also uses a customized shot peening process to create a stronger wheel. This is done by blasting the surface with small pieces of metal. This process remodels the surface of the component to a degree and bids to remove any potential points of failure, such as small imperfections, from the manufacturing process.

The wheels are available in 29-inch only and use 28 straight pull spokes front and rear. The rims are a 6013 alloy and feature an asymmetric profile. Roval say they're 20% more durable. Compared to what, I'm not sure but hey, it sounds impressive.


The wheels use DT Swiss 350 hubs with a 36-tooth ratchet, giving 10 degrees of engagement. While their single-ratchet start might no longer be the brand's flagship tech, it's still a personal favorite, if only for its simplicity and, in my experience, reliability.

The wheels have a rider weight limit of 275 lbs or 125kg. When you buy the wheels, you have 90 days to register them to activate a lifetime warranty for the original owner.

At 1650 grams, these aren't particularly light when compared to the modern crop of carbon rims. That said, you can also get lighter hubs. The latest generation of cutting-edge wheels can come in at around 400 grams less, which is huge in terms of racing. However, I would argue that the $1700 difference in price, give or take, is also quite large. There are mid-tier carbon wheels, but they could still be nearly double to save around 100 grams per wheel. In that context, these don't look too wayward.


Ride Impressions

The wheels found themselves at home on my Tempo, which I'd underforked to steepen it up, added some thin XC tires, as well as a few other touches to make it into the singletrack ripper I wanted. Yes, the Tempo is a very capable bike, but I was looking for an XC bike with comfort, more than a trail bike that's a bit livelier, and built it up as such. The Control wheels fit in line with this character.

They're comfortable and absolutely light enough. That said, they don't feel particularly sharp or snappy. When under power or acceleration they do feel a bit soft. Then, when going downhill, it's very easy to send a lot of flex through them. A combination of a not particularly stiff bike, these wheels, and thin tires made something that's a very good place to be for hours at a time, as well as being a bike where you can get the tire to touch the stays if you really want to.

I was very happy, though, and for every moment where you'd want something a bit more direct feeling, there'll be another where you snake through some rocks and feel the compliant rim find a way without passing on that harshness to your hands or failing to track. This is my comfort-inducing and confidence-inspiring cruiser, and the wheels play a big role in that.



Pros

+ Comfortable
+ Good if not exceptional value
+ Great warranty

Cons

- Not particularly light
- Easy to flex



Pinkbike's Take

Are these wheels going to win a World Cup? Probably not. Were they in any danger of that with me at the helm? Absolutely not. These wheels are reliable, comfortable, and decent value, and for me, that's more important. While not the stiffest through turns, I think they strike a nice balance in terms of feel and give a good tire profile to boot.—Henry Quinney
