Polygon has been known for making some wild-looking bikes. Remember their old Xquare One DH bike? The silhouette of the new Collosus is slightly less radical, except it uses a multitude of short links. Polygon calls this their Independent Floating Suspension (IFS) - the same 6-bar design found on their N9 enduro bike.

The new Collosus has already been in action at events like Red Bull Rampage and World Cup Downhill races, not to mention being prominently displayed at Sea Otter last year.

Two alloy-framed models start off with the DH-M at $3,499 USD. The DH-R model receives Fox Factory suspension and dependable DT Swiss wheels for $4,999, which is one hell of a deal. Balanced geometry, smooth kinematics, and strong brakes set the foundation of a quality downhill bike, which is what the Collosus strives for, but one specification was skimped on both options: sticky tires.

Collosus Details

• 200 mm travel, 203 mm fork
• Alloy frame
• 29 or MX wheels
• IFS 6-link suspension
• 62.6-63° head angle
• 445 mm chainstays
• Sizes: S, M (tested), L, XL
• 10-year frame warranty
• Weight: 17.7 kg / 39.0 lb
• Pricing: $3,499- 4,999 USD
• More info: polygonbikes.com


The two-tone metallic blue matched the moody start to the day. In the sun though, it's a whole other story. If you like bright bikes, you'll dig this paint job. For fans of less flash, only the base model is available in black.

Frame Details

Let’s be honest here - there are a ton of bolts on the Collosus. The 6-bar suspension is responsible for a number of those, which we’ll discuss in the next section, but there’s also the dropouts.

The dropout design uses a small bolt-on piece to rotate upwards, dropping the BB and retaining virtually the same head tube angle, should you want to fit a 29” rear wheel. It doesn’t change the chainstay length but a UDH works in both positions. You'll also find DH standards like a 200 mm post brake mount and 157 mm rear hub spacing.

Another adjustment is found at the lower shock mount. Housed in the seatstay, a flip-chip tweaks the head angle and BB by 0.4 degrees. When making that switch, you’ll want to keep the brace between the stays aligned. So far, this hasn’t produced any creaking, even amid last summer's Crankworx dust storm, nor have any of the other bolts on the bike for that matter.

Tidy internal cable routing and sufficient chainstay protection add up to make the Collosus pretty darn quiet on the trails. There is a hitch on the cable routing though - if you run your rear brake on the left hand side of the bars, you'll have to deal with the singular internal cable port living on the left hand side of the down tube.

A straight 56mm head tube opens up more adjustments for aftermarket reach or angle-adjusting headsets to fine tune the ride further. Shorter riders and freeriders alike will appreciate the clearance over the low-slung top tube for their baggy overalls, or whatever fashion is in right now. A 190mm single crown fork will clear the downtube for those looking to adapt the downhill bike into something more versatile for flippy-spinny tricks.

Suspension Design

The Collosus starts with a basic frame outline but is strung together with a series of links. Polygon calls this 6-bar suspension design Independent Floating Suspension (IFS) - not to be confused with the acronym IFS (independent front suspension) found on cars and other four-wheeled vehicles. Polygon says those links are arranged “to control wheel path and anti-squat independently to the leverage curve.”

Both builds come with a 250 x 75 mm air shock, likely for the convenience of fitting various rider weights, however, the leverage curve is said to work with coil shocks too. The curve is a smooth arc with the exception of a small bump at the very end. Through the last 20 mm of travel, there's an inflection point that turns up the progression in the rate.

The anti-rise is also higher than most 4-bar, Horst-link bikes. On one hand, a high anti-rise (above 100%) causes the suspension to be pulled into the travel while on the brakes, conserving geometry to a degree. On the other hand, some riders say that a higher value firms up the suspension unfavorably. I didn't find that to be the case here as the anti-rise starts just below a neutral value of 100%, but it can depend on when and where you tend to do your braking.

You can read a very in-depth article about anti-rise written by Jessie-May Morgan here.

Geometry & Sizing


The Collosus is a slack bike, even for a DH bike. In the high position it sits at 63-degrees but can drop down further to 62.6 degrees. The lower setting works best for steep tracks and flat out race speeds. On jump tracks and flatter trails, the front end can be unsettling to steer. The high mode reduces the wheel from flopping side to side too eagerly.

For sizing, Polygon offers four options in the Collosus. Small 20-25 mm gaps between each size mean that some riders could potentially size up or down to suit their style - shorter for reactive handling on jumps and tight trails, or longer for greater stability at speed through choppy tracks.

In my case, I chose the medium frame for more maneuverability around the Whistler Bike Park. If I were focused purely on racing the Collosus, the wheelbase of the size large looks more suitable.

Part of that need for a longer bike comes down to the back half. At 445 mm, the chainstays aren’t the longest. That number suits the size medium fairly well, however, that number doesn’t increase as the reach grows on longer frames.

If you're looking to change the rear wheel to a 29", the swinging dropout raises the position of the rear axle. Moving to the larger rear wheel barely budges the geometry from the 27.5" setting. You can inspect that closer here.

The dropouts only change the BB and head angle though, not the chainstay length. Perhaps that could have come in the form of a longer dropout.

Compare to other DH Bikes


Polygon nailed the component specifications of the Collosus build kits. Two choices keep it simple - the budget-friendly Collosus DH-M at $3,499 USD or the high-performance DH-R at $4,999, the one we’ve been riding.

On the DH-R, you get a solid mix of trusted components like Fox Factory suspension at both ends, DT Swiss alloy wheels, and SRAM X01 DH shifting with alloy cranks.

The DH-M receives RockShox Base suspension with a GX DH drivetrain and unlabelled alloy rims laced to Novatec hubs.

Both builds are fitted with the same brakes, steering controls, and tires. The TRP DH-R Evo brakes, although the outgoing generation, are highly-rated. Those feature resin pads and 203mm rotors front and rear. A Race Face Atlas bar and stem combo is another familliar sight with agreeable numbers. The only drawback to the otherwise pragmatic builds are the tires - Schwalbe Tacky Chan in the not -so-"Soft" compound.






Test Bike Setup

Getting rolling on the Collosus called for a few immediate changes out of the box. The first move was to trim the deer-catcher of a front brake line. Both brakes required a bleed anyhow.

Next was removing the inner tubes, installing tubeless values, and grabbing softer compound tires. Coincidentally, Schwalbe sent over their upcoming Albert Radial Gravity casing tires back in July, 2024, which performed brilliantly in the loose, dry conditions.

The shock was first set to 30% sag (180 PSI) as suggested and the fork to 11% (85 psi). Starting in the slack geometry setting seemed entertaining, but this didn’t place enough weight on the front wheel for A-Line hot laps. I bumped that up to 24% sag (215 psi) and set off again. After a couple more runs, I went on the hunt for rear shock volume spacers with hopes of gaining more support deeper in the travel.

Testing Info


I first got a hold of the Polygon Collosus at Crankworx last summer when the trails were blown to smithereens. Over a handful of days, it lapped a healthy dose of downhill tracks and cased some large jumps. Fast-forward through a few snowstorms to when the Collosus stretched its legs further south on well-trodden downhill tracks of Cypress in West Vancouver. These similarly demanding trails are a little rougher around the edges than Whistler's main veins but serve well to shake down bikes touting more than 160mm of travel. The "high" setting on the Collosus suited jumpy trails at Whistler and the "low" position for hammering laps down Cypress.




Performance

My immediate reaction to the Collosus on the trails was how it settled into a low, stretched-out stance. Your feet are close to the ground and you have a lot of front wheel ahead of you. That boosts confidence to let it fly on steep tracks. You feel locked in and behind the center of gravity of the bike.

On trails with less gradient, that can cause some understeer on low-angle trails. Attention to how you weight the bike is needed if you opt for the slack setting. Moving up a frame size may have mitigated that - one benefit of offering smaller gaps in reach.

Given that deep squish stance, one might think it’s sluggish on jump tracks, but that's not the case. Pumping the bike builds speed with little wasted energy. On paper, the Collosus isn't a particularly light bike, but it rides like one. Off the line, it pedals strongly and doesn't hang about. Short-ish chainstays and a 27.5” rear wheel all play a role in that too no doubt.

Placing the shock in the high mode will bring out the most in the Collosus if you're focused on jumping. I expect most riders gravitating to these trails over race tracks will trend towards less than 25% sag. I settled on four volume spacers but adding a fifth wouldn't be out of the question for riders who are keen to send jumps past their intended landings.

Another stand out of the Collosus was how it flattened out braking bumps. It relieved my mind of how beat down some of the trails in the bike park were at the time - not to the same degree as some high-pivot machines, but in a similar realm. Under braking, I appreciated how the balance of the bike remained composed. It didn't lurch forward and cause the steerer to feel twitchy and I can't say I noticed the suspension firm up either.

The blue and gold has a bit of a loud, Fast and Furious vibe to it, but behind that busy 6-bar linkage lies a smooth operator. The Collosus remains calm and cool in hectic situations. On top of that, through those pinball sections of trail, the chain and cables remain impressively quiet too.

I like what Polygon have offered up here with this frame design. The shock is easily accessible, the adjustments are clean, and the cables are barely visible. Sure, a bearing overhaul is going to be a lengthier process than some frames, but they've covered the livability well. There aren't any nooks where mud can clog up or crannies to crunch rocks.

Although the review spanned two different bikes, I experienced zero issues with loose bolts, pivots developing play, or even the slightest creak.




What's the Best Value?

This is a tough question. If you're serious about riding a lot of downhill laps, go for the more expensive DH-R. Five grand is no small drop in the bucket, but consider how much you'd have to spend on a comparable bike to get those same parts - the Commencal Supreme DH V5 signature has nearly the same spec but costs $7,400 USD.

I would also add, if you're looking to save your trail bike from being thrashed in a bike park and are willing to add to your collection, the DH-M is feasible alternative. Should you catch the DH racing bug later on, you have a solid starting point to work from.



How Does It Compare?

Polygon Collosus DH-R: $4,999 USD.
YT Tues Core 4: $5,999 USD.

The Collosus DH-R goes toe toe-to-toe in terms of value with the YT Tues Core 4. Both builds feature common parts from Fox, TRP, and SRAM. Out of the box, though, the YT takes the edge with not one component being skimped on - there's those ever important sticky tires, a super light carbon SDG I-beam saddle and post, and even ODI grips.

Both rides have short-ish rear center lengths and a low center of gravity. Where these two downhill rigs differ though is their stance, or where the axles land relative to your hands and feet. In the high mode, the Polygon is still slacker than the Tues' low-mode. That's not necessarily a negative, as it depends on what your intentions are with the bike. Either frame could be fitted with aftermarket headset to fine tune the angles and reach numbers further too.

In terms of adjustability, the YT wins a point for including a chainstay length adjustment. Although it's only a mere 5mm, they've done a tidy job on it because both positions will accept a UDH. While the Collosus uses that new standard mount, and can flip between 27.5 and 29" much faster, I'd like to see longer chainstays on the two larger sizes.

There's also the plentiful amount of pivots on the Polygon. With that said, the 6-bar linkage does seem to work more magic. There's very little feedback or noise from the chain, whereas the YT could use the help of an OChain Active Spider or STFU dampers to calm down those types of vibrations.

All of those pivots, and an aluminum frame, do pack on the pounds though. On the scales, the carbon Tues comes out 1.6 kg / 3.5 lb lighter, however, it'll cost an extra grand for those weight savings.



Technical Report



Schwalbe Tacky Chan tires: I’ve come to know the Schwalbe Tacky Chan well through the Downhill Bike Field Test last summer. I haven’t exactly gotten along with their on/off style of grip, especially up front. The shoulder knobs are spaced far from the center knobs, almost dropped in a sense. Spacious blocks can bite into soft dirt with a committed lean, however, that traction can quickly escape from underneath you on hard-packed trails.

Both front and rear are also the “Soft” compound which is misleading - the rubber is world away from Maxxis' 3C MaxxGrip. The Tacky Chan tends to skate on rock and glance off of roots more than, say, Schwalbe’s Magic Mary. Immediately swapping to a more universal tread and softer compound tires would be my recommendation.

Fox Factory suspension: Top quality suspension doesn't come cheap, unless you buy this bike. Out of the gate, you have high end suspension from Fox with all of the adjustability you could want. Polygon states that the Collosus works with a coil shock too, but I'd wager most riders will appreciate the adjustable progression of the Float X2's air spring on this frame.

TRP DH-R Evo brakes: These TRP brakes might be the out-going model, but they shouldn't be forgotten about. Their light action lever reduces hand fatigue and offers a ton of power, even with 203mm rotors. The only other brake I've tried that has an inital lever pull that light are the Hayes Dominion A4 - possibly too light for some riders' taste. The only downsides are that riders with small hands may not be able to position the lever throw in close enough to the bar. There's also some interference between the lever's handlebar clamp and the SRAM shifter.





Pros

+ DH-R model offers top value
+ Excellent rear wheel tracking
+ Calm and quiet chain management


Cons

- Taller riders or racers may want a longer chainstay
- Single-sided cable port isn't as clean for moto brake riders



Pinkbike's Take
The Collosus DH-R claims a spot in my books as a well-poised downhill race bike that can rival other bikes with World Cup wins under their belt. Pop a few volume spacers in that rear shock, swap out the tires and you're ready to race for a whole lot less dough than you thought.

The only notable aspect holding the Collosus back on faster-pace tracks would be the slightly shorter rear center. That of course will play in your favor on steeper tracks or if you're a freerider looking for responsive 200mm travel rig.— Matt Beer

justinfoil (Mar 31, 2025 at 10:36)
 @90sMTBEnjoyer: in other words, there are only 4 "bars" defining the axle path (and part of the leverage curve): the 2 links, the chainstay, and the bit of frame between the 2 forward link pivots.

The remaining bars, no matter how many, define only leverage curve, not at all impacting the axle path: seat stay, rocker, bits of frame between link pivots & rocker pivot & shock mounts.
  • 10
ChrisGX (Aug 6, 2025 at 5:07)
 @90sMTBEnjoyer: Yes, you are absolutely right. The axle path of the rear wheel is wholly determined by the the compact 4-bar linkage connecting the lower suspension arm (chainstay if you like) to the front triangle. There being nothing else that determines the axle path of the wheel, then by definition it is a 4-bar.

The other two bars - seatstay and rocker link - are just driving the shock and seving no other function. If you were of the mind to do it you could keep stacking additional seatstays and rocker links ad infinitum (subject to certain length, space and positioning limitations) just for fun (inspired by the Knolly designers perhaps) but under the skin these 8-bar and 10-bar or more linkages would still be entirely standard 4-bar linkages - all the extra bars don't effect rear wheel axle path at all.

You are right about what a true 6-bar is, too. All 6 bars must work in concert to determine the axle path of the rear wheel. By that definition Dave Weagle's DW6 (on the Atherton and Pivot bikes), Yeti's Switch Sixfinity and Scott's Ransom linkage are genuine 6-bar linkages. I can't think of any others right now

90sMTBEnjoyer (Mar 31, 2025 at 9:44)
 High (above 100%) anti rise actually forces the bike to pack down into it's travel while braking, neutral (below or around 100%) means braking has little impact on the suspension, and low (below around 60%) will make the bike to resist using it's suspension. Both high and low anti-rise values cause a bike to firm up in different ways, and can mean the difference between being comfortable on the brakes in rock gardens to slow down, or having to come off the brakes to get the suspension to stop skipping over rough terrain.
