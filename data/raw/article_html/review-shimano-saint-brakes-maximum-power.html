Shimano Saint


Shimano's Saint brakes are something of an outlier in this group, but it's not because of their power, features, or design. The biggest differentiator is their age, with these old dogs clocking over 13 years of continued presence in the Shimano lineup. Why did we include them in the conversation around the best brakes on the current market? Because they still compete with the best of them.

Saint Details

• Mineral oil
• Adjustments: Reach, free stroke
• 2 pad options
• 2x15mm, 2x17mm pistons
• Price: $550 USD (pair)
• Brake weight: 298g (front)
• Rotor weight: 171g (203mm)
• shimano.com

Backcountry


2013 was the year that Apple released the iPhone 5s - many numbers ago by my calculations. That phone implemented the first 64-bit processor ever used in a handset, and had a timeless hardware design with slick features like a fingerprint scanner. Like the 5s, the Saints have all the critical features of modern brakes, just without some of the time-earned refinement and fancy touches that we've grown used to. Based on how much I like the Saints, it might be time to trade in my current phone for that older model.

An interesting aside: while I was working on this, Apple released the first software update for the iPhone 5s in 12 years. Maybe they're listening, maybe I'm losing my grasp on reality. Regardless, it would follow that Shimano would release their Saint update to follow suit.


Setup

Shimano's funnel bleed is a simple procedure, but there are countless alternative tricks and methods that mechanics have developed over the years. Luckily, getting a decent feel is easy, and though you'll have to do some upkeep to keep them humming, it's not terribly involved.

The only meaningful adjustment on the Saint lever is the reach adjust, standing prominently from the lever blade itself. Easy to turn on the fly, and features a large range. With a normal bleed, the lever doesn't bite super close to the bar, but has enough range for smaller hands.

Livability:
Like just about every ServoWave Shimano brake before the current XTRs, the Saint does have issues with a wandering bite point in some situations. Not all riders are bothered by this, and certain setup tricks can mitigate the issues, but ultimately it is part of the program.


Feel

Shimano's ServoWave lever cam has a very distinct feel to it, and the Saint embodies that sensation fully. A nose of resistance breaks you into the stroke, which then moves very freely until you hit the bite point. It takes some getting used to, but a squeezes gets you acquainted with the sensation.

The Saints have quite a bit of free throw, and unfortunately the Free Throw adjuster does little (if anything) to alter this feel. As with so many brakes, there are some setup tricks to alter this feel, but that's best left to the forums and the comments.


One defining characteristic stands out on the Saints, and that's just how touchy they are at the bite point. With very little modulation to speak of, metering the massive power is left up to the user's fine motor skills, and some well-timed braking points.

Feel Ranking:
Once you're past the initial point of resistance, the lever swings with light action, all the way through the large dead band and into the bite point. That bite point is firm and aggressive, requiring good technique.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 597 g pull force (5th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.



Power

Though there are some competitive new alternatives in this space, the Shimano Saint is still the name many people think of when it comes to all-out power. That reputation is well earned, with the closest feeling to shoving a branch through your spokes as anything on test.

As mentioned above, the finesse required to modulate power on the Saints will be a challenge for some riders, exacerbated in challenging trail situations. But for those that want all the power at once, and know when to demand it, these binary stoppers are hard to beat.

On sustained descents where you're just pulling the brakes to reign in momentum, the power on tap is impressive, but it's the initial bite that leaves the strongest impression with these.

Power Ranking:
Turns out, nothing stops a Honda Fit quite like the Shimano Saint. These famously powerful brakes proved to be immensely strong on trail too, with one of the sharpest initial bites on test.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 22.4 m / 73.5 ft (1st overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.



Pros

+ Massive power
+ Distinct Shimano feel
+ Durable, simple, aggressive


Cons

- Large free strong deadband
- Some wander in the bite point
- Lack of adjustment beyond reach
