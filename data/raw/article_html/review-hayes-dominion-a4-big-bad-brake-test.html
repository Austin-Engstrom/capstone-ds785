Hayes Dominion A4



In the late 90s and early 2000s, the Hayes Mag brake was heralded for its immense power. The purple and champagne color brakes were one of the most popular choices for downhillers. In that era, fully kitting out your bike with Hayes brakes likely included buying aftermarket CNC'd Dangerboy or Razor Rock levers, too. The hype slowly faded when Hayes built some less successful brake models, and other competition came along from Hope, Shimano, and Avid.

After a hiatus from the gravity side of the sport, Hayes launched the Dominion A4 back in 2018. It would be fair to say that they were met with some skepticism. That was short-lived - positive reviews began to emerge and Hayes rebuilt a loyal fan base with the Dominion A4s.

Two factors that make the Dominions stand out to gravity riders are their extremely light lever action and the manner in which they quickly build power.

In terms of structure, the ambidexterous master cylinder houses a reach adjustment screw and a tool-free bite point dial built into the lever. At the caliper end, a couple one-of-a-kind setup aids can be found. Another bonus for consumers is that Dominions include a set of semi-metallic and sintered pads in the box.

Setup

Hayes chose DOT 5.1 fluid for the Dominions, a decision that’s becoming less common with brands like SRAM and Formula moving away from the caustic oil. The bleed process uses two syringes to move the fluid through the system (available separately). Hayes has shared a step-by-step procedure of how to bleed the entire Dominion brake, or just the caliper end of the system using the 2-Stroke dual bleed ports. Each half of the caliper has its own bleed port to move fluid in isolation from the rest of the system. This can be handy to purge the smallest amounts of air.

Another unique feature found on the Dominions is the Crosshair alignment system. Two set screws help to keep the caliper centered over the rotor while tightening the mounting bolts.

Each complete brake comes with a 180cm length hose. Although I haven’t experienced it myself, the kevlar reinforced hose is larger in diameter than most and can be stubborn to feed through some tube-in-tube carbon frames with internal routing.

Hayes offers four rotor sizes ranging from 160mm to 220mm in a six-bolt mounting pattern only. At 1.95mm, they aren't nearly as thick as some of the other hefty discs out there.

Liveability: I've heard of some riders breaking a Dominion master cylinder clamp in a crash, but they've survived any lay-downs I might have had using these brakes. With the exception of easily accessing the pad contact adjuster, working on the Dominions is generally headache-free.


Feel

The ultimate brake test is to walk over to any bike and grab hold of the lever. In all seriousness, when you do this with a set of Dominions, you’ll immediately notice how freely the lever moves inward - that showed in our lab experiment too. In fact, the lever action is so light that it can take some getting used to modulating the brake while trying to hold a wheelie.

The lever is shorter in length compared to the Trickstuff or Hope brakes on test, and the pivot is further away from the bar too. A bevelled edge positions the hook of the lever comfortably under your finger.

Adjusting the reach is taken care of by the tool-free dial built into the lever, while tuning the pad contact, or bite point, requires turning the screw located behind the lever with a long-ended 2 mm allen key.

Feel Ranking: These brakes surprise everyone who gives them a squeeze. Shape-wise, they're similar to the TRP DH Evo lever but with the easy-on feel of the Hope Tech 4 V4s. Their low-friction pull and short engagement sets them apart from any other brake out there.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 447 g pull force (1st overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.


Power

The Dominions reach their potential quickly, and that’s a good thing. Some riders may perceive the low lever force to be vague at first, but once the pads have made contact with the rotor, there is excellent, intuitive modulation. Reaching full power takes very little effort and is maximized when using the sintered pads. The most distinguishable difference from the semi-metallic pads is heat dissipation - the sintered pads have a far greater ability to deliver power when they’re running hot on prolonged steeps.

The championing characteristic of the Dominion A4 is how effortlessly you can deliver stopping traction to the tires. That should equate to less tired arms on the trail - you’ll just have to recalibrate your brake reflexes when balancing wheelies.

Power Ranking: The Dominions are up there in terms of power simply because of how mindless it is to achieve such a high braking force. They delivered in the Honda Fit Stop test too, falling in behind the two big bosses of power: the Shimano Saints and the SRAM Maven Ultimates.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 25.9 m / 85 ft (3rd overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.



Pros
+ Smooth, gradual power curve.
+ Extremely light lever movement.
+ Clever setup and bleeding features.
+ Great value.

Cons
- Extremely light lever movement.
- Pad contact adjuster is annoying to access.
- Pads rattle.

 DOT4 or 5.1 brake fluids are hygroscopic, meaning they absorb moisture from the air and keep the water dissolved throughout the fluid. This prevents water from forming separate pockets that could boil at 100 °C or cause localized corrosion.

Mineral oils are hydrophobic, meaning they repel water; any moisture entering the system can separate and collect in low points (bottom of caliper that gets at highest temp), increasing the risk of vapor lock during heavy braking.
