SRAM launched their new Motive XC / trail brakes earlier this year, but another brake was released at the same time that didn’t get nearly as much fanfare - the Maven Base. Even on SRAM’s own website it’s not immediately visible - it takes an extra click to find it after viewing the Maven Ultimate, Silver, and Bronze options. The thing is, for many riders, there’s a good chance this is the Maven brake they really want, with lots of power, very light lever action, and a reasonable price.

Maven Base Details
• 4-piston caliper. 18mm pistons
• Mineral oil
• DirectLink lever design
• Tool-free reach adjust
• Weight: 387 grams (actual, front caliper w/pads, hose, and lever)
• MSRP: $205 USD per wheel
• sram.com

Let’s start with what sets the Maven Base apart from the three other brakes in the Maven lineup. Rather than using two 18mm and two 19.5mm pistons like the original Maven trio, the Base uses four 18mm pistons. That downsizing is due to the other differentiator - the fact that the Base uses a DirectLink lever design, rather than a SwingLink lever design. With the DirectLink lever, the power is more linear, meaning that it remains consistent from the beginning to the end of the lever pull; with SwingLink, the power (leverage) ramps up the further the lever is pulled. It’s the same lever design used on the aforementioned Motive brakes.

One of the confusing things about the Maven Base brakes is that they’re slightly more expensive than the Bronze model, at $205 vs $185 per wheel. Realistically, I think SRAM could have given them a different name all together, maybe call them Super Codes or something, to help get them out of the Maven shadow.



Installation

I’m glad that SRAM made the switch to mineral after so many years of espousing the superiority of DOT fluid. On paper DOT does have some advantages, but mineral oil is less likely to damage a frame's paint job, and it also doesn't absorb water, which should allow for longer service intervals. Of course, you still definitely shouldn’t drink the stuff, and wearing gloves and eye protection is always a good idea when dealing with brake fluids - if you read the MSDS sheets for DOT fluid and mineral oil you see that care should be taken when dealing with either one.

Bleeding the Maven Base brakes is an easy procedure, especially if you’re familiar with SRAM’s bleed procedure. Attach a syringe to the caliper with SRAM’s Bleeding Edge fitting (everything has a special name), and thread the other into the lever. Push fluid from one end to the other until you don’t see bubbles, then close off the port at the caliper. Next, squeeze the lever a few times and then pull up gently on the syringe to remove any remaining air bubbles. Lightly push on the syringe plunger to equalize the system, then unthread it, keeping a rag nearby to clean up the excess fluid that’ll be present when it’s removed. SRAM’s instructional video does a good job of going through the steps.


Piston Massaging

There is one step that wasn’t really necessary with SRAM’s previous brakes - the piston massage. It’s a funny name for a procedure that’s absolutely necessary, unless you want to end up writing late-night forum posts about your inconsistent brake lever feel. Trust me - this step is important. Luckily, it’s easy to do.

SRAM even has a $4 chunk of plastic for this specific purpose, or you can use two Centerline rotors. With the pads removed, put the spacer into the caliper, and then squeeze the lever until the pistons contact the spacer. Remove the spacer, and carefully push each piston back into its home with a plastic tire lever. Be gentle - if you get wild you run the risk of popping a piston entirely out of its seat, and then you’ll be sad and there will be mineral oil everywhere. Do this a few times and the pistons should be moving smoothly, and the feel at the lever should be consistent.

If you're not comfortable pushing on the pistons, or a little short on time, a pad advance procedure can also be useful. This involves inserting the thinner side of the included plastic pad spacer between the brake pads and squeezing the brake lever (you'll need the wheel removed for this step). Next, insert the thicker side of the spacer, which will push the pads further back into the caliper. Re-install the wheel, squeeze the lever a few times, and make sure everything's operating properly.


Performance

I installed the Maven Base brakes on a Santa Cruz Bronson, where they took the place of a set of Maven Ultimates. I’ve gotten used to the power of the Mavens in the months since they came out, but I still tend to de-tune them, running a smaller rotor size or going to the thinner Centerline rotors over the thicker HS2. I love steep trails, and powerful brakes, but since I’m on the lighter side, at 160 pounds, and I don’t need the same amount of stopping power as someone that’s 30 or more pounds heavier. For those bigger riders, that extra power is going to be very welcome, and could mean that automatically going to the largest diameter rotors around won't be necessary.

When the Mavens first came out there was a lot of online hand-wringing and theorizing about the harder-to-pull lever. There’s no denying that the initial force required to pull the lever on the regular Mavens is slightly higher than it was on the Codes, but I think some of those initial complaints were related to an early production run - I’ve been on at least six different sets of Mavens in the last year and didn’t have any lever-related issues as long as they were bled properly and the piston massage was performed.

That said, for riders who prefer a lighter lever action, the Maven Base brakes are the way to go. It takes minimal force to get the lever moving, and while the Hayes Dominions still take the cake when it comes to an effortless initial pull, the Base brakes aren’t far off. There’s a light initial action, and then a very defined point where the pads contact the rotor. The feel is closer to what you’d expect from Shimano’s brakes, with a sharp, crisp bite as the pads make contact, but the overall amount of power is noticeably higher than Shimano's XT or XTR brakes.


There’s not as much modulation from the lever compared to the SwingLink-equipped Mavens, but I haven’t had trouble switching between the two brakes - a couple pulls and my brain re-calibrates to tell my fingers how much force to exert. With the Base brakes, max power can be reached quickly, and then it doesn’t change throughout the lever stroke. With the SwingLink levers there’s a bit more modulation; the power hits a little deeper into the pull than it does with the Mavens. That means the Base brakes could take a little getting used to if you’re a brake dragger, but again, it’s pretty intuitive.

The consistency and power of the Base brakes has been impressive - I’ve taken them on plenty of steep, sustained trails and the lever feel has remained the same from top to bottom. On one particular brake-burner of a traiI was able to get them to howl, but the stopping power remained the same, and that trail is one that’ll make almost every brake make some noise, whether it’s Shimano, SRAM, or TRP. I wouldn’t hesitate to put these on an e-bike or a DH bike - there’s enough power for any scenario.

Organic pads are the stock configuration, but as always, for riders in wetter areas metallic pads are the way to go. Personally, I’d be happy if organic pads went away across the board - I eventually end up to swapping to metallic pads on every brake that comes, and the performance always increases. It is another way of tuning the feel, though, and between pad compound and rotor size / thickness there’s a decent level of customization available.


Pros

+ Very light lever action
+ Powerful and consistent
+ Reasonable price

Cons

- More of an on/off feel than a dimmer switch
- No version with pad contact adjust



Pinkbike's Take

The Maven Base brakes eliminate any of the lever-related gripes that surround the SwingLink-equipped Mavens, all while delivering an immense amount of power and consistency. I’d love to see a higher end option with a pad contact adjust, but that’s a minor detail - as it is, these brakes nail it when it comes to price, power, and overall performance. Highly recommended.

— Mike Kazimer

Skidding is bad; so if you’re skidding you modulate to keep the tires moving. It’s the operator that provides the modulation through relaxing the brake finger and it really has little to do with the lever or cam action of some brake systems. As Kazimer says, skilled riders adapt to the brake action.

Where the pads contact and create friction with the rotor is more correctly referred to as “bite point”. SRAM traditionally gradual and even (not with Mavens I guess); Shimano is direct, shorter throw. Both are effective and have pros and cons. The size of your pads and rotors, the conditions of your pads and rotors, and wet and muddy crap and even dry dust and dirt all affect how well the friction between pads and rotors is applied on any given day. Bigger rotors may ultimately be more powerful, but the increased diameter also makes them less progressive (they bite more due to increased surface area) so modulation is pretty key to running bigger rotors for better performance.

Modulation is a skill. It needs to be learned and can be improved through conscious application and practice. “Not skidding” is a key element of control. Learning to apply your brakes for the right situation and maintain consistency (5 - 10 - 20 up to 90 - 100) is also a key skill to riding better. Aggressive braking as a means to braking keys to maintain flow and speed is an advanced tactic, but pretty much impossible if you don’t know how 70% braking differs from 95%.

How to improve:
1. Rear: Practice micro skidding and modulating. Lock the rear and then build your feel and skills to relax your finger enough to stop the skid but maintain high braking power. (Locking your rear wheel is NOT good braking and does not provide maximum stopping)

2. Both: Practice riding behind other people and keeping exactly the same distance behind them by using 15 or 25 or 50% braking SMOOTHLY. Practice and build a mental model finger proprioception of what 15-25-50% feels like and how it affects your bike (slows down, stand up the front, make suspension harsh). Use both brakes in different amounts as needed front/rear (10/30, 30/10, 20/20…)

3. Front: Learn to do stoppies. A front wheel stoppies means tou are REALLY using 100% of your front brake. I’d say most people never use more than 50% front brake, unless they practice in lessons. If you can stoppie on your front wheel, and hold it for a second or two, you now know what 100% front brake feels like. Learn to modulate the front and tou expand your capacity to brake later, earlier, in a shorter distance.

Good hunting.
