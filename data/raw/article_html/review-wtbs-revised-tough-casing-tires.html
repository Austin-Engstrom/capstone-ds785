Review: WTB's Revised Tough Casing Tires

Oct 15, 2024
by Matt Beer  
Share

WTB is increasing the performance of their enduro tires by making them more durable and stickier. These changes will be available in four of their most aggressive tread patterns: the Judge, Trail Boss, Verdict, and Vigilante.

The new SG1 layup adds extra support and security to the top of the casing and nearer to the bead. A rebalanced Tri-Tec rubber compound is said to work better in wet conditions while also lasting longer.

All four tread patterns are available in 29 or 27 .5” diameters, excluding the Vigilante (29” only), with black sidewalls only.

SG1 Tough Casing Tire Details
• Improved durability with new SG1 casing
• Reformulated Tri-Tec compound
• Available in High Grip or Fast Rolling compounds
• Verdict 29x2.5" weight: 1525g
• Vigilante 29x2.5" weight: 1653g
• Judge 27.5x2.4" weight: 1360g
• Price: $89.95 / €74.50 / £80 (all Tough casing variants)
• wtb.com
All new SG1 Tough casing tires will retail for $89.95 USD, which is less expensive than most competitors’ gravity options, but there is a catch - there is an added weight penalty for the durability.

Specifications


WTB has some of the least flashy sidewall hot patches and simplest tire tech naming schemes in the game. They have two compounds, "High Grip" or "Fast Rolling," and two casings, "Light" or "Tough".

WTB states that their "acceptable tolerance for weight variation is 10%."

Claimed weight:
Vigilante 29” – 1575g
Judge 27.5” – 1455g
Verdict 29” – 1390g.




Features

SG1 casing

The SG1 technology is an addition to the “Tough” casing options. The 60 TPI carcass is still tubeless-ready and comes folding, plastic-free packaging.

There are two main changes, the extra layering under the tread in the center of the tire and a taller Apex shield around the bead which is now 15mm versus 5mm.

With all of those robust additions to the SG1 line of tires, there is a considerable increase in weight. The new SG1 Vigilante 29" I received had a weight difference of 383g compared to the older model in the same casing. Oof. The new Verdict wasn’t too far off of that delta either, while the 27.5" Judge saw an increase of 210g.

Tri-Tec rubber compound

By the name, you can guess where this is going. Both the High Grip and Fast Rolling compounds use three different rubbers of varying amounts. The softest is on the side knob, then there’s a slightly more durable one in the center, and those are backed up by a firmer base.

Since WTB changed the casing name, I’m surprised they didn’t change the compound name too because although it’s still made of three rubbers, the formula is different. They say they’ve tweaked the natural versus synthetic rubber ratio and rebalanced the carbon and other reinforcing agents to give more grip in wet conditions and somehow last longer than before.




Setup

Installation was straightforward for all three tires on a multitude of rims. They had no trouble seating with a regular pump and never leaked any sealant through the sidewall.

Primarily, these tires were tested under the weight of an e-bike, but I also put them on a regular enduro bike to see how much that added weight affected the perceived efficiency and suppleness. I ran the Vigilante and Verdict on the front between 21 and 23 psi, and the Judge on the back wheel at 25 to 27 psi.

The most time was spent on the Verdict doing back-to-back comparisons between the old and new versions.




Ride Impressions
Verdict


The open tread pattern of the Verdict makes it a great candidate for loose and muddy conditions. The rows of square knobs shed mud well. There is a large gap between the center and shoulder lugs. Each square knob has a small sipe, or relief, to allow it to conform on harder surfaces.

Although the Verdict has excellent bite in soft conditions, especially when you get the side knobs to bite into off cambers, committing to leaning it over on hard pack services can be a little bit more daunting.

It’s also not the best tread design in terms of braking performance. Due to the profile, the center knob stand taller than the shoulder knobs which may provide less contact when braking in a straight line. They are also more square rather than rectangular so there’s less to dig in. I’d advise you to keep this one in the front and pair it with a Judge out back.

Judge


Speaking of that tire, the Judge holds a similar appearance to other popular rear specific tires on the market. It has alternating square and rectangular blocks pairs down the center and very supportive side knobs that are spaced closer to the center row than the Verdict.

I only had the chance to try the Judge on the rear. Braking performance is stellar on just about every surface, whether you are upright or leaned over.

Vigilante


As for the Vigilante, it’s a great all-arounder that’s very predictable in mixed terrain. Compared to the Verdict, it doesn’t have quite the same bite in deep mud or soft conditions, but it’s a little bit less squirmy on hardback surfaces. That also means it’s more consistent when leaning into turns, particularly hard packed berms.

Support, grip, and durability

WTB has made a huge leap forward with the performance of their gravity oriented tires. There’s ample support even under the forces of an e-bike, but the casing is also decently active for use with enduro bikes.

I’d say they’re closest in comparison to the feel of a Schwalbe Super Gravity casing. They’re not quite as supple as a Maxxis DH casing, though.

It’s not so much the casing stiffness that’s an issue, but the overall weight of the tire is considerably heavier than before. If you’re pedaling to the top of the hill, you’re going to feel that extra weight penalty very quickly.

The new SG1 casing and reformulated Tri-Tec rubber makes these tires far superior to the old, especially on wet rocks and roots. In terms of reliability, I never experience any punctures or burping despite my best efforts with the heftier e-bike.

I can’t speak too much to the long-term durability of the rubber just yet because I’ve only had them for a few weeks, and while doing back-to-back comparisons with the old tires the trail conditions have been on the soft side.



Pros

+ Improved grip in wet conditions
+ Extra shielding doesn't impact suppleness
+ Versatile tread patterns
+ Lower price than much of the competition

Cons

- Some of the heaviest enduro tires out there
- Not as compliant as some competitors' gravity casings (which are lighter)
- Vigilante is not available in 27.5" diameter



Pinkbike's Take

The revisions that WTB made to their new tough line of tires will definitely be appreciated by eMTB and gravity riders who demand a resilient tire. The improvements to the rubber compound are welcomed, especially when trail conditions get wet. Whether the large weight penalty will be worth swallowing is another question though. —Matt Beer

 @AndrewCuretonrlD: and if you want math it's very simple. Torque is moment of inertia multiplied times angular acceleration. Moment of inertia increases 1 to 1 as the mass increases, and squares as distance from the axle increases.

If you have a tire that weighs 18% more (1600g) than another tire, it will require 18% more torque to rotate that tire at the same acceleration as a 1350g tire.

 @thenotoriousmic: “sticky” is a layman’s simplification for the parameters that affect the grip of a a tread compound. Durometer is a measure of spring rate but it is not a measure of rebound speed because polymers have self-damping.
