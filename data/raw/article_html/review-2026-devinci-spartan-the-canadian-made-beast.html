Review: 2026 Devinci Spartan - A Canadian-Made Aluminum Beast

Mar 26, 2026
by Mike Kazimer

The Devinci Spartan’s history dates all the way back to 2013, when Stevie Smith rolled up to the DH World Champs in Pietermaritzburg, South Africa, aboard a new bike designed specifically for that extra-pedaly course. Three generations of the Spartan have come and gone since that time, which brings us to the fourth iteration that launches today.

The 2026 Spartan no longer has the high-pivot layout found on the previous version (or a carbon frame for that matter). Instead, it uses a Canadian-made aluminum frame to deliver 165mm of rear travel via a Split Pivot suspension design. While the prior models were aimed at enduro racing, this one is much more of a “find steep, gnarly stuff and try to ride down it” type of bike - call it freeride or big mountain, no matter the term this is one long, slack machine.
Devinci Spartan Details

• Wheel size: Mixed (29" compatible)
• Travel: 165mm, 170mm fork
• Aluminum frame (made in Canada)
• 63.5 or 63-degree head angle
• Chainstay length: 450 or 457mm (size L)
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL
• Weight as shown: 37.8 lb / 17.1 kg (size L, no pedals)
• Price: $5,999 USD / $7,499 CAD
• devinci.com

The stock builds come set up with mixed wheels, but a flip chip at the lower shock can be used to run a 29” rear wheel. The chainstay length is also adjustable by 7 millimeters, thanks to another set of flip chips at the front of the seat stays.

There are three complete builds and a frame only option - I’ve been testing the GX AXS model, which retails for $5,999 USD. Component highlights include a Fox Factory 38 fork and Float X2 shock, SRAM Maven Silver brakes, GX AXS T-Type drivetrain, Race Face wheels, and a Maxxis DHF / DHRII tire combo, both with the thicker DoubleDown casing.

Frame Details

Devinci’s Quebec headquarters are located in a region that’s known as the Aluminum Valley, so it makes sense that the Spartan uses a 6061-T6 aluminum frame. There won’t be a carbon version either - Devinci decided that aluminum was the way to go with this model, citing durability and sustainability as two of the driving factors behind that decision, although I’m sure the lower cost and ease of production were also parts of the equation.

The frame’s lines share some similarities with the shorter travel Troy, including the distinctive kink in the middle of the downtube. Personally, I like the looks; you get the sense that this is a bike with a purpose, where function trumps fashion. All of the bolts are easy to access, and there aren’t too many nooks and crannies for mud to accumulate in.

The Spartan uses Devinci’s “Shed” in-frame storage system, which provides some room for stashing the essentials inside the downtube. The door itself feels pretty rudimentary, and it’s not the quickest to re-install after it’s been removed, but it didn’t rattle or fall off during testing, so that’s a start.

The cables are routed internally, through ports in the head tube, and two clips in the storage door area keep them snugged up against the inside of the frame to prevent any annoying rattling. Other details include 12 x 148mm rear spacing (Devinci ditched SuperBoost when the Troy was released in 2024), a threaded bottom bracket, and three ISCG tabs for running a full strength bashguard.



Devinci's Shed storage compartment.

Two flip chips, one for the wheel size and the other for the chainstay length and head angle.



Geometry

For years, every bike that launched seemed to be getting longer and slacker than the previous version. That trend has started to slow down a little as the limits of what’s comfortably rideable are approached, but compared to the previous Spartan this new model is significantly longer and slacker.

The head angle now sits at 63.5- or 63-degrees depending on the orientation of the chainstay flip chip. That flip chip also changes the chainstay length, which measures either 450 or 457mm on the size large and XL bikes, and 440 or 447mm on the small and medium frame sizes. The reach numbers range from 445 on the small to 505mm on the XL, and increase in 20mm increments between sizes.

If all those numbers make your head spin, the overall takeaway is this: the new Spartan is a very long, slack bike, with a wheelbase that’s nearly 30mm longer than the outgoing model.




Suspension Design

The Spartan uses a 205 x 65mm stroke trunnion mounted shock for its 165mm of travel. Like the rest of Devinci’s full suspension lineup, it uses a Split Pivot layout, where the rearmost pivots are located around the rear axle, and the brake is mounted on seat stay. The Spartan has a linear, progressive leverage ratio, which allows it to work with both air and coil shocks.

Test Bike Setup

The Spartan arrived with its SDG Tellis dropper post out of the bike, so I slapped on a little grease, stuck it into the seattube opening, and… nothing. It wouldn’t fit. I double checked that the post was the right size (it was) and then did some grumbling. It turns out there was an issue with some of the early Spartan samples, and the seat tubes weren’t reamed properly. (Maybe that was the day Yoann Barelli and Steve Vanderhoek were working the factory floor). Devinci assured me the issue has been resolved, and even sent over a photo of the reamers they’re using to make sure it’s corrected for good.

Other than the seat post struggles the rest of the setup process was very straightforward. I trimmed the bars to my preferred 770mm, set the Fox 38 to my typical pressures, and by the end of the test period was running the Float X2 at 180psi, which resulted in 28% sag.

Conditions were extra-wet for much of the test period, so I did swap out the Minion DHF tire for a Highroller III. The Minion was my go-to for years, but these days, if I was picking from the Maxxis catalog, I’d rather run a Highroller III or an Assegai.

Testing Info

Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, and the surrounding area, with conditions typically on the wetter side of things, although there were some perfect-fit days in the mix.



Climbing

The Spartan falls into a similar category as the Trek Fuel LX, or the Raaw Madonna - they’re stout, aluminum framed bikes that don’t exactly leap up the climbs, but they’re also not bad places to be while you’re working your way uphill.

On the Spartan, the climb lever for the Float X2 is easy to access, which meant that I was much more likely to use it than if it was buried down near the bottom bracket. I’d typically flip it closed for long gravel road slogs or spinning on pavement to a trail head, and open it up for more technical sections.

I’ve said it before, but a slack head angle and long chainstays don’t automatically create a bad climber. Sure, the handling is more sluggish than a shorter, steeper bike, but the extra-long footprint of the Spartan helps give it loads of traction and stability. It isn’t the snappiest pedaler, but it doesn’t feel overly wallowy either - on rougher climbs with the shock open there was plenty of grip, and I was able to get it up chunky climbs without that much fuss.

It’s no lightweight, but the overall level of heft is close to what I’d expect for this style of bike - these days it seems like most aluminum enduro sleds are coming in around the 36 - 39 pound mark.



Descending

My first few rides on the Spartan didn’t really work out the way I wanted. Sometimes a bike will show up and I’ll click with it almost instantly, while others require an adjustment period. That’s how it was with this green machine. I’ve ridden plenty of long, slack bikes over the years, and I’ll always have a soft spot for burly aluminum enduro/freeride bikes, but at times this one felt a bit longer and slacker than I wanted - I had a few front end washouts, and a few moments in the steeps where I didn’t feel like I was totally in charge.

I ended up dropping the handlebars down a little lower, and running less air pressure in the fork to get my weight in a better spot over the front wheel. That helped, and things started to settle into place, although I can’t say I ever felt completely at home on the Spartan. Some of that has to do with the overall wheelbase - it’s almost 1300mm, a number that's around the limit of what feels comfortable for me on an enduro bike, at least for my 5”11 height on the steeper, tighter trails that I prefer.

The elusive M/L size would likely be the ideal fit for me - given the Spartan’s chainstay length and head angle I think a shorter reach number would help make things feel more manageable. The longer / slacker setting does reduce the reach by 5mm, but then the wheelbase gets even longer. I tried that setting just to see, and while it’s entertaining to have a bike with geometry lifted straight from a DH bike, it makes the bike even more terrain specific - the extra-slack setting definitely isn’t the way to go for anything other than the steepest terrain, and ideally those trails have room to let the bike run.


In zones with higher average speeds - a bike park, for instance - the Spartan’s length wouldn’t feel as cumbersome, and I’d have no qualms about using it as a daily driver in the Whistler Bike Park. It has more of a muted, stuck to the ground nature, but once it’s airborne it has the lazier, stable handling that’s more typically associated with a downhill bike.

As far as suspension performance goes, the Spartan does a good job with maintaining grip on small-to-mid size bumps, and it breaks into the initial portion of its travel quite easily. On bigger hits it feels like you’re smacking the ground with a dead blow hammer - it’s quite muted, and the hit is dissipated quickly, although there’s not as much to push against when those impacts come in quick succession - that first part of the travel is gobbled up pretty quickly before the shock ramps up. I ended up running the rebound almost all the way open on the Float X2, and could have still gone quicker in order to help the bike recover from those repeated impacts.

The Spartan is coil shock compatible, and I’d strongly consider going that route in order to gain a little more support, and bump up the level of traction even further.

How Does It Compare?

Many of the Raaw Madonna’s geometry numbers are quite similar to the Spartan, but all of the small differences add up to create two bikes with very different on-trail characteristics. On the Raaw, the lower bottom bracket, taller stack, and slightly steeper head angle make it easier to have an upright position and steer from your feet - it’s almost like riding an off-road scooter, except that it’s way cooler.

With the Spartan, the bike feels more sprawling, and it takes more physical effort to push it through corners or get through slower speed chunky sections. They’re both what I’d consider “big” bikes, in that they do their best work in steep, gnarly terrain, but the Raaw has a wider window of happiness, where the Spartan really needs room to run.

The Trek Fuel LX / MX falls into the burly aluminum bike category too, and it can be set up to be slightly slacker than the Spartan depending on the shock links that are used. The Fuel LX’s chainstay length and reach are both shorter than the Spartan, and as I wrote in my review of that bike, “Other than that slack head angle, the other dimensions aren’t outliers - the moderate reach and chainstay length feel well matched, and help keep the bike manageable in twistier sections of trail.”

I’d classify the Trek and the Spartan as freeride bikes in the more old-school definition of the term, bikes meant for seeking out gnarly maneuvers deep in the woods, but the Trek is a better match for the slower speed jank.


Which Model is the Best Value?

For the price, the top of the line GX AXS build I reviewed does a good value - other than potentially swapping the front tire, everything else is well suited to the bike’s intentions. $6,000 is still a lot of money, though, which is why I’d suggest the next model down, the Spartan 90, for riders who are trying to get the most bang for their buck. That model comes with SRAM's cable-actuated Eagle 90 drivetrain, SRAM Maven Base brakes, plus a RockShox Select+ fork and Vivid air shock, all for $4,799 USD.

You can save even more by dropping to the $3,899 USD Spartan Deore model, but the SRAM DB8 brakes and RockShox Domain Gold fork are going to be a noticeable step down from the spec on that Spartan 90.





Technical Report

Chainslap protector: The Spartan’s chainslap protector is okay, but not amazing. It’s on the firmer side, and doesn’t mute the chain noise as well as some of the better options out there. Overall the Spartan is a pretty quiet bike; running some softer chainslap protection tape or an STFU would make it even more stealthy.

SRAM Maven brakes: It’s old news by now, but the Mavens are very, very powerful, and they were a good match for the Spartan. Riders that want the lighter lever feel that the recently released B1 levers deliver will need to shell out an extra $90 to purchase an upgrade kit. For some riders, the brakes will be totally fine in their stock form, but I will say that the revised pivot kit does give them a really nice and light action, which I prefer.

Fox 38 fork: My test bike had a 2026 Fox 38 fork, but full production models will come with the brand-new 2027 Fox 38. You can read all about the fork here.

Frame storage door: The Spartan’s storage door doesn’t like to sit flush, and it can be a little finicky to get back in place once it's removed. I’d think of it more of an emergency storage compartment, rather than something you’ll want to fiddle with on every ride.




Pros

+ The answer for riders who think most bikes aren't long or slack enough
+ Very stable, especially at high speeds
+ Adjustable chainstay length that actually makes a difference

Cons

- There's such thing as too long and slack
- Some of the finishing touches (storage compartment door, chainslap protection) could use refinement






Pinkbike's Take

The new Spartan is a lot of bike to handle, and realistically, for most riders the 150mm Troy is going to be the better option. That bike is a little shorter, a little steeper, and still very capable. That said, for riders who want a big brute of a bike, one that works best on more open, steep, and higher speed trails, the Spartan fits the bill - it’s fast and stable, just make sure you have terrain that warrants a bike like this.— Mike Kazimer
