Review: Formula Cura X Brakes - 2 Piston Consistency

Formula's two-piston Cura X brakes were launched a little over two years ago, positioned as the hopped-up version of the Cura brakes that have been in the Italian company's lineup since 2017. The brakes are designed for cross-country and trail bikes, although as you'll see in a bit, they can be used for an even wider range of riding styles, even if they don't have four pistons and calipers that weigh as much as a softball.

The X in the model name indicates that the brakes received a carbon fiber lever, titanium bolts, and a braided Kevlar hose. Those finishing touches save around 34 grams per brake compared to the standard Cura, and add $100 to the price.

Formula Cura X Details

• 2 pistons
• Brake fluid: mineral oil
• Carbon lever blade, titanium hardware
• Kevlar reinforced hose
• Weight: 218 grams (front lever, caliper, hose, pads)
• MSRP: $250 USD per wheel
• rideformula.com


Details

The Cura X brakes run on mineral oil, which is a little friendlier to work with than DOT fluid. You oviously shouldn't be drinking the stuff or dumping it in your eyes, but it's less likely to eat away at paint or irritate exposed skin. That fluid pushes on two 24 millimeter pistons (for comparison, the piston diameter in the 4-piston version of these brakes is 18 millimeters) that are housed in the forged aluminum caliper.

One design feature that could create a small hurdle is the lack of a banjo fitting on the caliper. That means the angle of the hose is fixed; there's no way to rotate it to give the hose a better path into a frame, at least not without purchasing an adaptor and a different hose. I didn't run into any issues on the two bikes I tested the brakes on, but it is something to keep in mind.

Moving onto the levers, those use a split clamp design, with different 'MixMaster' back plates available to allow them to be paired with SRAM or Shimano shifters. The carbon lever blade itself is fairly long, especially compared to the length of Shimano's levers – the size and shape are similar to the dimensions of TRP's brake levers.

The lever reach is adjusted by turning a small protruding dial, and I was able to make that adjustment without any tools. One some of Formula's brakes that dial can be very hard to turn, but with these ones I'd say it was only medium hard.




Installation

Getting the Cura X brakes up and running was about as easy as it gets. The Formula bleed kit (sold separately) includes two syringes with fittings that thread into the caliper and the lever. If you're familiar with the bleed procedure for SRAM's brakes, it's essentially the same with Formula. Fluid is pushed from the caliper and through the lever until there aren't any bubbles visible, and then a couple of final steps help ensure the last stubborn bubbles are extracted from the caliper and lever.



On the Trail

Formula supplied the brakes with an assortment of rotors, brake mounts, and pad compounds, which made it possible to try multiple setups and adapt them to two very different bikes. I started by installing the brakes on a Raaw Madonna, partially because that bike has external cable routing, and also because I was curious if these two piston brakes could supply enough power to adequately keep things under control in the steeps. On the Raaw I ran a 220mm front rotor and a 200mm rear with metallic pads in order to eke out the most power possible.

With those big rotors and metallic pads the brakes were strong enough to handle sustained steep maneuvers without any major issues. They don't have the massive power of something like the SRAM Mavens, and they do require a firmer pull on the lever to really slow things down in a hurry, but they worked well enough that I didn't feel like I'd brought a knife to a gun fight. The feel at the lever is similar to how TRP's brakes feel - the initial pull of the lever is light, with a very positive sensation once the pads contact the rotor.

This wasn't entirely surprising; when the original Cura brakes hit the market Finn Iles was racing World Cup downhill with them on his DH bike. Times have changed, though, and we've seen the power level of brakes increase in recent years; realistically, an enduro bike isn't the ideal place for these brakes, but it was impressive to see that they could hold their own in that scenario.

After the Raaw I put them on a Specialized Chisel FS, running a 160mm rear rotor and a 180mm up front, where they replaced a set of Code RSC brakes that I'd been running with the same rotor sizes. The difference between those two brakes comes down to lever feel rather than a dramatic perceivable power difference, even though the Codes have four pistons versus the two in the Cura X. SRAM's Level brakes would be the more typical matchup, and in that case the Curas handily win. The Levels simply don't have enough power for anything other than mellower trail riding. I do slightly prefer the lever shape of the Codes (or Levels) vs the Curas – they have more of a curve to them, and cradle my finger a little bit better. That's a small nitpick; Curas do have a lighter initial action, and I was able to achieve a comfortable setup without much fuss, and without any pad contact adjustment.

How do they compare to Shimano's two piston brakes? I've had good luck with the two-piston XT and XTR brakes in the past, and I can't declare a clear winner in that match-up. The Cura's don't feel quite as on / off as the Shimano's, and the maximum power occurs a little later in the lever stroke, but the difference isn't overly dramatic. Pad availability is typically better for Shimano, but Formula is a large enough company that sourcing replacements shouldn't be too difficult.



Weight & Price

Shimano XTR two piston: 210 grams / $325 USD

Formula Cura X Weight: 218 grams / $250

SRAM Level: 245 grams / $300 (4 piston is 10 g lighter than 2 piston)

Formula Cura: 252 grams / $150

Hayes Dominion T2: 254 grams / $290

Shimano XT 2-piston brakes: 294 grams, $170



Pros

+ Very consistent performance, no wandering bite point
+ Plenty of power for most applications
+ Easy to bleed
Cons

- Weight savings is fairly minimal for the extra price compared to Cura
- Lack of banjo fitting at the caliper could be an issue with some frames



Pinkbike's Take

The Formula Cura X brakes are great, full stop. (Pun intended). They're easy to set up, deliver plenty of power for all but the biggest bikes, and have been trouble-free over months of riding.— Mike Kazimer
