Tires matter just as much as suspension and frame geometry, but without solid brakes, it's all worthless. Brake control starts at the brain, analyzing what your eyes see and how your fingers react at the levers, so what makes "good brake" comes down to more than just sheer power.

By our standards, a decent gravity brake should deliver consistent, efficient power that's easily accessible, without costing a fortune, either.

Below, you'll find all of the brake specifications, compelling test stats, category standouts, our top three brake recommendations, and links to the full reviews.




Test Lap and Setup

Dario and I cooked up a lap through the Whistler Bike Park that consisted of varied terrain and was repeatable. Within that course, the trails covered fast sweeping corners, sharp roll-overs, and a chunky rock garden. The only thing missing was an absolute brake-scorching sustained descent, something rarely found in a bike park.

Each brake was set up as the most powerful gravity brake based on the manufacturer's recommendations (at the time of testing). That consisted of the thickest rotor options on all accounts and mostly metallic pads. The only exceptions were Magura and TRP, which recommended organic pads. Rotor diameters varied between 200 and 203mm based on the manufacturer's specifications. The pads and rotors were bedded in using the same procedure on flat ground before any test laps began.

Brake Ace helped out by loading up both of our bikes with front and rear sensors. Brake Ace uses a strain gauge sensor built into the adaptor to measure data ten times a second. The sensors measure front-to-rear brake bias, temperature, average power, major braking events, GPS location, and more.

Brake Ace sensors delivered our data.

Lever Pull Force Test

There's a lot of debate around how hard it is to pull a lever, so we set out to put that argument to rest. We realize that lever setup is personal, dependent on hand size, and can affect lever force. To keep it consistent, all of the brakes were set up to my preferred position.

Next, the lever was clamped vertically on a handlebar, and a string was taped to the finger purchase near the end of the lever. The lower end was tied to two water bottles and zeroed on the scale. Dario then wiggled the brake rotor between the pads while I poured water into the hanging bottles. When the rotor could no longer fall freely, the bottles were weighed.


As a result, the Hayes Dominion came away on top with the lightest lever engagement, followed by the Trickstuff Maxima in second, and the Hope Tech 4 V4 in third.

We also used the average power derived from the Brake Ace data to compare it with the lever-pull force results from the lab. This is where the Trickstuff Maximas show their might. They produced the highest lever pull force-to-power ratio.




Car Stop Test

Over the course of this big review, the most critical test was stopping a car with bicycle brakes. In fact, we're kind of surprised it worked at all.

For this test, Dario towed me behind his Honda Fit, and I attempted to stop the train over ten rounds with each brake. On our cone marker, Dario stopped accelerating and coasted through at 20 km/h. I applied the brakes after passing the cone until the car stopped.

There are some caveats: keeping the tow strap tight was daunting to manage, both in terms of entry speed and avoiding any front wheel tangles. I also did my best to apply the brakes consistently while maximizing traction.

All things said, the result was consistent with what we found on the trail. The Shimano Saint brakes came out on top by an authoritative margin. Following next were the SRAM Mavens and the Trickstuff Maxima. At the bottom were the Lewis LHP+U4 and the Magura Gustav.






Category Highs and Lows



Control

Best: Hope Tech 4 V4
The Hope has a long lever that rides on a bearing pivot. It’s very smooth, but it can take more lever travel to get into power. The difference between the Tech 4s and the Trickstuff is that the power turns on earlier and in a more linear fashion. If you’re a fan of short levers or immediate grabbing brakes, then adapting to the Tech 4 V4’s long lever travel and smoother modulation may take some time to learn. Once you figure it out, though, they offer supreme control.

Worst: Shimano Saint/XTR
Shimano brakes all function in a similar way; the short, hooked levers need to travel a fair distance of dead space before the pistons touch the rotors. In sections where you need to brake suddenly, the pads can clamp down on the rotors with a solid “cah-chunk”.

If you grab too much lever, it can lock the wheel when you least expect it, or not pull enough. Figuring out where that point in the lever travel is tricky, as they both deliver power quickly once engaged. Shimano does admit that their brakes have a higher degree of deadband with their Ice Tech rotors, but that keeps them running drag-free more often. It’s worth noting that I’ve found thicker rotors to alleviate that deadband and guesswork.


Weight

Lightest Brake: Formula Cura 4
The Formula Cura 4 brake was the lightest on test at 260g. Despite the caliper being very slim, it holds four 18mm pistons. That doesn’t include an adjustable banjo out of the box, though.

Heaviest Brake: SRAM Maven
The biggest looking brake takes the cake for heaviest too; that’s SRAM Maven, which weighs in at 367 grams, due in large part to that beefy caliper.

Lightest Rotor: Trickstuff
Trickstuff wins the competition for lightest rotor, at 155g. The Cura 4’s rotor comes in 41 grams heavier at 196g.

Heaviest Rotor: The Maven’s HS2 rotor sits mid-pack at 199g. It’s seriously overshadowed by the 2.3mm thick Lewis Heavy Duty rotor at 268g.



Power

Most Powerful: Shimano Saint
We had a feeling that it would be a hard-fought battle between the Shimano Saint and the SRAM Maven, coming into the Car Stop Test. A few others had moments of glory, but the results spoke for themselves; the Saint has immense power. The Saint brakes stopped Dario’s Honda Fit 2.5m sooner than the Mavens.

Based on the average power recorded by the Brake Ace equipment, the Trickstuff Maxima’s 636 W edges out the Saints' 611 W. But, keep in mind that the Maxima dropped down to 4th place in the Car Stop Test - the Saints stood atop that list by a wide margin.

Ultimately, it’s the user’s ability to tap into that raw power that makes us award the Saint with top honors in this category.

Least Powerful: Lewis LHP+U4
When we take into consideration the Car Stop Test results, weak average power, and our ride impressions, it’s no surprise that the LHPs landed at the bottom here. No matter how hard we pulled on the lever, they lacked the friction and power attained in other brakes at lower lever forces, too.




Liveability

Best: SRAM Maven
When SRAM first launched these brakes, there were mixed reports of wandering bite points (personally, I didn’t find that to be the case). Since then, general opinions have changed. SRAM also added a how-to video to their YouTube Service channel. The bleed procedure is less complicated than other brakes, in part, due to the Bleeding Edge tool.

SRAM’s vast dealer network sourcing spare parts means you’ll be more likely to find the parts you need, whether you’re wrenching at home or on the road.

The tool-free dials make matching one brake to feel like the other, dead simple. The pad contact dial doesn’t change lever reach position either. On top of that, there are endless ways to build a Maven brake with two pad compounds, three rotor sizes, and, more recently, two new lever SwingLinks.

Worst: Trickstuff Maxima
Bleeding the Maximas requires a special type of oil (bionol) that you likely won’t find at your local shop. The bleed screw on the master cylinder is a small M4 thread, a piece not found in a universal bleed kit. The oily procedure is lengthy, difficult to achieve a solid bleed, and not easy to replicate between front and rear brakes.




Value

Best: SRAM
It took a while for SRAM to deliver with a set of big stoppers, but they hit the mark, and then some. The power is massive, should you decide to bulk up on rotors and metallic pads, but the price isn’t outrageous. A tamer setup can be achieved too with smaller diameter discs and organic pads, or, as mentioned, the less progressive SwingLinks. Adding to the tuning options, the ease of ownership and the ability to source spare parts. On paper and from our point of view, the power-to-price of the Mavens is an unbeatable gravity-based brake.

Worst: Trickstuff
If the price alone doesn’t stand out as a hurdle, then the low liveability of the Maximas should. Bleeding them is a pain. There’s a ton of power, but that doesn’t come quickly - it’s buried deep at the bottom of the lever engagement. That would be more accessible, except the brake lacks any adjustment outside of the lever throw.


Personal Picks:

Amongst the seven other brakes on test, there are a bunch that sit in the middle ground that are great. After extensive tests, laps, and chit chat, it doesn't surprise me that Dario and I ended up with the same three brakes we'd most likely recommend. If we were spending our hard-earned dough, here's how they'd line up.



Dario's Picks

1. SRAM Maven
Power and adjustment were my main reasons here. Getting the bite point to hit exactly where you want it and having the power come on quickly were easily achieved.

2. TRP EVO Pro
The consistency and simplicity of this system make them an easy choice for all sorts of riders. The revised lever has a nice level of adjustment, but even the older model feels competitive and high performance.

3. Hope Tech 4 V4
The sensitivity and controllable power curve of the Hopes put them in contention for my all-out favorite, but a couple of nitpicks kept them from the top. Great feel, easy adjustment, big fan.



Matt's Picks:

1. Hope Tech 4 V4
The characteristic that won me over here was the immediate yet gentle rise in power. Basically, the earlier in the lever pull you can reach the meat of the power, the more efficient you will be at braking. But it can’t be an overwhelming surge - it has to arrive smoothly in a way you sense the friction. The Tech 4 V4s do just that. Deeper down, extra power is gained gradually.

Helping position the English brakes as my top choice is the build quality, found in the two-way lever adjustment dials, the one-piece caliper, and Hope’s extensive spare parts catalogue. Oh, and the reusable hose fittings. A new thread-on bleeding cup doesn’t cut down time in the workshop, but it does help to produce an extra crispy bite point.

2. SRAM Maven Silver
I like that SRAM hit the limiter with the potential power of a fully loaded set of Mavens. I’ve yet to hear any complaints about them lacking power. The lever action is also a basic action; pull a bit to get power, pull the bit further and get it all. They don't have to turn tires into dirt daggers, though. The immense power can be toned down substantially with the organic pads -

As Dario pointed out, the tool-free adjusters allow you to dial in the bite point and reach without compromise. Being under the SRAM umbrella, parts for Mavens and knowledge about the brakes are widespread, something that only Shimano can compete with.

3. TRP Evo Pro
The Evo Pro makes a strong case for being the most “normal” brake here. The revised lever shape from the previous DHR Evo fits more hand sizes than before and now includes tool-free reach and bite point adjusters. Powerwise, it’s not comparable to the Maven or Saint, but a short amount of deadband makes it an effective brake. Price, weight, and liveability all fall in the middle of the road, making it tough to ignore.

VerticalDynamics (Mar 14, 2026 at 4:17)
 I understand that this test is not meant to be a highly scientific laboratory experiment, but the results should still be interpreted with some caution. From a systems engineers perspective, I see several potential limitations in this test setup:

• Operating regime mismatch - the towing test may push the brake system into a load case that differs significantly from the typical operating window of a mountain bike brake (combination of pressure, sliding speed, and thermal conditions).

• Different energy dissipation dynamics - Even if the total kinetic energy might be comparable, the way this energy is dissipated (low speed, very high inertia) differs strongly from real MTB braking scenarios.

• Tribological non-linearity of pad-rotor interaction - Brake pad friction is highly dependent on temperature, contact pressure, and sliding speed. The test may shift the system into a different tribological regime than what occurs during normal riding.

• Pad pressure sensitivity - Many brake pad materials do not behave linearly with increasing contact pressure. Extremely high pad loads may reduce effective friction or change the friction characteristics.

• Thermal behaviour differences - Real riding typically involves higher rotor speeds and more airflow, while the towing test likely creates different heating and cooling dynamics.

• Pad compression and deformation - Brake pads are not rigid bodies. At very high loads they can compress or deform, which may limit effective braking force and alter system behaviour.

• System elasticity and hysteresis - Hose expansion, seal deformation, pad compression and rotor flex introduce compliance and hysteresis that can change the effective leverage and pressure response during braking.

• Non-linear lever kinematics - Many brake levers have progressive leverage ratios over the stroke, meaning the system’s mechanical advantage varies depending on lever position.

• Sensitivity to system condition - Small variations such as imperfect bleeding or minor air inclusions can significantly change system stiffness and therefore braking behaviour.

• Test may favour specific material characteristics - Because the test represents a very specific load case, some brake systems or pad compounds may perform better simply because their friction characteristics happen to align with this particular (not fully realistic) regime, not necessarily because they perform better in real riding.
