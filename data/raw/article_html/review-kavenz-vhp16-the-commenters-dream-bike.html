Despite the consistent promise of gearboxes propelling this sport into the future, they still haven't quite made it to the mainstream. That leaves most of the development work up to smaller brands like Kavenz, who are willing and able to design around new technologies.

In a way, Kavenz is uniquely well positioned here, as their frame concept is quite adaptable, modular, and mates fairly well with the constraints of the Pinion gearbox. This aluminum VHP 16 I've been riding for the past few months is available in a standard drivetrain configuration, and as you see it here, as a portent of some potential future.
VHP16 Details

• 160mm travel, 170mm fork
• Aluminum frame
• MX wheels or full 29"
• 64° head angle, 77.5° seat angle
• 440-533mm reach, 639-652mm stack
• 419-439mm chainstays, +15mm at sag
• 6 sizes available
• Pricing: €3,735 (frame & gearbox)
• Weight: 39.18 lb / 17.8 kg (XL bike, no pedals)
• kavenz.com

Despite the polarizing and compromised drivetrain on this version of the frame, the VHP16 is one of the best high-pivot bikes I've ridden to date.—Dario DiGiulio



The bike has a good stance to it.
Frame Details

There's a lot going on here, but most of the complication has to do with the drivetrain and its constituent elements. Beyond that, the Kavenz is fairly typical, with a press-in headset, internal routing, and easy-to-access hardware on the suspension pivots. One major bonus to those pivots: the VHP platform uses the same 6902 bearing in every pivot on the frame.

The VHP16 has two versions: gearbox (AKA the G2) and standard drivetrain. The G2 is priced at 2,521€ for the frame only, while the standard layout is 2,348€. The catch with the G2 is that the frame price doesn't include the Pinion Smart Shift bundle, which will set you back another 1,214€. At about the same price as SRAM's X0 Transmission drivetrain, the Pinion group isn't exorbitantly priced, but you'll have to commit straight away if you are considering the gearbox option.

Stress-inducing.

Bolt-on dropouts and yet another pulley wheel.

I've been riding the VHP16 G2, with the Pinion gearbox and all the pulleys and gears that come alongside that. Kavenz has done their best to keep the majority of frame elements common between the two versions of the VHP16, while adding the provisions necessary for the gearbox's chain to stay in-line and properly tensioned. Quite a bit of assembly is required upon delivery, but the included instructions do a fairly good job of getting you through the process. There's a huge assortment of spacers included to get the chainline dead-on between all five cogs and pulleys, so some careful assembly is critical to a well-functioning bike.

Bash / Tensioner Woes: I had to do some hardware store shopping for two bolts that attach the bashguard / lower tensioner to the gearbox, as the supplied hardware did not provide enough purchase to withstand the abuse a bashguard goes through. This BOM issue has been resolved, but double checking that you're installing that guide with two M5x16mm bolts is key.

Due to the initial short-bolt issue, the longer ones were screwed into compromised threads, and eventually gave way when the bash took a strong hit, impacting the tensioner's ability to hold the chainline. The bike still works, but integrating a key component into a notably frail and sacrificial part felt flawed from the get-go. Luckily, Kavenz has redesigned the part, adding additional support to the bash, and will eventually fully separate the bashguard and tensioner, hopefully eliminating that issue.

The drivetrain. Don't worry, I did peel the plastic off after shooting this.

Even with the gearbox, dropper insertion is quite good on the bike. I've been running a 213mm BikeYoke Revive for the majority of the test, but swapped a 242mm Wolf Tooth post in recently to get more dynamic room on the bike.

Pinion's Smart Shift gearbox requires an external charge port to be located somewhere on the frame, and I think Kavenz did an excellent job of packaging it in a tidy spot that doesn't impede things aesthetically or add any unnecessary complication. They've also packaged the battery in the downtube, with a set screw to hold it in place and prevent any rattling or shifting under use.

All of the VHP bikes use the same main frame, and by altering the lower shock mount, rear shock, and the adjustable dropout length, you can effectively transform the VHP16 (as tested) to become the VHP 12, 14, or 18, if you so please.

The VHP12 is a 29" downcountry bike with 120mm of frame travel and a 130-140mm fork. It uses a 185x47.5mm shock. Medium geo: 475mm reach, 65.4° head angle, 78.9° seat angle.

The VHP14 is a 29" or MX trail bike with 143mm of frame travel and a 160mm fork. It uses a 205x57.5mm shock. Medium geo: 465mm reach, 64.4° head angle, 77.9° seat angle.

The VHP16 is a 29" or MX enduro bike with 160mm of frame travel and a 160-180mm fork. It uses a 205x65mm shock. Medium geo: 460mm reach, 64° head angle, 77.5° seat angle.

The VHP18 is an MX freeride/DH bike with 182mm of frame travel and a 180-200mm fork. It uses a 225x75mm shock. Medium geo: 452mm reach, 63.5° head angle, 77° seat angle.

Some cable routing of my own design.

Materiality.

I used this bike as one of my test machines for a giant brake roundup we've been working on, so some convenient external routing was critical - hence the stick-on routing.


Geometry

As you can see above, the VHP platform's geometry depends quite a bit on what travel mode you're running the bike in. I won't get into the weeds on all of the possible combinations, and instead focus on the MX variant of the VHP16 that I've been riding.

Kavenz does offer semi-custom geometry for the VHP frames, allowing a buyer to choose from a selection of measures on a few different variables. Instead of walking past the weeds and into some further unknown, I'll leave those options off the table for now. I tested a stock XL, and that's that.

The geo chart still has one complicating variable, in the form of adjustable chainstay lengths. The base length is a +0mm (recommended size S & M), I've been riding the bike with a +10mm (recommended size L & XL), and there's a +20mm option as well (recommended size 2XL & 3XL). The base rear center length is 419mm, so mine start at 429mm with the +10mm dropouts. That rear center grows 15mm at sag, and over 20mm at the peak point through travel.

+10mm dropouts installed.

Enough chainstay chat for now, as the rest of the VHP16's geometry is well-sorted for the use case. The 493mm reach on the XL is a little shorter than some brands' standard, but I enjoy the feel of it when paired with the fairly high 652mm stack. There are only two head tube lengths across the 6 size run, so stack heights don't scale as well as some of the other geometry figures - luckily for me, the XL feels like the sweet spot for the longer bikes.


The 64° head angle feels plenty slack to me, but is a little steeper than some options in this space. Kavenz wanted to keep the handling sharp enough for good handling across the board, and when considering the sweep of geometries on tap with the frame configuration options I'd say the VHP16 lands at a great middle ground. Same goes for the seat angle, which sits at 77.5° - could be a bit steeper, but the high anti-squat of the bike keeps you from sagging out over the rear axle.

Suspension Design

Kavenz employs a Horst link layout for all of their VHP bikes, with a high main pivot and idler wheel to control chain growth. That idler allows for rather high anti-squat values, without resulting in significant pedal kickback at the feet.

The anti-rise curves are fairly level, sitting low enough to preserve geometry and allow for suspension movement while braking.

The VHP 16's axle path has quite a bit of rearward displacement as the suspension cycles, with a maximum growth of 20.8mm around 75% of travel.

Leverage.

With around 30% total progression, the leverage rate of the bike should play well with both air and coil shocks. Even when using the chainstay length modifiers, the leverage doesn't change too drastically, just increasing slightly with the longer lever arm created by a longer chainstay, as you'd expect.

Those who want to dig into every last kinematic output can rejoice, as Kavenz has published an impressively thorough document for reference. It includes every model and permutation of their VHP bike, so you can compare outputs across the platform.













RIDING THE
VHP16

Test Bike Setup

The VHP16 came to me as a frame with the gearbox installed, so the remainder of the build was left up to test components and a healthy parts bin. I chose components I was familiar with, as the frame itself is enough of a variable in this case.

The Fox 38, cockpit, and tires came off my personal bike, as did the Industry Nine Hydra 2 wheelset. Those wheels felt like a key spec choice, as the gearbox itself has a fair bit of deadband before engaging, making high engagement hubs a useful addition to the build.

As mentioned before, I used this bike to test a wide variety of brakes out through the summer, so it sported many a set over the test period.

Kavenz provided a Fox Float X2 with their chosen shock tune, which I set to the recommended 28% sag at 190psi.

All of my initial riding on the Kavenz was alone, grinding up fire roads to break in the gearbox and get used to the feel of the system. Overall, I'd say the ideal scenario is winching yourself uphill to access steep, rough descents with exciting riding. Luckily for me, there's a lot of that in our neck of the woods.



Climbing

Climbing the VHP16 is a mixed bag. On one hand, the bike itself does a good job of preserving geometry, feeling efficient, and maintaining traction at the rear wheel. On the other hand, my initial impressions on the gearbox and the secondary drive system were profoundly negative. I hated pedaling this bike, and avoided doing so until I eventually forced myself to deal with it and commit to the bit.

At this point, after months of pedaling the VHP16 around, I can genuinely say that I'm happy I doubled down and put the miles in, as time has been kind to the climbing experience. The Pinion went from feeling awful, to bad, to its current state, which I'd call perfectly adequate. There is far, far less resistance felt through the pedals, and the pings and pops from to the gearbox have diminished as the miles have added up.

It's still not a bike I reach for if I'm trying to push pace on the climbs, or if riding trails where high-speed pedaling is required for momentum or features, but it finally feels efficient enough for group rides and big days out.

Pinion quotes their fresh gearbox efficiency at a peak of 93-95% efficiency, measured in output at the chainring vs. the input at the crank. The gearbox is more efficient in the larger effective gears (those used for climbing), and less so in the smaller gears (those used for sprinting / descending) due to the friction in the output shaft. Pinion claims that the efficiency improves by 1-1.5% after a sufficient break-in period.


The Pinion gearbox is still only one element of the drivetrain, with the chain system adding its own complication and efficiency losses. I can't speak to any comparisons between the chain and a belt, but I will say that compared to other high pivot bikes I've spent time with in the past, the Kavenz drive system is fairly quiet. The main idler isn't annoyingly loud, surely helped by the constantly straight chainline, and the novel o-ring tensioner pulleys have proven to be a smart solution.

Though it's hard to separate the bike and the drivetrain in this case, I do think Kavenz has done a good job of keeping this long-travel rig feeling alive, even despite the high curb weight. At a hair under 40lb / 18kg, it's far from a light bike, but it carries that weight well and doesn't bleed too much energy into suspension movement.

The traction and bump absorption delivered by the suspension makes technical climbing a little bit more doable, though the challenge of shifting mid-output does complicate that a bit. Essentially, the system will deny you a shift if too much power is going through the system, so time your shifts right and avoid doing so while pedaling when possible. If your cadence isn't changing massively, the bike motors up ledgy, rooty jank well, but don't expect to ping through gears as you might with a standard drivetrain.


Descending

Given the extra work it takes to get the VHP16 to the top of the hill, you'd certainly hope it made the most of things on the descents. Luckily, that's very much the case, as Kavenz delivered a very well-sorted high pivot enduro bike, competitive with the best of 'em. The balance and handling will depend on how you choose to set your bike up out back, with wheel size, chainstay length, and to some degree drivetrain configuration all affecting the descending performance. For me, in the MX-wheeled +10mm chainstay setup, the VHP felt like it balanced stability and fun in a way that was instantly encouraging.

The static wheelbase of the bike is fairly compact, with a tucked-in rear end and a head angle that isn't as extreme as other options in this category. As you push into the bike, things open up and build stability, giving traction at the front wheel and support through the feet. I found this characteristic remained true even under heavy braking, where other high-pivots of this sort can start to fall apart. The Kavenz gives traction when you actively load the bike, without squatting too much under braking and robbing you of bump performance through rougher sections of trail.


Steeper turns are a notably fun time on this bike, as you can pivot around the front and swing the short back end around, with things splaying out upon touchdown and keeping the chassis stable. Some rides on the North Shore confirmed that this bike was great on slower-speed stunts as well, where the tidy wheelbase comes in clutch. The combination of factors here makes for a bike that performs well at speed, while keeping things fun when the trails are pokier and more about slow-speed handling.

I've struggled with how un-intuitive some high pivots feel through corners and under braking, but the VHP aligns with bikes like the Trek Slash that manage those moments nicely. A brief adjustment period is all it took for me to get a feel for what the bike was doing and taking advantage of it. Expect the growing rear center, and trust that things will hook up as you push through the feet.


The suspension feel is very sensitive, and does a fantastic job of erasing bumps. I was able to add quite a bit of compression damping to the shock without any negative feedback, while giving a decent platform to push into. As you'd expect with the rearward axle path, the VHP rolled over square edge hits without upsetting the chassis.

As for what the gearbox provides on the descent, it's a fairly simple story. The bike is really damn quiet, with absolutely no chainslap or flail to speak of. The ability to shift while coasting is another bonus, allowing for some predictive shifting in situations that require such a thing. It took me a few rides to get used to this feature, but I've come to really enjoy it on trails with abrupt shifts between descents and climbs. Due to the increased drag in the higher-speed gears, the VHP is not a bike you'll want to sprint at any point; I'd recommend working with momentum to keep the speeds up. This was only ever an issue on days when you had to pedal into jumps that need a certain speed, requiring a bit of extra oomph to get the Kavenz up to velocity.

How does it compare?

Four long-travel high pivot bikes, all with their own responses to the prompt. I've ridden all four of these bikes (3 of them this week), and though sizing is a bit varied across the models, the ride characteristics are the biggest differentiator.

Slash: I'd call this one of the more normal-feeling high pivots I've ridden, maintaining a predictable feel through the feet while still giving that extra bit of top-end comfort through rough terrain. The braking performance is the closest to the Kavenz in terms of staying active and avoiding too much squat. We didn't award this Bike of the Year for nothing, it sets a very high bar.

Vanth: To me this feels like the most compromised of the bunch, with compromises made to integrate the gearbox into a bike that could use more development on other more critical fronts. Chief amongst those details is the fact that even a 240mm dropper post isn't long enough for me to run at the minimum safe insertion, since the seat tube length is shared across the full size range. To me this basically renders the Large and XL bikes unrideable for a swath of their potential customers - a tragedy on an otherwise interesting and novel bike. I will give major bonus points for their clever tensioner system, and the simple choice of running a belt instead of a chain; it's quieter and easier to live with.

Dreadnought: The geometry is the star of the show here, and that show is hyperfocused on stability at speed. I rode the Nought as a full-29" bike in the +0 chainstay position, giving it a starting rear center of 460mm that grows 25mm through travel. The suspension performance isn't as sensitive as the Kavenz, though the bike does feel composed on big hits and through rough sections, as long as you stay off the brakes. As a whole the bike doesn't feel as well-rounded as some of the others here, but if keeping the front wheel glued to the ground is your main objective then it's hard to beat.

VHP16: I think the modularity of the VHP16 straddles the gap between the Slash and Dreadnought nicely, keeping things fun on a wide variety of trails and retaining an excellent suspension feel. Competing with the Slash on this front is a major accolade, as the Trek really holds its own on rough descents. The livability of the Pinion integration is a different matter entirely, but with a normal drivetrain configuration the VHP is up there with the best of them.

Which Model is the Best Value?

Well, there's only one option here, but there are two flavors of the frame to discuss. The G2 frame is considerably more expensive when you factor in the cost of the Pinion setup, but obviously the standard VHP frame doesn't include the hypothetical cost of a drivetrain. Realistically, most standard drivetrain builds are going to be much cheaper than an electronic gearbox, but high end wireless kits like XTR and XX will hit a similar price.

For the variability, uniqueness, and quality of the Kavenz frame, I think the base value is quite good. The aftermarket adjustability of the bike means you'll be able to alter the experience to match what you're hoping to get out of the bike down the line, which keeps options open.

I'd far sooner spring for a standard drivetrain layout, keep your options open, and pocket the cash difference for a riding trip or some fancy cheese.


Technical Report

Pinion C.12 Smartshift: In and of itself, I think there's a compelling case to be made for the new Pinion system. Integration of said system is where the friction lies, as the required tensioners and other ancillary bits are part of what held this Kavenz build back. I have my complaints about the drag and rough spots in Pinion's system, but generally it has delivered on what they set out to do - put a drivetrain in a box that does a pretty good job.

Industry Nine Hydra 2 Wheelset: I published my long-term review of these wheels when they launched, and my thoughts haven't changed much in the time since, despite accruing a lot more mileage. They've proven easy to true, robust enough for bike park usage, and as worry-free as proprietary aluminum spokes can ever be. Critically, the hubs have been rolling along same as day one, with no axle or drivering issues to report.

Hope Tech 4 V4 Brakes: You don't see them in the product photos here, but the Tech 4 V4 featured heavily on this build, as part of a larger brake comparison test Matt and I are putting together. That'll be live sometime before 2030, but suffice to say that the Hope's have really impressed me. The control they have over a massive well of power makes riding steep and technical terrain easier, and the lever feel is quite nice to boot.



Pros

+ Quiet descents, coast shifting
+ Good geometry with meaningful adjustments
+ Exceptional traction & bump absorption


Cons

- Gearbox drag
- Flawed chain tension system
- Heavy frame


Pinkbike's Take

The Kavenz VHP16 is an extremely unique bike, but in a way that novelty hides what it does best: ride rough and challenging trails very well. Their integration of the Pinion gearbox needs work, and the gearbox itself will weed out a lot of potential users, but the geometry, suspension, and modularity of the frame add up to a compelling package for the right rider. Despite my frustrations, I kept pulling it out for the right rides.—Dario DiGiulio
