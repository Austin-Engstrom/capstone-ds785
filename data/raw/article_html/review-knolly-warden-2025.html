Bike design is a holistic process, with many equally important details to balance and factor in. How one goes about chasing those details and optimizing things for a given purpose can yield drastically different results, and is part of what makes the mountain bike world so interesting. Knolly is a brand that has always approached things differently, with some radical geometry figures and a novel suspension design.

Billed as the most composed descending bike in their lineup, the Warden aims to take on the gnarliest trails around. I've been putting it to that test for the past few months - read on for the long-term impressions.

Frame Details

The straight top tube might be the most apparent update to the Warden's frame, but there are a host of details to highlight besides the new design language. Each size features room for a water bottle, with an additional pair of mounts on the underside of the toptube for tools or spares.

BC tough.
Rear cable orifice.

Dropper post insertion is quite good on the bike, with maximum insertion depths ranging from 260mm on the XS to 420mm on the XL. That means even the smallest bike in the lineup should be able to run a 170-190mm dropper post.

Cables are routed internally through the front triangle, and external on the rear stays. While they do run unguided and require some fishing to snake through the frame, the sharp bends that the routing requires keeps them from rattling and causing a ruckus.

Good clearance.
Hard to forget which bike you're riding.


Geometry

Knolly describes the Warden's geometry as "freeride-focused," with the lengths and angles landing at some fittingly extreme figures to match the expected terrain. Located at the lower shock mount, the bike features a Neutral/Slack geometry adjustment, with the Slack position dropping the head angle to a very raked out 62.5°. The higher mode is still fairly extreme, landing at 63.25° on all sizes. That flip chip also affects seat angle and bottom bracket offset - the seat angle lands at either 77.75° in Neutral or 77° in Slack, and the BB drop is either 0.5mm in Neutral or 9.5mm in Slack.


Knolly's approach to the relationship between stack and reach is a bit unusual, and worth digging into. You'll note that reach numbers are quite long, especially if aligned with other brand's named sizes - though I'd generally caution against buying a bike purely based on the t-shirt size. Those long reach numbers are paired with very short stack heights across the board, verging on the extreme in the larger sizes. The logic here is that one would run a long stack of spacers underneath the stem to reach the desired stack height, while simultaneously bringing the reach in a bit as those bars get closer to you. In practice, there's only so much adjustment on tap, as steer tube length, aesthetics, and the eventual tradeoff of reach reduction come into play.

My setup ended up relying on a full stack of stem spacers and a rather high riser bar, but more on that later.


Suspension Design

Knolly implements their unique Fourby4 suspension design in all of their full-suspension bikes, with distinct implementations to cater to each use case. Principally, the design is a Horst link layout, with an additional link driving the shock. The outputs aren't drastically different than a standard 4-bar on paper, but feel quite unique in application. The 205x62.5mm shock delivers 170mm of travel, with 28.3% progression in the Neutral geometry setting, and 29.8% in the Slack position.

I reached out to Noel Buckley, Knolly's founder and chief engineer, to get a sense for the kinematic outputs they settled on for the Warden.

On anti-squat:
Obviously somewhat suggestive based on rider height, frame size, cassette cog, chainring size, etc... Easy to fudge as well to get a "determined result". And... I think my opinion on using anti-squat as the definitive "Be All, End All" in terms of describing pedaling efficiency is pretty well known: "I.e. It might be useful on a rear suspension road bike with a rigid fork, climbing on pavement at a fixed grade in ideal weather." We like a bike that's active and able to follow terrain. We're not afraid to say that you can use the lockout lever on the rear shock if you're riding and climbing a ton of pavement. The reason for this is quite simply, if you build too much anti-squat into the frame, you can't remove it and it will negatively affect your traction and pre-load your rear suspension due to increased chain tension. So, we aim to take a balanced approach to non-technical terrain, technical terrain and difficult terrain when pedaling, while allowing the suspension to do its job with terrain following.

Anyway - some numbers...
95% - 108% static dependent on cassette cog.
About 5% less at sag.

On anti-rise:
We like a bike that's active under braking. We do like to have some fork dive offset, but we also want to ensure that the rear of the bike is free to follow the terrain and not pack up due to excessive braking forces. What some riders call "brake jack" is actually the rear suspension unloading when the rear wheel breaks contact with the ground: we want to ensure that this never happens to our customers. Our philosophy has always been to ensure that the more serious the trail conditions, the better the ability of the bike to respond to those conditions. It's easy to make a bike that works well when things are calm; it's when things are going sideways that you want your bike to be performing at its maximum and this is especially true for bikes like the Warden which are designed explicitly for aggressive riding.

On rear travel and long-stroking:
The Warden ships stock with a 205 x 62.5mm stroke shock, which generates 170mm of travel. The bike can use the full 65mm stroke of the shock which generates about 180mm of rear travel, which is the maximum travel that the bike's rear suspension is capable of. On the front end: forks from 170 - 203mm travel are allowed.

RIDING THE
Warden

Test Bike Setup
The purple size large Knolly came with familiar parts tip to tail, making most of the setup fairly easy. That said, the bike still required a fair bit of puzzling to get dialed into a place where I felt comfortable. One key facet was shock setup, which was more of a drawn-out process than I'm used to, due to the bike's unusual suspension characteristics. Though the numbers seem fairly standard on paper, the ride feel says otherwise - more on that later.

I started with 30% sag, but found myself incrementally increasing pressure until I settled around 225psi, putting me in the mid-20% range for sag. I stuck to a fork setup that felt comfortable, with 105 psi and close to centered LSC and HSC. Shock compression was more regularly adjusted, depending on terrain. I found that I generally preferred the support a firmer LSC provided, but avoided closing things up too much so not to choke it out - opening things up unsurprisingly helped the bike sit deeper on steep terrain, where grip was paramount. HSC was more neutral, and sat at 3-5 clicks for the most part.

The most critical change I made was to the bike's cockpit, in order to compensate for the very low stack height. Getting my hands higher helped the handling massively, and felt like a pretty critical change for my riding style and preference.

Testing Info

The Warden does best on natural terrain where gravity does a lot of the work, and where controlling speed is the key to success. I took the Warden on many such rides, where a patient pedal up yields some fantastic raw descending. There are many good long descents in this world, but Whistler's Top of the World to Creekside is pretty hard to beat.



Climbing

Put simply, this is a lockout bike. I've had quite a few 170mm enduro bikes this year, and of the bunch this is the most lethargic to pedal. You can achieve a more supportive and energetic feel, but doing so requires a shock setup that compromises some of the bike's descending performance. As mentioned in the setup section, I ended up settling on a firmer shock setup to achieve the feel I wanted. You can get the bike to a place where you're not reaching for the lock switch on every sustained climb, but I think it's okay to just accept that step if you find yourself climbing sustained roads on a regular basis. Singletrack climbing benefits from the grip provided by the rear wheel, even if the overall efficiency isn't great. I found the Warden easy enough to get up and over some weird technical obstacles, thanks to the active suspension and very high bottom bracket.

One geometry element that can work against you on the climbs is the combination of the head angle and stack figures. The Warden sports a very slack head angle at either 63.25° or 62.5°, and the stack height sits at a very low 628mm in the size Large / S4. Where a low stack can feel racy and powerful on cross-country bikes, that feel pairs better with the steering angles typical to that category, not those more often seen on downhill bikes. The combination here makes for a front end that wants to wander and flop, unless you're actively keeping the bike in line. Once you get a feel for how the bike wants to be ridden, you can get along just fine, but I wouldn't call the handling intuitive. Despite that, you can pedal through all the jank, thanks to a higher than normal bottom bracket and traction-rich suspension.


Descending

The mountain bike world is full of unique and purpose-built creations, and the Warden strikes me as one of the more niche. The geometry of the bike is unusual and challenging in many situations, but might make sense for some people in some places. Specifically, I think that place might be the North Shore, where steep rolls, awkward tech, and generally rough trail beds are predominant. I found the Knolly hard to ride well in most trail settings, but when riding on its home turf things made the most sense. Still, I think there are some points to pull apart here, and discuss how the geometry and suspension work together in a unique way.

First, the suspension - traction-heavy and even-keeled. I'm very impressed by how little feedback reaches your feet through the roughest and most chopped out trails, even under hard braking. Knolly's Fourby4 does offer something special here, though you will have to spend more time on shock setup to reach an ideal balance. As mentioned in the Climbing section, some compromises are required to get the bike to offer the support required to make speed on some trails, but if you're indexing more heavily on the compliance and grip end of things, you should be happy with a fairly standard setup. I was willing to give up some of that traction for more support through the stroke, but not everyone will want to make the same compromise.

The geometry of the Warden might make sense for some specific moves and situations, but overall I'd say it's the bike's weak point. Given the low stack, long reach, and high bottom bracket, the body position is very much over the bike, as opposed to the fuzzy in-bike feel that tends to be a goal for designs in this category. Bringing the cockpit up to the highest point on the steer tube (as cut by factory) and adding a 50mm rise OneUp bar did help, but ultimately those are unusual setup constraints many other bikes don't require. Generally, I felt that the bike was more stable and easy to handle in the Slack geometry setting, mostly due to the lower bottom bracket height. Unfortunately, that also gives you the more extreme head tube angle, sitting at a static 62.5°. This requires a conscious weighting of the front wheel through corners, but does provide plenty of stability at speed.

I'd be curious to try the bike in the Slack position with a +1° headset to bring the steering back into a more multi-purpose position, but that's a bit beyond the purview of a typical review. Overall I'd say the Warden is well suited to folks who want to point straight down something steep and hold on through the rough - just be sure to grab a set of high-rise bars first.


The Warden is a quiet bike, thanks to the general lack of chainslap and simple-yet-effective cable management. I'd still prefer the cables to be externally routed, but despite the required fishing and fuss, the internal routing that Knolly implemented does at least keep things from rattling. One small complaint about that routing comes down to the rear orifice where they go into the frame, which essentially acts as a catchment for all the water and debris that your rear tire kicks up. Water can easily follow the cables into the frame, and the bolt showed rust after only a couple months.

How does it compare?

These bikes have similar goals, but go about achieving things in very different ways. Both sport 170mm travel front and rear, both aim to provide a stable ride and composure through rough terrain, but they differ massively on the geometry front. Where the Knolly achieves wheelbase via a slack headtube angle and long reach, the Frameworks adds length to the chainstay - 18mm, to be exact. As a result, the Knolly necessitates more conscious weighting of the front wheel in corners and through awkward tech, where the Frameworks allows you to keep your weight a bit more central and neutral, freeing up some body English to navigate things.

The other stark difference between the two geometries comes down to the stack height. The Frameworks boasts 50mm higher stack than the Knolly, and in my experience that benefit comes without any negative tradeoffs. As you'd expect, the riding position is far more upright both up and down the hill, with plenty of rider area to work with. Though climbing isn't the main attraction with either of these rigs, I'm going to give the nod to the Frameworks on that front as well. A very steep seat tube angle and supportive-enough pedaling platform make for a bike that is far easier to pedal all day, with a comfortable upright seated position to boot.


Which Model is the Best Value?

Of the three complete builds available, I'd opt for the middle of the range Eagle 90 build. The frameset options will probably be compelling to some, but for those that want to pack as many of the best products on one build, the Eagle 90 kit is probably the one I'd recommend. The Mavens are vastly better brakes than last-gen Shimano XT or Deore, and the RockShox supension suits the Warden's nature - namely the extra-firm lockout on the shock. Beyond that, the builds are mostly the same, so one could just buy the Deore build and upgrade bits here and there as they see fit.


Technical Report

Shimano XT 4-Piston Brakes: One of these brakes was plagued with issues since the get-go, exhibiting some unusually sticky lever action, and the typical wandering bite point. I was able to get them feeling decent with a good bit of work, but ultimately treated it as an opportunity to spend time on the new Magura Gustavs - birds and stones.

Maxxis EXO+ Tires: The Assegai/DHRII tire combo is a modern classic for good reason, but this casing choice makes no sense on a bike built for freeride lines and gnarly trails. You can run high pressures and hope for the best, but inevitably a flat or rim dent is sure to occur if you're riding this bike in the intended terrain. My guess is this was a spec choice made purely for build weight, so hopefully you have a shorter travel bike you can rotate the light tires onto.

Fox X2 Shock: The last-generation X2 that I've been riding on this bike has done a good job of managing forces and surviving the test unscathed. That said, it seems the bike is now shipping with the updated monotube version of the shock, which should alleviate any lasting longevity concerns one might have about Fox's fattest air shock. I think the tune works nicely with the bike, and though the suspension took a good bit of fussing to get dialed, it was helped by the many adjustments you can make to the X2's damper. I haven't ridden the Warden with the Vivid, but given the progressive nature of the bike, I'd be curious how the hydraulic bottom out interacts with that end of stroke travel.



Pros

+ Suspension feel is quiet and composed
+ High quality frame
+ Dual crown & long-stroke potential


Cons

- The low stack, slack head angle, and long reach can cause awkward handling.
- Delicate setup to balance traction and support
- Climbing performance dependent on shock lockout


Pinkbike's Take

Knolly's unique suspension design offers an impressive amount of grip and composure through rough terrain, but the Warden's geometry feels compromised and challenging in most situations. You can make setup alterations to help the bike feel more well-rounded, but ultimately it feels like a purpose-build tool for a very specific niche. As I mentioned earlier, it's going to work best for riders whose typical trails are steep and rough, just be prepared to do some cockpit and suspension adjustments to find the sweet spot.—Dario DiGiulio
