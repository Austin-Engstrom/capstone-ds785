Review: Schwalbe's Shredda Gravity Tires are a Loamer Cheat Code

Schwalbe's recent release of their radial casing tires made quite the splash, with material properties and a ride feel that differs greatly from existing tire tech. Nested in that release was something wilder still: their new Shredda Gravity design. These are possibly the most aggressive tread we've seen on a mountain bike tire to date, with a comically deep tread depth and massive lugs. The Shredda has a front and rear specific tread design, with both tires molded with Schwalbe's Ultra Soft rubber in a 29x2.5", with a 27.5" option available for the Rear.

Positioned as an eMTB-specific design, the Shreddas certainly have some moto influence, at least in the aesthetics department. Wanting to see how that grip shaped up on pedal bikes and their electric counterparts alike, I've been running both the front and rear Shreddas for a couple months now to see how they stack up. This has conveniently coincided with some of the wetter weather the Pacific Northwest has seen in years, as it turns out that's pretty much the ideal weather pattern for these.
Shredda Front
• Compound: Addix Ultra Soft
• Casing: Gravity Pro Radial
• Sizes: 29x2.5" only
• Actual weight: 1494g
• Price: $104 USD
• schwalbetires.com

Shredda Rear
• Compound: Addix Ultra Soft
• Casing: Gravity Pro Radial
• Sizes: 29x2.5", 27.5x2.5"
• Actual weight: 1509g
• Price: $104 USD
• schwalbetires.com


Shredda Front.
Shredda Rear.


Shredda Front Tread

The Shredda Front uses a pretty typical 2-2-2-2 center block pattern, with a consistent row of edge lugs, but it's the knob height that really sets this thing apart from just about anything else on the market. The lugs are massive, with enough support to keep them from folding over on anything firm. Every other pair of center lugs has a short bridge to help with braking traction and overall stability, but otherwise the knobs are interrupted. There is minimal siping, and not much else to mention - this thing is just about digging in and clearing soil, no matter how deep it gets.




Shredda Rear Tread

The Shredda Rear is a bit more involved, with a 3-2-2-3 center pattern and a consistent edge lug pattern with slightly varied spacing. The space between lugs is similar to the Front tread, but the knob height isn't quite as extreme. The 2-block pairs are joined with a bridge, and the 3-block sets are spaced wide enough to serve as a great cornering transition. It somewhat resembles the Magic Mary, but with a more open and aggressive pattern. Siping is similarly minimal to the Front, to keep the knobs stable.




TESTING

Download the Trailforks App

It's hard to feature a testing area for the Shreddas, because all the trails where they shine are usually quite well hidden. Think steep, off-camber, and with the deepest soil you can conjure up in your mind. They also feel pretty happy in mud, but more on that later. Ultimately, they're a very location-dependent tread pattern, so don't go buying a set for your local hardpack tracks.

PERFORMANCE

The Shredda Gravity tires are a singular offering for now, coming only in Schwalbe's stickiest rubber and burliest Radial casing. Weighing in at about 1500 grams each, these are certainly not going to appeal to those concerned with keeping things spritely, but the mass pays dividends on the descents. The lack of a 27.5" rear variant is odd to me, considering the eMTB-specific claims Schwalbe is making and the fact that most long-travel e-bikes have mixed wheel sizes these days. It sounds like a smaller rear offering is in the works, so hopefully we'll see that before the deep winter soil dries up.

*Editor's Note: as of publication, there is a 27.5" Rear option, rejoice.

Despite being quite durable and supportive (once you've honed in on a slightly higher pressure), Schwalbe's new Radial casings are impressively pliant and comfortable, offering a similar feel to running lighter tires with inserts. On my non-electrified bike, I stuck to running 25 psi in the front and 27 psi in the rear during drier months, but have recently dropped both by about 2 psi for the very wet, very sloppy winter conditions we're currently enjoying. The additional weight and speed of an eMTB warranted a couple psi more than the pedal bike, but your mileage may vary. All testing has taken place on 30mm internal width rims, with no flats, leaks, or wobbles to report.

ROLLING SPEED
I mean c'mon, just look at them. These are probably some of the slowest rolling tires I've ridden, but they're not quite as heinous as I'd initially feared. The drag is in keeping with other mid-spikes and mud tires, with only a bit more of a rumble on hardpack and road surfaces. The rear tire (as a front) feels totally acceptable, and the front is just a bit behind that speed-wise. As a pair, expect to burn some extra watts, but that's the price you pay for digging deep.

TRACTION
Schwalbe's Ultra Soft rubber stacks up amongst the best when it comes to smearing grip, with predictable and acceptable loss in traction over wet rocks and roots. In the dry, the slow rebound and tacky grip impress just the same, especially if you have the larger contact patch that the Radial casing can offer. That said, the contact patch with the Shreddas is going to be somewhat minimized by the fact that you're dancing on spikes, as opposed to a more consistent and dense tread pattern like the Albert. That only really comes into play on hard surfaces like rocks and roots, and ultimately it just seems like the Shredda Front was designed to avoid those and aim for the patches of dirt between. The Shredda Rear does have a bit more contact patch to play with, and has felt predictable on slabs, root beds, and through rock gardens.

Braking traction is as impressive as you'd expect, with the tires digging in no matter how loose the soil gets. A true mud spike might still be the better choice for sticky, slimy mud, but the Shreddas bridge that gap pretty nicely, and feel comfortable on firmer trails thanks to the soft casing.


CORNERING
The Shredda Front digs in like a rubber cleat, allowing for some pretty wild maneuvers anywhere there's soft dirt. With a very simple and linear pattern, there's a bit of a breakaway between the center lugs and the edge, but it never really lets you drift. There's also the matter of where you're likely to ride this tire, as it's really not for hard conditions or embedded trails. The Front can hold its own there, but feels a little vague compared to shorter-lugged alternatives. I'd reach for this when I'm riding the deepest loamers, and perhaps for sandy loose soil in some steep desert zones, but otherwise the Rear is possibly the better option.

Despite being shoehorned as a back-tire specific design, the Shredda Rear has proved to be an excellent all-rounder for the front of the bike in soft conditions. The pattern corners perfectly in soft soil, and doesn't feel too vague or washy on harder-packed trails. I probably won't be running one all summer, but if I had to choose between the two this is the one I'd reach for as a front tire for any given ride.

DURABILITY
Most of my Shredda testing has taken place in the cold wet months of fall and winter, where rubber wear is at an all time low. That said, I have more than enough miles on the Front and Rear treads to say that they do hold up nicely, especially against undercut and knob shear, which were my initial concerns given the extreme design. If you were committed to the naming convention, and did run the Rear on the back of an eMTB, I'd expect the center knobs to diminish fairly quickly, as the Ultra Soft rubber wears away fairly quickly under braking and climbing miles. On the bright side, you could wear the tires well past the 50% mark before they started resembling other more traditional mid-spike tires on the market.

On the casing front, Schwalbe's new Radial Gravity casing has been impressively durable, even through the initial trial period of seeing just how low I could run pressures. They saw plenty of rim hits during that period, but have remained unscathed. These tread patterns are definitely not designed for harder, rock-strewn trails, but my experience with the Radial casings tells me that they'd survive just fine should you choose to do so.


Pros
+ Dominate the landscape
+ Extreme tread depth digs into deep soil
+ Excellent casing, sticky rubber

Cons
- Heavy, slow on hardpack trails
- Front tread is squirmy on hard surfaces




Pinkbike's Take
The new Shredda Gravity might just be the most extreme tread pattern we've seen of late, but it's not all for show. If - and only if - you have the right conditions, these tires deliver incredible traction and predictability. — Dario DiGiulio

Turns, bermed turns, ascending and descending... so possible increase in displacement in the conditions representing the best parts of mtb trails

Thicker, heavier casings at lower pressure rebound slowly but also don't track as well because of the casing stiffness. Thinner, lighter casings are more responsive to tracking the ground, even with higher air pressure.
