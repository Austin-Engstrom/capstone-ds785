Starting from scratch takes commitment, and that's exactly what Rossignol has done with this redesigned Heretic. A new alloy tube set, robust bearing hardware, and adjusted kinematics make this bike an entirely different beast from the old version.

The travel figures have gone up as well - the rear end now sits at 165mm, and the fork is properly outfitted for enduro racing or stunts at 170mm. Rossignol stands by the dedication to 29" wheels, however, the frame is now MX-compatible. Given that this is an alloy frameset, it doesn't land on the light side at 16.5 kg (36.3 lb), but it's not a total tank either.

With four models in the lineup, the Heretic offers great value at nearly all levels. This GX model costs $6,200 USD and is laden with crowd-favorite components all around.

After giving the Heretic a thumbs up in our First Ride, we spent the better part of the summer ragging on this very purple machine to find out how the frame hardware and components would hold up over the long run.

Frame Details

Rebuilt and oversized, the Heretic pivot hardware is solid. A large main pivot with a locking collet-style axle keeps everything where it should be. On top of that, the welded 3-piece rocker link is sturdy-looking.

You won’t find internal frame storage in the downtube, but if you look under the BB, you’ll notice the rubber protector doubles as a home for a multi-tool and tire lever. It can be a bit of a nuisance to remove, and it'll get caked with mud, so save it for emergencies. Thankfully, it never rattled or wiggled loose.

Further supplies can be bolted to the underside of the top tube, plus there’s room for a water bottle inside the front triangle on all sizes. Cable routing runs through the downtube and into foam tubing, then it's locked in place by bolt-in guides.

The rear triangle looks well guarded, and the cable routing is neatly laid out. A rubber mud flap on the chainstay yoke does the trick without any squeaks, but the chainslap protection stops short of the dropout pivot and higher along the seatstay. It's a minor woe and nothing some mastic tape can't solve.

A slick mud flap and double-sided bash guard.
The rocker link and pivots are seriously beefed up compared to the previous Heretic.

Suspension Design

Rossignol stuck with a 4-bar Horst Link design, but made considerable changes to the kinematics. The most notable is the lower starting leverage rate with a more gradual increase in progression. That's said to jive well with either air or coil 205x65mm shocks.

Although I didn't experiment with a coil shock, Rossignol equips their top-of-the-line "Super" Heretic model with a RockShox Vivid Coil Ultimate.

The anti-squat and anti-rise values aren't huge deviations from the old Heretic, but I found the new bike to pedal and brake with much more stable, neutral handling.

The old Heretic (pink line) was much more progressive and tended to push through the initial part of the travel too easily.
The dashed orange and purple lines show the anti-squat of the new Heretic in a 50-tooth cog.
Anti-rise is a shade lower than the old Heretic, yet still higher than some other Horst link bikes.




Geometry

The reach of Heretic frame sizes is skewed slightly compared to most enduro bikes out there. Each size is about 10mm longer in that aspect than what I’d call the average fit. That puts the medium at a 464mm reach in the high, 29 geometry position. This seemed more appropriate for my height (178cm) and how intended to ride this park-duro bike.

In regards to the flip-chip, the high setting has a contemporary 64.5-degree head tube angle. Flipping that dropout pivot chip will cut the head angle down to 64 degrees. Rossignol actually calls these two positions Flow (high) and Attack (low). The flip-chip will cut down the reach by 5mm when dropping it into the low position.

If you’re tempted to run a 27.5” rear wheel, I'd take a close look at the geometry table. Doing so chops off the reach measurement by 8mm and puts the BB drop down to -35mm. The head angle also rakes out to 63.5 degrees and the seat tube angle tips back further again.

RIDING THE
Heretic

Test Bike Setup

I didn’t have to puzzle much in order to feel comfortable when I first hopped on the Heretic in the Utah desert. The components listed were well-proven and familiar. My only complaints were the short 150mm dropper post and light-duty tires. With the length of the seat tube, there’s no reason why a 180mm option couldn’t be fitted to the size medium frame. I swapped out for that longer post and some more durable casing tires, along with a softer compound up front.

For the suspension, I set the Fox 38 to 100 psi and added a second volume spacer from the stock setup. The rebound was set to the faster side of Fox’s recommendations, and the compression was more open.

The rear shock arrived without any volume spacer inside, and that meant increasing the air pressure to avoid bottom outs. Some small bump compliance was given up, so once back on home soil, I added a volume spacer. That allowed for the top end to stay supple while ramping up and took the sting out of heavy landings.

I started out with the sag at 25%. I settled around 190 psi, which equated to 21% sag, which is a much lower sag number than you usually hear of. The more I rode the Heretic, though, the more air I kept adding to the rear shock, and the better the bike handled. The low-speed compression was set to -11 clicks from close, and the high-speed to -8. On the rebound side, the low-speed was usually -11 or -12, and the high-speed stayed at -4.

Throughout the summer, I put the Heretic through every kind of ride: all-day, big mountain pedal missions around Squamish, and lift-assisted laps down A-Line. While its first instinct isn't to win the race to the top, it will happily plod up a road and come alive when it's time to have fun on feature-filled trails.




Climbing

Let’s not kid ourselves. The Heretic isn’t ever going to be a fast bike uphill. It’s a respectable weight for an alloy enduro bike, and competitive with others in its class, but climbing isn’t the Heretic’s forte.

The slacker seat tube angle sets the riding position further back into an overly relaxed position. Sliding the seat forward helped improve the situation, but only by a small margin. The climb switch gives a helping hand here, and also keeps you from smashing the cranks into the ground.

Realistically, the Heretic, like some other enduro bikes that advertise they can cope with both rear wheel sizes (such as the Giant Reign) is best left as a 29er if you’re planning on pedalling to the top. The MX mode may work for a gravity-focused rider with an uplift nearby, but if you are dead set on swapping out the rear wheel for a 27.5” option, sourcing shorter cranks would be advised.

Another element that detracts from its ability to clean challenging trail ups is the 18-tooth Star Ratchet hub. Unless you’re able to keep the cranks spinning through rock gardens, there’s a large lag in the engagement. That means you'll have to push the pedals further around before transferring power to the rear wheel. Luckily, that’s a simple upgrade the DT Swiss wheel, but that doesn't come at an insignificant cost.




Descending

You know what? For a full 29er, I dig how quickly the Heretic handles. It’s just as maneuverable as some other MX enduro bikes I’ve bounced on. It’s compact, yet balanced. The chainstay length in the low mode is a sensible 442mm. That also allows it to bob and weave through jumpy bits of trail without a laggy sense.

At the same time, the 29-inch wheels and deeper standing position give it security when mobbing high-speed sections of the bike park. Instead of relying on a super slack and floppy head tube angle, which can feel sluggish to pump through jump tracks or twisty turns, the balance of the geometry and big wheel combo gives it a grounded sensation.

Although the MX setup can work in the right gravity-based conditions, it’s a stretch for everyday trails or enduro racing. The bike becomes a totally different ride. You’ll have to bump up the air pressure in the shock as the dynamic geometry sways to a rearward weight bias as the angles seriously relax. Timing your pedal strokes perfectly is still challenging, and switching out for short cranks would be a smart move.

That does leave me wondering, who would want a compact 29er bike? I’d say someone who appreciates the roll over and grip of 29-inch wheel, yet still wants to rip bike parks with confidence. It spells success for enduro racing with big travel and brawn, but it’s all within a reasonable wheelbase.

When rallying through the bike park trails, you will notice feedback from the chain in two areas: one is noise, and the other is the chain feedback through your feet. In a few areas, the frame protection falls short of where the chain hits. Adding a few pieces of mastic tape helped quiet down those “tings”.

In order to aid the Heretic on technical climbs, I installed that 54-tooth Star Ratchet in the hub I mentioned earlier. Funny enough, I noticed how that upgrade also reduced the number of heavy kicks the cassette transferred into the pedals. An O-Chain will aid in that sense too, which is another component I tacked on for a short period of time. As expected, that calmed down the kickback, too.

A pleasant surprise was the little amount of upkeep I had to do on the hardware. The Heretic lived up to the hype of being durable and reliable. After the initial break-in and a quick bolt check, the pivots stayed tight, smooth, and creak-free. I tip my hat to the team behind the full redesign, as this bike suffered its fair share of braking bumps and a couple of deep sends.






Technical Report

Maven Bronze brakes: Nowadays, most SRAM brakes come with the HS2 rotors. Rossignol has chosen the thinner Centerline rotors and organic pads, which slightly reduces the amount of available braking power. In addition, the organic pads don't have the surge in power halfway through the lever stroke that the metallic pads do. I installed metallic pads (with fresh Centerline rotors) for part of the review to tap into that extra power the Mavens are known for, especially on extended fall line trails. This combo could be the sweet spot for riders who are currently using HS2 rotors but would prefer a smoother transition to peak power.

Maxxis EXO+ tires: Ask any rider who’s tried the Assegai-DHR II tread pairing - they’re exceptional, but in this case, these aren’t the optimal specifications. The Heretic can motor downhill, and it isn’t in a hurry to go back up, so it would make sense to take the hit for a couple hundred more grams. Selecting Double Down casing tires front and rear, as well as a softer front tire compound, would make the most of the Heretic’s descending capabilities.

SDG Tellis V2 post: Another solid product, but the wrong spec. The medium frame has a long enough seat tube to accommodate a 180mm post slammed in the frame. 150mm is too short for the rider height recommendation set out for the size medium frame.




Which Model is the Best Value?

As a tech editor, I admittedly get drawn towards bikes and components on the higher end, mostly for performance reasons, but sometimes for weight. The Heretic Deore 12 build kit breaks that thinking. At $3,300 USD, it’s a heck of a deal with a workhorse of a drivetrain and buttery Bomber suspension. There’s also no carbon frame higher up the ladder to grasp for - the base model uses the same frame as the most expensive model.

The Deore brakes may lack the power of Mavens, and you’ll need to keep an eye on the wheel tension for those WTB i30 rims to last as long as possible, but all things considered, it’s a fair price in an ever-inflating market.

How Does It Compare?

It seems as though the 4-bar, Horst link suspension is becoming “the way” to build a mountain bike, and rightfully so. You can achieve a smooth leverage curve, build burly pivots, and still have room for a water bottle inside the front triangle. One of the people’s choice bikes using that design choice is the Raaw Madonna. It’s also aluminum, can handle both rear wheel sizes, and has similar travel and geometry numbers.

One factor that separates the two bikes is the sizing chart. The Madonna is available in 5 sizes, so the reach might align with your ideal number. The head and seat tube angles are in the same realm, as are the chainstays; 445mm on the size M Madonna, and 442mm on the Heretic (low position).

Some nifty setup changes can tailor the Madonna further to your liking, such as the chainstay length, but also the rocker link - yes, Raaw offers two rocker links to suit different rider weights.

Running a 27.5” rear wheel comes without any drawbacks, too. By switching out the lower shock mount, you can retain the same geometry laid out for the 29” rear wheel.

Considering the Madonna can be tuned to a finer degree, you can expect to pay more for the frame. For example, the Heretic SLX 12 model is the same price as the Raaw Madonna rolling chassis (that excludes brakes and the drivetrain).

Although it’s built darn tough, the extra oversized bearings do increase the weight of the Madonna - a size medium clocks in at 3.9 kg (8.6 lb) without a shock.




Pros

+ Solid parts choice for GX model, and still at a decent value.
+ Finds the sweetspot of being playful and composed.


Cons

- Dropper post is too short on all size frames.
- MX setting is too low and too slack for enduro riding and racing.


Pinkbike's Take

Rossignol have come a long way with redesigning the Heretic. From day one, I've enjoyed pushing this bike into burly terrain and sending sizeable hits. The MX mode doesn't convey that same sensation, as the full 29er package rides best. Unfortunately, that may turn away a fair number of riders from an otherwise solid enduro deal.— Matt Beer
