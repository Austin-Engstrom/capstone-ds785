The Crankbrothers Stamp EVO is a Comically Large Flat Pedal

Apr 8, 2026
by Dario DiGiulio

There may be more ways to design a flat pedal than there are to skin a cat, and the market reflects that. Every brand has a slightly different take, with small differences here and there to try and stand out from the crowd. Boldly standing out on merit of size alone is the new Crankbrothers Stamp EVO, which may just be the largest flat pedal you can buy.

These huge aluminum steps have a few unique features, but their primary calling card is going to be the size. With two options to choose from, small-footed folks have a variant too, but I'll be focusing on the Large version today.
Stamp EVO Large Details

• Weight: 464g (pair)
• Pins per side: 13
• Usable platform dimensions (W x L): 114 x 129mm
• Internals: 2 plastic IGUS bearings
• Two pedal colors, four plastics colors
• MSRP: $200 USD / EUR / GBP
• crankbrothers.com

Features

Both Large and Small Stamp EVO use an aluminum body with plastic bashguards on the outboard side, allowing for aesthetic repair and color accents, should you please. Those plastics come off easily once you've removed the outer corner pins, and will keep the aluminum from getting marred when you lay the bike down.

The Large EVOs are 114mm wide, and 129mm long (AKA: huge), giving them ample room to stomp around on. Crankbrothers recommends a minimum shoe size of a US 10 to run that pedal, with the small fitting US 3-10. I wear an US 11.5 (45 for the rest of y'all), for visual reference.

With 13 grub screws per side, you can wind some in and out to alter the relative height. One key exception is those outside corner pins, which have to be set in the middle, as they go all the way through the pedal to hold the plastics on.

The guts - a full parts breakdown can be found here. Photo from Crankbrothers.

The internals are simple and serviceable, with two IGUS bearings holding the spindle in place. I don't have nearly enough time on the pedals to comment on durability, but so far they're spinning smoothly and are free of play.

At the leading edge, the pedal is 15mm thick - the pin at that leading edge is 23mm end to end. The middle of the pedal is 12mm thick, with a 17mm end-to-end pin towards the outside edge. Concave, but not extreme.

Plastics doing their job.

Comparisons

The obvious comparison here is the previous high water mark for gigantic flat pedals: the Pedaling Innovations Catalyst. Very long, but relatively narrow, these have been the primary option for folks searching for the most for the past decade. The Stamp EVO matches the length within a millimeter, and adds 20mm of usable width.

Pedaling Innovations.
Cardboard proxy compared to Stamp EVO.
Tectonic.
Compared to Stamp EVO.

The Tectonic Alibi has a similar length at 128mm, but a narrower width at 108mm. That shape feels effective without adding too much bulk, and matches my foot size well. Once the whole forefoot is accounted for, I don't find myself hunting for more surface area on a flat. I'm sure folks with bigger feet might warrant more standing room, but they'll have to chime in below to flex their big dogs.



Closing Thoughts

Riding with the Stamp EVOs has been uneventful, save for the occasional person pointing and commenting on my gigantic pedals. The grip is just fine - not stellar, not horrible. With the shoes I'm used to riding in, I'd say they feel slightly less bitey than options with sharper/smaller diameter pins, be they threaded or straight-sided. Part of the issue could be how spread out the surface area of the shoe is, and the fact that there are times where your foot isn't even in contact with all of the pins at once. For folks who like a single locked-in position, these probably aren't your best fit.

For those on and off the bike a lot, or who like to reposition often, the jumbo Stamps might be worth trying out. Because there's so much surface area to stand on, it often feels like you've landed in a pretty decent spot, even if it's not the perfect position on the pedal.

I bet you're wondering: Dario, they're so big, don't you hit things on trail? Yes, yes I do. As the plastic guards can attest, the edges of the pedals feel like they contact more random trailside obstacles than the average, so keep your technique clean. Those scuffs and contact moments haven't amounted to anything too scary, but it's something to be aware of, especially if you're doing a lot of rocky, tight, awkward riding.

As to the guards themselves, I think they warrant existing on a pedal this big, only because they will probably pick up more scars than the average in a shorter period of time. I don't mind my parts showing some wear and tear if it feels earned, but in this case you may be happy to have a refreshed set of plastics now and then.

I'm curious if these appeal to the folks out there with massive feet, but for me I'm pretty satisfied with the scale of pedals as they are. Those Tectonic Alibis are still my personal favorite, so maybe that's big enough in my book.
