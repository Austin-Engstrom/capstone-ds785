Orbea’s all-new Rallon Enduro is undeniably one of the hottest bikes of the year. I don't think there was one negative comment when this bike first launched. It’s sleek, light, and feature-packed. Oh, and it uses the same frame to transform into the downhill model. To both the manufacturer and the consumer, it makes a lot of sense; two bikes in one. [Note: see my full review of the Rallon in DH mode here. It's a very different machine.]

Will anyone take the time to strip down one bike and swap out frame parts, plus various other components, to take advantage of this product design? Probably not. But I wouldn’t concern myself with that tedious conversion. In its enduro form, keeping up with downhillers on the Rallon isn’t a far-fetched idea.

Compared to the outgoing Rallon, the 2025 Rallon EN is a totally different bike. For starters, the travel has been bumped up 10mm at either end. The 7th-generation Rallon now rides on 170mm of travel with a 180mm fork. This bike is a beast, yet it’s relatively light for how much travel it has. Add in the fact that it can hug the ground like the DH version, yet responds quicker, and you have a true long-travel, go-anywhere machine.

Frame Details


The frame features found on the Rallon Enduro are the same as the Rallon DH, which I reviewed a couple of weeks back. In case you missed that, both Rallons include downtube storage under the water bottle mount, completely internal cable routing, and a hidden multi-tool in the rocker link pivot.

The full carbon frame uses design aesthetics similar to the Wild, Orbea’s full-powered E-bike, too. The lines aren’t just for looks, though (okay they might be largely for looks, but it does look great). An aggressively low top tube, unique seat tube alignment, and extra mass near the BB add up to a low centre of gravity, a trait unsurprisingly shared with the Rallon DH.

Hiding in the lower shock mount zone is the shapeshifting Gravity Link - the component responsible for transforming the Rallon frame from downhill to enduro modes. There is one gravity link for the DH version of the bike, which requires a different length shock, and two versions for this enduro model to account for different wheel sizes.

Onboard the Rallon Enduro Gravity Links is an extra weight bracket, but not as much as the DH version. Where the DH link has weights on the side, the enduro links have a 4-way adjustable flip-chip instead. This flip chip yields the option for high or low shock progression as well as two head angle/BB positions. That’s on top of the angle-adjusting headset, making it possible to rake the Rallon Enduro slacker than the DH version.



Boost Concentric 2: Orbea's single-pivot linkage-driven suspension. Technically, you could run the DH Gravity link and long shock to get 200mm of travel (with a 27.5" rear wheel only), but the anti-squat decreases significantly.

Suspension Design

Calling on the same front and rear triangles as the Rallon DH, as well as the rocker link, something has to change in order for the travel to be reduced. Nothing about the function of the Boost Concentric suspension design changes, aside from the shock length and where it mounts to the mainframe. It’s the manipulating Gravity Link that dictates where the shock sits.

The Rallon Enduro requires a 205x62.5mm Trunnion-mounted shock - disregarding the shock stroke limitation here would result in frame damage. 170 millimeters of rear wheel travel seems to come with less of that diving mid-stroke characteristic than the Rallon DH had.

I suppose, if you were tempted for more travel, the DH Gravity Link and a 225x75mm shock could be installed. You'd definitely want a climb switch in that case, as the anti-squat drops significantly (see graph here). Keep in mind, you'd be limited to a 27.5" rear wheel if you started out on a 29er.

What results from those pieces is an anti-squat value starting close to 100%, much higher than the Rallon DH, but a nearly identical leverage rate and anti-rise figure - just over a shorter shock eye-to-eye and stroke.

The leverage rate in the more progressive setting.




Geometry

If you checked out the Rallon DH review, you might remember that Orbea only makes these elegant carbon frames in four sizes. While I think that might be tolerable in the downhill department, they’re leaving out riders on the upper end of the size range. The max reach for an XL Rallon Enduro is 502mm.

The number of geometry adjustments is plentiful. In terms of angles, you have three positions for the headset cups, and two more settings down in the Gravity Link. Those take the head tube angle from 64.8 degrees all the way down to 63.2, a hair slacker than the DH version.

Towards the middle of the bike, the seat tower stands out. Not for height, because that’s kept to a minimum, but the angle - it’s one of the steepest around. In the slackest setting, it’s still 78.8 degrees. We like steep seat angles, but has this one gone too far? We’ll talk about that below in the “Climbing” section.

Two chainstay lengths are possible: 442 or 450mm. This is achieved through non-adjustable entire rear triangles. Customers need to choose their preferred chainstay length at the time of purchase. Orbea says they will make both chainstay options available aftermarket, but no pricing or availability is public as of this review. Unlike the mixed-wheel-only Rallon DH, the Enduro can use a 29” rear wheel with either chainstay length.

RIDING THE
Rallon EN

Test Bike Setup

To say I was excited to get this bike back on home soil was an understatement. It had tons of travel, weighed less than expected, and felt more intuitive to ride than the Rallon DH.

My pick on frame options was a size large, 450mm chainstay length and 27.5” rear wheel since I’d be focusing on freeride moves, bike park laps and exploring beyond. For that reason, the headset stayed in the slackest setting, but I did rotate through all four lower shock mount positions.

If I were more focused on enduro racing, I’d stick with the size large, but likely opt for the bigger wheel and shorter rear triangle, increasing roll over capabilities and maneuverability in tight turns.

I mentioned how tall the stack height is on this bike. After my first shakedown ride, I dropped the stem height to just 10mm above the headset.

Finding the sweet spot for the suspension came easier than with the Rallon DH. The Fox Float X2 was set to 186 PSI with 0.6 cubic inches of volume spacers. The Float X Live Valve spent the most time with the “Shore Tune” loaded up and sat at the same pressure. The fork pressure landed at 100 PSI with one volume spacer installed.

Climbing

One of Orbea’s main priorities when building the Rallon DH was to minimize pedal kickback - it was intended to be a downhill race bike first and an enduro bike second. With suspension layouts like this, pedal kickback is generally proportional to anti-squat, or the suspension’s ability to resist squishing under pedalling forces. I did wonder what effect this would have on the climbing ability of the Rallon Enduro.

On paper, the anti-squat is lower than most long-travel bikes, but don’t let the numbers fool you. There’s something about that ultra-steep seat tube angle that holds your weight forward and deters the bike from bobbing with each pedal stroke. In the real world, the Rallon Enduro pedals on par with, or better than, other bikes in the super enduro category. And I say all of that in regards to the Fox Float X2. If you choose the E-LTD build, with the Float X Live Valve Neo, or decide to add that to your custom build, it’s an even more positive story. But more on that later.

As great as that steep seat tube angle is, there could be downsides for taller riders. When positioned in the slacker head tube angles, the theoretical top tube length could become very short in comparison to the reach. In other words, the seated position could be cramped on an otherwise stretched standing position. Mix in a tall stack, and it certainly creates a new school position for climbing enduro bikes - a hunched-over XC attack position it is not. It’s not right or wrong, but it is very upright.

Some riders can find that it puts unwanted pressure on their wrists while climbing. Again, that can be dependent on your bar height, too. On the size large, however, I didn’t personally find that to be an issue, even on the longest rides.




Descending

Whatever you want to call it, a super enduro, big mountain, freeride, or gravity bike, the Rallon covers all of those bases. Sadly, for riders who pedal to the gnarly trails, this category is being eaten up by eMTBs at some other brands.

The Orbea's proud front end and low centre of gravity make handling bike park speeds less sketchy, and a lot of riders, including myself, favour the response and wheelbase of an enduro bike for jump trails. It’s crazy to think that a bike with this much travel is so versatile, especially considering how well it descends.

There’s no question that the bolt-on Gravity Link weights add stability to the bike. I favoured those on the downhill bike 99% of the time. On the Rallon Enduro, it was more of an even split. For the bike park or shuttling, it makes total sense. It’s not necessarily more work to spin causally uphill, but I wouldn’t say it’s as beneficial for everyday pedal laps. That’s the beauty of the singular 395g weight for the Rallon Enduro, though; spinning those two bolts on is a 2-minute job, based on where you’re riding on the day.

On mellow trails, there are certainly more responsive, shorter travel options out there. That said, the Rallon EN is manageable due to its respectable weight. The tall front end and deep stack take more time to get the bike tipped into turns. That’s not necessarily a disadvantage, because tall stack heights and large BB drops go hand in hand with stability, which the Rallon had boatloads of. The other side of the coin here is that it simply takes more time for a taller object to tip from side to side with the same force, so the rider will have to be more aggressive to change direction.

Luckily, there are a multitude of ways to configure a Rallon, but some options are set in stone, or carbon rather, like the chainstay. I made my choice with the 450mm rear triangle and stuck to a 27.5” rear wheel for increased maneuverability and body clearance. If I were 100% focused on racing enduro on this bike, I’d lean towards the shorter chainstay and bigger wheels to roll over objects easily while keeping the rear center length in check. That’s based on my past experience with bikes that have the capability to flip between rear wheel sizes.

Orbea says that they designed the Rallon platform around the DH version. I enjoyed that bike, but I did feel the suspension could use more support, and the frame made an awful lot of noise. Thankfully, the Rallon Enduro didn’t possess either of those issues. It was more composed and much quieter. Possible reasons for that could be the higher anti-squat values and the wireless drivetrain with a stronger derailleur clutch.

Riders focused on pure gravity performance should look to the Float X2 (or potentially the standard Float X, but I didn’t try that option), while more enthusiastic pedallers may appreciate the Fox Live Valve technology on the Float X Neo shock. The Float X2 offers more control through damper adjustments and doesn’t have as binary a feel as the Float X Neo.

The high progression shock setting translates to excellent grip down spicy trails where traction under braking is mandatory. This also sets the rider down further into the bike, behind that tall stack, exuding confidence. It’s especially beneficial on highly technical, but slower-speed trails that demand big travel and attacking angles, yet without the sluggish nature of a full-on downhill bike.

I’ll die on the “travel doesn’t weigh anything” hill, meaning I will happily pedal to alpine ridges or secret stashes when others will have to push a downhill bike or be “under-biked”. That’s where the Rallon Enduro stands out as being one of the best in the big-mountain bike world.

Technical Report

Fox Float X Live Valve Neo: The inclination-detecting brains of the Live Valve expand the Rallon into a true “all-mountain” machine. That does come at a small cost for raw downhill performance. There isn’t the same level of damper tuning to be had as on the Float X2, and the Live Valve doesn’t feel as intuitive. At times, and depending on the tune applied, the ride can be firmer than expected. But it does feel amazing on the climbs. Is that extra $1,000 worth it? If you’re more concerned with pedalling performance than flat-out descending control, then perhaps so.

Audio levels: Compared to the Rallon DH, the noises caused by the usual suspects are decibels lower. The Rallon Enduro isn’t totally silent, but they’re acceptable. The wireless drivetrain does erase that cable rattle inside the frame. Despite not having a lower chainguide pulley, there’s flapping in that area, too. A new noise not heard on the DH bike came to life. The water bottle knocked against the underside of the top tube. Once again, the trusty mastic tape saved the day, padding that area.

Dropper Post Length and Standover Height: Orbea managed to make a 240mm dropper post slam all the way into size small Rallon frame. 240mm of drop may be impractical if you want to use the seat to steer, but that’s the way it should be with all frames; the rider should have the freedom to choose whichever post height and drop they want. Coupling with the seat clearance, the top tube is low slung - tack on that extra weight and flip the geometry to the slackest settings, and you can really tuck into this bike.




Which Model is the Best Value?

Three complete builds are separated by a few thousand dollars. Without an aluminum frame option, the barrier to hopping on a Rallon starts above $5,500 USD. That’s no small sum; however, the E10 is topped off with reasonably capable components. At the other end, just a dollar short of five figures, the top-of-the-line E-LTD receives all of the electronic toys (other than a motor), like the Fox Float X Live Valve Neo shock and a SRAM X0 Transmission groupset.

In the middle of the pack lies the $7,199 E-Team, my pick of the litter. Mainly, this build stands out as the highest value for me due to the premium suspension and stronger brakes.

Otherwise, if you’re looking to equal that level of performance, you’d blow the $7,200 mark, according to my rough math. Let’s say you bought the E10 and decided to upgrade the 38 Grip damper to the Grip X2. That’d cost roughly $420. Then, stronger brakes, like the SRAM Maven Silver, would cost $265 apiece. That’d leave $550 to find a burlier rear shock. Minor advances are also made up the drivetrain totem pole when splurging for the E-Team.

There is always the fully custom bike builder from Orbea, called MyO. Choosing your own paint scheme will only cost you in waiting time.

How Does It Compare?

It would be rude to sit here discussing lightweight, carbon super enduro bikes and not mention one of the leaders in that category, the Transition Spire (Henry's punching air right now). It’s long, light, and slack, yet it can happily handle rides below its pay grade, and that's a good thing. The Rallon Enduro ticks that same box.

As versatile as the Spire may be, the frame adjustments are simpler and more limited than the Rallon. Then again, we’re talking about a bike that debuted back in 2021. Features like internal frame storage weren't as common outside of Specialized bikes, and bolting extra weights onto the frame would have been blasphemy.

29-inch-wheeled enduro bikes were the play back then - if you remember, even downhill bikes trended towards double wagon wheels. Transition doesn’t recommend the Spire to be fitted with a 27.5” rear wheel, or the handling would go out the window on lower-angle trails.

There’s also only one geometry adjustment on the Spire, and that comes at the lower shock mount, changing the head tube angle/BB height, as well as the seat tube angle. Although they do make two chainstay lengths, they’re size specific (446mm on the S, M, L and 452mm on the XL and XXL) and do not have any further adjustment, such as flip-chips.

What does that all mean on the trail? The Spire sits slacker in most modes, but it isn’t as ground-hugging. At speed, the Rallon hunkers down with greater intent.

Suspension-wise, they aren’t too far apart. The Spire offers more support uphill on the pedals and downhill when pushing into the bike.




Pros

+ Suspension travel-to-weight makes it far more versatile than expected.
+ Dialled, aggressive geometry (with sensible adjustments).
+ Extremely low stand over height and dropper post insertion.
+ Predictable suspension performance matches the bike's capabilities.
Cons

- Bottle fitment is tight, even on the size large.
- Not cheap, no aluminum option.
- Taller riders may be limited by sizing options.


Pinkbike's Take

Just when long-travel enduro bikes seemed to be falling out of fashion, Orbea bucked the trend and knocked it out of the park with the Rallon EN. The overall low weight and adaptable geometry can transform it for sendy Saturdays in bike parks to Sunday slogs in the alpine. There are undeniable similarities to the 200mm-travel downhill version of the Rallon, but the bike feels most at home in this enduro mode.— Matt Beer

Dougal-SC (Dec 2, 2025 at 13:38)
 @IsaacWislon82: I do my own analysis and have done since before there were any available software or blogs.

When you've done enough of them the trends are obvious. Faux bar can't pedal or brake well which is why the best bikes don't use it. Using an axle pivot can fix the braking but does nothing for pedalling/acceleration/climbing.

Fauxbar/swingarm/abp etc all need platform/lockout shocks to climb well. Because they squat heavily with the rearward weight transfer of acceleration.

It is only by moving the effective pivot rearward with a true four bar, or upward with a high pivot bike, that acceleration/climbing behaviour is improved. Which is why we have those designs and why they've always pedalled and climbed better.
