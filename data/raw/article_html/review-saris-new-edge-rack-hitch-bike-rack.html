Review: Saris' New Edge 2" Hitch Rack

Bike racks come in all shapes and sizes, but a common variety seems to be emerging amongst the mountain bike community. The fold up tray rack is pretty ubiquitous, with versions from most brands out there, both small and large alike. Saris already has one offering in this realm - the modular MHS system - but they're adding a second option with the Edge. This rack is fairly simple, with one arm holding the front wheel and a strap securing the rear, but there are some little details thrown in to try to set it apart.
Edge Details
• 80lb weight limit per tray
• Fits wheels 24” to 29” x 3” or 27.5” x 5”
• No frame or fender contact
• Built-in cable lock
• RV rated, 2" hitch
• Weight: 67lbs / 30.4kg
• Price: $1000 USD
• saris.com

The Edge has a very high load rating per tray, and adds compatibility with fatbikes and smaller-wheeled bikes to the mix. The 80 pound limit is well in excess of what most folks will need, but consider it extra assurance should you hit a nasty bump in the road with your 60-pound eMTB on the rack.


Fits long bikes.

The telescoping arm has some tricks up its sleeve, with a spring-loaded design that keeps things tight, and a built in detent to make sure the hook doesn't interfere with fenders, forks, or brake lines. The spring function keeps the arm tight to the tire automatically, and all you have to do is give it a solid push to secure things for the drive.

For the rear wheel, things are more typical, but there's one added detail to improve the standard ratchet design. In addition to the very long ratchet strap, there's a sliding block that allows you to raise the rear tire by 2.5", for better clearance should your saddle interfere with the other bike's handlebars.


In Use

Loading / Unloading

The loading process is the same as with any other one-arm rack, but things are made a bit easier by the spring loaded hook and limited range of motion on the arm's pivot. You can't over-clock the arm, it just lands in the same spot every time, right in front of a long fender. The spring helps keep things snug, and is extra handy when unloading the bike and dropping the arm down - the hook quickly returns to the shortest position out of the way.

The built-in lock is a nice touch.
Not the most secure, but good in a pinch.

I find ratchet straps to be a bit annoying to use, as you have to thread them through the spokes every time you load the bike, making sure to not trap them down with the rear wheel when loading things. Unloading is fairly easy, as you just depress the plastic catch and yank the strap out.

I haven't had too many occasions to use the rear wheel spacer on the Edge, so mostly it has remained off to the side as more of a tire chock. If you should need it, the block slides around pretty easily with two hands, and stays securely in position once ratcheted down.


On and Off Road

This is a Honda Fit folks, don't get too excited. The biggest hinderance to off road usage on the Edge is the lack of clearance, as the rack does nothing to lift the bikes above the hitch receiver. For taller vehicles, this might not be an issue, but every car out there benefits from more clearance out back, even if just for steep driveways.

The anti-rattle tightening feature works well here, and keeps the rack securely in place in the hitch, without any loosening through the test period. I find two-arm racks to be more secure in true off-road situations, as the extra rigidity that comes from the second set of arms helps reduce how much the bikes can wag. The Edge lets the bikes flop a fair bit, but hasn't done anything scary in my time with it. Part of this extra movement is probably due to the extra tire clearance in the front wheel basket, where there's a fair bit of clearance for the tire to rotate without any support on the sides.

When the rack is stowed upright in no-bike mode, there is a noticeable rattle between the rack and the main pivot point, as the body rocks back and forth in the detent. This probably won't be apparent on RVs and off the back of massive vehicles, but it was hard to ignore when driving around town in my smaller car. Stowing the rack in the 3/4 position mostly alleviates this, but that's not the cleanest solution if you're looking to keep the rack folded tight to the back of your car.

Easy one handed deploy, usually a two hand stow.

What Could Be Improved?


The aforementioned rattle is an annoyance, but could be less of an issue for some users depending on the vehicle type. My bigger complaints come down to the finer details of the rack, and the potential durability issues that might come up. The overall chassis is sturdy, with a massive pivot point and beefy trays, but the arms have some play in them that is sure to develop further over time, as pretty much all telescoping hook armed racks are wont to do. The ratchet hardware is already showing some rust, and though it hasn't affected things in use yet, it might do so after a few more months of road grime immersion.

I've noticed that after heavy rains the rack drains water for a while afterwards, meaning quite a bit can pool up in the trays if the rack stays on your car. It's possible that won't amount to much in the long term, but only time will tell.

My last frustration is with the ratchet straps, namely the damage they can do to the rear rim of the bike. As your bike shimmies back and forth in the rack, the tightened ratchet strap constantly rubs on the location where installed, leading to noticeable scratching of the rim, especially in wet weather or when the bike is dirty. Some ratchet designs employ a slide-on boot to prevent this, but the Edge doesn't include that preventative measure. Plus, those boots are easy to lose as you use the rack, making them something of a half measure.


Durability

The Edge rack has been living on my car for the past two months, and aside from the concerns listed above, things haven't changed a bit. That's far from a long term test in the world of bike racks though, and time will tell how well the Saris survives. Saris offers a limited lifetime warranty, mostly against any defects in manufacturing, excluding rust, paint damage, and wear and tear.




Pros

+ One hand opening, slick arm design
+ High load rating, RV approved
+ No frame contact
+ Holds large tires

Cons

- Cost is high for middling quality
- Ratchet straps are fussy and can scratch wheels
- Rattles when stowed upright


Pinkbike's Take

The Saris Edge packs a bunch of features into a fairly sleek package, but lacks some refinement when it comes to finish quality and elegance. The design of the front arm makes loading quick and easy, and easily avoids frame contact, but the ratchet strap is a little fussier and can scratch rims. Though it wouldn't be my top choice for a regular car, folks with extremely heavy e-bikes and an RV might find it ideal.— Dario DiGiulio
