Canfield Lithium V3 Review: Long Travel Without the Lethargy

Jun 8, 2026
by Dario DiGiulio

Canfield is one of those brands with a fierce following and a dedication to creating bikes a little outside the norm. With one of the more distinct suspension designs on the market, they aim to offer a novel suspension feel that other layouts don't quite mirror.

At a time when many bikes feel like they're converging on a common design, Canfield's approach is very much their own, and the ride quality achieved is certainly worth experiencing. The Lithium is on its third iteration, and has seen some nice updates to modernize things - having ridden the previous version, I'd say the improvement is significant.

Pinkbike's Take

In a world where many long-travel bikes are starting to feel alike, the Lithium stands out as a versatile and fun ride, regardless of how gnarly your terrain might be. There are some weak points and details that could be refined, but boy can it rip a corner.

It's not the most capable bike in all-out rough and steep terrain, but embodies that all-mountain ethos of doing a good job in most places.— Dario DiGiulio

Frame Details

The raw aluminum frame of the Lithium hides some complication you wouldn't notice on first glance. Where the front triangle is fairly simple, and heavily oriented around the suspension layout, the dropouts hide some unique adjustments.

Wheel size and chainstay length are adjustable via an on-board flip chip dropout system, allowing the user to convert the Lithium from MX to 29, short chainstay to long. Each change takes only a few minutes, and is best done in a bike stand, though you could carry the task out trailside in a pinch.

Adjustable chainstay length and wheel size.

Well integrated and self contained.

The cable routing is very barebones, with unguided cables channeled through bolt-in gates at the front of the bike. Though it comes without any silencing measures, some foam tubing goes a long way in quieting the bike down - a fairly critical step in my experience. At the risk of sounding like a broken record, I’d probably give the frame external routing, but that’s just me.

The cable ports themselves are simple, and unfortunately one of the female threads (frame side) on my bike arrived stripped, making it challenging to tighten that port down completely. Some electrical tape took care of things for the duration of the test.

Seatpost insertion for the 30.9mm post is quite deep, though the seattube lengths aren’t particularly short. I was able to run a 240mm length post in my XL bike, and conveniently that fully slammed post landed right at my perfect pedaling height.

The frame hardware is simple and easily accessible, though a long-handled tool is useful for reaching the desired torque specs. The frame uses 6902 Max bearings throughout the linkage, to keep replacement simpler. I had some creaking pop up after a couple weeks of riding, but a quick regrease and torque check took care of that noise. Despite the complication, the rear dropout adjustment proved reliable, with no loosening or noise to report.




Geometry

The Lithium's geometry chart is relatively simple, with few adjustments on tap for the front half of the bike. You get what you get, regardless of wheel size or chainstay length adjustment. The 64.5° head angle is a bit steeper than the typical enduro sled, but plays into how nice the bike can feel through a corner. The 76.7° seat tube angle is on the slack side by modern standards, with the actual seat angle pitching the rider back as the saddle height gets higher.

The chainstays are adjustable via a bolt-on dropout and integrated flip chip, with three options straddling the two wheel sizes. 29" long mode gives you a 443mm chainstay, 29" short and 27.5" long yield a 435mm length, and 27.5" short shrinks down to 427mm. Options for everyone, within a fairly standard range.


At 350mm, the bottom bracket height is on the higher end for similar bikes, but the dynamic position puts you lower than the on-paper figures might indicate. Combined with stack heights that sit right in the middle of a bell curve, and you're left with a nice rider area that feels roomy enough, with a higher rise bar adding some front end height for those that prefer that position.



Suspension Design

The Lithium’s frame revolves around the Canfield Balance Formula (CBF) suspension layout, which looks to isolate the rider from drivetrain feedback and any kinematic changes that may incur through travel. In my experience, it allows for a wider range of shock setups than some other systems, with anti-squat staying consistent through travel. CBF optimizes for a consistent instant center at the top of the chainring, preventing chain tension from disrupting the suspension action through travel.

The Lithium’s 165mm of rear-wheel travel is delivered via a 230x65mm shock, and is compatible with a wide range of coil and air shocks. My test bike was built around a Fox Float X2, with a base tune that generally suited the bike quite well. The only hitch I encountered on shock setup came down to the rebound tune - I found myself running the low-speed rebound fully open, and the high-speed close to that point. Though I found a happy place with this adjustment, there’s always the curiosity for what more you could get eke out with a different base tune or wider range of adjustment. Lighter riders may require a lighter rebound tune, but I can’t speak to that experience.

Test Bike Setup

Canfield has a rather extensive build menu, with six stock options and plenty of smaller component choices to make about small parts and details. Crank length, chainring size, and stem length are all moving targets, so you'll be able to hone in on those select details if you please. The only stock part I fully swapped out was the e*thirteen Base handlebar, which was a bit low-rise for my taste, and has a sweep that feels off to me. Otherwise, I tuned up the XT brakes as I always do, with thick rotors (in this case, some 2.3mm Hope discs) and TRP's blue brake pads.

The CBF suspension system works well with a very wide sag range, but I found myself served best by the standard 30% mark. As mentioned, I ended up at the extreme of the rebound damping range, with LSC all the way open - compression was more central in the range, and felt rideable at the extremes, though you'd certainly notice a dynamic difference.

As mentioned, the Lithium V3 comes with chainstay length and rear wheel size adjustability - long or short, big or small. I tried a few of those options, and ultimately found that the long chainstay mode and full 29" setup were my favorite for the bike's overall character.

Testing Info

My time on this bike started in Whistler and Squamish, both on pedal trails and in the bike park, before heading home to flog it on some familiar trails in Bellingham. Conditions ranged from foul to perfect, and the Lithium saw a very wide variety of trails - loamers to A-Line, mellow to heinous.


Climbing

Long-legged enduro bikes have gotten progressively heavier and stickier with time, adding capability and composure to the descents at some cost to the climbing pep. That’s not to say that big bikes climb poorly, they just err on traction and a comfortable body position (ideally) to keep you moving up the hill.

The Canfield Lithium bucks that trend a bit, offering a more energetic climbing characteristic than I’m used to experiencing with bikes in this travel bracket. 165mm may indicate a sluggish feel, but that’s not the case here. You do lose out on some of the slow-speed grip when compared to some other options, but I’d say the balance struck is ideal for most use cases. The bottom bracket isn't particularly low, so pedal strikes are less of a concern in awkward technical terrain, allowing for a more consistent cadence when you're trying to scrape up something goofy.

My only complaint about the climbing performance of the Lithium comes down to the seat angle, which feels a touch slacker than some contemporary bikes. Some riders will prefer this, but I found myself consciously weighting the front wheel on very steep climbs, where a steeper seat angle can naturally keep you centered and equally weighted between the wheels.

I’d place the weight of the Lithium as a standard middle-of-the-road, with an all-aluminum parts kit and medium weight EXO+ tires balancing out to a package that’s easy to live with on long days out. Those tires might seem a bit under-gunned for the travel, but they survived my abuses. The massive built-in carry handle helps when it’s time to portage the bike over obstacles, should you want to get adventurous.

One detail that might not pair so well with long days out is the lack of a bottle mount inside the front triangle. You do have provisions for a cage beneath the downtube, but cow poop, clearance, and aesthetics might prevent that from being an ideal option.



Descending

Most people seem to overestimate their bike needs when purchasing a new model. More travel has a certain appeal to it, and the promise of sending in on some gnarly line is a perfectly valid reason to size up on capability. The Lithium presents as a bike in the big bruiser category based on its travel numbers (165mm/170mm), but the character of this thing is far more adaptable than expected.

The suspension quality of the Lithium is impressive, so long as you fall into the right category of rider. For those looking for something athletic and energetic, that generates speed well in compressions and corners, this is worth a look. Those looking to plant their heels and plow might find the bike (at least in this setup) provides a bit more feedback and pop than you'd like. I found myself getting along with the Lithium very well, both on natural, bony terrain, and flowier, more carved riding alike.


It seems that a big part of the Lithium's character here comes from the even-keeled nature of the CBF suspension, which alters in feedback very little as you push through the travel. Small and large compressions have a similar level of feedback, keeping the bike predictable through corners, even when chopped out and uncertain. The frame has a nice level of flex to it, giving as you push into the bike, but never feeling unnerving, even in the most high-g corners.

The cornering performance was probably the single standout feature of this bike, and the head angle is partly to thank for that. 64.5° is a bit steep by modern standards - at least for a long-travel bike - but the precision and front wheel grip on offer here is worth whatever assumed challenges you might have in steeper terrain. I rode some of the steeper trails I've hit so far this year aboard the Lithium, and never found that front end figure to be unsettling; it's nice to be able to change direction quickly. In comparison, I rode the previous Lithium a fair bit, and never really got along with the cornering performance. The chainstays were much shorter on that bike, and while the V3 model's aren't especially long on paper, they do feel like a better balance with the front end of the bike, helping out that cornering equation.


Things aren't all rosy, as there are some sore points on the Lithium's descending performance. The main culprit here is noise. As I touched on in the frame details, the cable rattle on this bike is intense, and needs to be dealt with when building the bike up. I added some after my first ride, and found that things did improve a bit. Chain slap is also quite aggressive, but the Shimano XT Di2's lack of clutch may be partly to blame there. Canfield also went with a very low-profile slap protector, which does little to damp the noise.

Finally, I'll revisit my first point, as some riders might not jive with the more all-mountain feel of the Lithium. It's an efficient, lively bike, not a super damp truck. Some people just want a machine to plow on, and this feels more at home railing a corner and popping over a root mat, rather than rattling through them.

How Does It Compare?

I dug back through my mental archives searching for a bike that felt similar in disposition to the Lithium, and the match came as a bit of a surprise. I think the Stumpjupmer 15 is a close match for the overall capability and handling feel that the Lithium offers, though the way they achieve that is very different. Aligned in head angle, these bikes are quite different when it comes to their vibe.

The Stumpy punches well above its weight class in terms of rear-wheel travel, and the Lithium has a sharper quality to it than most long-travel enduro bikes. Both corner very nicely, and feel like good tools for long days out. Both have slacker seat angles than I'd like, and also feature some serious adjustability. The Stumpjumper offers a bit more in that aspect, for those keeping score. Small frame details are very well sorted on the Specialized, where the Canfield is a little rough around the edges.

Ultimately, this comparison is as much for me to make the point that you can achieve a similar feeling with drastically different bikes, so long as a few of the ingredients align. For those looking to split them apart on suspension feel, I'd say that the Canfield is more predictable through travel, where the Stumpjumper does an excellent job of erasing the bumps you occur along the way.


One other bike that comes to mind is the Propain Spindrift. A very long travel bike on paper, the Spindrift has a more do-everything quality than you'd expect, and felt to me like more of a leggy trail bike than an enduro rig meant to smash through terrain exclusively. The Lithium's suspension maximizes consistency, where the Spindrift's support is the defining characteristic. With some similar adjustments on the table, it could hit a similar mark to the Lithium, for riders shopping around.

Which Model is the Best Value?

With six stock options to choose from, Canfield has done an excellent job of offering a strong value for the price with the Lithium V3. I'd recommend the Core build I rode to just about anybody, as you get plenty of options between the brands that tend to polarize people. Fox or RockShox, Shimano or SRAM, you can have it your way and enjoy a bike with very nice suspension, a solid drivetrain, and good brakes. I'd probably upgrade that latter element to the off-market spec that I'm running, but that's a smaller detail and price tag.



Technical Report

Shimano XT Brakes: I've said it before I'll say it again: these brakes massively benefit from a thicker rotor. Be it a Hope or TRP offering, I'v really enjoyed how Shimano brakes feel with less deadband and a more reliable disc, so the 2.3mm options on the market feel like ideal pairings. Add to the mix some good aftermarket brake pads (I've had good luck with blue TRP pads), and you've got yourself some mighty fine stoppers.

Tire Choice: The EXO+ tires that come stock on all builds of the Lithium are a potential weak point for riders pushing the bike in the bike park and on gnarly terrain. They survived my test period, but that was relatively brief in the grand scheme of things, and I did take care to not demolish them on any dumb moves. If you can manage the thinner casing, the lighter weight is definitely welcomed.

DT Swiss E1700 Wheels: I'm having a bit of an aluminum wheel renaissance, thanks to drying trails and the faster, rougher speeds that come along with those conditions. A little more compliance is more than welcomed in those scenarios, and the E1700s offer that in a package that feels reliable and simple to live with. Not bombproof, but sturdy, these should handle plenty of abuse with reasonable maintenance.



Pros

+ Energetic, predictable suspension performance
+ Geometry provides good handling on varied terrain
+ Excellent value for complete builds

Cons

- Very noisy frame, as stock
- Not the most composed through rough terrain
- Geometry could be improved for climbing

phops (Jun 8, 2026 at 17:50)
 You are thinking about progression wrong (but so is most of the consumer base).

If you want the suspension to handle big hits, you want the opposite. Think about it in terms of energy absorption, which is the area under the spring rate curve. If you have a progressive rate, the first half doesn't have the same capacity as the latter half. Which means you blow through travel, and end up in a region that feels very stiff on your legs.

Whereas with a linear rate, you end up with the first half of the curve doing a lot more energy absorption, and you while smaller hits feel worse, larger hits feel way better.

A little progression is overall good for general setup, but generally, unless you want freeride level stiffness, you want to raise overall spring rate rather than add more progression.

infectious1 (Jun 9, 2026 at 14:13)
 @phops: You are talking in absolutes here, your description about progression makes sense when we are discussing an extremely progressive frame design with a small volume air can (eg 40%+) wherein the high initial sensitivity and lack of support result in a small area of the suspension curve (near the end) being responsible for absorbing the majority of the forces.

However, many frames with progression within the 22-35% range are tunable and can still maintain good initial sensitivity, with mid stroke support and the suspension effectively handling the forces throughout the majority of the travel while also preventing consistent overload (Bottom out wherein your body and your frame end up absorbing the remainder of the forces). Bikes within the moderate progression range allow for better tunability (especially with the different air can and volume spacer configurations, compression, and spring rate).

Bikes with more linear designs (20% progression) often suffer from worse small bump sensitivity, decent midstroke support, but overload and transfer of forces at bottom out. Your only solutions here are far more narrow and often necessitate maxing out volume reducers in the shock, running less sag (higher spring rate), more compression etc which puts more responsibility onto the shock and can severely limit the performance of the bike across a range of riders and riding styles.

Having had bikes with progression in the range of regressive, linear, linear to mildly progressive 7-11%, low progression (11-19%) and designs with moderate to high progression from 21-35% progression, I personally prefer the moderate progression bikes because of the better predictability, tunability, reduced reliance on the damper, and the wider range of adjustment available without having to purchase special air cans, shock re valving, running excessive compression damping etc. Having regular harsh bottomouts or losing small bump sensitivity to prevent such bottom outs is not a compromise I want to make routinely.

Reading between the lines of the review the bike seems to have less small bump sensitivity, excellent midstroke support, and feels less composed/more unsettled at high speed/rough terrain. All of these characteristics are what I would expect from a bike with too low of progression for my preferences. Since the CBF tends to have high anti-squat a little higher progression would likely improve the small bump compliance, maintain midstroke support, and improve the bikes composure on rougher terrain while still maintaining the efficient pedaling characteristics (and maximizing the area under the force curve better without overshooting it entirely [bottomout]).

phops (Jun 9, 2026 at 15:35)
 @infectious1: If your goal is smoothness for beginner-intermediate trail riding, mid level of progression is the best, because you are likely not going to encounter big hits.

If your goal is performance riding at speed, you want linear setups with higher spring rate. You are never going to be going slow enough for small bumps to matter, your bike will skip over them (especially with 29 wheels).

If your goal is freeride or DH racing, you want both, because you are going to be going hard enough to use all of your suspension, but you also don't want it bottoming out leading to instability.
