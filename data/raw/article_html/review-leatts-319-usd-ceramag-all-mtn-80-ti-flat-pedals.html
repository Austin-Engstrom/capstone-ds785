Review: Leatt's $319 USD CeraMag All-Mtn 8.0 Ti Flat Pedals

Sep 25, 2025
by Jessie-May Morgan  
Share
Tweet
You must login to Pinkbike.
Don't have an account? Sign up

Join Pinkbike  Login
113 Comments


Leatt recently expanded its remit beyond the helmets, protection and clothing it is known for, launching a range of components including pedals, handlebars and grips. The headline items are the CeraMag components: ceramic-coated magnesium, chosen for its favourable low weight as compared to the aluminum alternatives. Though not the absolute lightest money can buy, Leatt's 8.0 CeraMag Ti flat pedals reviewed here are competitively lightweight weighing in at just 291 grams for the pair.

CeraMag All-Mtn 8.0 Ti Flat Pedal Details
• Weight: 291 grams (for the pair)
• Area: Narrow - 114mm x 100mm / Wide - 114mm x 107mm
• Thickest point on body: 18 mm
• Axle material: Titanium (steel available)
• Pins per side: 10
• Maximum pin height: 4 mm
• Color: White
• Warranty: 1 Year
• leatt.com



Details

Those who haven't chosen to delete high school chemistry from their memories might recall what happens to magnesium ribbon when it comes into contact with water. Very little. The group two element will burn in water, but you have to set it on fire in air first, then throw it into the water, and then it will burn, but only for a short time. To get these pedals to catch fire while riding, you'd probably have to ride over a bed of very hot coals and scrape them along the coals as you went, at which point I am quite sure you will have run into more pressing issues.

The use of magnesium in bike components is far from uncommon, with most high-end fork manufacturers casting the lowers from the lightweight material. It's not exactly rare to see it used in pedals either, but aluminum is far more popular owing to ease of manufacturing, and the fact that it doesn't need to be coated to prevent premature corrosion. That's where the ceramic coating comes in; it protects the surface of the magnesium from corrosion, something that is most likely to occur in very humid, salty environments. Thus, if you have developed a habit of riding your bike through rockpools at the beach, these probably aren't for you.


The platform itself is almost completely flat. I can measure a front-to-rear concavity of no more than 1 mm. Leatt tell me that this can be as much as 1.5 mm in some examples of the pedals owing to the variability involved in the application of the ceramic coating. I am told that more recently, an extra machining step has been added in the manufacturing process to improve consistency in platform depth.

There are 10 removable pins on each side, though not all are made equal. The pins along the leading and trailing edges protrude by 4 mm. They are standard rear-loading grub screws. Meanwhile, the three top-loading ones positioned along the sides (two outboard, one inboard) are a little shorter and have a conical top. These can be removed with a spanner or adjustable wrench, and can be replaced with the grub screws that are available separately. In my opinion, these should be supplied with the pedals to allow riders to tweak the platform to their preference.

The internals are serviceable; inside you'll find a titanium axle, a needle bearing, a cartridge bearing and a bushing, with two quad-lip seals to stave off water ingress. A rebuild kit will set you back $34.99 USD, and it includes everything save for the axle, along with a bushing removal tool. You will need another specialist tool: a thin-walled 8 mm socket.

The titanium axle plays a major part in achieving that impressively low weight of 291 grams for the pair. It does bring with it a usage limitation, though: the EFBE 4 rating states it is approved for use on drops that are no larger than 120 cm. The more affordable 6.0 version of this pedal ($186 USD) with a steel axle and an aluminum body is approved for DH and freeride. I am also told these ones have a more pronounced concavity of 1.9 mm.


On Trail Performance

I tested the narrow version of the CeraMag All-Mtn 8.0 Ti Pedals on a variety of bikes, but exclusively using my go-to shoes, the FiveTen Freerider Pro. For context, my shoe size is a UK 5 (EU 37, US 7). The platform dimensions are perfectly adequate, with the sole of the shoe making contact with all 10 pins.

The pedal body spins freely on the axle with just enough friction to prevent uninhibited spinning. The Freerider Pro shoe has a fairly flexible sole by most standards, giving a good feel for the shape of the pedal body underfoot. The pedal is noticeably flat, lacking an appreciable concavity that many riders look for in a flat pedal. That said, they are comfortable; I can just about perceive the central axle through the sole, but I haven't noticed the development of any pressure points.

It quite quickly became evident that these pedals don't offer the security of many other pedals on the market, including the Crankbrothers Stamp pedals and the Renthal Revo-F. I've had a few more slips off these than I am comfortable with. I wonder whether the conical pins at the edges have something to do with that. Leatt say these are intentionally shorter to allow the rider the ability to change the position of their foot on the pedal. I imagine their shape results in less bite into the rubber of the shoe sole. You can swap them out for the taller grub screw options, but these must be purchased separately.

I think what this pedal needs in order to improve the shoe-pedal connection is a deeper concavity and taller pins. To my mind, that would allow for more force to be pushed through the pins, and thus more rapid wear of the sole, but ultimately a more secure connection. The center of the foot would press down further, giving all of the pins more rubber to lock into.

They are very lightweight, though, and that in itself makes them an attractive option for XC riders who prefer to ride flats over clipping in. The leading edges of the pedal have deflected off rocks and roots without drama, despite there being an edge there for things to hook up on.


Durability

The Leatt 8.0 CeraMag Ti Flat pedals have seen half a season of riding on some trail bikes and, most recently, on an XC bike. Conditions have been mostly dry, but they've seen their fair share of boggy Cairngorm singletrack along the way. As you can see, they've had a good bashing off the granite rocks, but all pins have remained securely in place - though the conical ones at the edges are now nicely chamfered. Unthreading the pins, I see no sign of corrosion on the pedal body underneath the pin head. The internals still have life in them, though a refresh is due soon, with the smallest amount of free play now detectable.



Pros

+ Very lightweight
+ Two platform size options

Cons

- Eye-wateringly expensive
- Pins are a little on the short side
- Very subtle concavity
- Not the most locked-in shoe-pedal connection



Pinkbike's Take

Leatt's 8.0 CeraMag Ti Flat Pedals are some of the most expensive money can buy. While they are competitive on weight owing to the magnesium body and titanium axle, they aren't so competitive on the most important performance characteristic: grip. Their low weight (291 grams for the pair) will make them an appealing option to XC riders or bike-packers that prefer flats over clip pedals, but there are more concave, albeit heavier, pedals out there that I would prefer to rely on for a more locked-in shoe-pedal connection in fast and rough terrain.—Jessie-May Morgan
