Review: Cannondale's 2025 Scalpel 1 is a Capable, Modern XC Bike

Jan 20, 2025
by Sarah Moore

REVIEW
Cannondale Scalpel 1

WORDS: Sarah Moore
PHOTOS: Tom Richards


The latest version of the Cannondale Scalpel was launched in March of 2024, keeping in sync with the cycle of updating it every four years to align with the Olympics, and it had a fantastic first season under the former Cannondale Factory Racing crew.

Alan Hatherly won an Olympic bronze medal, became the 2024 XCO World Champion, and took the 2024 World Cup XCO overall title on board it. It also won the XC Marathon World Championships under Mona Mitterwallner and Simon Andreassen. For 2025, Jolanda Neff has signed with Cannondale Factory Racing and will be competing on the Scalpel.
Scalpel Details

• 120 mm travel rear, 120 mm fork
• 29" wheels
• Carbon frame
• 66.6° degree head tube angle
• Size specific chainstays
• Sizes: S, M, L, XL
• 1780 g frame (Lab 71) / 1980 g (Series 1)
• 11.15 kg (25.35 lb) as tested, size Medium
• Price: $4,000 - $14,000 USD
• cannondale.com

The Scalpel has been in Cannondale's lineup for more than 20 years now, and the new version has 20mm more travel front and rear compared to the previous 100mm Scalpel that I tested as a part of the 2021 Field Test. There's also a slacker head tube angle, a longer reach, and a shorter seat tube length in order to accommodate longer travel dropper posts. All models have 29" wheels.

While the exact lineup varies from country to country, there are five different models available in North America, with prices ranging from $4,000 to $14,000 USD. All models now come with dropper posts. While the higher-end European models use the single-sided Lefty fork and all models in Europe have a TwistLoc remote lockout, in North America there aren't any lockouts or Leftys to be found, other than on the highest end Scalpel Lab 71 model.

Pinkbike's Take



Modern cross-country bikes are incredible and the Cannondale Scalpel is one of the best there is. It feels like a rocket ship on the climbs as you would expect, but descends with a composure that makes it a fun choice even when you aren't toeing a race line.—Sarah Moore



Frame Details


There are two different carbon frames for the Scalpel, with the top of the line Scalpel Lab 71 model getting the Ultralight Series 0 Carbon frame and the Scalpel 1 I rode getting the slightly heavier Series 1 Carbon frame. The frame weighs 1980 grams for the Series 1 frame without shock and 1780 grams for the highest end Lab 71 frame. For comparison, a Scott Spark SL weights 1870 grams, an Orbea Oiz is 1750 grams, and the new Specialized Epic 8 is 1965 grams for the regular version, and 1795 grams for the S-Works version.

While the previous generation of the Scalpel used what Cannondale called Asymmetric Integration, a concept that involved moving the hub and cassette 6mm towards the driveside, which Cannondale claimed created a stiffer wheel, a better chainline, and allowed for shorter chainstays, the new version uses a 55mm chainline which is becoming the new norm and doesn't require you to re-dish a brand new wheel to fit the frame.

The Scalpel has also switched over to a threaded bottom bracket from the PF30 standard that was used on the previous Scalpel. While those two updates will make things a little easier for mechanics, they might not be quite as excited about the thru-headset cable routing that all models of the Scalpel now use. The higher end models receive Cannondale's new SystemBar XC-One, a one-piece carbon cockpit with a mouth-like port at the front that the brake, dropper, and remote housing run through. It might not be the easiest to work on, but it sure is sleek looking, especially in the red version on the Scalpel 1. It also comes in a decent 760mm width with the stem coming in a 60mm length on the small and medium bikes and 75mm on the large and XL bikes.

The dropper post is 31.6mm and the size small gets a 125mm drop while medium through XL get 150mm of drop. All models have ample room to fit two water bottles.


Other details include 2.4” tires on 30mm internal width rims, a threaded bottom bracket, and an integrated wheel sensor that delivers speed, route and distance info, registers your bike, keeps you on top of maintenance, and more, all through the Cannondale App.






Geometry


The Scalpel's updated geometry puts it right in line with the latest modern cross-country machines. The head tube angle has been slackened by one degree from the model I rode in 2021, putting it at 66.6-degrees. The seat tube angle is a degree steeper at 75.5º, and the reach numbers have grown by 10 to 20 millimetres depending on the frame size, with the size medium getting a 450mm reach, a full 15mm longer than the previous model.

Unlike the previous model, there are now size-specific chainstays, starting at 434mm on the size small and going up to 446mm on the XL in 4mm increments. In theory, that should help maintain a similar balance between frame sizes.

For the sake of comparison, here are a couple of other modern XC bikes and their head angle / seat tube angle / reach (size M).

Specialized Epic 8: 65.9° / 75.5° / 450mm

Orbea Oiz: 67°/ 76.5° / 450mm

Scott Spark RC: 67.2° / 76.6° / 441mm

Yeti ASR: 66.5° / 75.5° / 445mm





Suspension Design

Back when we tested the previous generation Scalpel in 2021, there were two models to choose from, the 100mm race bike or the 120mm Scalpel SE which we dubbed the "downcountry" version. Now, all race models get 120mm of front and rear suspension, so there is no "SE" model in the lineup anymore.

The Scalpel uses a single pivot flex stay with a linkage driving the 120mm shock through its stroke to keep weight at a minimum. Cannondale says that they customize the kinematics on the Scapel by frame size with their Proportional Response Suspension system.

RIDING THE
Cannondale Scalpel 1

Test Bike Setup

The Cannondale Scalpel 1 comes with Cannondale's integrated SystemBar XC-One Flat bar and stem combo which has a 760mm width and 60mm stem on the size medium. It has a 5° upsweep, 8° backsweep and -6° stem and I was happy to get along with it out of the box.

Most of the testing took place on the Maxxis Rekon Race and Maxxis Aspen WT that came on the bike, but I also rode the bike with a set of Schwalbe Rick XCs towards the end of the test period.

I rode the bike with 185 psi in the Fox Float SL Factory EVOL SV rear shock, which put me right at 30% sag, and I ran 82 psi in the Fox Float Factory 34 SC fork. Tire pressures were 19 psi front and 21 psi rear.

Testing Info

Testing took place over the course of the summer and into the fall in Squamish, British Columbia. Trail conditions were dry for the most part, but I got a couple of really rainy days, including a fun race, towards the end of the testing period in October. Squamish's technical cross-country trails are perfect for the Scalpel and the trails on this loop are where the Cannondale feels at home. While I received the bike after this race took place, the course from our local cross-country marathon race, the Haulback, is a perfect example of terrain well-suited to the Scalpel.



Climbing


The climbing position on the Cannondale Scalpel felt comfortable right out of the box and I took it on a much longer ride than I would usually recommend right off the bat since I just felt at ease on it immediately. The extra length compared to the previous model makes it feel like you're in a more aggressive climbing position and have more weight over the front end of the bike despite the extra 20mm of travel on the fork. That being said, it doesn't feel like you're too stretched out by any means and my lower back agreed with the position, which can't be said for all cross-country bikes (I'm looking at you, Pinarello Dogma XC).

On tight singletrack, the 66.6º head tube angle meant that steering was a bit less quick than on some other cross-country bikes I've ridden, but it never felt like I couldn't maneuver the bike exactly where I wanted to and I actually enjoyed the more composed feel of the steering.

As for pedalling, the suspension platform feels very efficient and the bike really does feel like a rocket ship on the climbs. Unlike many cross country bikes, the North American version of the Scalpel 1 does not have a lockout or electronic suspension, but it really didn’t feel like that was something it was lacking, even on paved roads and smooth gravel climbs. The European models do come with a lockout, but I prefer having a clean handlebar set up and fewer controls to worry about when I'm redlined, so it was great that the suspension felt firm and efficient without a lockout even when doing hard efforts out of the saddle.

When climbs got more technical and required more than just pure power to get up and over obstacles, the Scalpel had ample traction and I never felt like I was bounced off line. The Scalpel made it enjoyable to go out and find technical climbs and challenge myself on them. The bike also felt comfortable over small bumps and trail chatter which made long rides less tiring on my upper body overall.

The Scalpel 1 isn't beating any cross-country weight records at 25 lbs (11.34 kg) without pedals in the size medium I rode, but it's still a very respectable race weight and very responsive. For a lighter frame and build, you'll have to look to the $14,000 USD Scalpel Lab 71, which uses the same frame that the Cannondale Factory Team races on. Alan Hatherly's Scalpel in size large came in at 22.5 lb (10.23 kg) when he won the Andorra World Champs on it.

If I'm nitpicking, I would have preferred a different dropper post since there are only two positions on the 150mm Fox Transfer SL that the Scalpel 1 comes with so you can't just lower your dropper post a couple of centimeters in rolling terrain. I also found the 34 tooth chaining a bit aggressive, and I prefer it when cross-country bikes have 32 -tooth chainrings, but it’s appropriate for the intended use. Some longer grinds were done at a slightly lower cadence than I would have preferred due to the 34T, but I'm also someone who generally rides with quite a fast cadence. Cadence is quite a personal choice and a chainring is an easy swap to make, in fact many racers change it depending on what race course they're competing on.




Descending


The Scalpel is a weapon on the climbs, as cross-country bikes have to be to be competitive, but it's also really impressive when things point downhill. That's in part due to the geometry with the Scalpel's 66.6º head tube angle and long reach and partly due to the mix of components. The bike comes with 760mm handlebars, a 150mm dropper post, 4-piston brakes with 180/160mm rotors, and 2.4" tires. The Maxxis Rekon Race and Maxxis Aspen WT tires that come on the Scalpel might look like scary tires with their low-profile tread, but they did a great job of holding traction in a variety of conditions.

Descending on the new Scalpel feels more intuitive than it was on the previous model with its longer reach and slightly lower bottom bracket. It especially feels much more intuitive to corner and it maintains traction evenly throughout the corners. The suspension on the Scalpel is supportive and firm without feeling like it beats you up too much at the end of a long descent. Obviously, it won’t be as comfortable as descending on something with more travel, and my triceps and hands definitely got more sore on the Scalpel than they would on a longer travel bike.

However, I think the main talking point is that you’re actually having a fun time trying to find flow and pop off of things. You don't have to be "fully on" and concentrated on the descents and you can take a minute to recover from your climbing effort instead of white knuckling your way down descents on the easiest line possible. It also makes the bike more versatile and I found myself reaching for it for more rides as a result. The Scalpel is what we're calling a modern cross-country bike, but I do think it eclipses what we were calling a "downcountry" bike at the XC/DC Field Test just four years ago. There’s a reason why the Scalpel SE is no longer in the lineup, and that's because the race version of the bike is a more capable climber and a more fun descender than that bike was with its updated geometry and 120mm of travel.

The one thing I disliked on the descents is that the Fox Float Factory 34 SC fork is quite noisy and there's a distinctive noise that I can only describe as "squelching" that comes from it every time it's compressed. It was so noisy that even the people I was riding with noticed it when I popped out of the exit of a trail. It was silent on the climbs, but quite noticeable on the descents when it moved through its travel. Otherwise, the bike was quiet on the downhills. It's also worth noting that it's not an issue you would have if you live in Europe, since the Scalpel comes with the Lefty fork there.

How Does It Compare?


Both the Specialized Epic 8 and the Cannondale Scalpel were released in 2024 and have 120mm of travel, fancy integrated one-piece handlebars, and several World Cup podiums apiece. The geometry of the two bikes is also quite similar, with both having a 75.5° seat tube angle and a 450mm reach on the size medium. The Epic 8 gets a slightly slacker head tube angle at 65.9° compared to the 66.6° of the Scalpel. The Epic 8 has 435mm chainstays across the board, while the Scalpel's chainstays range from 434mm on the size small up to 446mm on the XL.

Both bikes thoroughly impressed me and it's really no surprise that they've been so successful on this year's World Cup circuit. As far as climbing position and geometry of the bikes, they're very similar and both maintain excellent traction on tricky sections of technical climbs. The frame weights are also almost identical if you're comparing the Lab 71 version to the S-Works version and the Scalpel 1 to the regular Epic 8.

On the descents, both are also really fun to ride. I'd say that the Epic 8 felt slightly more forgiving and left me less tired at the end of long descents, but the Scalpel felt sturdier and a touch more stable at speed.

I rode the S-Works version of the Epic 8 and the second-tier level of the Scalpel 1 so it's not quite apples to apples if we talk components, but I will say I preferred the AXS dropper post on the Epic 8 to the Fox Transfer SL. I also thought that the Flight Attendant system on the Epic 8 was a bit distracting with the noises that it makes on the climb, but that was preferable to the squelching noise that the fork on the Scalpel 1 made.

This is perhaps one of the more difficult comparisons I've done since both bikes are remarkably similar and the choice between one and the other will largely come down to brand preference and which components you'd like to ride. Both are excellent choices for cross-country racers and riders who just want to ride really light and fast bikes. As far as pricing goes, Specialized offers more models, but the Cannondale starts at $1,000 USD less with a $4,000 USD option.



Which Model is the Best Value?

Out of the five version of the Scalpel that Cannondale offers in the United States, it's the $6,500 USD Scalpel 2 that I think offers the best value. That model has a SRAM GX Eagle T-Type wireless electronic drivetrain, RockShox SID Select+ suspension both front and rear, and Cannondale's HollowGram XC-S 27 carbon wheelset.

The frame is the same Series 1 Carbon frame as the $9,500 USD Scalpel 1 that I rode, although it's undoubtedly a bit heavier without the premium components that the Scalpel 1 is specced with. That being said, since it's exactly the same frame for $3,000 USD less, so there's the opportunity to pick and choose which components you upgrade and save some weight.

Technical Report

Maxxis Rekon / Rekon Race tires: I'm a fan of the Rekon tires. They look scary with their minimal tread, but somehow manage to hold grip much longer than you think they should. I preferred them over the Schwalbe Rick XCs I put on the bike later on in the season since I felt they maintained traction better in both dry and wet conditions.

Fox Transfer SL dropper post: The Transfer SL post saves weight by only having two positions – all the way up and all the way down. I didn't have any issues with it from a reliability standpoint, but it's not as intuitive to use as a regular dropper and it's quite loud when it changes position, letting your fellow riders know exactly when you've pressed your dropper post lever. Sizes medium through XL get 150mm of drop and with that amount of drop, I found myself wishing there was a middle setting available, especially for rolling terrain.

SRAM Level Silver Brakes: Many of Pinkbike's editors have found SRAM's Level 4-piston brakes to be underpowered, even for XC usage, but with the set on the Scalpel 1 I always felt like I had enough power to slow down on the descents. The 180mm front and 160mm rear rotor might have added a couple of grams to the weight of the bike, but it's definitely worth it.




Pros

+ Comfortable and efficient climber
+ Capable and fun descender
+ More versatile than most cross-country bikes

Cons

- Noisy fork and two position dropper


Pinkbike's Take

Cross-country race bikes of the past were steeds that I would only hop on if I was committed to training for a big race since I felt like they compromised the fun factor of mountain biking overall and really restricted the terrain that I was excited to ride. The Cannondale Scalpel might be classed as a cross-country bike and have the accolades to back that claim up, but it's also a fun bike to ride.

It still feels extra speedy on the climbs, so you can do more climbing in a ride than on any other type of non-motorized bike, but once things are pointed downhill, you're not just holding on for dear life until the trail points upwards again; you're actually able to find flow and enjoy the descent. Sure, your arms and hands will get tired more quickly than on something with more travel, but not at the expense of having fun. There's a reason why the "downcountry" category has gone out of fashion and I believe it's because cross-country bikes like the Scalpel have eclipsed them on the fun and the fast. —Sarah Moore
