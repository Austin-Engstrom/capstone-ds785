Extreme Suspension is a serious brand name, but that’s because their mountain bike suspension products are too. The high-end Italian dampers are built on years of engineering and race experience, which makes evolving their goods ever more ambitious. The Arma V4 is the latest iteration of their premium, gravity-oriented damper.

Along with addressing top-out concerns through a newly developed hydraulic mechanism, EXT has improved the user experience on the Arma V4 by reshaping the adjusters to use common MTB tools. It's a little thing, but it makes things easier out on the trail.
Arma V4 Details

• Adjustments: low-speed compression, high-speed compression, rebound, bottom-out control
• 14mm DLC-coated steel shaft
• Position sensitive rebound (PSR)
• Spring rides on thrust bushings
• Locking preload collar
• Weight: 448g (250x75mm, w/o spring)
• Made in Italy
• MSRP: $1,155 USD / €1,069 EUR
• extusa.bike

The Arma V4 is a mono-tube shock with an oversized 14mm, diamond-like carbon (DLC) coated steel shaft and other low-friction components throughout. Positioned primarily for downhill riding, the Arma V4 offers four damper adjustments: rebound, low-speed compression (LSC), high-speed compression (HSC), and hydraulic bottom-out control (HBC) - the function that popularized EXT shocks.


The 4mm allen key head for the low-speed compression adjuster lies inside the 5mm high-speed compression dial (black). The hydraulic bottom-out control also uses a 4mm (silver head).

The 14mm DLC steel shaft and slimmer bottom-out bumper.

Arma V4 Details

From the outside, the Arma looks to have the same functions as the 3rd generation, but inside, there are two dramatic changes at play.

In the reservoir, the internal floating piston (IFP) separating the nitrogen gas and oil has been replaced by a bladder design. The low pressure of 55 psi is said to avoid cavitation and have low hysteresis (the lag as oil flows between compression and rebound). The flexible bladder is also said to lower friction in the system by eliminating the dynamic seal previously found in the IFP. It also eliminates the need to change IFP lengths per shock travel, making production or after-sales changes simpler.

The biggest news may be the position-sensitive hydraulic system developed to eliminate top-out when the shock rebounds. A one-way valve increases the rebound damping force as the shaft returns to full travel in the final few millimeter of stroke.

At the other end of the travel, EXT still applies a hydraulic bottom-out control, activating through the last 20% of the shock stroke. This is internally adjustable for the corresponding stroke length, so you don’t lose HBC when travel is reduced. Externally, it can be tweaked to your liking with 10 clicks of adjustment.

Two smaller, yet welcomed additions are the removal of the plastic debris-clearing washer on the shock shaft and the high-speed compression adjuster. The HSC is now turned with a 5mm allen key head instead of a socket.

The spring preload collar is fixed in place by a small C-clip that can be removed with your fingernails or a small pick. There's also a set screw on the collar to keep the preload from backing off as the spring is compressed.



Sizes and Weight

As we mentioned, the Arma is largely a gravity-oriented shock, so it’s funny to see that EXT offers (or can build) it in smaller sizes that you’d normally find on a trail or XC bike. You’d probably want a climb switch, which the Arma doesn’t offer.

In any case, the Arma V4 comes in standard sizes from 210x47.5mm to 210x55mm, 230x57.5mm to 230x65mm, and 250x67.5mm to 250x75mm.

Then there’s the trunnion sizes ranging from 185x47.5mm to 185x55mm, 205x57.5mm to 205x65mm, and 225x67.5mm to 225x75mm.

On the scales, our 250x75mm shock weighed 475g with the hardware (448 w/o), which is 108g lighter than the 2026 Fox DHX2 (583g). EXT claims their springs are amongst the lightest on the market. The EXT 475 lb spring weighed 392g, whereas the 450 lb Fox SLS weighs 406g.

It’s also worth pointing out that EXT spring will fit most dampers on the market with the help of their adaptive thrust bearing spacers.



Setup

I began with the Arma V4 installed on an Intense M1, and then later in the season installed it on the new Scott Gambler. I was curious to see how this pair worked together since one of Scott’s engineers happened to be running the same shock on his Gambler. We discussed the stock tune, which happened to be the same as the M1, however, we both agreed that the compression damping was a bit firm for our liking. I reached out to Alba Distribution for assistance. The lighter compression tuned opened up much easier on small chatter and square-edge bumps.

The range of adjustment remained wide enough to dial in the compression, closer to what I’d prefer if I were racing, where shorter laps with higher control would outweigh all-day bike park comfort.


Setup Details
Rider weight: 80 kg/176 lb (with gear)
Spring: EXT 475, 1.5 turns of preload
Sag: 30%
Bike: Scott Gambler w/long chainstay, low BB, 30% progression shock mount
Travel: 210mm
Average leverage ratio: 2.8
LSC: -7/14 (7 clicks from closed, 14 in total)
HSC: -8/15
R: -5/-14
HBC: -3/10

A major win for the Arma V4 is the move to allen-key head compression dials. Considering that the Scott Gambler houses a multi-tool in the shock tunnel cover, making changes on the trail is even more convenient. The LSC adjuster is turned with a 4mm allen key, while the HSC uses a 5mm. Compared to before, adjusting the Arma’s compression dials would mean returning to your staging area and hauling out the toolbox each time. The HBC is still adjusted with a 4mm allen key and the rebound remains tool-free.



Performance

EXT has always leaned into firm or heavy damping, and that’s still prevalent in the Arma V4. The original tune was deployed for the Intense M1, but also aligned with the Scott Gambler since it has a similar leverage curve. I found the compression damping, particularly the HSC, to be very firm in the middle position. Unless that was set to wide open, square-edge hits weren’t absorbed well and the impacts were transmitted through the bike.

After chatting with EXT and Scott, Ben Arnott at Alba Distribution sorted out a lighter compression tune, and we were in business. With the new tune installed, my preferred setting landed near the middle of the damping range. Finding that magic combo wasn’t difficult. Feeling the difference between two, or sometimes just one click of the rebound, is discernible on the trail. Each click is mechanically well-defined and felt by hand.

Top-out, that clunk experienced when the shock shaft returns to full stroke, has to be one of my greatest pet peeves when it comes to an otherwise perfectly functioning suspension component. I wasn’t bothered by that on the previous Arma, but it has plagued other EXT shocks I’ve ridden, as well as other high-end dampers like the Ohlins TTX2M. The new position-sensitive rebound is a surefire solution to remove any chance of top-out from occurring, even if you like super-fast rebound.



How Does It Compare?

In talking about rebound, Ben pointed out that EXT’s HBC circuit doesn’t have any effect on the rebound damping due to the oil flow pathway. That can’t be said for every shock that has a hydraulic bottom-out control.

I’m not making a direct comparison in terms of ride quality, but dollar for dollar, the Vivid Coil is a sweet deal at nearly half the price. Otherwise, if you’re willing to shell out for a premium shock, the Arma V4 holds some unique selling features that separate it from the rest of the bunch.

Like the Vivid, the Arma only has one rebound adjuster, compared to the Fox DHX2, which has both high and low-speed rebound. This separation can be exceptionally useful for balancing the rebound of a bike with a highly progressive leverage ratio. Although I don’t doubt some suspension wizards could navigate that with further setup or tuning, having two rebound adjusters within arm's reach is convenient.

On the other end, the Arma’s HBC is now matched with a slimmer bottom-out bumper. For how simple they are, elastomers do wonders for stopping that clang at the end of the travel, but they’re a spring, so the restoring force is not damped. I found the larger bottom-out bumper on the DHX2 to have slightly more kick-back than the Arma when using full travel.

User Experience

One small quibble I’ve come to notice, and maybe it’s just bad luck, but EXT’s hardware seems to fall on the low side of frame dimension tolerances. More than once now, I’ve noticed that the reducing sleeve sits loosely in the shock mount location.

When setting the sag, the preload collar turns smoothly and can be locked in place with the nylon grub screw. It’s a small nuisance, but I’d like to see the puny flat-head screw replaced by one with an allen key head to match the other damper adjusters. Trail-side convenience is also a factor since most multi-tools typically don’t house a flat-head of that size.

If you’re not paying attention when installing the spring, you may misjudge seating the thrust bearing, only to find out after mounting the shock on the bike that it was misaligned, which in turn causes the spring to sit loosely in place.

The thrust bearings are totally worthwhile. I never once experienced any binding from the spring on the shock body. The Arma is stocked with a nylon guard on the shock body for peace of mind. It may come down to the fact that EXT makes quality springs as well.

On the topic of noises, I’m happy to report for all of you riders that focus on the sounds your shock makes, rather than the performance it brings, the Arma V4 is the quietest EXT shock I’ve ridden. And not just because it was hidden in the Gambler’s downtube either.

In the past, the debris-clearing washer on the shock shaft of EXT dampers has also caused some rattling (an easy solution was to glue that in place). Now, the DLC steel shaft offers a high level of protection without the need for that plastic washer, which may have been overkill anyhow.



Pros

+ Position-sensitive redbound eliminates top-out.
+ Compression adjusters are more accessible
+ Quieter hydraulic operation (for those that care).

Cons

- Premium performance comes at a price.
- Competitor offers high and low-speed rebound controls for lower cost.




Pinkbike's Take

EXT has packed unique features into the new Arma V4 while also addressing small bothers found on the previous model. Together, those changes lift the performance of the Arma even higher than before, making it one of the best, but also the most expensive, coil dampers on the market.
— Matt Beer
