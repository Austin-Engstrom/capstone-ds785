SRAM Maven Ultimate

SRAM's introduction of the Mavens was something of a catalyst for this test, as they seemed to have set a benchmark for accessible power in a mountain bike brake. Since their release, the Mavens have become a staple of stock builds and World Cup race bikes alike, establishing them as one of the big brakes to beat.

Maven Details

• Mineral oil
• Adjustments: Reach, pad contact
• 2 pad options
• A1: 2x 19.5mm, 2x 18mm pistons
• B1: 4x18mm pistons
• Price: $580 USD (Silver, pair)
• Brake weight: 367g (front)
• Rotor weight: 199g (200mm)
• sram.com

BUY AT:
Thunder Mountain

The massive caliper and lever assembly give them a noticeable presence on the bike, and the Van Halen graphics on the Ultimate calipers doesn't exactly blend into the background. Despite a completely novel architecture, they feature the adjustments typical to SRAM's other gravity brakes like the Code. One critical difference is the move from DOT fluid to mineral oil, allegedly with longevity and maintenance ease in mind.


Setup

The Mavens mark SRAM's departure from DOT fluid, now using a mineral oil made for them by Maxima. The bleed fittings may be different, but the procedure is identical to that of the Code, Level, Guide - any Bleeding Edge aficionado should feel familiar. The stock bleed procedure is consistent and easy, with only one special trick required to get a consistent feel. That trick is what SRAM calls the piston massage - essentially just a cycling of the pistons to get them all firing in tandem. The included pad spacer is all you need to perform the task, and you're off to the races.

Reach adjustment is tool-free, thanks to the prominent knob on the pivot of the lever. It's easy enough to adjust while pedaling, with noticeable detent clicks between each position. The Pad Contact feature modifies the free stroke at the lever, and offers a very wide range of adjustment relative to other options.

SRAM offers two different pad compounds for the Mavens: Metallic and Organic. They both have their merits, but given the higher power output of the metallics, we opted for those. There are also two rotor options: HS2 and Centerline; the 2mm thick HS2's larger braking surface made it the logical choice for those looking to get more braking power out of the system.

Livability:
Easy bleed procedure, best-in-class ergonomic adjustability thanks to Pad Contact and reach dial. Piston massage is a slightly annoying extra step, but is critical for performance and consistency.


Feel

SRAM's Swinglink cam drives the piston in a way that feels intuitive and easy to adapt to. The force required to pull that lever does seem to differ from copy to copy on the Mavens, with some actuating very easily, and others requiring more force to break into the stroke.

The lever itself is very neutral in shape, and feels usable at the minimum and maximum of the reach range. There's no extreme shaping to it, but the flare at the end is more than enough to reef on. Depending on where you set up your Pad Contact adjuster, the free throw on the Mavens can be quite far, or as minimal as any option on test. In either extreme, the power is quick to ramp up, and requires some rider calibration to adjust to. Once you have, the bite point can be effectively feathered, and a short pull past that point gives you access to full power.

Feel Ranking:
Far from the lightest pull on test, but very consistent and easy to intuit. Lever force differs set to set, without much indication as to why.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 898 g pull force (8th overall, performed with A1 lever)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.



Power

Power is the name of the game here, with two 18mm and two 19.5mm pistons per massive caliper. The amount of wheel-stopping force these can generate is immense, and only matched by a few of the most extreme options on the market.

Those looking to temper that power can do so via rotor selection, with HS2 rotors providing a claimed 7% more power than Centerlines. Each 20mm increment in rotor diameter increases the mechanical advantage by 14%, so starting with a smaller rotor might suit some riders.

Power Ranking:
Top spot, followed by the Shimano Saint, the Hope Tech 4, then the Trickstuff Maxima. Enough stopping force to change the way you ride, in a good way.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 25.0 m / 82 ft (2nd overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.

Original (A1) = red.

New (B1) = gold.

Updates

Since we conducted the bulk of this test, SRAM went and released an updated version of the Mavens. The new brakes look very similar, but feature a new SwingLink and caliper to achieve a lighter feel and similar power.

The new B1 brakes take the same lever body as the original A1, and use a SwingLink with higher leverage to move through the stroke more easily. To compensate for the increase in power, the caliper now has 4x18mm pistons, instead of 2x18mm and 2x19.5mm.

You can read our in-depth impressions here, but the short story is this: the lever action is much lighter, the freestroke is a bit larger, and the power isn't as massive at the wheel. They still have a ton of bite, but easing into that max force is a less aggressive ramp, yielding a softer initial feel. Generally, the B1 feels like an easier to use brake that will suit trail bikes, with the A1 befitting longer travel gravity bikes.



Pros

+ Massive power
+ Consistent, wide range of adjsutment
+ Good ergonomics


Cons

- Power ramp too aggressive for some
- Extra setup steps are key to performance
- Lever actuation differs brake to brake
