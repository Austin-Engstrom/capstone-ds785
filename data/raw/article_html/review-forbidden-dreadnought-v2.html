Review: The Forbidden Dreadnought V2 Has a Need for Speed

Forbidden Dreadnought V2

The allure of high-pivot bikes are strong, and Forbidden is a brand that's built all of their bikes around that suspension design. They can melt away small bumps and give a sense of having more rear wheel travel than stated, yet that design can come with caveats such as complicated drivetrains and uneasy shifts in rider weight bias.

Forbidden’s second generation Dreadnought follows the evolution of their high-pivot suspension design, which moves to a multi-link design and touts other widgets. This enduro battleship has been as much of a mainstay between race tape as it has in bike parks, often with a dual-crown fork.

For 2024, they’ve also increased the rear wheel travel from 154mm to 160mm and revised the kinematics considerably. They’ve also introduced modular drop-outs to carry either a 27.5” or 29” rear wheel and various chainstay lengths.

Being constructed from carbon only, the price tag keeps the entry point higher than other aluminum enduro bikes on the market. The component selection doesn’t spare any expense either. Ranging between $6,499-9,299 USD ($8,499-11,999 CAD), all of the builds use RockShox suspension, Maxxis tires, Crankbrothers wheels, and T-type shifting from SRAM.

For every action, there’s a reaction. The Dreadnought is a prime example of Newton’s Third Law of Motion. In some scenarios, it can descend wide-open trails at a frightening pace, yet at other times it can be a handful to navigate at slower speeds.— Matt Beer





Frame Details

From afar, the Dreadnought V2 bears a similar shape to the original, but get up close and you’ll notice a lot more bolts. Interchangeable dropouts give riders the option to change between a 27.5 or 29” rear wheel, although that can be decided when ordering. Savvy thinking allows a single 200mm post mount brake adapter to be used; the axle feeds through the adapter allowing it to pivot, acting as one mounting point while a series of three bolt holes on the upper portion account for the varying dropout lengths.

Smart use of the boxed shaped-frame above the bottom bracket continues to provide a gear storage solution, at least enough for a spare tube. Under the top tube is an accessory mount and a full size water bottle fits inside the front triangle. Cleanly routed internal cables are clamped down by the grommets and ample rubber on the inside faces of the swingarm keep the chain very quiet.

Part of that calm, quiet ride is due to the chain security. That includes a substantial cover on the idler pulley, skid plate to protect the chainring, and a lower jockey wheel. You’ll notice that the Druid V2 doesn’t require this extra pulley since the frame has less travel and therefore less chain growth. Forbidden uses an 18-tooth idler pulley wheel positioned slightly off-axis to reduce friction when cross-loading the chain in the larger cogs.

During the First Ride, we noticed an increase in drivetrain friction compared to the Druid. We experimented by removing that pulley and expanded on that further down in the Technical Report.

The Dreadnought is built around a 205x60mm trunnion shock and can handle air or coil sprung dampers.

Suspension Design

High-pivot suspension is defined by the main swing arm pivot’s height above the chainring. In the case of the Dreadnought, that main pivot is located well above the chainring, slightly offset from the pulley. This moves the rear wheel away from the front triangle through the majority of the travel. To visualize this, pretend the swing arm is the hand on a clock. A high-pivot’s rearward axle path would move from 7-9 o’clock through the suspension travel.

Bikes like the Dreadnought require a secondary chainring, or pulley, near the main pivot to account for the increased chain growth as the rear axle moves further away from the bottom bracket through its travel.

Now with a multi-link design, the Dreadnought V2's kinematics can be controlled with greater independence between one another. The leverage ratio start and end point values have been increased substantially, which should help lighter-weight rider’s opting for a coil-sprung rear shock find the correct spring rate. The curve has also been smoothed out considerably for more consistency on the trail.

The anti-squat is considerably higher than before (136% at sag vs. 116%) and should reduce pedal bob further. Under braking, the anti-rise remains similar aside from a reduction later in the travel.

Another change is the axle path. It still moves rearward for the majority of the travel, but towards the end of the travel, the axle moves vertically compared to the V1’s single high-pivot which is rearward through 100% of the travel.





Geometry

Stability largely relies on wheelbase and that’s something that the Dreadnought has heaps of. If you look at the reach and head angle numbers, they’re pretty typical of an aggressive enduro bike (471mm for a size S3 and 63.1° heat tube angle), but it’s the rear end that really adds to the overall length. And that just continues to grow up larger, up to and beyond the sag point.

Forbidden calls the balance between the front and rear centers One Ride geometry. Across the four frame sizes, the front to rear center ratio remains the same. The smallest of those has virtually an equal reach and rear center, or chainstay length, of about 430mm (431mm chainstay in MX mode). That doesn’t sound too wild until you consider the chainstay length of the S3 frame.

Most brands typically align their standard size “large” to hit a 480mm reach with chainstays under 450mm in length or less. Forbidden shifts both of those numbers by about 10mm. That equates to the S3 frame having a 471mm reach and 460mm chainstays (0mm offset dropout).

Regarding setup and my choice of frame size, its unusual for me (I’m 178cm for reference) to want anything shorter than that in terms of reach at least on this style of bike. But given the 1288mm wheelbase, I can understand why two of the Forbidden Synthesis team races, who are taller than I am, went with an S2 frame. Both Alex Storr and Rhys Verner chose S2 frames with dual 29” wheels and the 0mm offset dropout, landing the chainstay at 446mm (Editor's Note: Since writing the review, both riders have moved to the +10mm setting). The 29" wheel dropout also steepens the head angle by 0.4 degrees and increases the reach by 4mm. Due to frame clearance concerns, there is no -10mm dropout option for the 29" rear wheel, regardless of the frame size.

If you’re unsure on sizing, I’d advise scaling down. Test riding a Dreadnought if possible would be a smart move too, or at the very least swinging a leg over one in a parking lot.

RIDING THE
Dreadnought

Test Bike Setup

In the Geometry section, I explained why I chose the S3 Dreadnought. I started with the stock MX wheel configuration but later switched to the -10mm dropouts (450mm chainstay). Since the bike already required a lot of input to change direction, I never went to the longest 470mm setting or experimented with a 29” rear wheel.

The 30mm rise handlebars were cut to 765mm with 20mm of spacers under the stem.

Regarding suspension setup, guidelines are clearly laid out on Forbidden’s website: set the rear shock sag to 28-30% sag while seated and turn the clickers as directed. On the first outing, the Vivid Air was pumped to 197 PSI to provide 30% sag.

The shock arrives with 2 volume spacers from the factory. I’d go on to increase the pressure to 205 PSI, but eventually revert to 197 and add a third volume spacer. The HBO adjustment was kept fully closed.

The digressive rebound tune (R85) was an interesting choice and meant I ran the red dial fully open. We’ll dive into those choices in the Technical Report below. On the compression side, I settled on placing the high and low speed compression dials at -3 and -2 clicks from closed, not far off of Forbidden’s recommendations (HSC -2, LSC -2).

For the 170mm Zeb Ultimate, I ran the pressure lower than usual (around 78 vs. 84 PSI - both with 1 volume spacer) as it helped to keep more weight on the front of the long wheelbase through corners.

Trails in the Dreadnought’s repertoire were a mix of fresh and old in the Diamondhead area in Squamish, as well as visits to the North Shore and Whistler Bike Park. Even a few high-speed laps on Harper Mountain in Kamloops were added to the mix to really let the Dreadnought open up.

Climbing

During the Dreadnought’s First Ride, I immediately picked up on the increased drag from the lower pulley, which isn’t part of the Druid’s high-pivot equation. Extended climbs are where this is most prevalent, as well as in the largest cogs when the rear wheel is turning slower. It’s not terribly impeding compared to other bikes with idlers. In fact, it’s on par or better in terms of perceived efficiency, yet clear as day after riding other non-high-pivot bikes back to back.

The stretched out geometry makes the footprint of the Dreadnought lengthy. The rear wheel takes some coaxing to pull around tight switchbacks. We’re not quite into Grim Donut V2 territory, but it’s a long ship to swing around 180-degree turns. Looking ahead and anticipating how wide you need to take those corners is mandatory. However, once understood and anticipated, that length aids its technical climbing ability.

Worrying about the cranks bumping obstacles isn’t as much of a concern as I first thought. A high anti-squat value and 165mm cranks help the bike to maintain decent ground clearance and continue pedaling over lumpy ground. A central rider position and rearward axle path make blasting up rocky steps and over logs a breeze as the bike keeps moving forward.

27.5” rear wheels can hamper some enduro bikes’ uphill performance, but that’s where the Dreadnought differs. Although I didn’t try the larger wheel I can imagine how those characteristics would be exploited further, based on past experience.




Descending

Make no mistake, this isn’t just a long-travel Druid. They both share that same trait of seemingly offering more travel than stated, however, on our First Ride, some aspects of the bike blew me away. Once we towed the Dreadnought through more varied trails, some quirks started to show.

Smashing high speed trails are where this beast comes to life. Point it straight and it will gobble up everything in sight. There is a catch though: repetitive square-edge bumps. Being a high-pivot bike, I expected the Dreadnought to erase those stutter bumps but it never gave that "wow"-facter like the Norco Range or Devinci Chainsaw did. Part of that reason may be down to the shock tune on the Vivid Air (R85 C26). Forbidden found a digressive rebound tune to handle the returning spring force best.

The shock tune on the Dreadnought V2 is R85 C26, with 2 Volume Spacers. What that translates to is a digressive rebound tune and a light compression tune. This shock tune was chosen based on the changes made to the leverage ratio and the shape of the leverage curve. Compared to the V1 Dreadnought, the V2 has more support in the mid, less overall progression (25% vs 31%) and less end stroke progression (less ramp at the end of the curve). This works better with large volume air springs, like the Vivid. It also takes advantage of the hydraulic bottom out control seen on many shocks these days. Overall, this gives a more predictable feel to the bike throughout its travel.— Forbidden Bikes

This reduces that kicking or bucking motion as the rear wheel returns from repetitive, medium-size hits. I can see why that makes sense in some instances. Some riders find high-pivot bikes can push their weight forward in those instances, particularly on jumps. I found the Dreadnought to avoid putting me in that unwanted situation and that could be where that shock tune comes into play.

I also had the chance to briefly try a Fox DHX2 coil shock (digressive rebound/light compression tune) on the Dreadnought and found that to break into the travel and handle each consecutive impact more gently and consistently.

On descents that best replicate mogul skiing, bouncing from one loamy lump to the next, the Dreadnought provides serious traction by splatting itself to the ground. This can make the bike more involved to turn, especially in steep zones where braking and changing direction needs to happen simultaneously (think Val Di Sole for enduro bikes). I wouldn’t say that this was an issue of the bike being too big for me, though it did require more consideration than most other bikes when it comes to how you weight the bike as it moves through the travel. The balance on each wheel can change considerably. Moving to the -10mm dropouts made a world of difference in regard to maneuverability.

Back in the open, where sight lines are much further ahead, that elongating chainstay brings boat-loads of stability. The bike glues itself to the ground - not so much that you can’t pop over obstacles, flick a Scandi turn, or pull a manual. The Dreadnought encourages you to let it fly on high speed trails.

As is the case with most high-pivot bikes, it’s a catch twenty-two situation. They can light up in certain scenarios and struggle more than say a Horst link suspended bike. Thankfully, the Dreadnought has enough parameters that could be changed to suit those devoted to the ways of a high-pivot bike. Remember, there’s still two wheel size options, three dropout settings and the possibility to run a coil shock too. Downsizing to a smaller frame and opting for a coil shock would be something to consider, based on how tall the rider is and the terrain they most frequently ride.





Technical Report

RockShock Vivid Air rear shock: We've been impressed with the Vivid's performance on other bikes but we had some hiccups with one particular shock when the IFP lost pressure. On top of that, the suppleness never seemed to be equal to the Vivid we tested on a Nomad during our Shock Week last summer. Although our time with a Fox DHX2 on the Dreadnought was short, a coil shock would be our preference for the Dreadnought.

SRAM Maven Ultimate brakes: It’s been a long time since we saw prototypes of SRAM’s comically large prototype brake calipers, but I’m glad they’re here. Maven Ultimates are a major improvement over the Code RSCs in terms of power, especially when you're on the brakes for considerable lengths of time. The Dreadnought came with organic pads and 200mm front and rear. These give much less bite than the metallic option yet don't fade out as quickly as they would on a Code.

MRP chainguide: Another component that I thought may interfere with the small bump sensitivity was the added tension from the lower pulley. I removed that for a few rides and didn’t notice a major improvement - just more noise from the chain. In actuality, the derailleur clutch could be a bigger culprit as it flaps around unpredictably giving inconsistencies throughout the travel. It's no elbow to MRP because the pulley rolls smoothly, but it's still an extra component that isn't used on most other suspension designs.




Which Model is the Best Value?

If you’re seriously looking at a Dreadnought, then expect to drop a bit of coin. Carbon-only frames do push the entry point higher than competing aluminum enduro bikes.

The cream of the crop X0 RS U build doesn’t cut corners but it's the RS S base model that seems the most sensible. It largely has the same components as the RS S+, including the GX AXS T-type drivetrain.

The main differences are the damper used in the ZEB chassis (Charger 3 vs. the older RC) and adjustments on the Vivid Air. Other than that, I wouldn’t worry about the Maven Bronze brakes as they are still plenty powerful and have a crispy feel.

Upgrading the fork damper to the Charger 3.1 and replacing the rims with something burlier could be considerations down the road.

How Does It Compare?

A hot competitor in the world of smashy enduro bikes is the Santa Cruz Nomad. No, it’s not a high-pivot, but for riders looking to lap bike parks, send it off of ridiculous things, and race enduros, these two bikes are built to tackle such endeavors.

Neither one would ever win an XC race, however, they are designed to pedal everywhere on the map. The Dreadnought actually wins out in climbing effectiveness by riding a touch higher in the travel and setting the rider in a more upright, centered position on the bike. Efficiency-wise though, as in just spinning up a road, the Nomad’s traditional drivetrain is less draggy over the course of a full day.

Point them downhill though, and you can get wild. The Dreadnought is 10mm shorter on travel out back but makes up for that in some sense with the rearward axle path. Both bikes have a noticeably high amount of tension from the drivetrain as the rear wheel breaks into the travel. The shock tune on the Dreadnought could be the culprit but a device like the O-Chain Active Spider remedies a lot of the clanging on the Nomad. At the end of the stroke, the Nomad handles bottom outs best from a more consistent ramp up.

Each frame is stiff with aggressive geometry that can be ridden hard by riders at the top end of the sport. At the same time, the Nomad doesn’t need to be pushed to go fast or muscled as hard to turn. For that reason, it’s less demanding to ride than the Dreadnought.

As for maintenance, the Dreadnought doesn’t have as many bolts as something like Commencal’s Supreme V5 downhill bike but the Nomad is hands down simpler. There’s no pulley wheels to keep running smooth and the links have grease ports to quickly remedy any creaks. Santa Cruz also offers a lifetime supply of replacement frame bearings for the original customer too.

When it’s time to slap down the credit card, neither one is cheap, or available in aluminum. A Nomad frame and shock (Fox Float X2) costs $4,049 USD, $250 less than a Dreadnought frame kit.

Looking at the premium build kits, they’re very comparable with Forbidden choosing RockShox Ultimate suspension and Santa Cruz going the Fox Factory route. There’s also carbon rims, an X0 T-type drivetrain, and Maven brakes, stalemating at a cool $9,299 USD.




Pros

+ Calm and quiet ride.
+ Centered, upright seated position gives a helping hand on technical climbs.
+ Comes alive at higher speeds.

Cons

- Not the small bump performance expected from a high-pivot bike.
- Long chainstays that continue to grow through the travel can be a handful on steep trails.
- Digressive shock rebound tune has a peculiar ride characteristic.


Pinkbike's Take

After being blown away with the capabilities of Forbidden's 130mm-travel Druid, I thought the Dreadnought would be all that and more, but it didn't eat bumps like the magic carpet ride I dreamt about. Convincing this beast to turn means being forceful in where you point the bike and when to lean it in. With that said though, when you get the Dreadnought on high speed trails and optimize the shock setup, there aren't many other enduro bikes that will unlock the same level of speed. — Matt Beer
