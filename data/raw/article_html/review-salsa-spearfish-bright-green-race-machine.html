The Spearfish hasn't seen an update since 2019, and serves as a solid time capsule for how much cross-country bikes have changed in only a few years. Reach figures have grown by about 30mm per size, the head angle is a degree and a half slacker, and the travel has been increased by 20mm.

With progressive numbers across the board, and frame details that cater to folks looking to push distance and speed, the Spearfish jumps into modernity and keeps XC fun.

Frame Details

The Spearfish is an all-carbon affair, no matter which trim level you select. Just how light that carbon is depends on the build kit you select though, as Salsa is rolling out a two-tier catalog for the bike. "Deluxe" models will come with a lighter frame, shaving about 255g off the standard frame weight, while maintaining the same ride characteristics and finish details.

The frame has moved from SuperBoost to the far more common Boost 148 rear axle, which is nice to see.

Frame weights for a size Medium are as follows, with the previous generation bike thrown in for good measure.

Deluxe 4.0 1940g | Carbon 4.0 2195g | Current 3.0 2482g

Every Spearfish frame comes with a huge array of threaded mounts, for carrying bottles, tools, and even hard-mounted bags if your ride warrants it. All sizes can carry at least two water bottles, while the Large I've been testing can fit 3 if you get a little creative. There's an additional pair of mounts on the toptube for a feed pack or computer mount.

There's a minimal strip of chainslap protection and a clean little bolt-on chainguide mount, but no provisions for a bashguard. As a bike erring on the XC side of things, I don't think this is a miss - especially with the recent resurgence of chainring-mounted bash protectors.

Cable routing requires some fishing - fitting of the name - and has both the derailleur and brake snaking through the chainstays, with all 3 cables popping out at the left side of the headtube. That exit point will make routing a bit simpler, as you can reach the exit holes with the fork removed.

Geometry & Sizing

As mentioned in the intro, the Spearfish features far more modern geometry than its predecessor, which was fairly traditional on the whole. The updated frame features a high/low flip chip at the lower shock mount to alter things slightly - I'll be referring to the Low position unless otherwise noted. The 66° head angle is quite close to the cutting edge in the XC world, with only a few bikes sporting slacker figures at this point. More unusual is the 77° seat angle, which is significantly steeper than most other race-oriented short travel bikes out there. Both of those can be steepened up by 0.3° in the bike's High setting, should you want to draw the riding position forward a bit.

The front end geometry is fairly long per size, with 20mm gaps in reach between almost all 5 sizes - the smallest two are a bit closer together. Stack grows by about 9mm per size, giving the bikes a decently scaled front end as you scale up. One figure that doesn't scale at all is the rear center, which sits at a short 431mm in all sizes. 35mm bottom bracket drop is a bit higher than some ultra-modern XC bikes, but the dynamic fit feels a bit lower than the numbers indicate.



Suspension Design

Salsa implements the Split Pivot patent on their bikes, which places a pivot at the rear dropout, concentric to the rear axle. This linkage-driven single pivot has a fairly high starting leverage, but the shock tune allows you to add enough compression to avoid moving through travel too readily. I found the balance of grip and support impressive, but more on that later.


Anti-squat is high across the gear range, making for an efficient pedaling platform even with the shock open. That efficient feel in combination with the steep seat angle puts you in a fairly high and upright position while pedaling, but luckily the bike opens up nicely when turned down hill.

Test Bike Setup

I fiddled with the Spearfish quite a bit, but not necessarily because I had to. It was fairly easy to find a happy spot with the stock setup, particularly when it came to the suspension. 100psi in the 120mm 34SL felt optimal through the winter, and 195psi in the Float SL shock was pretty spot-on for my 13.5mm sag target. The fiddling came into play as I toyed with how capable you can make the bike feel, given the decently stable geometry and active suspension feel.

I found the stock Teravail Camrock tires to be far too minimal for wintery conditions, as they lacked bite in any sort of loose dirt and weren't too friendly with roots. I can see them working well in desert conditions, and on summer hardpack, but I found a Specialized Ground Control far more comfortable up front for the majority of the winter testing.

Testing Info

The Spearfish is definitely a cross country bike, but don't let that designation fool you. I found the bike comfortable on a wider variety of terrain than you'd expect of a light and efficient 120mm bike, pushing into fairly proper trail bike territory without getting too scared. Still, efficiency and covering ground are the name of the game, so a good mix of effort and excitement seems to be the ideal.



Climbing

The Spearfish has an energetic feel to it, balanced with a surprising amount of grip at the rear wheel. It's not going to compete with a long travel bike when it comes to keeping the wheel stuck to the ground, but for the travel and intended purpose it does an impressive job of digging in when you put down power. Balance is key here, as the bike manages to stay high in travel despite this characteristic. Helping matters is the very steep seat tube angle, which is by a margin the most upright on any XC bike I've ridden. This detail may make for some quirky bike fit considerations, but I had little issue getting the seated position dialed for my preferred feel.

That upright body position means you'll use some big-bike techniques when navigating technical climbs, with a slight drop of the seat going a long way to make sure you can shift your weight around and load the bike through funky terrain.

I'd happily choose the Spearfish as a race bike, be it for longer endurance racing or something a bit more pinner. The overall weight isn't going to blow minds, but carbon bars, cranks, and wheelset are all that separate the build I have from something optimized for top-level racing. For my more mortal purposes, the Spearfish strikes a really nice balance of comfort, efficiency, and traction through real trail riding, which encourages the speedy riding I'm looking to do a light little bike.



Descending

At some point while recording the video for this review I described the Spearfish as surprisingly capable, and I think that holds true through my long term experiences aboard the bright green race machine. For how balanced and supportive it felt on the climbs, I expected the descending to suffer some drawbacks, but the Spearfish continually impressed with what it could handle downhill.

I played with a couple different shock setups - primarily just toying around with sag and compression damping - but ended up on a pretty straightforward 30% sag and medium compression setting. The LSC dial on the shock has a fairly noticeable effect on how high you ride in the travel, and for chopped-up natural terrain I preferred the open mode - folks riding/racing smoother buff trails will probably prefer the feel of things with that batwing adjuster closed.

The components feel very well suited to the Spearfish's goals, with strong brakes, stout and tunable suspension, and a drivetrain that'll deal with whatever you throw at it. Definitely not the lightest collection of parts, but that pays back a bit on the descents, as the bike doesn't feel too nervous in hard corners and in technical sections.

The one detail I'd wish to change on the Spearfish would be the rear center length. 430/431mm feels too short for the longer sizes of this bike, and starts to take away from the stability in higher speed section of trail. Sure, it does play a positive role when whipping the bike through tight turns and squiggly pedaling bits, but I haven't felt a big detraction on that front from bikes with slightly longer rear ends.

All told, you could beef the Spearfish up into a little trail bike, or keep it lively and light as a long-legged XC machine. Either way, it should do well, as the suspension has a nice balanced character to it, and the geometry generally backs that up.

How does it compare?

XC bikes are improving in leaps and bounds right now, and I'd say these two have stood out the most from the recent crop I've ridden. Both feature cutting-edge geometry, light frame weights, and provisions for cargo and long-haul rides. The Spearfish allows for a bit more wiggle room on frame and fork travel, but the Epic could probably be short-stroked to similar effect. To me, the biggest differentiators come down to suspension performance and weight.

Though I haven't had the opportunity to ride them back to back, I'd give points to the Salsa on the suspension front, as the combination of pedaling efficiency and traction is quite nice. The Epic does well here, but gives up a bit in the name of efficiency and weight with the flex stay rear end. There are lighter XC frames out there, but the Epic's frame weight is remarkably light for what that bike can handle on trail. The Spearfish certainly wins on price, though only the highest end model gets carbon wheels, so things even out if you factor in that variable.

The Epic's geo is a bit more stable feeling, thanks to a lower bottom bracket height, longer rear center, and ever-so-slightly slacker head angle. Not a massive difference, and a gap that is closed a bit by how the Spearfish dynamically rides, but worth noting.

Either way, you're stoked - cross-country bikes are rad right now.


Which Model is the Best Value?

While the frame weight of the Deluxe model is an impressive 250g lighter than the standard layup, I imagine most people will end up on the non-Deluxe bikes for monetary reasons. The Spearfish C Deore 12 being only $500 more than a Deluxe frame is reason enough to give it a closer look, but the spec choices are solid throughout. The Deore is great, the SID is an appropriate fork for the bike, and even the M4100 brakes can be made quite good with some nicer rotors and metallic pads.

Those looking for the Deluxe experience won't be disappointed with the GX AXS bike I have on test, as there aren't any weak points in the spec, save for little details that will come down to user preference. Tires, bars, etc. are ultimately up to your tastes, but major items like brakes, suspension, and drivetrain are great.

Technical Report

SRAM Motive Brakes: I've already spoken about these at length, but the Motives have proven to be a massive improvement over their previous Levels. I wouldn't be taking these off this build anytime soon, thanks to power that feels ideal for a bike like this and lever action that is light and dead consistent.

Teravail Camrock Tires: The Camrocks didn't blow me away, as they seemed to prioritize rolling speed above all else. The chemical grip was lacking, as the rubber felt plasticky even on dry roots and rocks, but at least they roll quite quickly. I'd happily run these in the dry summer months, or in grittier desert conditions where rolling speed takes precedence over how well a tire bites into soil.

Fox 34SL Grip X: Though I'm confused by the Grip X damper's position in the Trail and Enduro worlds, it does very well in the 34SL's XC application. The rebound is quite noisy, and will probably annoy silence purists out there, but the damping feels appropriate for what is becoming an increasingly capable fork category.

Pinkbike's Take

The Salsa Spearfish has been a killer little bike, with a speedy and efficient feel while pedaling and surprisingly fun traits when you point things downhill. Though it's billed as an XC bike, this frame could be many things depending on how you build it. For the most part, the geometry feels forward-thinking, and the suspension performance is quite good for the intended purpose. —Dario DiGiulio
