Review: 2026 Propain Tyee Trail - Does Less Travel Really Make a Difference?

Jun 1, 2026
by Mike Kazimer  
Share

The Tyee Trail is a new addition to Propain’s lineup, but it’s technically a modified version of the Tyee Enduro that launched a few years ago. By swapping out the upper shock link, Propain were able to reduce the travel to create a 145mm travel bike that’s more of a do-everything / all-mountain bike, rather than being an enduro smasher.

The Tyee Trail is equipped with a 160mm fork, and can be run either with full 29” wheels, or with a mixed wheel setup - a flip chip on the seatstays helps preserve the geometry.

Propain has two complete builds available for $4,049 and $5,999 USD, but it’s their online configurator that’s especially appealing. By clicking a few buttons you can swap out things like brakes, suspension, or dropper post length to create a custom build kit of your own.
Propain Tyee Trail Details

• Travel: 145mm / 160mm fork
• 29" or mixed wheels
• Carbon frame (alloy available)
• 64.2° head angle
• Chainstay length: 446mm (L)
• Sizes: XS, S, M, L, XL
• Price as tested: $6,094 USD
• Weight: 33.1 lb / 15 kg (size L)
• propain-bikes.com

The bike I reviewed would retail for approximately $6,094 USD, with a build kit that includes a Fox 36 fork and Float X Factory shock, Shimano XT drivetrain, SRAM Maven Base brakes, and DT Swiss XM 1700 wheels.

Pinkbike's Take

I was initially a little skeptical about the Tyee Trail - could less travel and a little lighter build kit really make that much of a difference compared to the Tyee Enduro? As it turns out, the answer is a resounding “yes.” The more supportive suspension feel is a better fit for the Tyee Trail, a bike that feels right at home on trails with a mix of jumps, berms, and tech. It’s not the absolute plushest, but its snappy, engaging character makes it a very fun, versatile machine.— Mike Kazimer



Frame Details

There’s been plenty of internet chatter lately about how all bikes look the same, and while I don't agree, I do understand the basis of the complaint, especially considering the ubiquity of the Horst link suspension design. The Tyee is one of the exceptions to the rule, and Propain’s Pro 10 suspension design gives it a look that stands out from the crowd. Personally, I like the frame shape - it’s different enough without being visually jarring, and that’s true for both the carbon and aluminum versions.

The Tyee received ports in the head tube in 2025, which means riders are no longer forced to go with thru-headset cable routing. It’s still possible for the masochists out there, but everyone else can run their brake and derailleur lines inside the frame, but on the outside of the headset bearings.

My gripe about the seat tube length that I mentioned in 2023 still holds true. It’s on the taller side, and the insertion depth is limited due to the angle and the way it tapers, which means shorter riders may have issues trying to fit a longer travel dropper post. I have pretty long legs, so I was able to run a 210mm OneUp post, but that’s not going to be the case for everyone; I would have been out of luck if I wanted to run a Fox 210mm Transfer post for example. Propain does have a chart that helps make it easier to figure out exactly what posts will work depending on a rider's inseam.

There’s plenty of room for a water bottle, plus accessory mounts under the downtube to mount a tool or other accessory holder. The chainslap protection is robust, and does a decent job of keeping the bike quiet - I’d put in in the middle of the pack as far as the overall decibel level out on the trail goes.


A flip chip makes it possible to run either a 29" or 27.5" rear wheel while preserving the geometry.

The Tyee's bearings and even the seatpost collar have an extra rubber seal to help keep water at bay.




Geometry

Not surprisingly, the Tyee Trail's geometry is pretty close to that of the Tyee Enduro. The head angle is just .3-degrees different due to the difference in fork travel (the Trail version runs a 160mm fork, the Enduro a 170). The Tyee Trail's upper shock link reduces the leverage ratio slightly, drops the travel down to 145mm, and lowers the bottom bracket.

The chainstay length is the same 446mm on the M, L, and XL sizes, and it's 430mm on the S and XS sizes. Those two smaller sizes also roll on dual 27.5" wheels.




Suspension Design

Propain’s Pro 10 suspension design uses two co-rotating links, with the shock “floating” in between them. On the Tyee Trail, a 210 x 50mm shock delivers 145mm of rear travel.

The hallmark trait of Propain’s bikes is the fairly high anti-squat values, which increase slightly as the bike goes through its travel before dropping off for the last 30% of the shock’s stroke.

The Tyee Trail has a progressive leverage curve, with a change of around 19% - that’s a number that’ll work well with air shocks, but running a coil isn’t totally out of the question.

Test Bike Setup

Propain’s online configurator offers all sorts of options, allowing riders to choose everything from stem length to brake model, which hopefully means there’s not too much parts swapping required when the bike arrives.

I ended up running a 210mm OneUp post instead of the 180mm Fox Transfer that the bike arrived with, and switched the 35mm SixPack stem out for a 40mm stem to suit my personal preference.

I started off by running the bike in full 29” mode when we were filming the Value Bike Field test down in Sedona. I wanted to see how the bigger wheels worked on the chunky desert terrain, and the same goes for the Albert Radial tires.

More recently, I’ve been running the Tyee Trail with mixed wheels and Specialized’s new radial Butcher and Eliminator tires. Those tires are pretty hefty, but the MX setup is a great for hitting jumps and riding steeper trails without getting buzzed.

Climbing

Propain’s bikes have historically been snappy pedalers - even the 180mm Spindrift feels much, much more efficient than what you’d expect. That’s the story with the Tyee Trail as well, although the Schwalbe radial tires do help take some of the edge off that initial firmness, in a good way.

There was a time when “climbs like a hardtail” was the highest level praise for a full suspension bike, but that’s no longer the case - modern riders have come to expect their bikes to take the edge off when climbing and descending, all without bobbing too wildly or feeling too mushy. With the Tyee Trail, it has a very satisfying surge when you step on the pedals - it stays higher in its travel, and feel quicker out of the gate than other bikes in this category. That trait is great on smoother climbs, but it can be a little less beneficial when things get really chunky.


Take the trails in Sedona, for instance. Many of them contain rocky, stairstep-like sections that require the right amount of power and speed to successfully clean. With non-radial tires, the Tyee Trail borders on feeling too firm off the top; it doesn’t get into its travel as easily as something like the Specialized Stumpjumper, or even the Santa Cruz Bronson, another dual-link bike. Add in those softer radial casing tires, though, and the Tyee Trail feels excellent. The influence of radial tires hasn’t really been discussed all the much in the context of suspension feel, but in some cases it can make a bike feel like it has a lighter compression tune. After all, tires are the first thing to encounter an obstacle, and in this case they do a great job of muting the initial impact, and pair very nicely with the firmer suspension feel earlier in the stroke.

As far as the actual climbing position goes, the geometry feels very similar to other bikes in this category - the 77-degree seat angle and 64.2-degree head tube angle are fairly typical, and it didn’t take long to get comfortable with the Tyee’s overall handling. The fact that it tends to sit a little higher in its travel gives it a little quicker handling than the geometry chart might suggest - it’s certainly not sluggish, and even at slower speeds it’s still very manageable. The overall weight isn’t ridiculously light, but it’s also not ridiculously heavy, and gram-obsessed riders could shed some grams by throwing more carbon parts (and money) at it.


Descending

When the Tyee Trail was announced I was curious about just how much difference a different link and build kit would actually make compared to the Enduro version I’d already reviewed. As it turns out, the change in ride feel is significant, and I’d say its for the better, at least for riders looking for a bike that’s more of an all-rounder. For the trails close to my home in Bellingham, Washington, the Trail version is pretty ideal - it has enough travel for just about anything, and it feels very responsive. It’s best on trails that feel like oversized pump tracks, where there’s free speed available if you find the right rhythm. It’s a great jumper too - there’s a nice poppy nature to it, even with those moderately long chainstays.

The geometry numbers are very similar to what you'd find on many enduro bikes, so it's really the amount of travel on tap that makes the difference - the head angle is slack enough that the steepness of the trail doesn't really pose much of an issue, and the overall wheelbase provides a good amount of high speed stability. Yes, more travel would make it easier to go faster on extra-chopped up sections of trail, but for trails that are a touch less rowdy the Tyee Trail feels like the better match. It feels zippier, to use the technical term, and rather than being an enduro bike that feels a little closer to a trail bike like the Tyee Enduro, it’s a trail bike that feels like, well, a trail bike. (Feel free to swap the phrase “trail bike” with “all-mountain” bike if that makes more sense for your personal bike categorization rules).

Similar to what I mentioned in the climbing section, radial tires help take the edge off the choppier sections of trail, and give the Tyee better small bump manners than it would otherwise. It’s not the plushest mid-travel bike out there, but even on the chunky Sedona trails it held its own - the Float X is a good match, and the new, larger bottom out bumper helps keep the bike from making any bad noises or rattling its rider’s bones when the end of the travel is reached.


Which rear wheel size is better - 29” or 27.5”?

These days I prefer mixed wheels for enduro and DH bikes, and 29” wheels for XC bikes. In the mid-travel category I’d say I’m wheel size agnostic - it really depends on the terrain and what type of ride characteristic I’m chasing. On the Tyee, the dual 29” setup offers better rollover when climbing, and it feels faster once it’s up to speed while descending. Somewhat counterintuitively, I preferred the Tyee Trail's cornering performance with the dual 29” wheels: it felt like I could load them up and really rip them around a turn better than I could with the mixed-wheel setup.

For steeper terrain or jump-filled trails I’d go with the mixed wheels - there’s more butt-to-tire clearance, whether you’re tipping it into a steep chute, or getting airborne off of a lippy jump.

How Does It Compare?

The geometry numbers of the Santa Cruz Bronson or Hightower and the Tyee Trail are pretty similar - they both have a 64(ish) degree head angle, and 440-something millimeter chainstays. The suspension feel is quite different between the two, though; the Bronson has more traction, and gets into its travel more easily than the Tyee trail. Yes, the Bronson has 5 millimeters more travel than the Tyee Trail, but the difference feels greater than that. The Tyee is a snappier pedaler; it feels closer to the way that Santa Cruz’s bikes felt a couple of generations ago, with less suspension movement under hard pedaling. The Bronson’s stack height is 10mm higher, which comes in handy on steeper terrain - the Tyee Trail is a good candidate for a 40mm rise bar in order to match that front end height.

For rougher terrain the Bronson is more comfortable, but on smoother, more rolling trails the Tyee has a firmer platform to push against when pumping through rollers.

When it comes to features, the Tyee has the ability to run either wheel size, while with Santa Cruz you’ll need to choose either the Bronson or Hightower depending on which way you want to go. The Bronson gets the point for overall frame finishing details - the tube-in-tube routing makes brake or derailleur housing swaps easier compared to the Tyee. The Bronson also has in-frame storage, which isn’t as much of a necessity now that tiny backpacks are back in fashion, but it is nice to have a spot to hold a tube and mini-pump just in case.

Which Model is the Best Value?

Going with an aluminum frame is always the way to go for riders who want to save some coin and don’t mind a little extra weight. The aluminum frame is around a pound heavier, and $800 less expensive.

Propain’s configurator makes it easy to spend where it makes sense, and save where it doesn’t. Personally, I’d go with a mechanical drivetrain, aliuminum wheels, Maven Base brakes, and then splurge a little on nicer suspension - that’d put the price around $4,000 for a well spec’d aluminum all-mountain bike that shouldn’t need any upgrades for a while.

Technical Report

Shimano XT drivetrain: It’s nice having the option to built up a bike without batteries. Shimano’s XT drivetrain still works well, and it actually has an adjustable clutch, unlike its wireless counterpart.

Schwalbe Albert tires: I’ve probably already written more than enough words about how radial tires improve the feel of the Tyee Trail, but a new tire has recently become available that I’d recommend over the Albert - the Schwable Romy. The Albert is a good general use tire, but it can get overwhelmed in muddier conditions. The Romy has a more open tread pattern, and has a great blend of rolling speed and grip. I’d recommend an Albert up front and a Romy in the back, or going with a dual Romy setup.

DT Swiss XM1700 wheels: These aluminum wheels held up very well, and even after spending time being smashed into desert sandstone they're still spinning true. The newer version available in Propain's configurator has DT's DEG DF hubs, which allow riders to chose how many degrees the freehub rotates before engaging the star ratchet in order to reduce the amount of pedal feedback. That hub should be a good complement to Propain's suspension layout.




Pros

+ Great pedaling performance, ideal for riders who want a snappy, efficient feeling bike
+ Wide range of parts spec options

Cons

- Not the plushest mid-travel bike out there; may not suit riders looking for maximum traction and comfort
- Longer and tapered seat tube limits maximum dropper post lengths
