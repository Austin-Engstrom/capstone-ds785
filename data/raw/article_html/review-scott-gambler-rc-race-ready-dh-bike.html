Scott Gambler RC Review: A Race-Ready DH Bike That's Easy to Live With

If you’re familiar with Scott Bikes, you’d recognize the Gambler as their downhill bike even if it didn’t sport the brand’s badge. The “hidden” shock isn’t a gimmick, and the number of pivots isn’t a game of who has the most marbles; it’s the result of a carefully calculated suspension design.

What the Gambler does boast is 210mm of travel and an assortment of adjustments, including shock rate, reach, chainstay, BB height/head tube angle, and wheel size. It’s been put through countless test laps thanks to team riders, engineers, and local heroes. That includes both racers and freeriders alike.

Two models in the line are divided by a large price gap. The Gambler RC costs $9,299 USD, whereas the Gambler 10 is a softer blow on your wallet at $5,899. Savings on the Gambler 10 are attributed to an aluminum rear triangle and less performance-oriented suspension, however, after a few upgrades, it could be the more appealing option.
Gambler RC Details

• Carbon frame (alloy rear triangle on Gambler 10)
• Wheel size: MX or dual 29"
• 6-bar suspension design
• 2-position progression adjustment
• Travel: 210 mm (203 mm fork)
• 62.5-63.5 degree head tube angle
• 445-460 mm chainstay (high BB)
• Sizes: M, L (tested), XL
• Weight: 16.84 kg (37.13 lb)
• Price: $5,899-9,299 USD / $7,999-12,499 CAD
• More info: scott-sports.com

The bigger questions to answer are, what’s all the fuss about with a ton of pivot points? What adjustments are right for you? And, how does all of that hardware hold up in the long run?

Frame Details

Scott doesn’t mess about with carbon construction - they’ve been playing that game for decades, so throw your preconceived notions about noise and stiffness out the window. The huge walls of the frame serve a purpose besides holding a unique aesthetic, too; they protect the shock and small eyelet bearings from debris and contaminants. Removing the lower shock cover is done by hand - no tools needed. In fact, a multi-tool is strapped to the inside of the cover.

All of the pivots and the rear axle use T30 torx-head bolts. The torque values are etched on them, so there’s no excuse for stripped bolts. The unique two-piece lower link and press-fit bottom bracket shell combo threads together, carrying with it an integrated bashguard.

When implementing a chainstay length adjustment, Scott engineers had to tackle the hurdle of the universal derailleur hanger to mount SRAM’s XX DH Transmission derailleur. The best approach was to offer a bolt-on 157mm dropout, one for each chainstay length. On the other side, a flip-chip is all that’s needed to move the axle position, and a reversible brake mount accounts for proper rotor alignment.

The engineers learned that the laterally mounted brake adaptor on the previous Gambler model could flex under high frame forces. That caused braking consistency issues, so a top-loading mount was favored.

In relation to brakes, riders who run their brakes moto-style (front brake on the right) aren’t forgotten about. Dual internal routes run the entirety of the frame for a quiet and sleek brake line.




Suspension Design

What’s the point of a 6-bar suspension design, anyway? By adding in those extra links and pivots, each aspect of the suspension can be tuned with further independence. In the Gambler’s case, the design allows for a high anti-squat for a downhill bike without needing to rely as heavily on chain dampers. The Scott DH Factory team often uses custom linkage, as each rider has their own preferences.

The anti-rise is on the lower side, hovering around 60% throughout the travel. As a result, the rear wheel remains more active under braking. In turn, this can “lift” the rear wheel of the bike, making the geometry steeper. It’s a compromise that I’m not usually fond of, however, it was less apparent on the Gambler than on other bikes I’ve ridden with similar anti-rise values.

At the forward shock mount lies a square flip-chip insert with four positions. Placing the bolt in the chip at the high or low end changes the progression between 25 and 30% overall. Each leverage curve is a smooth arc and gives a predictable ride.

Geometry & Sizing

Woof! Where to begin explaining this puzzle? Let’s go with the easy pickings first. The Gambler comes in three front triangle sizes: M, L, XL. The size large I rode sat around 460-470mm. Even with all of the adjustments possible, it’s fair to say the Gambler is on the cusp on measuring shorter than other brands' typical reach numbers, but only by a few millimeters.

To counteract some of that loss, Scott includes two headsets with the Gambler package: a set of centered, zero-offset cups and 6mm offset option to increase or decrease the reach. It’s tough to pinpoint the exact reach as that can hinge on other settings, because the reach can change by as much as 24mm in total on one frame size.

Out back, two axle positions give a 15mm change, but four chainstay lengths are possible with the 27.5” rear wheel. In the high BB setting, the chainstays are shorter, 445 or 460mm. Moving to the low BB setting lengthens the chainstay by 5mm (450/465mm), but cuts the reach by 8mm. The 29” wheel only works in the long dropout setting and should be placed in the low BB position. That results in a head tube angle of 63.5 degrees, but retains the same 14 millimeters of BB drop as the low MX setting.

I wouldn’t fret over getting the numbers exactly to your liking. Instead, I'd focus on how your style relates to the general setup. For example, freeriders will make the most of a 27.5” rear wheel, short chainstay length, high BB setting, and 25% progression setting. You could also experiment with the higher progression option, but keep in mind that you'll have to change spring rates and that the dynamic sag may vary.

Two headsets let you dial in the reach with either +6, 0, and -6 mm moves.
The four-way adjustable forward shock mount gives two progression settings and two head angle/BB options.

Test Bike Setup

As you can imagine by now, the amount of adjustability can be overwhelming. Scott leaves those choices up to the rider, but some guidance would be helpful. One crafty feature Scott carried over from other models is an integrated sag indicator. Measuring the sag would otherwise require precision eyeballs to work around the shock tunnel.

Scott test riders rode every rear shock under the sun to make sure they all jived with the Gambler. The stock shock of choice is a Fox DHX2 Factory - a great damper, but a small inconvenience if the supplied spring doesn’t work for you. The progression and BB/head tune angle adjustments are independent from one another, however, changing leverage rates will likely require another spring change. Changing the chainstay length will also affect the leverage rate as you're lengthening the lever acting on the shock. I bounced between a 450 lb spring in the less progressive setting/short chainstay and a 475 when flipping the chips in the other direction. That usually spun the sag indicator around the common 30% mark in the progressive setting.

The Fox 40 was set to 86 PSI with 5 volume spacers, trending towards more open rebound and compression damping than recommended.

Right off the bar, in Châtel, France, I swapped for a 30mm rise bar. By the end of the first day, I was super comfortable hanging it out on the bike through jump trails and in the tech.

A few more changes were made as time went on. Back on home soil, in the Whistler Bike Park, I increased the reach to +6mm. The only option I didn’t experiment with was a 29” rear wheel, but you might see a bigger wheel make an appearance in another article.

The Gambler received a healthy dose of jumps, tech, and high-speed tracks. Conditions also varied from the mud of the Alps to late summer dust in Whistler. No matter what type of tracks I eyed up, the Gambler didn’t shy away from the task.



The clay mud gets dragged onto the graphite-filled rock in Châtel. It's like goose shit on glass, but the Gambler held its course well.


Descending

Complex high-pivot contraptions may be mesmerizing in rough sections, but they can falter in other areas, or at least take some adaptation in riding technique. On the Gambler, it’s smooth sailing everywhere. That goes for any of the possible positions, too.

25 and 30% of overall progression are sweet numbers to work with. Neither figure is off in left field, nor does it make it difficult to set up the shock for. The change between the two is useful for matching a range of track types or riding styles. The less progressive option gives more support earlier on in the travel - ideal for flatter tracks or jump trails. For max traction in steep, slippery trails, which there were plenty of in the Alps, the 30% mode sits further into the travel at sag and dances over webs of roots, needing only the softest touch to initiate suspension movement. The suspension ramps harder and later, so you can understand the benefits of having two progression positions to choose between.

Sliding down the Champery World Cup track takes the highest confidence. In the longest, lowest, slackest mode, the Gambler delivered exactly that. The bike settles in as you’d expect a race-type bike to do. I thought the 465mm chainstay might take more effort to throw around in the steeps, but that wasn’t the case at all. Funny enough, the low BB mode makes the chainstay longer than the reach. Even with this seldom-seen front-to-rear-center balance, it’s quite maneuverable. It’s possible that taller riders may wish for a longer and higher front end on the XL.

Looking at the massive carbon surfaces, you might think that it’s extra-stiff or would vibrate you off the trail. I was pleasantly wrong about that assumption. Whatever methods the engineers applied here make for a strong, yet forgiving ride.

So it’s not brutally stiff, but what about the noises? Surprisingly, the downtube doesn’t turn into a massive echo chamber either. Through the roughest sections of track, the chain can hit some part of the frame or lower skid plate. If you look at a few team members’ bikes, an STFU silencer attends to that. Rarely do they opt for a chain damper on the cranks, though.

Every aspect of the Gambler is damn good. Collectively, the predictability, composure, and sensibility of the suspension sold me. It’s the type of ride that will always save you when you get into trouble, rather than bite back.

Tracking well over the mud-caked roots.





What's It Like to Live With?

Scott made wrenching on the Gambler as straightforward as it could be. Compared to other bikes made up of one hundred pieces, bolt checks won't have to be made every lap, and shock adjustments can be completed with one set of hands. The concise manual explains cable routing management, settings, and a full list of spare parts, should you need one.

After surviving major cleanups, the bearings are still rolling smoothly. The frame doesn’t retain water in the shock tunnel or crunch rocks in the linkage. Despite being blasted with copious amounts of mud, the covers stayed in place and didn’t rattle off after repeated use. The hardware is robust and the pivots have never developed any creaking.




What's the Best Value?

The full-carbon Gambler RC has all of the quality components you’d expect to find on a premium downhill bike. They’re identical, if not equivalent, to what you’d find on a high-end Santa Cruz V10 build. In U.S. dollars, the Gambler RC costs $300 more than a V10 X01. Neither top-end model is exactly a killer deal, but they feature proven components.

The Gambler 10, on the other hand, is more enticing. It only costs $5,800 USD. Settling for an aluminum rear triangle, but keeping the carbon front half, is a fair trade-off. That leaves you with $3,400 set aside for spare parts, travelling, or upgrades. Speaking of, there are a few components I’d replace from the get-go. First on my list would be a Charger 3.1 fork damper, and possibly more honed DH brakes. I don’t have much experience with the Alex rims listed, but I’d run them until they were egg-shaped, then replace them with a reputable alloy option.

I know what you’re thinking: why not build up a frame? Unfortunately, frame kits are only available for European markets.

How Does It Compare?

Another hot bike with a matrix of setup choices is the Orbea Rallon DH. I mainly rode the Rallon DH in the lowest, longest, slackest setting to gain perspective from a downhill racer’s point of view. Both bikes exhibit a low center of gravity, yet they are decently light for a bike being put through the paces on the World Cup circuit.

In race mode, the Gambler sets itself apart by sitting slacker with a longer chainstay length. I found the Rallon to be more disconnected in terms of balance. The stack height is generally taller, yet the front axle still feels underneath you - great for loading up into berms, but it didn't encourage me to attack down the steeps as confidently.

Sifting further into the materials chat, the Gambler has a more even blend of flex front-to-rear. It’s neither uncomfortable nor noodley. On the Rallon, the front end is stiffer and produces some unwanted vibrations at high speeds or across braking bumps. Behind you, the rear triangle is softer. That can be helpful to maintain traction, however, some riders may find it to be on the more flexible side.

Suspension-wise, the Gambler as a whole is much more composed, controlled, and quieter. There’s less energy loss when trying to pump and carry speed.

The final point to take away is that the Gambler doesn’t confine your geometry choices. When purchasing a Rallon, you’ll have to decide between 442 and 450mm (which aren’t particularly long to begin with) and live with it. Similarly, the head tube only allows for an angle change (again, which isn’t that slack for a race bike), not a change in reach. You can also run a 29” rear wheel, which the Rallon DH cannot fit, and the interchangeable dropout lets you decide when and where you want to change the chainstay length.





Technical Report

Maxxis Minion DHR II, 3C Maxx Terra, DH casing: Say what? Yes, the high-end Gambler RC is not specified with the grippiest rubber available from Maxxis. I still hear complaints about tire prices from folks riding around on five-figure bikes, so this choice should save your tire from erasing knobs after a big day in the bike park. Unless you're attempting to tackle the gnarliest, steepest rock faces, it performs relatively well, even in the wet.

Fox DHX2: The new mono tube layout of the DHX2 is a smooth operator when it comes to changing stroke direction. With the new design, both rebound knobs are located at the rear end of the shock and can be rotated without needing to break out any tools. I've yet to experience any top-out clunk on this generation either.

Maven Silver Brakes: These are strong, consistent stoppers. On the most vertical tracks, there was no fading from the metallic pads and 200mm rotors. Being able to set up the lever throw and bite point equally on the fly is another big win.




Pros

+ Very predictable suspension characteristics.
+ Low, stable center of gravity.
+ All of the adjustments you could ever want.
+ Tidy packaging given all of the technology packaged into the frame.


Cons

- Taller riders might find the XL too low and short.
- A raw carbon finish would be more attractive, but I'm sure the marketing team would disagree (I'm grasping at straws here).




Pinkbike's Take

If it's not easy to tell, every time I parked the Gambler for the day, I left with a smile on my face. Part of that came from being a retired privateer racer; flashbacks to daily linkage rebuilds and constant bolt checks on complex frames had me worried at first. Those thoughts were put to rest quickly - the Gambler was put away wet and continued late in the season with minimal attention. It goes to show that the crew at Scott did their homework, putting the Gambler through equally polite abuse before signing off on the project.

What's more is that there isn't anything weird happening at the linkage. It's not as mindblowing as some high-pivot bikes can be in specific instances, but it's composed, comfortable, and predictable. Those are the critical elements of a proper downhill bike. Job well done, I'd say.— Matt Beer
