OneUp has had flat pedals in their component lineup for nearly a decade, but it wasn’t until earlier this year that they began offering an option for riders that wanted to clip in. The Clip pedals are intended to work for everything from XC to DH and all the riding categories in between.

The pedals use an SPD-compatible mechanism, with adjustable spring tension and four removable pins on each side. They have 5-degrees of float and a 12-degree release angle.

The aluminum platform is 95mm long and 80mm wide, and the cleat mechanism itself is 26.8mm thick. OneUp claim it’s the thinnest clipless pedal in the world at 14.4mm, and so far I haven’t found anything to dispute that claim.
Clip Pedal Details
• SPD compatible
• Chromoly steel axle
• 90 x 80mm aluminum body
• Internals: bushing + 3 cartridge bearings
• 4 removable pins per side
• 8 platform color options
• Weight: 416 grams with pins / 406 grams without
• MSRP: $160 USD
• oneupcomponents.com

For comparison, Shimano's XTR pedals are 17.2mm thick. Keep in mind that the ground clearance difference between the two is only 1.4mm, so it's not going to be a drastic change - you'll still probably keep hitting the same rock you hit on every ride no matter which brand pedal you're on.

The pedals are priced at $160 USD, and are available in eight different colors, including purple, orange, green, and bronze. Cleats are included, along with a plastic cleat spacer to fine tune the amount of room between pedal and shoe. A pedal nut bearing tool is also provided, which can be used to remove the axle for a quick re-greasing.




Terminology Talk

Clipless pedal reviews inevitably result in a semantics debate breaking out in the comments section, and somewhat understandably so. I’m old enough to have started riding with toe clips and straps, so the clipless terminology makes total sense to me - ditch the toe clips for a pedal with a binding mechanism and presto, you have clipless pedals.

I also get that lots of riders barely even know what clips and straps look like; for that contingent, calling these pedals “clipless” is a head scratcher. For this review, I’m sticking with clipless, partially because I’m stubborn, and partially because I like the incongruity of the term. Now back to the review.


Pins

I’m still not convinced that pins are necessary, or even helpful at all, on a pedal with a fixed clip-in mechanism. I don’t want my shoes to hang up when I’m trying to un-clip, just like you wouldn’t want a random screw preventing or hindering your release on a ski binding.

After a couple rides with the pins installed I took them out completely and never looked back. Could they help with traction when you’re not clipped in and resting a foot on the pedal body? Potentially, although I didn’t have any issues slipping off the pedal during those half in / half out moments - the pedal body is nice and wide, and the grooves in it help add some traction when things are slippery.



Retention

My first round with these pedals was cut short when OneUp issued a stop sale notice - OneUp said that “in rare and specific conditions, certain riders may experience difficulty unclipping from the pedal.” I was one of those riders, and at that point I’d already had several instances where the cleat would hang up, delaying the moment of release, and at times making it almost impossible to get out of the pedal depending on the orientation of my foot.

The solution was a revised binding plate (the metal bar that sits in front of the rear portion of the clip-in mechanism). I sent my pedals back to OneUp for the change, and restarted the review process when the updated pedals arrived.

With the updated plate in place, it was much easier to get in and out, enough that I was able to increase the spring tension closer to the middle of the range. The performance has remained consistent, and I haven’t had any further release issues.

I've used OneUp's cleats and Shimano's SH51 cleats with good results. The Shimano cleats seem to provide a slightly easier entry and exit, even though visually the shape is nearly identical. The combination of the wide platform and the shape of OneUp clip-in mechanism creates a very secure interface - there's minimal up and down or fore-aft movement once you're clipped in, which make the pedals feel extra secure.


OneUp vs Shimano

I really like the Clip pedal's platform shape and the additional support it provides - I did a ride with an XTR pedal on one side and a OneUp pedal on the other and the difference was noticeable. Of course, a shoe’s stiffness and sole design plays a part in this equation, but for shoes with a flatter soles the OneUp platform provides a nice perch to rest on. In fact, the platform is wide and supportive enough that it may allow some riders to go with a slightly softer shoe than they'd normally run.

The OneUp pedals also offer a higher maximum spring tension - that’ll be a plus for riders who still pop out of Shimano’s pedals even when they’re cranked all the way down. I’m usually in the middle of the spring tension range with Shimano pedals, so that’s not an issue for me, but it’s worth a mention

When it comes to price and weight, Shimano’s XT pedals are $10 less and weigh 14 grams more. XTR pedals are 16 grams lighter, but they’re also $60 more expensive at their full retail price. Shimano’s pedals have a two year warranty, versus one year for OneUp.


Durability wise, I’ve had very, very good luck with Shimano's pedals over the years, especially the XT model. With XTR, the little rubber dust seal does have a tendency to pop out, but both models can go a really long time without needing service. When it is time to service them, fresh grease is usually all that’s required. For the OneUp Clip pedals, service is fairly simple, and the bushing removal / installation tool is only $10. My pedals don’t have any play in them yet, and there was plenty of grease on the axle when I pulled them apart, so things are off to a good start.

When it comes to color options, OneUp handily takes that one - there aren’t any purple pedals or green pedals in Shimano’s lineup.

At the end of the day, my overall recommendation goes to Shimano’s XT M8120 pedals due to the slightly smoother entry and exit, lower price, and proven longevity, but OneUp’s Clip pedals put up a good fight - they’re a solid contender in the sea of SPD-compatible options.



Pros

+ Wide platform provides extra support
+ Very competitive weight
+ Good range of spring tension

Cons

- Pins don't provide much benefit
- Entry and exit isn't quite as smooth as Shimano (but it's close)



Pinkbike's Take

OneUp’s Clip pedals aren’t a dramatic departure from what’s already on the market, and that’s okay. After all, the SPD design has been around for 35 years at this point - it’s withstood the test of time, so it makes sense to offer an option with a few refinements, like a wider, thinner platform, rather than completely starting from scratch. I ran into a few hiccups at the beginning of the test period, but since then the performance has been much more consistent, and I’m not in a rush to switch over to something else. 

— Mike Kazimer

Someoldfart (Jul 18, 2025 at 9:03)
 You’re servicing them incorrectly. When you detect a little play, pull the axle out, clean the outside and inside of the body. For the body I stuff a blue lint free paper towel in with a chop stick and simply wipe the outside of the bearings well. Adjust the cup and cone with a 7 and 8 mm wrenches. Apply new grease inside and out and watch the dirty grease get purged out. Wi-e clean and ensure that the rubber seal is back in place. Done. No need whatsoever to dismantle the bearings from the axle.
