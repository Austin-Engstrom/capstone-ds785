Review: The Adventure Dog Channel - Training the Ultimate Trail Dog

Aug 26, 2024
by Lexi White  
Share

Photo by Katie Bennett

Most anyone who owns both a dog and a mountain bike has dreamed of being chased down the trail by their best furry friend. There’s something special about spending the day pedaling in the woods with your pooch that goes way beyond giving them some exercise.

If you’ve ever been out for a ride with your dog, you know the pure joy that comes from watching them run at full tilt to keep up with the bike as you rip down some of your favorite singletrack together. Taking a water break and hydrating from the same bike bottle. Sharing your trail snacks and taking in the views at the top of a climb. High fiving in the parking lot after another awesome ride. It’s just the best.
The Adventure Dog Channel by Molly Avrin Details
• 195 total vides, 20 bike videos
• Avg Video Length: ~6:30
• Total Time Commitment: ~8 hours (for me)
• Training hiking, biking, skiing, joring & more
• 1x/week members only group virtual session
• Price: $36/month or $380/year
• petesadventurepack.com




But trail dogs can be a controversial subject amongst mountain bikers. With lots of complaints around unruly, uncontrolled dog mayhem on trails that pose a real safety hazard, along with the unmitigated piles of poop that inevitably don’t get picked up, it’s easy to understand why trail dogs often get a bad rap in the MTB community.

Much of this controversy could be avoided with a little more time spent on training our trail dogs and giving them the skills they need to hit the trails with us safely. I won’t downplay the time and effort that goes into doing that training, but if you’re going to take your dog out with you on trails that are shared with other riders, making sure you’re able to ride safely is the least you can do, for them and for your dog.


Photo by Katie Bennett

The Adventure Dog Channel by Molly Avrin

Luckily for those interested in turning their four-legged best bud into a responsible trail dog, there are resources available that claim they can help you chase down that goal. Working with a professional trainer in your local area is one way to get knowledge and advice if you’re looking to train a well-mannered trail dog, but if you don’t have access to someone in your area who has experience working with dogs and bikes, you’re not out of luck.

Molly Avrin of Pete’s Adventure Pack has created an in-depth digital resource for all things trail dog training – the Adventure Dog Channel – and you can access it from anywhere in the world with monthly and yearly membership options. I've seen, bought, and consumed many different online dog training courses (dog nerd alert!), but I've never run into one that focuses on training your dog in the rules of adventure sports, so this course felt quite unique. A monthly membership will run you $36/month and annual memberships go for $380.


So who is Molly, and should you trust and pay her to help you train your dog?

Molly runs Pete’s Adventure Pack, a professional dog training business out of Salt Lake City, UT where she offers everything from one-on-one private sessions to full day training camps and group adventure sessions. Molly has over 17 years of experience working with dogs of all types and temperaments and a lifetime of experience adventuring in the outdoors. She says that the Adventure Dog Channel is the culmination of what she's learned with step-by-step training programs for everything from the foundational training basics to more advanced techniques for teaching dogs how to safely ride, ski, hike, and camp.




What’s In the Channel

The Adventure Dog Channel spans many topics, starting with all the most basic, foundational dog training and life skills a dog would need to be a good companion, let alone a good trail bud. From leash work to recall, the channel offers a lot of upfront guidance on how to work with your dog on basic training before bothering to teach them more advanced skills like how to run next to a bike, which is a nice addition as many of those skills dovetail into the bike work.

For the dogs who already know the basics pretty well, you can skip right to the fun stuff and dig into the more adventure-focused course, although I’d wager most people and pups would benefit from at least a small refresher on basic skills. There’s surely a few tips in the series for tuning up dogs of all training levels. From there, the channel covers nearly any outdoor activity you could think of doing with your dog.

The courses are structured in a series of 2-10 minute videos that all build on each other within the different course topics. The bite-sized nature of the videos within each course make the programs easy to follow and give you small steps to progress through without getting overwhelmed by too much information at once. After you’ve watched and worked through one of the videos, you can mark it as complete and move onto the next. Some videos give you enough to work on that you may not come back for the next one for a few days, but you can easily pick right back up where you left off.

For the sake of this review, we’ll stick to the Biking Course content, but in addition to the already-massive library of videos, new content is continuously added to the channel based on requests and feedback from the community.

Speaking of community, another really valuable benefit that comes with your Adventure Dog Channel membership is Virtual Group Sessions. Held at least once a week, Molly sends out a Zoom link and invites anyone in the channel to join for live Q&A to help you troubleshoot any issues you’re having while you work through the courses. I think this is one of the best components to an online learning course, especially as it relates to dog training. Every dog is different, and accomplishing anything cool with your dog is rarely as easy as watching a handful of videos and expecting your sessions to go exactly as it was laid out online. Having the opportunity to talk things through, get questions answered, and hear the experience of other people working on the same things really brings it all together for me.


Bike Course Breakdown

I dove into all of the content on the Adventure Dog Channel throughout summer and applied the techniques in the courses to see if I could help my dog, Murray, become an even better trail dog citizen. Murray and I do a lot of adventuring together, from biking and skiing, to working as a Canine SAR team, so we’re always looking for new ways to sharpen our skills.

When you and your dog both hit the same feature and they do it with more style.

Murray and I love riding together, but we admittedly hadn’t done too much formal training around it. Since he’s a pretty highly trained dog to begin with, he sort of just coexisted well with me on the trail, and we didn’t think too much about the specifics. So with that in mind, we started from scratch with Molly’s bike videos and applied each step as if we knew nothing about riding together.

The bike course has 18 videos, with 2 new videos around introducing puppies to bikes, so currently 20 videos total. 20 videos might sound like a lot, but most of them are 5 minutes or shorter, so the overall watch time isn’t too bad. Starting with leash drills and bike drills in a parking lot and ending with an off-leash single track ride, the course takes you through each step of the process, building up to the ultimate goal of riding safely and sending it on the trail with your dog.

It might be tempting to skip some of the parking lot drills and head straight to the trail work, but I think it’s worth it to do your best to stick to the actual program and skip as few steps as possible. Even if you breeze through some of them, just ticking the box is worth it to make sure you’re both sharp before moving on.


For me and Murray, practicing riding together on leash was a new experience. We never did that before hitting the trails, and I was reluctant to go through the tediousness of taking steps back. But at the end of the day, making sure those pieces were in place didn’t slow us down much and helped as we got deeper into the course.

In addition to the dog training components, Molly offers up some advice on what gear to bring for your dog, choosing good trails for dog rides (directional, low-traffic, well within your skill limit), and general trail etiquette expectations for trail dogs.

Some Gear Tips to Consider:
• Water
• Collapsible water bowl
• Poop bags
• Slip leash
• Dog first aid kit with extra bleed stop (for broken toe nails)
• Treats
• You can also put a bear bell on your dog if you want to hear where they are behind your bike - this is especially helpful so you make sure you do not let them get too far behind you.

I always put a bear bell on Murray when we ride together, and it’s super helpful for keeping track of where he is without having to have eyes on him all the time. It’s also great for other trail users who can hear the bell coming and aren’t blindsided by a dog coming in hot around a corner or at an intersection. I also like this collar from Nite Ize that has a retractable leash built in. It's pretty handy if you find yourself needing to leash up unexpectedly on the side of the trail, walking through the parking lot, or any number of other reasons.

Don't forget to hydrate and take breaks on big rides with your dog.


Time Commitment

Doing anything cool with your dog is going to take training time. There’s really no way around putting in the extra hours to get the results you want. That said, the amount of time it will take to complete this course and have a bonafide goodboy trail dog is going to be different for everyone and every dog. Some dogs will come in totally green and start from scratch. Others will be able to breeze through the first few steps and jump right into the fine tuning. Some dogs will struggle with random components for reasons that are hard to predict and explain (that’s where the virtual group sessions come in handy). Generally speaking, I would go into the journey with a slow and steady mindset. Rushing through things is the perfect recipe for frustration and failure, so take your time and enjoy the process. At the end of the day, it’s just more time spent with your favorite little buddy anyway, so it’s all good.


Who Should Join

If you’ve been fantasizing about sharing your favorite sport with your favorite pal, but have limited access to local trainers or want a more convenient approach you can tackle on your schedule, give the Adventure Dog Channel a look and consider putting in the extra work to make sure you, your dog, and all the other riders out on the trail are safe when you’re out there riding together. Everyone will find different aspects of the channel valuable, depending on the level of training they and their dog already have, but no matter what place you’re starting from, there are helpful tips and tricks for all dog owners. You could even passive-aggressively buy a month-long membership for your friend who always brings their dog with them and ruins your ride.

And if you’ve more or less mastered riding with your dog, but are interested in introducing them to any of the other activities on the list of courses included, the membership would likely be well worth your time.


Photo by Katie Bennett

Pros and Cons

Pros
+ Progress at your own pace on your own schedule
+ Learn from anywhere, no need to be local to an area or trainer
+ Covers a wide range of sports/activities
+ Virtual group sessions add a lot to the online learning experience
Cons
- Training independently without real-time oversight and guidance can be difficult for less experienced dog people



Pinkbike’s Take

The videos in the Adventure Dog Channel are thorough and easy to follow, offering practical and approachable steps to training a solid adventure dog. Having the option to work through a program on your own time without having to worry about scheduling with an in-person trainer is convenient and affordable, especially because the virtual group sessions still give you the opportunity to get live feedback. There’s no one right way to train a dog to do anything, but the lessons outlined in the channel are a great place to start and should give most people and dogs a strong foundation to build on. And whether you jump into this particular product or not, training your dog to have the skills it needs to know what to do and how to behave on a ride means you can both focus less on keeping track of each other and more on having as much fun as possible. Which is why we’re out there in the first place, right?—Lexi White

Trail running is a little easier on dogs that trail riding. When I was running ultras, I would take dogs on shorter runs of up to twenty miles, but beyond that you're asking for trouble.

Trail riding is probably the hardest on dogs because they have to maintain a faster pace and they don't get to rest like they can when hiking or running. Maximum ten miles or you could be asking for trouble. This can vary greatly based on terrain and weather. Be aware that certain surfaces are harder on dogs, ie sharp rocks.

Clearly there are dogs that are more capable, and then there are dog owners that push their dogs further than they should, but once you've experienced your best friend bonking, it'll leave you wishing you'd chose differently.

Wisdom comes from experience, but listening to others with hard earned wisdom can save you and yours a bunch of heartache.
