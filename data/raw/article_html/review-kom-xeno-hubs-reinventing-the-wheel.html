Review: KOM Xeno Hubs - Reinventing The Wheel

Oct 14, 2025
by Seb Stott  
Share
Tweet
You must login to Pinkbike.
Don't have an account? Sign up

Join Pinkbike  Login
136 Comments

Reinventing the wheel may be a stretch, but KOM's Xeno hubs represent a radical redesign of bicycle hubs. Rather than the hub shell and freehub body sitting next to each other on the hub axle, the freehub spins inside the hub shell and runs the whole width of the hub. It's kind of like the two components are doing a firm handshake instead of a fist bump.

Why would you want that? According to the British company, their patented design makes for a much stiffer hub, with less chance of breaking an axle, longer bearing life and less stress on the pawls. The design is also claimed to reduce drag while pedalling because only two bearings are spinning at any time, compared to four or five with a standard hub.

The downside? This layout forces the freehub ratchet to the brake side, which requires proprietary rotors and potential clearance issues.
KOM Xeno Hub Details
• Performance claims: Reduced drag, longer bearing life, stronger axle
• Straight-pull spokes
• Only compatible with KOM disc rotors
• Available with all major freehub and axle standards
• Modular, serviceable design
• E-bike approved
• Customizable colours
• Price: £550 (rear hub) / £275 (front); £1,175 (wheelset with DT Swiss EX51 rims) - prices include rotors
• Claimed hub weight: 260 g (rear hub), 140g (front)
• Wheelset weight: 2,079 g (effective)
• Lifetime Warranty on axles and hub shell
• More information: k-o-m.co.uk

From a first principles engineering perspective, I'm convinced the KOM design is superior. The question is, is it worth making the switch to a new way of doing things?

Six large pawls are housed inside the ratchet ring on the brake side.
The ratchet ring sits inside the rotor, but is separate from it.

Design

All hubs have an axle, a sleeve that fits concentrically around the frame's thru-axle. Most regular hubs have a hub shell that connects to the spokes, with two bearings between it and the axle. Next to this, there's the freehub body that holds the cassette, with two or three bearings between it and the axle.

A cutaway showing the KOM freehub axle inside the hub body and the hub axle inside that.

A cutaway of a relatively standard hub.

The main hub axle that goes around the thru-axle, with the bearings on which the freehub axle spins.

The freehub axle, which connects to the cassette on the right and the rotor and ratchet on the left, goes concentrically around the hub axle. The hub body spins on the two large-diameter bearings.

In the KOM hubs, the freehub fits concentrically around the full width of the axle, with two bearings between it and the axle at either end. The hub body goes concentrically around this freehub, with two large-diameter bearings between it and the freehub, allowing it to spin independently while coasting.


This approach has three main advantages:

• The full-width freehub adds stiffness to the system, reducing flex in the axle, which reduces the risk of axle fatigue and failure, bearing misalignment and bearing wear.
• The two bearings that connect the freehub to the axle are as outboard as possible, further reducing stress on the axle and the bearings.
• While pedalling, only the two inner bearings between the freehub and the axle are spinning, reducing drag compared to conventional hubs, where the bearings in the hub body and freehub all spin while pedalling. (During coasting, it's the two larger-diameter bearings that are rotating, but this is the same number of bearings as a conventional hub.)
• The stiffer hub and brake-side ratchet sidesteps the risk of uneven loading of the pawls due to axle flex, which can damage freehubs in conventional hubs with radial ratchet designs (this is less of an issue with axial ratchet rings like DT Swiss hubs).

Force diagram from Sheffield Hallam University's hub stiffness test.
The results: the KOM hubs showed significantly less vertical deflection with a load applied to the hub.

We have proof that the KOM hubs are stiffer thanks to an independent test from Sheffield Hallam University. They tested the KOM hubs against four other high-end MTB hubs with a 4,000 Newton (408 Kg or 900 lb of force) vertical load and measured the deflection. The Xeno hubs deflected about a half to a third as much vertically as the other hubs in this test, and a tenth as much angularly (bending). So we know they are stiffer, but whether that matters is an open question - even in the least stiff hub, we're talking about a millimetre of vertical deflection under nearly half a ton of load.

To achieve this layout, the ratchet is moved to the brake side (I'll not call it the non-drive side to avoid confusion). This required KOM to produce their own disc rotors to fit around the ratchet ring. They offer these rotors in all common sizes: 160mm,180mm, 200mm, 203mm, 220mm & 223mm. At £50 each, they're not extortionate compared to other rotors, and your first set is included with the hubs.

On the plus side, this layout has an added benefit of allowing more space for the ratchet, so KOM use six oversized pawls engaging a large-diameter ratchet ring simultaneously, resulting in even less stress on the pawls for a given input torque. This could be particularly relevant to e-bikers.

The ratchet ring is also easily replaceable, so you could swap it out for more or fewer points of engagement (the standard is 120 POE) or if it wears out. In fact, the whole hub is designed to be modular and easy to service with basic tools. KOM say spare parts will be available for sale "for decades."

Loads of room here, but the rotor bolts can catch the frame or brake mount on certain frames.

Compatability

The Xeno hubs are available with interchangeable freehub bodies (XD, Microspline and HD); in 142, 148, and 157 mm widths, and with all rotor sizes from 160 mm to 223 mm. However, the four-bolt brake pattern is a larger diameter than a standard 6-bolt or centerlock rotor, and this won't fit with every bike. KOM has a list of compatible bikes on their website, and instructions to measure your bike to make sure. But if you're planning to keep the wheels for longer than your bike, there is a risk they won't fit future frames.

I've had no issues with a Forestal Siryon and Crossworx Dash 290, but a Privateer 161 wouldn't fit without modifying (filing down) the brake mount.

KOM offer matching front hubs with the same brake interface, but while they claim their bearing layout minimises axial preload to further reduce friction, the rear hub is the real innovation here. So if you're not concerned about matching aesthetics, you could consider a rear wheel only.


Cost and weight comparison

For the bare hubs, KOM claim 260 g for the rear and 140 g for the front. For comparison, DT Swiss's premium 240 straight-pull hubs weigh a claimed 207 g rear and 118 g front. So a set of 240 hubs is 75 g lighter according to official figures.

The 240 hubs cost £319.99 (rear) and £159.99 (front). So the KOM hubs are £230 more expensive for the rear alone, or £345 for the pair. That price includes the KOM rotors of your chosen size. Spare or replacement rotors cost £50 each, which is similar to a standard rotor.

My 29" KOM wheelset with EX 511 rims, 28 spokes on the front and 32 on the rear, weighs 2,515g - that's including the KOM 200 mm rotors, which are 2 mm thick. A set of 200 x 2 mm SRAM HS2 rotors weighs 410 g, and a set of twelve rotor bolts weighs 26 g; so taking that off, the "effective" wheelset weight is 2,079 g. For comparison, a set of DT Swiss EX 1700 wheels (with the same rims on their mid-range 350 hubs) weighs 1,985 grams.

The complete KOM wheelset tested with DT Swiss EX 511 rims costs £1,175 (+ £50 for custom colours), compared to £760 for a DT Swiss EX 1700 wheelset. So there's a £415 price difference for a wheelset, but those KOM hubs include rotors.

You can also get the KOM wheels with Reserve rims, from £2,275.

I tested the wheels on a Crossworx Dash 290 and a Forestal Siryon

Performance

KOM claim their hubs offer noticeably less coasting drag, but I'm not convinced. Measuring such things in the real world is incredibly hard, but in one simple test, I spun up the KOM wheel and a DT Swiss E 1700 wheel to the same speed and timed how long it took to stop. While I can't guarantee the initial speed was identical every time, I tried this crude test three times and got the same result: the KOM stopped first. I also made sure that neither brake was rubbing by retracting the pads.

KOM hub VS DT Swiss drag test

by seb-stott

The KOM hub is on the right, the DT Swiss hub on the left.

This test is imperfect because it doesn't take into account the vertical load on the wheel while riding, and perhaps the KOM system's stiffer axle reduces bearing drag when the wheel is loaded, but you'd need a controlled indoor test rig to measure that. KOM haven't provided any evidence to support this claim except for this video showing a rider on KOM hubs pulling away from his mates, which could be explained any number of ways. When I tried doing roll-down tests (timing how long it takes to coast down a gentle hill), the times were all over the place, even with the same wheelset, making this claim impractical to test in the field.

When pedalling, the KOM hubs could have an advantage since they're only spinning two bearings instead of four, which could reduce drag. But a lab test conducted by Classified found that a DT Swiss 240 hub only had 0.2% power losses when unloaded, so there's not much room for improvement there. Again, the potential gains could be greater when the hub is loaded, but according to this Hambini article, the losses in a typical hub are around 1% when loaded, so even if the KOM hubs were friction-free, this would be too small to measure in the real world.

Sound check.

I can't notice any difference in stiffness either. The rim and spokes will no doubt dominate wheel flex when compared to any hub.

I can report that the KOM brake rotors offer good braking performance, easily on par with the in-house rotors from Hayes, Formula or Magura, whose brakes I tested with. The 120-point ratchet is relatively quick to engage, never skipped in testing, and makes a satisfying whirr. But realistically, the hubs fade into the background like any other.

I've had these wheels for six months, but it's too soon to say if they are any more reliable than conventional hubs. I have several wheelsets that have years of use without issues, so it's impossible to know if these will last any longer.


Pros

+ Hubs are proven to be stiffer by science, which may increase with bearing life and reliability for hard use cases.
+ Backed by warranty, modular design, and spare parts promise

Cons

- Requires specific rotors, but these are included, are available in all sizes and perform well.
- Large rotor bolt diameter won't fit every frame.
- Expensive.
- Heavier than some other premium hubs.




Pinkbike's Take

The ground-up redesign of the KOM rear hub is something that engineers are sure to get hot under the collar about. I'm convinced it's a superior layout. But it's solving a problem that isn't exactly ubiquitous. While broken ratchets, snapped axles, and premature bearing wear aren't unheard of, and some hubs were notorious, these problems are far from universal among conventional hub designs.

That said, if you're a big rider or e-biker looking for a set of wheels to last, they're worth considering. The proven stiffness, oversized ratchet, warranty, modular design and spares should offer confidence that they'll go the distance. The proprietary rotors needn't be a dealbreaker - they're available in all sizes, they're reasonably priced and perform well with multiple brake brands. Besides, rotors are a wear item. The lack of universal frame fitment is a concern if you change frames regularly but want a wheelset to last for years. But, if you're building a custom "forever bike," they have appeal.— Seb Stott


Note: since this article was first published, KOM have pointed out that a rotor (of any size) is included with each wheel or hub. The article has been updated to reflect this. - Seb
