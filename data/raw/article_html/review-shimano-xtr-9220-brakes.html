Shimano XTR 9220

XTR grey remains a special color in the bike world, representing some of the fancier components one can bolt to their bike. Despite the consistent growth in popularity of aftermarket brakes, Shimano's finest offerings still have a serious fanbase, one that loves the distinct feel their brakes offer. The most recent update to the XTR platform saw revisions across the board, but that distinct feel remains unchanged.


XTR Details

• Mineral oil
• Adjustments: Reach, free stroke
• 2 pad options
• 2x15mm, 2x17mm pistons
• Price: $670 USD (pair)
• Brake weight: 294g (front)
• Rotor weight: 171g (203mm)
• shimano.com

BUY AT:
Jenson

The bridged lever design has been brought in-line with the handlebar, catering towards internal routing and tidy aesthetics. The lever body has been reinvented to accommodate that new form, with a pull-link actuated master cylinder, a new lever blade shape, and nicely integrated adjustments. In order to eliminate the wandering bite point their previous brakes had become associated with, Shimano implemented a lower viscosity mineral oil, as well as an updated seal design in the caliper.


Setup

Shimano's bleed process remains fundamentally unchanged, despite the move to their new Low Viscosity mineral oil - patience and a funnel are still your keys to success. Doing a basic bleed is as easy as it gets, but getting a perfect job done is still critical to maximizing performance here. That's true of all brakes, but something about the Shimano system seems to show a bad bleed more readily than other models on test.

Once set, you have two adjustments on tap to change the feel of the brakes. The Free Stroke screw now uses a hex key as opposed to a Phillips head, but still does very little to alter the feel of the brakes. The reach adjust is a far more meaningful change, and is now housed cleanly into the lever body. The recessed dial has strong detents that keep it in place, but it is a bit harder to adjust one-handed if you're so inclined.

One of the more atypical details on the new XTRs is the upswept lever blade, which has a 5° slant to it. This is meant to better match an aggressive descending body position, and requires a bit of calibration when setting up your lever angle. On trail, the difference fades away quickly, and the brakes feel normal.

Livability:
These may just be the easiest Shimano brakes to live with yet, thanks to the revised architecture and fluid choice that doesn't require constant attention to mitigate the wandering bite point that has plagued their system. The pads rattle less than they did as well, so you can leave the mastic tape on the shelf.


Feel

Shimano's Servowave lever cam has a very distinct feel to it, and that remains largely unchanged on this new generation of brakes. A slight nose of resistance breaks you into the stroke, which then moves very freely until you hit the bite point. It takes some getting used to, but now that the brakes are consistent in that end point, you can start to intuit where the contact will happen.

There is quite a bit of free stroke on the XTRs, which is a benefit or detriment depending on your preference. Unfortunately, the Free Stroke adjuster does very little to alter this, so any modification is more dependent on cheeky bleed tricks to change the feel.

The new XTR lever body moves the lever pivot point closer to the bar, with the goal of having the lever sit parallel to the bar at the bite point. This feels true for the vast majority of setup preferences, though those who like a to-the-grip bite point will probably prefer the ergonomics of some of the other brakes on test.

Shimano is well known for the sharp bite point their brakes have, and that's largely still the case here. I'd call the initial come-on a little less binary than the previous generation or the Saints, but they're still fairly snappy compared to some of the more linear feeling brakes in the group.

Feel Ranking:
Once you're past the initial point of resistance, the lever swings with very light action, all the way through the large dead band and into the bite point.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 823 g pull force (7th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.



Power

There is a moderate increase in power over the previous generation XTR brake, but I wouldn't call it a highlight of the revised system. The silver stoppers have plenty of power, but even compared to the Saints they're noticeably less aggressive. Because the power comes on quite quickly, you can get to that stopping force without pulling into the brake too much.

Pulling just a bit past that initial bite point yields full power, and while the raw sum of that power may be less than others here, we both found it quite usable on trail. The XTRs also fared mid-pack in the illustrious Honda Fit stop test, proving their worth.

Power Ranking:
If you're after the most powerful Shimano brake, your best bet is still the Saint. The new XTRs have a good sharp bite, but don't feel quite as deep-feeling as other brakes on test.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 28.2 m / 92.5 ft (6th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.



Pros

+ Consistent bite point
+ Distinct Shimano feel
+ Good ergonomics


Cons

- Large free stroke deadband
- Distinct Shimano feel
- Lack of adjustment beyond reach
