Review: Transition's New Regulator CX eMTB

Mar 13, 2025
by Mike Kazimer

The Regulator is the newest addition to Transition’s eMTB lineup, essentially an electric version of the most recent Sentinel. Billed as an “all-mountain workhorse” the Regulator is available in two different configuration - the CX model, which uses Bosch’s full power, 85 Nm Performance CX motor and a 600 Wh battery, or the SX model with Bosch’s 55 Nm SX motor and a 400 Wh battery.

Sizes M-XL of both models are compatible with Bosch’s PowerMore range extender, a downtube mounted external battery with a 250 Wh capacity.

The CX and SX both roll on mixed wheels, and have 150mm of rear travel with a 160mm fork. The geometry is identical between the two models, although the CX does have a flip chip on the lower shock mount, and the SX doesn’t. The difference really comes down to power and weight - the CX has a more powerful motor, a bigger battery, and a burlier fork than the SX. On my scale it weighed in at 47.25 lb, a very reasonable number for a full power eMTB.

We’re starting to see the SL and full power categories merge, and I have a feeling that trend is only going to continue as battery capacities increase and motor performance improves. It’ll be interesting to find out which version ends up being more popular. Personally, if I’m going to ride a bike with a motor I want as much range and power as I can get. Otherwise, I’d rather be on a regular bike and not need to worry about battery life at all, other than my own, and that’s something a quick stop at the nearest convenience store can easily fix.

Frame Details


The Regulator’s carbon frame casts a familiar shadow, and it’s really just the larger downtube and motor bulge in front of the bottom bracket that gives it away as an eMTB. Transition only made the thru-headset cable routing mistake once, on the original Repeater. On the Regulator, the cables are routed through ports on each side of the head tube. Each port is wide enough for two cables, so a clean setup should be possible no matter which side you run your rear brake lever on.

Motor / Display

The Regulator CX uses Bosch’s 5th generation Performance CX motor, which puts out 85 Nm of torque and has a max output of 600 watts. That’s paired with a 600 watt hour battery enclosed in the downtube - removing it requires dropping the motor, which isn’t that hard, but it’s also not something you’d really want to do with any regularity.

Bosch’s minimalist System Contoller display is mounted in the top tube, where different colored lights indicate the motor mode and battery level. Each illuminated bar is equivalent to 20% charge (there are five of them), and it’ll change color when the level drops by 10%. The motor modes are controlled by a wireless remote located on the left side of the bars. Those mode can easily be customized in Bosch’s Flow app, allowing riders to fine tune the amount of assistance each one provides.

The charging port is located underneath a plastic hatch on the non-driveside. Other details include room for a water bottle (or a range extender) inside the front triangle, a new chainslap protector, and Transition’s own upper chain guide.








Geometry

The Regulator’s numbers closely mirror those of the non-motorized Sentinel - the head angle, reach, and chainstay lengths are the same between the two models. A flip chip on the Regulator CX’s lower shock mount can be used to change the head angle from 64.5-degrees to 64, which also drops the bottom bracket by 5 millimeters and slackens the seat angle by .5-degrees.

There are 4 sizes, S - XL, with reach numbers ranging from 425 - 510mm. The S and M sizes have 442mm chainstays, and the L and XL sizes have 448mm chainstays.



Suspension Design

The Regulator uses a 205 x 60mm trunnion mounted shock to deliver 150mm of rear travel. For riders looking for a little more squish, a 205 x 65mm shock will bump up the travel to 160mm. 27% shock progression means that both air and coil shocks should work well with this design.

Test Bike Setup

175 psi in the RockShox SuperDeluxe shock put me right at 30% sag, and I stuck with that amount for the duration of testing. I ran 69 psi (nice) in the Zeb Ultimate, with the compression adjustments set in the middle of the range, and rebound 9 clicks from closed.

Schwalbe's radial casing tires require a little more pressure than non-radial tires to keep them from feeling too squirmy; running 3-5 psi more than I would in a regular tire does the trick for me. In this instance that equated to 23 psi front / 25 psi rear. I used the Alberts for most of the test period, but I did try out some Shredda tires when conditions were especially grim. The traction those provide in loose, muddy conditions is borderline ridiculous, although the extra drag is noticeable when climbing, even with a motor to help out.

Other than trimming the bars to my preferred 770mm width there's really nothing else to report - the Regulator is a simple, no fuss bike to get up and running.

Testing Info


Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington during the heart of winter. That meant the bike was fed a steady diet of mud, along with a stint where the trails froze solid and were covered with a few inches of snow. There were some perfect dirt days in the mix too, but overall conditions were on the wetter side of things.


Climbing

These days approximately 30 - 40% of my rides are on e-bikes. I still love non-motorized bikes the most, but when the days are short and the weather is foul it’s nice to be able to bundle up and cover a ton of ground without much effort. The Regulator is a great bike for exploring - it’s not some big behemoth that only works well flying up fire roads and then descending gnarly trails. Instead, it’s impressively quick handling without feeling twitchy, especially in the high geometry setting. If you’re the type of rider who enjoys getting up weird, technical climbs almost as much (or maybe more) than descending, the Regulator fits the bill.

Several factors combine to make this bike such a great climber. First, there are those teeny-tiny 150mm cranks. Short cranks mean you’re less likely to smack a pedal, so you can keep pedaling and motoring uphill even in chopped-up terrain. They may look a little funny, but the performance is worth it, at least as long as there’s a motor involved. This was a smart spec choice by Transition, and I’d expect other companies to follow suit, at least once more product managers get to try them out.

The Bosch motor is part of the equation too, with a very smooth power delivery. There’s a bit of an arms race going on at the moment, as motor manufacturers try to up their systems’ torque, but that extra output needs to be controllable; otherwise the bike will want to spin out or spit the rider off. The CX motor response well to a variety of pedalling speeds, and you don’t need to pedal wildly to hit the sweet spot. The majority of my time was spent in the eMTB mode (indicated by a purple light). That mode adjusts the level of assistance dynamically depending on rider output, automatically switching between Trail and Turbo modes. There's plenty of power for getting up silly steep sections, and the whir of the motor is unobtrusive - the pitch wasn't annoying to my ears, and I’d consider myself pretty sensitive to noise.

Other factors include the Schwalbe Albert radial casing tires, which work great in pretty much all conditions other than deep mud, well-balanced geometry, and suspension that feels like traction is the main priority. The Regulator is fairly eager to enter its travel, but that’s part of what allows it to stick to the ground and claw its way up slippery, slimy sections of trail.

I'm typically someone that ends up preferring slacker to steeper geometry if a bike has a flip chip, but in this case the steeper setting ended up being my favorite. There's only .5-degrees between the two positions, so it's not a super dramatic difference, but the steeper position did make the bike feel more energetic and easier to handle, especially on tighter trails. The nice thing is that the swap only takes a couple of minutes - it's worth doing some experimenting to see which one feels best for your local trails.



Descending

I tested the Sentinel as part of our Fall Field Test last year and wasn’t totally blown away by its performance. It did what it was supposed to, but there were times when it felt longer than I expected, and I didn’t totally click with its handling. It’s a different story with the Regulator - the smaller rear wheel and the extra weight around the bottom bracket give it a much different riding characteristic despite having identical geometry to its non-motorized sibling. The overall suspension feel is nice and plush, with a good level of sensitivity off the top to absorb those chattery sections of trail.

It’s planted when it needs to be, but it also retains a good deal of maneuverability. One of my favorite local trails has a series of tight switchbacks followed by a sections with a series of steep drops a couple of bike lengths apart, basically like a set of dirt stairs designed by Salvador Dali. It’s not something you can just sit back and hold on through and hope for the best - good brake control and plopping down into the right spot is key. The Regulator took it all in stride, and I was impressed with how easy it was to place the bike exactly where I wanted - the descending position is centered and upright, and it really does have that ‘in the bike’ sensation, as cliche as that term may be.

Bigger drops and hits were handle with out issues, although I’d probably run a little less sag for drier, faster conditions - 30% felt good in the slop, but a little more support would be good for hitting hardpacked berms and jumps. The bike as a whole is very quiet - there weren't any clacking noises from the motor, and the internal cables all stayed satisfyingly silent..

There was a moment when it seemed like Transition was going to continue down the longer and slacker road forever, but their recent bikes have bucked that trend, in some cases getting a little steeper than before. With the Regulator, this geometry recipe works very well - there’s more to making a bike handle well than just a super slack head angle, and I’m pretty convinced that that 64ish-degree head angle is a good place for most trail and enduro bikes to be, at least when that number is complemented by a fairly high stack and longish chainstays.




What’s It Like to Live With?

So far the Regulator has held up well - all the hardware has remained secure, and the bike is running as quietly as it did on day one. I pulled the plastic motor cover off and was pleasantly surprised to see that it wasn't packed full of mud - there are only a few small slits where mud can enter, and the inside of the cover is smooth, which keeps the mud from packing up, unlike what I experienced with the Orbea Wild. My one gripe has to do with the seatpost clamp - it doesn't do its job very well, and had a tendency to let the post slowly slide down over the course of a ride. Some carbon friction paste helps, but I'd be inclined to swap it out for a nicer clamp. Oh, and I'd also like to see a more robust charging port cover. It's a common refrain with most eMTBs these days - the charging port covers tend to be a little flimsy and not very well sealed from the elements, and the Regulator is no exception.

How Does It Compare?

Let’s start by pitting the Regulator up against its big brother, the Repeater. The Repeater has 170mm of travel and SRAM’s Powertrain motor powered by a 720 Wh battery. In that matchup, I vastly prefer the Regulator. It’s 6 pounds lighter, has a better, more tunable motor, comes with mixed wheels as stock, and feels much more manageable on the trail.

Compared to the Orbea Wild that I recently reviewed, the Regulator is more of an all-rounder, where the Wild does best on properly steep terrain. The Wild does have the edge when it comes to flat-out blasting down the fall line; that's where the extra travel and slacker head angle comes in handy, but that difference would narrow if you increased the shock stroke length on the Regulator, and maybe bumped up the fork travel to 170mm. When it comes to pricing between the two, the Orbea has a slight advantage - you can get the Wild M10 with an XT drivetrain, 600 Wh battery, and a Fox Factory 38 fork for $8,600. The Wild does have thru-headset cable routing though, so that point goes to the Regulator.


Which Model is the Best Value?

There are only two models of the Regulator, but neither one of them presents what I’d call a screaming deal. As always, if you’re truly on a budget sticking with a regular bike is the way to go - you can get a well spec’d alloy Transition Sentinel for $4,500 USD. As for the two models offered, the XT build certainly isn’t cheap, but it does have a very solid spec for the price, with top of the line suspension and great brakes, and that’s the one I’d recommend

The Deore build is decent, with a Deore drivetrain and brakes, and a RockShox Domain fork, but seeing those parts on a bike with a $7,000 price tag is a tough pill to swallow. We’re all going to be pulverized by a giant meteorite one of these days, so you might as well splurge and get the nice bits on the XT model. Or at least that’s one way to justify it.




Technical Report

150mm cranks: I mentioned them earlier, but it’s worth another round of praise for these stubby little cranks. The additional ground clearance is a huge benefit - e-bikes make it possible to pedal in places you normally wouldn’t, so the less chance there is of smacking a pedal on a rock or root the better. I’m not as sold on super short cranks for non-motorized bikes, since the difference in pedalling feel is more pronounced when there’s no motor to help, but I don’t see any reason why all eMTBs shouldn’t have them.

TRP Evo Pro brakes: I spent the first half of the review period with TRP's DH-R Evo brakes, and then they were swapped for the Evo Pro brakes that the Regulator will come with. I didn't notice a huge difference in power (there's plenty) or brake feel (they have a nice smooth ramp up and aren't overly grabby); it's mainly the improved lever adjustability that's the difference. I would suggest swapping the stock organic pads for metallic ones if you're planning on riding in wet conditions - the metallic pads last longer and are more consistent when it's pouring rain.

Schwalbe Albert radial tires: I get a little nervous any time I see a ‘Trail’ tire spec’d on a full-power e-bike, but I didn’t run into any issues with the Alberts. They’re still a 1150 gram tire, so it’s not as if Transition spec’d tires with casings made of tissue paper. The Radial casing lives up to the hype, and the increased traction makes it easier to relax when heading into slippery, off-camber roots.

I also spent some time with Schwalbe’s Shredda Gravity tires installed. The increased drag was noticeable on the climbs, but on the flip side the traction in wet, muddy conditions is ridiculous. Going with heavier more DH-oriented tires does change the character of the Regulator slightly, but it still maintains most of the easy handling that I enjoyed in its stock configuration.

Bosch PowerMore range extender: The PowerMore external battery is priced at $525, or $2.10 per extra watt hour. That's a lot of money, but having that extra range is very nice - 850 Wh is enough juice to rack up some serious vert. If you're usually just going out for an hour or two it's probably not worth it, but any longer than that and it's worth flipping some couch cushions to try and find enough change to add one on.




Pros

+ Great handling on a variety of terrain
+ Full power at a reasonable weight (for an eMTB)
+ Short cranks unlock impressive climbing abilities

Cons

- Some riders may want larger internal battery capacity
- No XXL, and no range extender option for size S




Pinkbike's Take

E-bikes are evolving rapidly, and the Regulator CX is a prime example. The weight is very reasonable, the motor is smooth and quiet, and there's plenty of power for quickly zipping up steep climbs. It may not be the flashiest option on the market, but bells and whistles (or idler pulleys and high pivots) aren't necessary to create a bike that's tons of fun to ride just about anywhere.— Mike Kazimer
