Lewis LHP+ U4



Lewis Bike made quite a buzz when they dropped their first set of brakes. Their design is similar to the shape of the Trickstuff Maxima; the lever is fairly long and the pivot is closer to the bar. They're far from a direct copy, though. They have the most adjustments of any brake on test here, and they have been proven with Top 10 results on the World Cup DH circuit.

LHP+ U4 Details

• Mineral oil system
• Non-ambidexterous lever design
• Patented 2-in-1 reach and bite adjustment
• Ratio adjustment
• 4x 17mm stainless steel pistons
• 140, 160, 180, 203, 223mm rotors avail.
• Weight: 324g front, 268g rotor
• Price: $460 USD
• lewisbike.com


Setup

Split-olive washers are a joy and a pain. Stabbing your hand trying to open them sucks, but hey, at least they're reusable. Another small annoyance is that all of Lewis brakes feature left and right-hand-specific master cylinders, so if your British friend would like to borrow your bike, they'll have to swap the brake lines.

Lewis provides caliper blocks and a small aluminum bleed funnel with the brakes for smaller bleed jobs (like swapping lines). Complete and proper bleeds are tricky to pull off with just this top-side tool. You'll likely want to purchase a bleed kit from Lewis, to vacuum out any stubborn air bubbles.

A very svelte bar clamp holds the lever in place, and like all two-piece designs, the torque required to keep it firmly in place can be a fine line; too tight and it bites into the bar. Too loose and the lever will rotate as you pull. A dab of carbon paste helps to a small degree.

If you like to run your levers further outboard, like me, the tool-free adjuster can limit how close the lever clamp can sit to the grip - the same issue occurs with the Trickstuff Maxima's design. In that case, you'll have to slide the lever inboard to fit the pad contact tool into the allen key slot inside the lever reach knob.

Livability: Don't expect a solid bleed with just a gravity bleed from the provided funnel - a syringe will make life much easier. As with a lot of top-capped reservoirs, storing the bike vertically can introduce some air back into the system.


Feel

The LHP+ U4s can be highly personalized with reach, bite point, and ratio adjustments, so don't expect a quick setup. There can be a lot of bolt twisting before they're dialled in.

The lever itself has a broad, smooth, rounded edge, which is pleasant to grip. Much like the Hope Tech 4s and Maximas, a long blade yields more mechanical advantage and can make the brake feel spongy. Turning the ratio adjustment to the most progressive position can magnify a soft brake bite too.

Feel Ranking: How the LHP+ U4s feel can widely depend on their adjustment settings and partially the quality of the bleed - the more progressive the ratio setting, the crispier the bleed will need to be. Aside from the limited range of the lever clamp placement on the, most riders will find the LHP+ U4 comfortable and familiar.

Water Bottle Pull Test: 683 g pull force (6th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test simulated the force required to get the brake to the bite point. Water was incrementally added to a bottle attached to the brake's lever, until bite force was achieved.

The reach adjustment knob limits how far outboard the lever can sit on the bar before it bumps into the grip collar.

The rotors are thick at 2.3mm. The 200mm size weighs 252g, 50g more than a SRAM HS2 of equal proportions.

4x17mm stainless steel pistons with an insulating layer.

Power

There's no shortage of mechanical advantage built into these levers, yet they lack bite. I think that comes in two parts.

The first is the long lever blade, which needs to be pulled a further distance before the pads touch the rotor. Like all of the other brakes on test here with a ratio adjustment, you'll get that initial pad contact earlier in the more linear setting.

The second half of the power problem is the low friction force between Lewis' own pads and rotors. They lack that gritty bite that you can feel through the bike that the Shimano Saint and instead have more of a soft, gliding sensation.

Power Ranking: As they stand, the LHP+ U4s stood out as a brake with good modulation but lacking the power delivered by the other stoppers on test. In previous testing, I explored swapping pads and rotors with the Maxima's components. That boosted the friction up a notch.

Honda Fit Stop Test: 34.6 m / 113.5 ft (10th overall)
As you'll see in the full-length video review, this test was meant to highlight the raw stopping power of each brake. We attached Matt to the back of Dario's Honda Fit, revved up to a consistent speed, at which point Matt used only the bike brakes to slow the vehicle to a measured stop.



Pros
+ Highly customizable actuation with reach, pad contact, and ratio adjustments.
+ Smooth and light action lever function.
+ Good value.

Cons
- Tedious to install and set up.
- Pads and rotors lack the bite a big brake should have.
- Rotors are considerably heavier than competitors.

 There's a lot of noise in the comments calling these just another "Chinese knock-off." From my experience, that’s not really fair. I’ve actually interacted with the brand and used the product, which is more than most of the people ripping into them can say.
Customer Service:
I dealt with a rep named Miya. Shipping to Greece was surprisingly fast (7 days), and when I crashed a few months later and needed a service kit, they were just as helpful and quick to respond. No complaints there.
On the Trail:
I came from XT 8120s, and the Lewis felt more powerful immediately. I’m currently running them with 2.3mm TRP rotors and Hope Green pads, and the stopping power is really impressive—definitely an upgrade over my previous setup.
The Reality of Adjustability:
The levers have a massive range of adjustment, but as someone with shorter fingers, I found most of it wasn't useful for me. I ended up stuck on one specific setting (the most progressive one) to get the reach right. The "huge range" sounds good on paper, but for my hands, it didn't offer much.
The Good and the Bad:
• The Hardware: The screw-on barb and olive design is great. It makes the install much less of a headache.
• The Bleed: It took me a few tries to get the feel perfectly consistent between the front and rear, so be prepared to spend a little extra time on the stand.
• The Cockpit: The machining looks amazing, but I wish the Matchmaker adapters had more lateral adjustment. It’s a small detail, but it would make the cockpit much cleaner.
Final Thought:
I’m not on a mission to defend these just because I bought them, but so far, I don’t regret the purchase. I’ll see how they handle a few race weekends this spring to check the long-term reliability, but they’ve earned a fair shake.
