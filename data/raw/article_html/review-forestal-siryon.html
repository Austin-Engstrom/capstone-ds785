Note: When asked about the rattling water bottle mount and downtube protection, Forestal claimed these are indeed pre-production issues.

Most brands were making pedal-powered bikes for years before adding e-bikes to their range. For Forestal, it's the other way round - this is their first bike without a motor.

It takes its lead from Forestal's e-bikes, with a distinctive elevated swingarm, linkage single pivot suspension and a full-carbon frame. It has 170 mm of travel and a 29" wheel at either end. There is no mullet option or a flip-chip to adapt to a 27.5" rear wheel.

The Diōde: build we have on test costs a cool € 8,999, but there is a Halō model for € 6,999 or a frame kit for € 3,999.

Frame Details

That one-piece swingarm connects to a pair of links that drive the shock via a sizeable shock extender. That long extender increases the buckling load on the shock, and this is widely considered to contribute to shock wear over time. The shock is nestled between two carbon struts that tie the seat tube to the top tube. These are designed to stiffen the frame, but make it awkward to access the shock to measure sag or adjust the air pressure.


The downtube offers a handy storage compartment, and the hatch includes a Fidlock water bottle mount. But it's a relatively narrow opening, and the door on my test bike rattles when riding over rough terrain with a full water bottle attached. Forestal say this will be fixed for full production bikes. A full-size 800 ml Fidlock bottle fits fine, but there are no additional accessory mounts like you see on many modern frames.

While I'm complaining, the rubber downtube protection on my test bike started peeling off after a handful of rides. Again, Forestal say there is a different bonding tape for production bikes, which hopefully will fix this.

All cable routing goes through the headset - there's no other routing option, which could be a headache when it comes to servicing the upper headset bearing. The headset also has a steering limiter to protect against crash damage.


There's a small plastic fender to protect from debris. The 2.6" tires pictured are not production spec; they were fitted by Forestal due to supply issues and left too little room for mud or stones to pass through without scraping. There isn't that much room, even with the production-spec 2.5" Schwalbe Albert tires I replaced them with, which measure up narrower than many 2.5" tires from other brands.

There is an integrated upper chain guide and ISCG tabs, but no bash protection as standard. On a positive note, the bottom bracket is threaded.



Suspension Design

The swingarm connects to the middle of a short aluminum link, the top of which connects to the shock extender and the bottom connects to another short link, which then connects to the seat tube. It's a classic linkage-driven single-pivot affair, much like the old Saracen Myst, just packaged in a more tucked-away fashion.

Forestal Syrion suspension squish

by seb-stott
Views: 2,492    Faves: 1    Comments: 0



According to Forestal, the Syrion's leverage ratio starts very high, meaning less force is needed to move the shock into the start of its travel. The leverage ratio drops by 37% throughout the travel, making it one of the most progressive bikes out there, on paper. However, the all-important change in leverage ratio between sag (about 50 mm wheel travel) and bottom-out isn't too extreme. That means there's plenty of support to resist bottom-outs without making it impossible to reach full-travel.

There's a decent amount of anti-squat to hold the bike up in its travel. At sag, this ranges from 100% in the 52-tooth sprocket up to 118% in the 10-tooth sprocket. However, anti-squat drops off throughout the travel, so there will be less support the further the suspension compresses.

Being a single-pivot, anti-rise starts high and drops off slightly deeper in the travel. This means the suspension will sit deeper in its travel than most while braking, but may be less comfortable when braking over choppy ground.



Geometry & Sizing


There are only three sizes to choose from, but the 29"-only strategy may not be ideal for short riders anyway. Recommended heights for each size are as follows: S/M, 160 cm - 177cm / L,177 cm - 187 cm / XL, > 187 cm.

At 191 cm / 6'3", the XL fit me well. The reach is generous, but the stack height is relatively short, and the head angle is steeper than most bikes in this travel bracket, leading to a front-center length that's a little more compact than average. I needed a higher-rise handlebar to get the grips high enough.

I measured the effective seat angle at my pedalling height at 76 degrees - considerably slacker than advertised.

Test Bike Setup

I was happy with the suspension support with just under 30% sag and stock volume spacers. Because sag is tricky to measure on this bike, I only measured sag for the initial setup, then went off pressures alone thereafter, ending up with 235 psi. I set the Fox 38 to my usual settings, with 98 psi when used with the Grip X2 damper, one volume spacer, and compression and rebound fairly open.

My test bike was shipped with 2.6" Schwalbe tires, which are not the correct spec. I quite liked how they handled, but they were too big for the frame's clearance. I swapped them out for 2.5" Schwalbe Albert tires after the photos in this article were taken.

I also swapped the stock 180 mm rear rotor for a 200 mm one; the 25 mm rise handlebar for 40 mm, and the 180 mm-travel dropper (160 mm in S/M size) for 230 mm. While I think the front end is too low for an XL, you could chalk the handlebar up to personal choice; the rotor and dropper travel are simply too small for a modern enduro bike in my view.

With the 2.6" Schwalbe Radial tires with the Gravity casing, I was able to run pretty much standard pressures - 23-24 psi front, and 26-27 psi rear. But with the 2.5" size and Trail casing, these went up a couple of psi.

Testing Info

Testing took place in the Tweed Valley, on familiar trails at Glentress, Innerleithen (pictured) and Cademuir. I also rode at Comrie Croft. The DH trails at Innerleithen that we recently used for our tuned mass damper testing are where the strengths and weaknesses of a bike are most exposed.


Climbing

The Syrion pedals well for the most part, with enough support to feel efficient for its travel when sprinting or spinning, while dealing with bumps under power nicely. However, the real-world seat angle is a little slack, making the seated position too far back for attacking the steepest climbs. The front wheel is prone to go light, and this caused me to stall on technical climbs more than once. The suspension usually stays high in its travel, but seems to collapse slightly when the going gets steep and the shock is loaded, creating a slouching sensation and a lack of support when you need it most.

As always, shorter riders should find the slack seat tube less of an issue, but the bike I was riding was sized appropriately for me. The flip side is that your weight isn't too far forward on flat but bumpy climbs, where the Syrion is comfortable and efficient. The relatively high bottom bracket and 165 mm crank arms reduce the risk of pedal strikes.


Descending

The Syrion leaves little to complain about on the descents. The suspension offers a good balance of compliance and support with 28-30% sag and stock volume spacers. There's no need to fettle too much to compensate for any quirks.

Sensitivity is good for the most part, with a soft beginning stroke, lots of travel on tap and plenty of room to adjust the setup of the shock. The unusually high leverage ratio at the beginning of the stroke didn't throw up any strange behaviour on the trail. There's a good amount of bottom-out resistance, too, while still allowing access to all the travel when warranted. It's a well-judged setup.

Handling is pretty neutral too, thanks to middle-of-the-road geometry figures. It's not crazy slack, which keeps the steering light on flatter terrain or tight turns, and the chainstay is neither especially long or short, making the weight distribution relatively balanced and the ride feel easy to get used to. Despite its travel, it's no slouch on mellower flow trails, without feeling undergunned when things get gnarly.

When braking hard over rough terrain, there can be a little bit of harshness and skipping when the wheel leaves the ground or locks up. The bike feels stable and reassuring when braking over smoother terrain where the wheel is in constant contact with the ground, hunkering down slightly, but the suspension seems to amplify things when it starts losing traction or skipping. It's a corner case quirk and not a major issue, but it's a difference I noticed when tested back-to-back with the Pivot Firebird, which has a lower and more consistent anti-rise value thanks to its four-bar suspension.

Also, I occasionally noticed some chain feedback during very sudden compressions. I can't say if this is pedal kickback or chain slap, which could be exacerbated by the empty space under the upper chain span, allowing the chain to flail around more. This is another occasional edge case and certainly shouldn't be a deal-breaker. In fact, with the water bottle removed, the drivetrain is nice and quiet most of the time, so maybe there are pros and cons of the free-to-move chain, but an OChain-style chainring damper or STFU-style guide could be helpful here.

Overall, the Forestal is well-mannered and versatile on the trail, thanks to its long-travel yet efficient suspension and rounded geometry. The chain slap and braking behaviour are only minor flies in the ointment.




What's the Best Value?

The RockShox Select+ suspension on the Halo model is hardly a downgrade, especially with the new Charger 3.1 damper. If you can live with mechanical and hanger-mounted gearing, that seems like a good way to save €2,000.

Forestal Siryon
Pivot Firebird

How Does It Compare?


On paper, the Pivot Firebird's seat angle is slacker, but in reality, it offers a more upright position with more supportive suspension, making it a more encouraging climber.

When descending, there's not much between the two, but the Pivot's DW4 suspension feels a little more composed when braking over rough terrain. The Pivot also offers mixed-wheel compatibility, less fussy cable routing and a more secure downtube door. The Pro X0 model I tested is a little pricier (€9,500), but the Pro XT/XTR model (€8,600) is similarly bling.



Technical Report

Formula Cura 4 Brakes: With a 180 mm rear rotor, they are underpowered by modern standards. Upgrading to 200 mm rotors front and rear was a big improvement, along with massaging the pistons for a crisper feel. I'd still say the Hayes Dominion, SRAM Maven or Shimano four-pots offer more accessible power and bite.



Pros

+ Balanced and intuitive handling
+ Long-travel suspension is forgiving without being inefficient

Cons

- Seated position is a little too rearward for tall riders and steep climbs
- Some sub-par spec choices, like the small rear rotor and short-travel dropper.
- No geometry adjustment or mixed-wheel compatabilty; shock awkward to access, headset-only cable routing

Pinkbike's Take

While the seat tube could be more upright for the steepest climbs and the suspension isn't the most refined on the steepest descents, the Syrion still offers a well-rounded ride that performs well on a wide range of terrain. But It has a few practical pitfalls, such as the awkward shock location, headset cable routing, lack of wheel-size adaptability and a few spec choices. While these needn't be dealbreakers, I don't think the ride quality is special enough to look past them either.
— Seb Stott

Much like the old Myst, yes, but "classic"? Not really. I'd say a "classic" linkage has a fixed pivot point, and definitely doesn't have the entire linkage moving around on a secondary link.
