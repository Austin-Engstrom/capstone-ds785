The Trek Top Fuel continues to roll closer to trail bike territory with each revision, and what was once a purebred XC race machine is now mixed wheel compatible, and can be set up with 130mm of rear travel and a 140mm fork.

In its stock configuration, the Top Fuel has 120mm of rear travel, a 130mm fork, and 29” wheels. Trek mentions the word 'downcountry' on their website a few times, but I think that term's time in the sun has come and gone – this is a short travel trail bike through and through. Yes, some of Trek's XC racers did use a slightly modified version at select World Cup races this year, but even though the frame is 220 grams lighter than before it's still not as light as other brands' flexstay-equipped, racing-oriented frames.

For a trail bike, though, the weight is reasonable, especially considering the number of adjustments, the in-frame storage, and, the part that really matters, how capable this bike is out on the trail. I've been working my way through the various configurations over the last few months in order to figure out which settings stood out from the rest.

Frame Details

The overall look of the Top Fuel is almost identical to the previous version, other than the new flip chip design at the lower shock mount. That chip provides a high and low geometry setting along with two different progression settings, either 14% or 19%. Oh, and there's no more Knock Block steering limiter – you can crank the handlebar as far as you'd like now without any hard stop.

The in-frame storage door has been updated to improve its weather sealing, and the opening is a little larger to make grabbing that windbreaker or tool bag easier.

Other details include generous downtube and chainstay protection, room to run a longer travel dropper post, and a threaded bottom bracket.

Geometry

We've seen geometry changes slow down across the board, and the Top Fuel is no exception - this time around it's a story of smaller changes, like a slightly taller head tube, and different chainstay lengths depending on the size.

The head and seat angles come in at 65.5 and 75.9-degrees respectively with a 130mm fork; switching to a 140mm fork will put the head angle closer to 65-degrees, and the seat angle around 75.4-degrees. That's not the steepest seat tube angle around, but remember, this is a 120mm bike, so that number won't change as much at sag as it would on a longer travel bike.

Reach numbers range from 417mm on the size small (that size is only available with 27.5” wheels), up to 507mm on the XL. The S and M sizes have 435mm chainstays, M/L and L have 440, and the XL's measure 445mm. The seat tube lengths have been shortened compared to the previous version, providing more room for running longer dropper posts.





Suspension Design

As I mentioned earlier, in the flip chip's forward position there's a 14% leverage rate change, and in the rear, more progressive setting that increases to 19%. A 185 x 50mm shock is spec'd on all models, but the frame is also compatible with a 55mm stroke shock, which increases the travel to 130mm.

Now that the Top Fuel is approved for use with a 140mm fork, it's also possible to run it with mixed wheels – a 140mm fork and running the shock in the high position keeps the bottom bracket high enough to make that possible.

TOP FUEL

Test Bike Setup

As you'll see shortly, I did all sorts of experimentation with the Top Fuel, so that meant there were multiple setup changes. I did get in a bunch of rides in the stock configuration before beginning to tinker, in order to have a base line for comparison. 165 psi in the RockShox Deluxe shock put me right at 30% sag, and I ran 71 psi in the 130mm Pike. I ran two click of HSC from full open on the Pike, and the compression dial in the middle of its range, adding or subracting one or two clicks depending on trail conditions.

I ended up running a 200mm PNW Components Range dropper post for a good portion of the test period, and I also tried a slightly higher rise bar that wasn't as brutally stiff. And then there are the brakes - after having one too many moments where I didn't have the stopping power I wanted I swapped out the Level 4-piston brakes for Code RSCs. We try to avoid swapping brakes when possible, since that's not something most people will be doing, but after doing my due diligence with the stock setup it was nice to have less fade and more power from the Codes.

Testing Info

Testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, over the course of the summer and into the early fall. That meant the bike saw the full range of conditions, from wet and sloppy to dry and dusty, and everything in between. The loop above would be a good one on the Top Fuel, but realistically almost any combination of the trails in that area would be fun on this bike due to its very versatile nature.


Climbing

It's easy to obsess over a bike's actual weight, but there's something to be said about how a bike feels out on the trail. With the Top Fuel, it feels fast and eager to accelerate, even with tires and wheels that aren't all that light. I never used the climb switch, not even on my biggest ride on the bike, a 62 mile loop that included 10,800 feet of climbing. The Top Fuel manages to feel quick and efficient without any harshness, a feat that's not easy to accomplish. The suspension remains calm under harder pedaling, while retaining enough compliance to keep chunkier climbs from feeling jarring.

The geometry is nicely balanced too – the chainstay length and overall wheelbase have grown compared to the last model, but if anything I'd say that improves the bike's handling while climbing and descending. On the climbs, it helps create a more centered position, which makes it easier to maintain traction on steep sections, or to pause for a brief trackstand in the middle of a particularly tricky jumble of roots. I'm a big fan of technical climbs, and I'll still regularly stop and throw myself at a tricky section until I make it through without dabbing. On the Top Fuel, there weren't many dabs at all – I'd be tempted to call this a climber's trail bike, except that it works really well on the descents too.

I tried almost every possible setup option on the Top Fuel, starting with the stock 120 / 130mm setup, and switching things out until I arrived at the 130 / 140mm configuration. I was a little concerned about the seat tube angle getting even slacker with the longer fork, but I was able to achieve a comfortable position with a higher rise bar and sliding the seat a little more forward on the rails. On that topic, it would be nice if the seat angle got steeper as the sizes got larger, rather than the other way around.


Descending

It's pretty wild to look at the geometry of the 2016 Top Fuel compared to this version. That bike had a 70-degree head angle, and that's in the low setting. Needless to say, things have progressed dramatically over the last decade, and the current Top Fuel feels much, much less pointy than those older models did. It's not that they weren't fun to ride, it's just that they didn't have as much room for error.

With the Top Fuel Gen 4, the handling is quick without feeling sketchy – it's right at home on rolling, meandering trails, and it does a great job of maintaining speed on descents that require pumping and pedaling to avoid getting hung up. Just like on the climbs, there's support for those moments when an extra pedal stroke or two is required, and enough small bump sensitivity to keep the wheels gripping on slippery, awkward sections of trail.

I mainly rode with the shock in the higher progression setting in order to have a little more end-stroke ramp up for the jumps and drops that are all over my local trails. The tune on the RockShox Deluxe felt very well matched to the bike – I didn't have any harsh bottom outs, and the instances when I used all of the travel were all very warranted.


Configuration Quick Hits

29” 120 / 130: The Top Fuel's out of the box setup is ideal for quickly covering ground, with enough travel to handle just about everything save for super rough, steep descents. Toss on some lighter, faster rolling tires for the occasional XC race, or go with some even meatier rubber and maybe a higher rise bar and a shorter stem for more technical trails.

29” 120 / 140: This setting, which is achieved by swapping out the air spring in the RockShox Pike, give the Top Fuel geometry and travel numbers that are very similar to the 2020 Norco Optic, a bike that still holds its own, even though a new high pivot version has since been released. On the Top Fuel, I'd recommend increasing the rear travel at the same time, but the 120 / 140 setup it very useable – that rear shock does a commendable job in rougher terrain.

29” 130 / 140:The longer travel setting ended up being my favorite configuration, especially when I installed some silly-light Roval XC wheels and Maxxis Rekon tires. As it turns out, combining the rolling speed of an XC race bike with a little extra travel for those techier sections is a recipe for a really good time. The extra suspension travel wasn't a hindrance at all on the climbs, and it was very nice to have at the tail end of that big ride I mentioned, when my line choosing skills weren't quite as sharp as they'd been earlier in the day.

Honestly, I think Trek could do a slight reshuffle of their lineup. Call this bike the Fuel, and use the 130 / 140mm configuration as the stock setup. That would open the door for a lightweight 120/120 flexstay thing that's truly deserving of the Top Fuel moniker.

Mixed wheel 130 / 140: I prefer mixed wheels for longer travel bikes, but I'm not as sold for shorter travel options – you lose some of the rollover benefits when climbing, and on a bike like this that's a large part of the equation. This setup was entertaining, especially when cornering and jumping, but it didn't feel like the most optimized setup.

How Does It Compare?

If downcountry was a real thing, the Transition Spur would be the poster child. The geometry is similar between it and the Top Fuel, but the Top Fuel has the more versatile frame thanks to the geometry and shock adjustment possibilities, plus the in-frame storage.

The Spur excels in its category, and I found it worked best with a 120mm fork to match the 120mm of rear travel. It's not really a bike that I'd try to beef up to turn into more of a trail bike. With the Top Fuel, it's a slightly different story – it's more of a light trail bike, and it works well with a slightly longer fork. Even in the 120mm setting, the Top Fuel feels like it gets more out of its travel – the ramp up is less noticeable, which can make it feel like there's more travel on tap.

Another contender in this category is the Santa Cruz Tallboy. The Tallboy still feels like more bike than the Top Fuel – it has both wheels in the trail bike category, and has a more muted, slightly less lively nature than the Top Fuel.



Which Model is the Best Value?


Not surprisingly, the $10,499 Top Fuel XX AXS doesn't pose the best value. If you're shopping for a higher end bike but don't want to unnecessarily spend money, I'd recommend looking at the 9.9 XTR model, which retails for $7,000. That model has cable-actuated Shimano XTR drivetrain, along with top-of-the-line RockShox suspension, and carbon wheels.

For riders on a tighter, more reasonable budget, I'd suggest going the aluminum route. Yes, it'll be heavier, but it'll also leave more money in your bank account. The Top Fuel 8 is priced at $4,000 USD, and would be a great place to start. The drivetrain is a mash-up of XT, SLX, and Deore level components, and there's Fox 34 fork with a Rhythm damper.



Technical Report

SRAM Level brakes: This is fast becoming a tired refrain, but the Level 4-piston brakes simply don't have enough power for more aggressive riding. Swapping to Codes comes with a small weight penalty, one that's more than worth it for the increased power and reduced brake fade.

Integrated bar: I don't mind the rise, sweep, and roll of Trek's integrated bar / stem combo, but it's still too stiff for my liking, and that only increased if you trim down the bars.

Bontrager Gunnison / Montrose tires: I was pleasantly surprised by the new Gunnison / Montrose tires. They're a little narrow, measuring 2.3” rather the the claimed 2.4”, but the rear tire is fast rolling, and the rubber compound is grippier in the wet than Bontrager's previous attempts. The retail price is $70, which is a good amount cheaper than similar options from Maxxis or Schwalbe.

RockShox Reverb AXS Post: 170mm of dropper post travel is okay on a bike like this, but 200mm would be even better. RockShox still hasn't released a 200mm version of their fancy $645 wireless dropper, so I swapped this one out for a $199 cable-actuated PNW dropper post and was much happier.

Livability: The Top Fuel has a very straightforward frame design, and I didn't run into any creaks or loosening hardware during the test period. The internal cable routing works well – when I swapped the rear brake I fed the line from the rear of the bike to the front and it popped right out, no fishing around required. The shock and linkage bolts are easy to access, and overall I'd rank it high on the 'ease of use' scale.





Pros

+ Great mix of pedaling and descending capabilities
+ Wide range of effective adjustments
+ Clean looking frame with all the modern amenities

Cons

- Not as light as you'd expect considering the price
- Underpowered brakes



Pinkbike's Take

At its core, the Top Fuel is a truly excellent bike, a prime example of how well-rounded a modern trail bike can be. Sure, it's not as light as it once was, and there are a few spec choices that could be improved, but the ride quality and sheer number of possible configurations help make up for that. For riders on the hunt for a bike they can customize to perfectly suit their favorite local trails, the Top Fuel is well worth considering. — Mike Kazimer
