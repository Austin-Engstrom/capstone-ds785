There has been a gaping hole in Yeti's lineup until today; the MTe is their first lightweight mid-power e-Bike and it is powered by the new TQ HPR-60 with, you guessed it, a maximum torque of 60 Nm. There are two batteries to choose from; a 290 Wh and a 580 Wh, as well as a supplementary 160 Wh Booster Pack. Like the full-powered 160E, the MTe uses their Six-Finity suspension platform to deliver slightly less rear wheel travel (145mm), paired with a 160mm fork.

There are three solid build options to choose from - I have spent the last 6 weeks testing the lighter T4 XX model with the 290 Wh battery, while Kaz has been riding the heavier (but not very) T3 model with the 580 Wh battery, and we both have lots to share.

Frame Details & Motor

The Yeti MTe is first out the starting block with the new TQ HPR60 motor. The original HPR50 motor was lauded for being very quiet on the trail, but in terms of torque and power it has been the back-of-the-pack option for a few years - even amongst its mid-power competitors, the Fazua Ride 60 and the Bosch SX motor. That is semi-rectified today with the HPR60 now offering a maximum torque of 60 Nm, and a peak power output of 350 W. So, it is still the lowest power option out there (Fazua is 450 W, and Bosch SX is 600 W), but on the flip-side that should offer a decent advantage in terms of range.


The actual peak power available will depend on which battery you choose: the lighter 290 Wh battery will limit your maximum power to 250 W, while the 580 Wh will allow use of the full 350 W. It is merely a question of amperage, not a limitation written into the software. The battery is removable for charging off-the-bike, or you can charge on-the-bike using the very well-sealed port on the non-drive side. It's a closed downtube and the battery slides out from the bottom.


The motor integrates cleanly with the carbon frame, and importantly for Yeti it leaves enough clearance in key areas for their Sixfinity layout. For most riders the packaging is decent; it leaves clearance for a piggy-back shock and a water bottle, and a long enough straight seat tube to make use of a long travel dropper. That and the 20 kg weight of the T3 model with the bigger battery is quite impressive, especially considering the use of a 6-bar suspension platform and the adjustability that Yeti are bringing to the table with it. The small suffers a little on the packaging front; the T4 model I tested fits a 550ml bottle, but the T3 model with a piggy-back shock is going to run into problems. That said, Yeti says their 15oz (425 ml) Hot Lap bottle will fit no bother.


It's a Yeti so of course the frame is carbon. For the first time on a production bike they are adding Vectran into their carbon layup to enhance the frame's impact resistance. It is something they have been doing with their Special Projects DH bike for a while.

A wheel-chip between the rocker and the seat stay allows you to switch out the stock 29" rear wheel for a 27.5", correcting for the bottom bracket height reduction and preserving the geometry. Then, there are four possible locations for the lower shock mount. Two flippable flip-chips give four leverage curves to choose from, allowing riders to run the suspension's progression at 12%, 14%, 22% or 25%. It would be an aftermarket component, but there is also scope to adjust the reach and/or head angle, something that Yeti has confirmed would not void the frame warranty.



The bike is super quiet owing to excellent protection along the stays, and guided internal cable routing that is secured at the ports. The internal carbon tubes aren't quite full length; they stop shy of the exit ports at the headtube to allow for a regular or moto brake setup. A mini-fender at the lower link is a nice touch for keeping some trail debris away from the bearings, of which there are sixteen. Ouch. There are no surprises elsewhere; the MTe has boost spacing, UDH for that T-Type compatibility, a wee top guide for chain retention, and it looks sick.

Mike Kazimer has been testing the Yeti MTe T3 model in a size large with a 580 Wh battery - it weighs 44.1 lbs / 20 kg

Geometry

The MTe has the longest chainstay length of any Yeti in their current lineup at 449mm. While they afford their unassisted bikes a frame size-specific rear-center length, all four sizes of the MTe get the same chainstay length. The actual length isn't wild for the larger frame sizes, but it's quite a standout number on the geometry chart if you're considering the small with a reach of 430mm. The smaller sizes will have less of a rearward weight bias than the larger frame sizes, and I think it makes good sense to offer that 35mm rise bar so everyone has scope to get their hands higher should they feel the need.


The head angle is a sensible 64° given the bike's intentions, and the seat tube angle is an effective 77° - not the steepest, and it wouldn't surprise me to learn that taller riders want their saddle biased forward on the rails. The T4 XX model gets 165mm cranks, while the more affordable ones get 160mm cranks. I think e-Bikes benefit from shorter cranks, and there's good justification for going down to 150mm as Transition has done with the Regulator.


There are no geometry adjustments available per se, but the headtube can take a reach/angle set without voiding your warranty. The frame is also rated for use with a fork up to 180mm. All models and sizes are shipped with a complete 29" wheelset, but a 27.5" rear is also doable. A flip-chip at the seatstay-rocker pivot adjusts for the difference in BB height when switching between wheel sizes. It's a super easy job - it takes a few minutes and the washers and chips don't fall out of the frame, so you don't need five hands to reassemble. Love that.

Suspension Design

The MTe delivers its 145mm of rear wheel travel through the Sixfinity suspension platform, damped by a 55mm stroke shock. Unlike other six-link designs like the Specialized Enduro, here the rear axle path and thus the anti-rise and anti-squat character is determined by all 6 bars - not four. This allows the engineers more fine control over those parameters while still allowing them independent adjustment of the progression.

The actual rear wheel travel differs dependent on the progression setting; it is 145mm increasing a touch to 148mm in that most rearward 25% setting. A second set of lower shock mount chips allows for the 14% and 22% settings. The T3 X0 and C2 Eagle 90 models are shipped in the 14% setting. No matter which bike you buy, the second set of chips are provided so everyone has those four settings on the table.


Yeti run the very same shock tune across all models; it is not tuned specifically for any one of those progression settings. It is also the very same damper tune on all frame sizes. They say the external adjustments on the Float and Float X rear shocks should be sufficient to accommodate a wide range of rider weights as well as the wide range of leverage rate change that is possible. The shock's damper will be working hardest in the lowest progression setting; 12%, where the shock pressure will be lowest and the speed of the shaft movement will be greatest (thereby generating the highest damping force).

The 'inflection point' refers to the point at which the lower link switches from rotating clockwise to anti-clockwise

Looking at other aspects of the kinematic, Yeti has tuned the Sixfinity platform with a fairly consistent anti-rise character throughout the stroke. It never strays far from the 60% mark, indicating the suspension will be allowed to extend under braking to recover from repeated hits.

Anti-squat is on the higher end of the spectrum sitting north of 100% up to and a fair bit beyond sag.

Test Bike Setup

I have been testing the lightest variation of the MTe; the T4 model in a size small with the 290 Wh battery. It weighs a very respectable 17.2 kg or 37.9 lbs, which incidentally is actually a touch lighter than my staff ride enduro bike - the Marin Alpine Trail, which doesn't have a motor or a battery. Like all models and size options, this one arrived with a complete 29" wheelset. It comes with the shock positioned for 12% progression - flipping this one would send it to the opposite end of the spectrum to give 25% progression. Included in the box is an alternate shock mount that gives the 14% or 22% progression settings.

I initially set up the bike with the recommended 30% sag (16.5mm on a 55mm stroke), with the Float shock compression and rebound dials set to fully open - I added a little rebound damping later on to keep the rear end calm. With the Fox 36 and its GRIP X damper I opted for the manufacturer's recommended settings, which I am pleased to report do work very well for me.

Testing Info

I've ridden the MTe on pure flow trails, fast enduro tracks, slow and steep techy ones, and chunky downhill tracks. All of the testing has taken place in dry, flattering conditions. The T4 model I tested is shipped in the 12% progression setting with a complete 29” wheelset, and that is how I have ridden it most of the time. I'm at home on the 430mm reach, 50mm stem and the 35mm rise bar on the small, and I'm delighted with the 175mm dropper seat post - that's generous for a small. I struggled to use more than 50mm stroke in the 12% or the 25% progression settings with the stock 0.2mm spacer in the shock. Removing that allowed me to use all of the travel without any harsh bottom-outs.

I'll get the question of range out of the way first; on the first discharge cycle I got just 13.17 km and 859 meters (2,818 ft) of elevation gain, using only the most powerful mode. On another ride where I switched between the middle and highest assistance settings I managed 20 km and 982 meters (3,220 ft). The range extender doesn't fit the small. I'd like to see Yeti offer the 360 Wh battery, it could be a good compromise.

Mike Kazimer, on the MTe T3 model in a size large with the 580 Wh battery, was able to rack up 5,700 vertical feet and 27 miles (1737 meters / 43.5 km) with some battery left over, using a mix of the full and partial power modes.



Climbing

The MTe gives a comfortable seated pedaling position - it isn't overly steep but coupled with the long chainstays it is enough for me to feel confident sending the bike up steep pitches without too much risk of looping out. Running a lower rise bar and pushing the saddle forward on the rails would improve the bike's uphill handling further - if ever I was to go Munro-bagging aboard this thing with the poorly-educated ambition of cleaning the technical climbs.

Yeti has a custom tune on the TQ HPR60 wherein the most powerful mode is tweaked to give the full 60 Nm of torque from a low cadence, something that should prove to be advantageous on trials-like sections as it should, hypothetically, reduce the risk of catching a pedal. There is no appreciable over-run on this system though, and you'll still need to look to the full-power options for that trump card.

The system is very quiet, and the motor sounds more like the purring of a cat (albeit at a higher pitch) than the classic whine you get with most other drive units. The decibels increase the more you ask of the system, but for the most part it fades into the background - very socially acceptable on a mixed group ride. In terms of assistance, it does still leave a little to be desired as compared to the Fazua Ride 60. While both motors have a top-end torque of 60 Nm, the Fazua motor has a 12-second boost function in Rocket mode that takes the peak power to 450 W. I won't knock it too hard, though. It takes the edge off tough sections of track without asking for too much cadence, and its reduced power will naturally be beneficial for range.

If you're after a pure shuttle machine, look elsewhere. This is best used as a ride extender for those who like to pedal.


Descending


The bike is very quiet descending, too. That is a treat on any bike, but especially so on an e-bike. Most motors have a 'clack' coming from the clutch that arises from the oscillations in the upper length of chain, but the TQ HPR-60 motor is silent in this regard. Elsewhere, the comprehensive stay protection on the drive-side nigh-on eliminates noise from chain slap. I am yet to hear a single rattle from this thing; chapeau.

The MTe isn't as playful as others; the Norco Fluid VLT is standout in that regard. The Yeti's traction is however excellent, and I find it rides through rough chop much better than I might have expected a 145mm travel bike to. It has a stable, consistent and reliable feel that we've those long chainstays to thank for. The downside there; I found it wasn't so easy to unweight the front wheel at short notice. Being a short rider, I do generally have a preference for shorter chainstays. A short rear-center is especially useful when riding slower speed tracks where hunting out weird lines is fun. That said, I can appreciate the benefits of a more balanced weight distribution offered by a longer rear-center on faster, rougher sections of track.


It carries its weight fairly well for an e-Bike. The steepest most committing tracks did highlight the need for extra compression damping on the fork to keep the bike from pitching too violently on steep slab-to-dirt transitions, and the Grip X damper on the Fox 36 did not let me down. The SRAM Motive brakes are sufficient, but I can see a lot of riders benefiting from an upgrade to the more powerful Maven brakes that could make things more comfortable when braking late. To be fair, the T4 model gets a 200mm rotor up front but it's a 180mm in the rear - upgrading to larger rotors would probably be a good idea before forking out for more expensive brakes.

Out of the box, the stock configuration of the MTe T4 model is 12% progression with complete 29" wheelset. I did some back-to-back testing on one of the steeper, rougher local tracks, switching between the lowest and highest progression settings (correcting for sag) just to see if one suited me and that track better than the other. The difference in ride feel was clear – the ramp effect in the 25% setting was quite aggressive – it still felt predictable and calm, but came up on very firm support on those medium to larger hits which felt a little too aggressive at times. At least on that terrain I preferred the consistency of the stock 12% setting. There's plenty more testing to do here, though. The 12% progression setting can feel a little firm over repetitive hits on soft ground (think loamy braking bumps), and I have a suspicion that one of the higher progression settings could give a more supple feel in those scenarios. I look forward to doing more back-to-back testing.

The MTe was at its best on faster tracks where you're committing to the sculpted berms and support offered by the main line. It carries speed tremendously well and feels really quite flattering through rhythm sections and consecutive berms where you're low to the frame and pumping to generate speed. Carrying that speed out and into rock gardens, the suspension feels excellent, tracking the ground in a composed fashion.


I recently switched out the 29" rear wheel for a 27.5". I had no issue with clearance on the 29", but I think the smaller rear wheel makes the bike's handling a little quicker in rhythm sections and on twisty flow trails where the turns are closely spaced.

I've had a lot of fun on the MTe, fun that I am yet to have on a full power e-Bike with a big battery. If you're put off by the on-paper performance numbers of the TQ motor but enamored by the 120 Nm torque of the latest DJI and Specialized motors then this probably isn't the bike for you just now. It's easy to see why riders gravitate towards those ultra-powerful systems, but I continue to be more interested in mid-power options of which the Yeti MTe is a very well-executed example.

Mike Kazimer's Ride Impressions of the Yeti MTe T3

Historically, I haven’t been that impressed by most mid-power e-bikes - they always seemed to pale in comparison to their full-powered siblings, and they didn’t seem to offer that much of an advantage over a regular bike. With the MTe, things are a little different. For one thing, the ride quality is top notch, for any bike, not just for an eMTB. The level of grip is excellent, and there’s still plenty of support to keep the bike from feeling overly mushy on firmer, faster trails. It'll hold its own on rougher, steeper trails too - this isn't some high-strung trail bike that gets flustered when things take a turn for the technical; instead, it has handling closer to what you'd expect from an enduro bike on the descents.

Then there’s the way that it sips on the battery, making it possible to knock off decent sized rides without running out of juice - I was able to get close to 6,000 vertical feel of elevation gain over the course of 27 miles, and that’s spending most of the time in the highest level of assistance, and without fully draining the battery. Plus, range anxiety is less of a thing with the MTe, since if the battery does die you’ll be pedaling a 44-pound bike home, which is hard, but not nearly as hard as it would be on a 54+ pound behemoth.

The speed and power that the TQ puts out fit the overall character of the bike nicely. I started thinking of this as my adventure bike, the one I’d grab to explore rarely frequented trails, or when I wanted to maximize the length of a ride without feeling like the whole thing went by in extra-extra fast forward. The bulk of my time on the MTe was with it in the stock, full 29" configuration, but I recently swapped out the rear wheel for a 27.5" one, and that's the setting I prefer - the rear wheel is further out of the way in the steeps, and the motor helps minimize the amount of difference the smaller wheel size makes on the climbs.

I’d gravitate towards a full-power bike if blasting up a fire road and down a gnarly descent was my main goal, but the range and silent operation of the MTe meant that this was the test e-bike I kept grabbing, even when there was a faster, more powerful option sitting right next to it. Jessie-May will discuss that sky high price tag, which unfortunately will put this bike out of reach of most riders; I’d love to see a non-motorized version of the MTe hit the market - the Sixfinity suspension design’s performance is extremely impressive. - Mike Kazimer

How Does It Compare?

It may not surprise you to learn that, at 57 kgs or 126 lbs, I have struggled to enjoy many e-bikes, finding the added weight unwieldy and cumbersome. The Yeti MTe is a bit of an exception on that front, coming in at a weight that is similar to my non-motorized Marin Alpine Trail. That weight is distributed differently, of course. Concentrating a considerable amount of weight in the downtube is always going to have an effect on handling, and I do feel that on the MTe, but it is absolutely one of the better handling e-bikes I’ve ridden to date – up there with the Norco Fluid VLT, and the Orbea Rise LT.

The TQ motor isn't as powerful as the Bosch SX on the Norco, or the Shimano EP801 RS on the Orbea, and it should therefore offer more range for the equivalent battery size though I wouldn't be able to back-to-back test that theory. The downside is that 350 W (250 W on the 290Wh) will limit the bike's prowess on steep climbs. If you're looking for a bike that excels on the front, the Rise LT is where it's at.

The most affordable model of the Yeti MTe goes for $9,850 USD
The most affordable model of the YT Decoy SN 29 goes for $6,499 USD

The Yeti is the lighter of the two however, and its suspension has that buttery-smooth edge over most bikes I've tested. It feels like it would be the more capable, faster bike on most descents. It would also be my preference over the far more affordable YT Decoy SN 29 I tested recently; the two bikes have a similar set of geometry and travel numbers, but the YT runs the more powerful Fazua Ride 60 motor and a 480 Wh battery. I've had more fun riding the Yeti which may be no surprise given it is considerably lighter - yes, the battery is smaller, but with the reduced power the difference in range might not be all that big in the end. Regardless of range let's not forget the most important thing: it doesn't matter how many times a bike can get you up a hill, if it's not the one you want to ride down it, you've pedaled the wrong one to the top.

With budget considerations, know that the Yeti does have the pricier build options of the lot, though that C2 Eagle 90 build offers pretty good value when you compare it to spec offered elsewhere.



Which Model is the Best Value?


I won't compare the $14,300 USD T4 XX build to the other two; with its 290 Wh battery and weight-conscious spec it is a different kettle of fish. The price tag puts this model out of reach for most riders and to be honest, it is a little perplexing. For that money, there's surely justification for offering the customer the 290 Wh and the 580 Wh battery? Discuss.

Moving onto the 580 Wh builds, without a shadow of a doubt the C2 Eagle 90 Transmission model is the best bang-for-buck, retailing at $9,850 USD - still far from a budget option but it's a solid build all round, with powerful SRAM Maven Bronze brakes, the mechanical Eagle 90 Transmission, DT E1900 alloy wheelset and Schwalbe's Radial tires. It's not the lightest with a claimed weight of 20.09 kg / 44.3 lbs, but it is certainly no brute. This one has the Performance level suspension from Fox.

The $9,850 USD C2 Eagle 90 model offers the best value for money

UK customers should know of another option; the C2 Eagle 90 Factory will go for £9,499. It's the same as the C2 Eagle 90 seen above, only with Fox Factory level suspension components.


Technical Report

Fox 36 Factory Fork - GRIP XI find this fork very smooth and comfortable, and it offers a good usable range of damping at this lower end of the rider weight spectrum.

TQ HPR60 Motor The motor has very socially acceptable levels of noise when climbing and it is perfectly silent while descending. Bravo. Unfortunately on two of the discharge cycles the battery management system was misleading, reading 12% before going straight to zero. We are awaiting comment from TQ on this issue. This issue was only experienced with the 290 Wh battery.

RockShox Reverb AXS: It's super nice to have a 175mm drop seat post on a small frame, and this new electronic one from SRAM is great. It has a very intentional small amount of squish when positioned anywhere below full extension, which SRAM says makes things more comfortable when climbing very steep technical sections. I can't say I noticed it at all. It is however plenty fast enough to actuate and there's no play whatsoever - love that. Hopefully they get this wired into the main battery system sooner rather than later so we haven't got that extra battery to worry about.

DT Swiss 240 DEG Hub: After 100 km or so the freehub starting skipping, and within a few hundred meters it failed. Opening up the hub, there was no damage to the ratchets or springs but one of ratchets was getting stuck down into its recess and the spring force wasn't enough to push it back to engage. It's not clear if this arose as a result of too much grease inside the mechanism, or whether that ratchet is tilting in its seat with friction preventing it from returning to engage.

Pinkbike's Take

Torque and power to one side, let's not forget the most important thing: it doesn't matter how fast or how many times a bike can get you up a hill, if it's not the one you want to ride down it, you've pedaled the wrong one to the top. The Yeti MTe hammers this home with an enviable performance-to-weight ratio. It's never going to be a super-shuttle when compared to any full-powered affairs, and anyone looking for a power stage-winning race bike will need to look elsewhere. The MTe is a fine example of a lightweight e-bike, and those with deep pockets will delight in the fantastic performance of the Sixfinity suspension.
—Jessie-May Morgan
