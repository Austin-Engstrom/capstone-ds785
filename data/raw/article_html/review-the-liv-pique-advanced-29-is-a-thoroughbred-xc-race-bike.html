Review: The Liv Pique Advanced 29 is a Thoroughbred XC Race Bike

Jun 16, 2025
by Jessie-May Morgan

REVIEW
Liv Pique Advanced

WORDS: Jessie-May Morgan
PHOTOS: Finlay Anderson


The latest version of the Liv Pique arrived at the back end of 2023 as a lighter, longer travel cross-country bike with a more aggressive geometry - fit for evolving XCO courses. The most notable update was the switch from Maestro to the FlexPoint Pro suspension layout, a move that allowed Liv to shave a considerable 297 grams off the frame weight. In conjunction, rear wheel travel was increased to 115mm paired with a 120mm travel fork, as compared to the 100mm at each end of the predecessor.

The result is a modern XC race bike complete with remote lockout that weighs in at a very competitive 10.1 kg (22.26 lbs) in a size small. To anyone wondering, this bike is not the same as the Giant Anthem. If Giant doesn't release something very similar to this within the next 12 months, I will eat my hat.
Pique Advanced 29 0 Details

• 115 mm travel rear, 120 mm fork
• 29" wheels
• 67° head angle
• 76° seat angle
• 406, 420, 432, 445mm reach
• 435mm chainstays
• Sizes: XS, S, M, L

Though they share the FlexPoint Pro suspension and have similar XC racing intentions, the two bikes are really quite different. The Liv Pique has 15mm extra travel and a slacker head tube angle amongst other geometrical differences. And, considering it is primarily (though not exclusively, of course) aimed at women, the sizing is based around shorter riders in the region of 150 - 181 cm.

This is the third generation of the Liv Pique, designed to be competitive on World Cup XCO tracks for the likes of Jenn Jackson, your 2024 Canadian National Champion. She has since moved to the Orbea Fox Factory Team, leaving Marin Lowe, Tyler Jacobs, Linda Indergand, and Ronja Blochlinger as the 2025 Liv Factory Racing team members.

The exact lineup varies from country to country, but there are only two models available in the US just now; the $5,400 Pique Advanced 29 2, and the $7,800 Pique Advanced 29 1. The Advanced 29 0 shown throughout is available in the UK, Canada and Europe. Regardless, all models have the same carbon frame, a dropper seat post, 29" x 2.4" tires, and 3-position remote lockout.

Frame Details

Like many a XC frame manufacturer, Liv has chosen to make the swingarm of the Pique much lighter with the switch to flex stays. As compared to the Maestro layout of the predecessor (a dual-link affair), the latest generation Pique is missing one set of pivot hardware. The flex in the seatstay takes away the need for a set of bearings, making a large contribution to a massive 297 gram weight saving.

The claimed frame weight? An astonishingly competitive 1,468 grams in a size small (w/o shock). For comparison, the Cannondale Scalpel frame (M) weighs 1,980 grams for the Series 1 frame without shock, and 1,780 grams for the highest end Lab 71 frame. Full disclosure: the weight given to us by Liv is for an unpainted frame, but does include the UDH hanger, and all pivot and shock hardware.

P.S. Liv Factory Racing's Tyler Jacobs has her small built up to 10.66 kg / 23.50 lbs


Additional weight saving comes from the use of a new carbon layup that is both lighter, and you guessed it, stiffer. Liv say the new bike has a 10% increase in pedaling stiffness and a 6% increase in torsional stiffness over the previous iteration that they attribute to the straight top tube and wider, square-shape redesign of the downtube and chainstay.


Liv is one of few manufacturers that has steered clear of thru-headset cable routing, a decision that will disappoint very few. To my mind, that kind of carry-on should be confined to performance-oriented road bikes. Regardless, many modern XC bikes have gone fully integrated so it's nice to see Liv going to the easier maintenance route. The Advanced 29 0 model I tested has the SRAM XX SL T-Type AXS drivetrain, but the frame does have ports for cables should you ever wish to switch to mechanical. Cables are not guided throughout, and the cable for the shock lockout can rattle a little. The brake hose is routed externally along the chainstay - sensibly located guides mean the hose doesn't rub the stay as the bike is pushed through its travel.

The rear TyreWiz developed a fault early on, flashing red at all times even when the tire pressure was within the pre-set range
The chainstay protection is lacking in this key area leaving the frame vulnerable to paint chips, and the undesirable noise of chain slap

The Pique has two sets of bottle bosses in the front triangle to fit two proper size water bottles, even on the small frame. The dropper post is 30.9mm and the size small gets a 125mm drop while medium through XL get 150mm of drop.

Other details include 2.4” tires on 30mm internal width rims, a press-fit bottom bracket, and the Zipp 1ZERO HiTOP SW wheels on the Advanced 29 0 model come with TyreWiz real-time pressure sensors that flash red when the pressure sits outside your pre-defined range.




Geometry

The Pique's geometry is a complete overhaul, bringing it up to date with the latest modern cross-country machines. The head tube angle is 1.5° slacker now at 67°, and the seat tube angle is 2° steeper at 76°. The reach numbers have grown by 5-7mm depending on the frame size, with the size medium getting a 432mm reach. That's very short for a size medium, but here it's important to remember that Liv's target market is considerably shorter than the target market of most other manufacturers.

On that note, the jumps between frame sizes are really quite small with reach increments of just 12-15mm between sizes. That compares to most other bikes where the reach increments are in the region of 25-30mm. Liv's target rider height range of 4ft 11" up to 5ft 11" (150 - 181 cm). On the short end, their XS has a reach of 406mm, and at least by that metric is one of the smallest 29" wheel XC bikes that money can buy.



When catering to smaller riders, standover does become a point of discussion. Though it is not a metric that is relevant to how the bike feels when you're actually riding it, it is always nice to know that if you do need to take the occasional dab through technical terrain there is clearance to do that without fear of smacking yourself off the top tube. On the small that I rode, the standover is 767mm, which is only just about workable for my 30" inseam. It is actually a fair bit taller than the standover height on the size small Scott Spark RC, Cannondale Scalpel, Trek Supercaliber and Orbea Oiz. If that is a metric that is very important to you, know that most other brands will offer you more clearance in that department.

I will say that the 445mm reach (with a 70mm stem) sounds a bit cramped for a rider at the upper end of that spectrum, but at 163cm tall I can't speak from experience. I can say that the recommended size small with a 420mm reach and a 60mm stem is appropriate for me and I had no issues with the fit.

It's a little surprising to see that Liv has not afforded the Pique size-adjusted chainstays. The stays are a consistent 435mm across the four-strong size range, meaning the front-to-rear weight distribution - a key metric that impacts ride feel - will vary a fair bit between frame sizes. At this kind of price point we do see other brands like Cannondale and Yeti giving each frame its own size-adjusted swingarm.

For the sake of comparison, here are some other modern XC bikes and their head angle / seat tube angle / reach (size S) / chainstay length. The head angle still sits on the steeper end of the spectrum, but the Pique is fairly middle-of-the-road elsewhere.

Suspension Design

The Pique uses a single pivot flex stay with a linkage driving the 165mm x 45mm Trunnion shock to deliver 115mm of rear travel. They call it their FlexPoint Pro suspension platform, making the hard-to-substantiate claim in their marketing copy that it is the most active, lightweight XC suspension system on the market.

All three models benefit from a remote lock-out for both the shock and fork, and all sizes and models are equipped with the very same custom compression damping tune on the Fox Float SL shock.

RIDING THE
Liv Pique Advanced 29 0

Test Bike Setup

The Liv Pique Advanced 0 comes with Giant's Contact SLR XC carbon bar that I cut down from its stock 760mm to 740mm. It is a 35mm clamp, flat, 9° backsweep affair, paired with an 8° rise (or drop) 60mm stem on the XS and S (70mm on larger sizes). I spent a heap of time riding a 35mm rise bar on this bike to take weight off an injured shoulder, but have since gone back to the Giant bar and find it to be perfectly acceptable.

I ran the stock Maxxis Aspen tires (EXO, 120 TPI) throughout the test period; as a dry conditions tire, they left a lot to be desired in soft conditions but they are insanely quick on dry hardpack terrain.

I rode the bike with 112 psi in the Fox Float SL Factory EVOL rear shock, which put me around 27% sag, and I ran the recommended 62 psi in the Fox Float Factory 34 SC fork. Tire pressures were 17 psi front and 20 psi rear.

Testing Info

My testing of the Liv Pique took place over an extended period of time, and in two main locations. It has seen the sculpted trails of the 2023 World Championships XCO course in Glentress, and also more natural riding around the eastern edge of the Cairngorms National Park. More recently, I have enjoyed the efficiency of the Pique on longer rides that largely consisted of rough gravel-like terrain - terrain that would be completely horrible to ride on an actual gravel bike - interspersed with natural, unpredictable rocky singletrack that absolutely warrants the use of a full suspension bike with high volume tires.


Climbing

The seated pedaling position on the Liv Pique was initially a little alien to me, having spent most of the last few years riding longer travel bikes. The seat angle is slacker than most bikes I ride (though not most XC bikes), which I appreciate has good merit on a bike that is capable of taking a rider over long distances quite comfortably, and for relatively little effort. I found myself offsetting the rearward bias with a forward saddle position to get my hips in a more ergonomically familiar position. That was of great benefit on steep climbs, helping to keep the front end down - here I also appreciated the 32-tooth chainring. On longer flatter rides I chose to put the saddle back to a neutral position to take the weight off my wrists.

Zipping along on gentle gradient fire roads, the bike is at its best in the middle of the three compression settings. It will bob quite considerably otherwise, so that middle shock position is good for keeping the bike from oscillating high in its travel while keeping it supple enough to manage some of the chunkier gravel. I only used the full lock-out for tarmac commutes, and yes, it is very very firm.


That third position of the remote lock-out also firms up the fork massively. There was no scenario where I felt that desirable, so I backed off the tension on the barrel adjuster such that the second press of the remote only locks out the shock. I find it's best to keep the fork supple especially when climbing steeper rocky bits. Allowing the fork to compress a little allows the pitch of the bike to be more manageable, holding more weight at the front.

This push-to-lock system is preferable to the push-to-unlock system that other brands employ

That middle shock setting is preferred for out-of the-saddle efforts, too. In the open setting I find the suspension platform isn't as firm as it could be when laying down power in bigger gears. The remote lock-out lever on the bar is a push-to-lock affair, which I absolutely prefer over the push-to-unlock variation (Orbea, I am looking at you). The presence of a dropper lever and a remote lock-out always results in competition for handlebar real-estate, and the Liv Pique is no exception. I positioned it inboard of the dropper lever, as it's more important to me to have fast and reliable access to that over the lock-out. I kind of wish someone would make an electronic remote for the lock-out; it could be much smaller than the stacked levers we have here, and you wouldn't have to worry about a cable (just batteries instead).


On tight singletrack, the ergonomics of a 60mm stem took a little getting used to. It makes the steering feel a little slower than the the 67° head tube angle would suggest. Once I'd familiarized myself with the setup, navigating tight turns while climbing got a little easier, but never felt totally natural.

Over small bumps, the rear end is nice and supple. While the rear smooths out the chatter making things nice and comfortable, the fork has trouble matching that small bump sensitivity. It performs well when I'm out of the saddle, weighting the bar, but in the seated pedaling position the fork struggles to get moving into its travel. When climbs got more technical and required more than just pure power to get up and over obstacles, the Pique's traction was good. In the open shock setting, the rear wheel is able to move up and out of the way over consecutive hits without feeling wallowy. A lot of riders will want to switch out the Aspen for more grip in soft conditions, though.



Descending


The Pique is a whippet on mellow, undulating singletrack and long, grueling fire road climbs. I have no qualms with its kilometer-eating efficiency, but it isn't the most confidence inspiring XC bike when navigating rough descents. The head angle isn't quite as slack as some more modern XC bikes, but I wouldn't say that's what holds it back necessarily.

While the rear offers plenty of support in fast, compressive berms, its hesitancy to give up too much travel can make it feel overwhelmed in choppy sections. It navigates flowing sections of smooth trail with finesse but it is easy to get carried away with that, barreling into less predictable sections with more speed than the bike can manage drama-free. The bike can get bounced around, leaving me feeling vulnerable and having to reset my expectations. At 25% sag and no volume spacers I never used all 115mm of rear wheel travel; the ramp in that small volume shock is considerable.

The system as a whole has quite a lot of lateral flex. It's hard to point a finger at any one component, but that flex I feel when landing jumps and riding through rooty sections will be the sum of the light casing Aspen tire, the compliance in the remarkably light Zipp 1ZERO HiTOP SW wheels and the swingarm itself. It takes some getting used to. Compliance will be important in traction and comfort, but I would argue that too much can make a bike feel unsettled.

The 2-piston XTR brakes are a contributing factor here in limiting the bike's descending capability, not offering a great deal of stopping power. These days, four-piston brakes aren't just for long-travel bikes; the Cannondale Scalpel gets that more powerful option as stock on most models, and we regularly see World Cup XCO athletes taking the weight penalty to have more control on the descents.


Given the right track, the Pique can be an absolute joy to ride fast. I find its handling to be quite precise, and am confident pushing the bike into support for quick changes in direction. It's not overly playful, though - which is perhaps exactly what you might want from a XC race bike. I am happy enough hunting out the natural little trails gaps on it, but its enthusiasm doesn't necessarily match mine every time. The Pique is more of a wheels-on-ground affair, prioritizing efficiency over bump-eating compliance.

How Does It Compare?


The latest Orbea Oiz came well over a year before the 2024 Liv Pique Advanced with a geometry that is not dissimilar. I rode it over three days at the launch event in Spain, and have some thoughts on how it compares to the Liv.

Both bikes rely on a flex pivot to deliver their rear wheel travel; the Oiz has a smidgen more than the Pique, at 120mm. It does have the edge in terms of descending performance with more of a playful overall ride feel that gives me a little more confidence in rough terrain. It wouldn't be fair to say that one is outright a better bike than the other, but I have a good idea as to which one I'd choose for what.

To my mind the Pique is more of a thorough-bred race bike, and Liv has clearly taken every opportunity to maximize the rider's power-to-weight ratio with this particular build. If money were no object, and my intention with the bike was to ride XC marathons, or other endurance-style events, I'd probably pick the Liv. If I was looking for something that was just as fast, but wanted to take it onto rougher trails as a bit of a downcountry bike occasionally (sharp intake of breath) I'd probably go for the Oiz - its frame is rated for use with a 130mm fork, making it that little bit more versatile on the trail end of the spectrum.

As far as pricing goes, Orbea offers more models (eight!) to choose from and is likely to satisfy a wider range of budgets. Looking at the carbon options only, Orbea also has the edge with the value proposition. There is no direct like-for-like spec comparison to be made, but the Orbea Oiz pricing starts at $4,999 USD with the Pique starting at $5,400 USD. While the Liv is very light, the Orbea is a little lighter at a claimed 1,453 grams in a size medium, without the shock, versus the Pique's claimed 1,468 grams in a size small. For those who care: the Oiz does have thru-headset cable routing, while the Pique does not.

I'll throw in the Santa Cruz Tallboy here for an extra comparison. That might seem odd to some, but the truth is that (rightly or wrongly) people often make purchasing decisions based on travel numbers, and the Tallboy and the Pique aren't so different there. The Tallboy has a little more with 120mm rear wheel travel with a 130mm fork, and it is pitched as the downhiller's XC bike. Having ridden it earlier this year, I can say that statement is easily justified. It was the biggest lesson of all on why one shouldn't judge a bike on its travel numbers. On the trail, the two bikes couldn't be more different; the Tallboy will trounce the Pique on any descent thanks to its slacker head angle and more supple, calm feeling suspension platform.

That said it is hefty in comparison to the Liv Pique, with a frame that is almost a kilogram heavier at 2,830 grams. That should tell you all you need to know about the intentions of these two bikes. If you want to go fast and far, choose the Liv. If you want to have a blast riding trails, go for the Tallboy.



Which Model is the Best Value?

The first comment I wish to make: If Barbie became a XCO superstar tomorrow, surely this is the very bike she would choose? I kind of love it, and I kind of hate it at the same time. Two things can be true. Indisputably, the pink and gold sparkly colorway is polarizing. Alas, you can only get it if you choose to part with £11,999 for the Pique Advance 29 0 reviewed here. Similarly, you can only get this bike in the more understated Raw Carbon/Chrome, or Golden Haze/Carbon/Chrome if you are happy to settle for the more financially attainable Advanced 29 1, or Advanced 29 2 models, respectively.

Pique Advanced 29 1 - $7,800
Pique Advanced 29 2 - $5,400

To that end, the Pique Advanced 29 2 is going to be your best bang for buck. Claimed weight is still very respectable at 11 kg in S. You get the same lightweight carbon frame, Fox Performance level suspension components with the 3-pos remote, a Shimano XT mechanical drivetrain and Giant's XCR carbon wheels, and you get an adjustable length dropper seat post (110-140mm) giving more clearance to those who have the legs for it. I would absolutely spend any spare cash I had on upgrading the brakes to the 4-piston Shimano SLX.

Final note: While the Advanced 29 0 model tested here asks you to pay attention to the status of five batteries, the base model Pique has none. Joyous.


Technical Report

Maxxis Aspen tires: These are a highlight, but only really in the context of dry hard pack riding. They look scary with their minimal tread, and they very much are in soft conditions, but they impressed me with how much grip they offered in their intended conditions. They are incredibly fast rolling, making very light work of long stretches of gravel between trails.

Fox 34 SC Fork: The fork tracks well and I found it to have a good range of usable damping adjustment but its fore-aft stiffness is poor. It flexes visibly under hard braking sending vibrations up to the rider.

Remote Lock-Out: I had an issue with the cable routing on the shock's remote lock-out wherein it was locking itself out when the bike was pushed deep into its travel. It seems to be an issue of cable being fed up into the downtube in compression and then getting tugged on the rebound. It caused the plastic cable end-cap to crack. Replacing it with a metal Jagwire one and putting a cable tie onto the cable where it exits the frame solved the issue.

Shimano XTR 2-Piston Brakes: These leave a lot to be desired in terms of power, and I expect a lot riders will want to upgrade to a more powerful 4-piston offering, just as many World Cup XCO racers do. A number of other XC bike manufacturers opt for the small weight penalty of a 4-piston caliper, and I think that's going to be the right decision for most riders.

Liv XC Pro 32mm Foam Grips: While negligible in weight these grips aren't very comfortable. I find holding onto the squishy foam on long descents is quite fatiguing for my hands and forearms.

+ Efficient climber
+ Capable on flowing descents
+ Comfortable for very long rides
+ Reliable components

- Expensive
- Not the most composed on rougher descents
- Not the quietest




Pinkbike's Take

The Pique Advanced 29 0 is a no-holds-barred high-end XC race bike that will delight weight weenies who don't mind charging lots of batteries. Most of the spec has proven reliable and fit for purpose, and there is no denying the thrilling kilometer-eating capacity of this thing. It is fun to ride on smoother trail center terrain but can quickly become a bit of a handful when things get choppy. If I was putting my hand in my pocket for this bike, it would be for its long-distance comfort rather than its capacity to inspire great confidence on technical descents.—Jessie-May Morgan
