The mountain bike world, or at least the portion that dwells in online forums and bike shops, has been spending a lot of time on the topic of pedal kickback lately. Did something change in bike design that suddenly made this a hot topic? Not really, other than the introduction of devices designed to reduce the phenomenon.

DT has joined in with their new DF technology (DF stands for Degrees of Freedom). The system, which is retrofittable to any DT DEG hub, is elegant in its simplicity - it doesn’t add any extra weight, and doesn’t require any more maintenance than a standard freehub would. It still uses DT’s star ratchet system, but the inner ratchet ring has 6 protrusions that interact with the ring that’s threaded into the hub shell. That threaded ring has slots that allow riders to easily select from 0°, 10°, or 20° degrees of movement before the star ratchet system engages.

Deg DF Details

• 3 positions: 0°, 10°, 20°
• 90-tooth star ratchet ring
• Compatible with all DT Ratchet DEG hubs
• Includes ratchet rings, threaded ring, springs, special grease
• MSRP: $176.20 USD ($203.30 with threaded ring removal tool)
• dtswiss.com
When coasting, the spring tension from the star ratchet system pushes the inner ring backwards to its starting position. When the chain is moved forward, whether from pedalling or chain growth, the system moves by that pre-set amount, and then the freehub begins to engage. The ratchet rings have 90 teeth, so at the maximum you're looking at either 14 or 24 degrees of rotation before engagement.

The upgrade kit is priced at $176.20, that is, if you already have the tool needed to remove the ring that threads into the hub shell. If you need the tool, the total price bumps up to $203.30 USD. The kits include two 90-tooth DEG DF star ratchet rings, two springs, the threaded inner ring, and a small tube of DT's special grease.

There's also an e-bike compatible version that uses a 60-tooth ratchet ring and is designed to withstand the higher toque put out by a motor. There are only two settings for that, 0 or 10-degrees.

Only a few parts are required to upgrade a DEG hub. Photo: DT Swiss.


What's pedal kickback again?

Pedal kickback occurs when the chain tugs on the cranks as a bike’s suspension is compressed due to the increasing distance between the rear axle and the bottom bracket, potentially leading to a noticeable harshness underfoot. That’s the very, very simple explanation - a bike’s suspension layout, gear selection, and speed all play a role.

In addition, some of the harshness or feedback that’s transmitted through the pedals isn’t necessarily pedal kickback. Watch any of the Huck to Flat videos from over the years and you’ll see just how much the chain flails around during an impact. There’s a lot going on, even with a clutch-equipped derailleur, and having what’s essentially a steel whip flapping at a high rate of speed influences how a bike feels too.


Wouldn’t a low engagement hub do the same thing?

This question comes up a lot, and it's a valid one. A lower engagement hub is less likely to transmit pedal kickback, but the issue is that it’s not consistent. Sometimes you might have the full 20 degrees of float before the freehub engages, while other times it might engage almost immediately. With the DT Deg DF, the amount of float should remain consistent.


Photo: DT Swiss

Installation

Installation of the DEG DF is extremely easy; the only potential source of difficulty will be removing the original threaded ring from the hub shell. Those suckers can get really, really tight, and a good bench vice or a long breaker bar will be handy to help remove it. Once that ring is removed the new one gets threaded in and the 90-tooth DF ratchet rings replace the old ones - it’s as easy as that.

On the Trail

Testing the DEG DF was fairly simple - being able to turn it off by selecting the 0° position made it easy have a good baseline to start from, and switching to the different float settings only required taking my rear wheel off with an Allen key - no other tools were necessary. Most of my miles were spent with the hub installed on a Santa Cruz Bronson, and I also got in a bunch of late season laps in the Whistler Bike Park with it installed on a Trek Fuel LX.

Does it work? In a word, yes. I wouldn’t consider myself overly sensitive to pedal kickback, but this hub is clearly doing something that changes the feel of the bike underfoot. I ran flat pedals in order to maximize the chances of being able to notice a difference, and chose the chunkiest trails I could find in Whistler’s Creekside zone. After a lap in the 0° setting, which would be the same as running a standard high-ish engagement hub, I swapped over to the 20-degree setting. The difference was very noticeable - the bike felt smoother, and all the micro-tugs on the chain that I didn’t even think I was feeling previously were erased.

It was on the medium speed, stairstep-like sections of trail where I felt the most substantial change. It's a little tricky to put into words, since the hub is removing a sensation rather than adding a new one, but overall the bike felt less busy underfoot, and settled more easily into its travel.

I wouldn’t want to run the 20° setting on a pedaly ride with lots of climbing, since the lag between pushing on the pedals and the engagement point is quite noticeable - it’s not the ideal setup for rides where you’ll be pedaling more than coasting. On a DH bike, though, this would be the setting I’d choose for the maxiumum level of calmness.

The 10° setting is what I’ve spent the most time running, since the engagement is quick enough for technical climbing, and it still calms down the amount of feedback.



DEG DF vs Ochain

A good amount of my riding this past year has been on that aforementioned Bronson with an Ochain installed, which I normally run in the 6° or 9° setting (0, 6°, 9°, and 12° are the options). I was skeptical until I tested it, but I’m convinced that the Ochain has real benefits - there a noticeable the level of calm that it imparts on the overall feel of the bike. It’s almost like running a lower shock or tire pressure; there’s a little bit more give when things get rough.

On one of my testing days I ran the DEG DF hub and an Ochain active spider so that I could easily do back-to-back runs - both devices have an off position, so I could turn the DEF DF to 0° engagement and focus on the Ochain, or turn the Chain to 0° engagement and focus on the DEG.

I did turn both of them on simultaneously for a brief section of trail just to try it out, and that went as expected - it felt like I was running the sloppiest hub in the world, with a huge amount of crank rotation required to get the hub to engage.

Weight: The DEG DG takes this point, since it really doesn’t have a weight penalty, while the Ochain adds around 160 grams.

Price: If you already have a DEG hub, the upgrade kit is $176 USD. The Ochain R is $450, and that doesn’t include the 4-bolt chainring.

Service: There are a lot of small parts in an Ochain, and a rebuild takes time in patience. With the DEG DF, a clean and re-grease takes a matter of minutes.

Chain noise: The Ochain does a better job of quieting down chain noise, and it has a softer feel underfoot than the DEG DF due to the spring and elastomer setup that the chainring rotates on. With the DEG DF, especially in the 20° setting there’s the occasional ‘clunky’ feel when going from open to engaged mode, the same sensation you’d find in a lower engagement hub. With the Ochain, that ‘clunk’ is dissipated by the elastomers, giving an extra level of smoothness.

Verdict: There’s a place in the MTB world for both of these devices. The DEG DF takes the win when it comes to simplicity and relative affordability. For riders looking for the quietest and smoothest feeling setup, price and extra maintenance be damned, the Ochain is the way to go.

If you want to go even further down the rabbit hole of chain-damping devices, Dario recently put together a comparison review of five different options - you can check that out here.

One of the great things about hub-based designs like the DEF DF and the e*thirteen Sidekick is how easy they are to test. In an ideal world, skeptical riders could borrow a wheel from a shop or a friend and try it out on a familiar trail in order to see if they notice a difference. The fact that the DEF DF has a 0° or 'off' setting makes it even easier to evaluate.





Pros

+ Works as intended - the difference in bike feel is noticeable
+ Very low maintenance, extremely simple design
+ No added weight


Cons

- Requires DEG hub (not a con if you already have one)


Pinkbike's Take

The DEG DF upgrade kit is just that - an upgrade on a proven hub design. It doesn't add any extra weight, it's extremely simple, and best of all, it works. The difference will be more noticeable on some bikes than others, but at the end of the day, it's a low-maintenance device that has a tangible benefit when it comes to how settled a bike feels in rough terrain.—Mike Kazimer

kuhsaft (Sep 19, 2025 at 12:21)
 @PacificNorthWes: For high pivot bikes, you definitely want that lower guide. As @fitnj mentioned, without it the chain growth is massive and can easily rip the lower derailleur arm off.

MRP recommends with the MXg, "Rotate the guide counterclockwise as needed to ensure there is 8-10 mm of clearance between the top of the roller and the chainstay. The center of MXg roller should never sit below the BB to rear axle centerline while the bike is in a static position."

High pivot bikes actually have a huge amount of chain growth because the bottom chain path from the chainring to rear axle grows substantially. Think of it as a triangle, theres the axle-to-idler (small growth, what we measure for calculating pedal kickback), idler-to-chainring (consistent), and chainring-to-axle (grows substantially). Without the lower guide, the derailleur stretches from the growth, but, with the lower chain guide higher than that centerline, you can get none to almost no kickback since it'll "give back" some chain as the BB lowers.
