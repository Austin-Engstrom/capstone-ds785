The Fuel has long been the meat and potatoes, bread and butter, or whatever your favorite food-based analogy is in Trek’s MTB catalog. Over the years, though, it’s started to nudge its way into longer travel territory. Back in 2020 it had 130 millimeters of travel, which increased to 140 millimeters in 2022, and now that’s been bumped up to 145 millimeters, and that’s just the starting point.

Trek has decided to turn the Fuel into a bike that can be transformed from an all-round trail bike into a burly enduro / freeride machine by swapping out some links and suspension components. The number of possible combinations can seem overwhelming at first, but there are essentially three base models, and then the offshoots of those models.

The Fuel EX is the bike that Trek expects to be the most popular, with 145mm of rear travel, a 150mm fork, and 29 inch wheels. It’s essentially a slightly updated version of the bike that we named 'Bike of the Year' back in 2022. The next model is the mixed-wheel Fuel MX, which uses a different rocker link and lower shock mount hardware for its 150mm of rear travel.

The final model is the Fuel LX, which uses its own rocker links and the same lower shock links as the EX. This model has 29” wheels and 160mm of travel - it’s essentially a Slash without the high-pivot. For riders that start with one model and get the itch to turn it into something else, rocker link kits will be available for $100, and the lower shock mount kits are $20.

I’ve been putting the miles in on the aluminum-framed Fuel LX 9 EA90, which has a very smart parts spec considering the $5,200 USD price tag. That gets you a 170mm Fox 38 Factory fork, DHX2 factory coil shock, SRAM Maven Bronze brakes, and a cable-actuated Eagle 90 drivetrain. Of course, I ended up getting sucked into the configuration rabbit hole, and my current setup is slightly different from stock, but I’ll get into that in a bit.

Frame Details

The Fuel LX's frame shape starts out fairly similar to the Gen 5 Slash - it even still has that slight hump in the top tube. Yes, I wish it was straight too, purely for aesthetic reasons, but it does provide extra room for a big water bottle, and it’s not noticeable when you’re actually riding the bike, so I’m okay letting it slide. The Fuel now uses a standard zero stack headset, which opens the door for riders to install an aftermarket reach or head angle adjusting headset if the wide array of stock possibilities aren’t enough.

It’s at the seat tube where the frame shape starts to deviate from prior iterations - the seat tube is much steeper, and it's straight, which makes it possible to run longer travel dropper posts - even the size medium will fit a post with 200mm of drop

Other details included an updated frame storage hatch with better sealing, and a revised chainslap protector that works very, very well - this is one of the quieter bikes I’ve been on in recent memory.





Geometry

In its stock configuration the Fuel LX has dual 29” wheels, a 63.4-degree head angle, and a 473mm reach for a size large. Chainstay lengths vary depending on size - they measure 437mm on the small and medium, and then increase by 5mm for the large, XL, and XXL sizes.

Trek adjusted their sizing for the Fuels, ditching the size M/L and then altering the reach figures to ensure that there was a reasonable progression between each size. The biggest change is the increased amount of stack for the larger sizes - the size large has 17mm more stack than the prior model, thanks in part to a 125mm head tube length. More stack makes it easier to achieve an upright, modern riding position without needing to resort to super high rise bars or large stacks of spacers.




Suspension Design

Trek decided to go with different links for each model rather than just swapping shocks in order to ensure the suspension performance matched the bike’s purpose. For that reason, the longer travel models have more progression, giving them a softer initial portion of the travel and more ramp up to help handle those bigger hits.

There are two positions on the lower shock mount, which changes the amount of progression by around 5%. For instance, on the LX shown here, the choice is between 19.1% or 24.2%. Higher progression amounts typically work best with coil shocks, and the slightly lower amount should work well with air, although it still comes down to rider preference - it only takes a couple minutes to adjust the progression, so on-trail experimentation is an easy process.

The LX uses a trunnion-mount 205 x 65mm shock for its 160mm of travel. The Fuel MX and Fuel EX both use a 205 x 60mm stroke shock for their respective 150 and 145mm travel amounts; increasing the stroke length to 65mm without swapping any links results in 163mm of travel for the MX and 156mm for the EX models.

RIDING THE
Fuel

Test Bike Setup

My first few rides were with the Fuel LX set up with dual 29” wheels, the way that it showed up. It wasn’t long before I decided to swap to a 27.5” rear wheel - these days I’m a big fan of mixed wheels for longer travel bikes - it makes a lot of sense in the steep terrain that I prefer.

Trek’s official recommendation for turning this into a long travel, mixed-wheel bike is to swap both the lower shock mount links and the upper rocker links; that’ll keep the geometry fairly similar. There’s also the bonus menu option, that involves running the MX lower shock mount links while keeping the LX rocker links. That creates a very slack 62.9-degree head angle and lowers the bottom bracket substantially. That ended up being my favorite setup, for reasons I’ll go into shortly.

For riders that decide to go that route, I’d recommend 165mm cranks (I wish Trek had spec’d them to begin with on this model).

Other than rear wheel and geometry tinkering I didn’t need to change much in the way of components. I put a DoubleDown casing Maxxis DHRII on the 27.5” rear wheel I used, a step up in puncture protection from the stock EXO+ casing tire, and I swapped out the house-brand grips for ones that made my hands happier. The bars were trimmed to my preferred 770mm length, and I tried a 40mm stem and the stock 45mm stem, eventually settling on the stock length.

A 450 lb spring put me at 27% sag, which worked well for the harder packed, blown out conditions that prevailed - I'd go with a slightly lower spring rate for slippery, softer conditions. I ran 85 psi in the Fox 38 with one volume spacer and the rebound and compression set to my liking.




Testing Info

Most of my testing took place in Bellingham, Washington, and the surrounding area, including a trip up to Hope, BC, to ride the new First Blood trail. I’m always a little skeptical about trails that seem to receive an inordinate amount of internet hype, but I’d say it’s justified in this case - the amount of work that went into carving that trail out of a very steep hillside is impressive, and hopefully it's the start of even more purpose-built trails in the area. The climb is a bit of a grind, but it's not that bad, and I'd way rather be pedaling than crammed into a vehicle with a bunch of other riders.


Climbing

I’m going to avoid harping on the Fuel’s weight too much, but there’s no getting around the fact that this is definitely no lightweight, and that’s with the stock EXO+ tirese - add on some beefier rubber and you’re looking at a 39+ pound bike, and that’s without pedals, tools, or water. The carbon frame version does weigh nearly 3 pounds less, but it’ll also leave your wallet $1,000 lighter.

Heft aside, the Fuel LX stays calm while climbing, and the only time I reached for the climb switch was on paved road segments, and even then that was mainly to have something to do. The higher rise bars and steepish seat angle create a nice spot to settle into for those long grinds. For typical winch and plummet / groan and grin type riding, where a steep dirt road leads to even steeper descents the Fuel isn’t hard to live with - as long as the descent is worth it, the extra weight isn’t the end of the world.

It’s on flatter, more meandering approaches that the extra effort to get the bike moving is noticeable, but that’s also related to the geometry and build kit - the LX model isn’t what I’d recommend for cruising around on blue trails.

On slower speed, technical approaches the Fuel remains very manageable - even when I put it into the extra-slack and low mixed-wheel setting I didn’t need to fight with it to navigate tighter switchbacks or get up rooty steps. Again, all of this is in the context of it being an enduro bike - I don’t want to make it seem like you’ll be snagging KOMs on the climbs, and I also don’t want to undersell the point that it does take extra effort to lug this thing around. That didn’t stop me from taking it on multiple rides with over 4,000+ vertical feet of climbing - I can handle some big big slogs if there's a reward at the end.


Descending

I rode the Fuel in three different configurations - the stock version, with dual 29” wheels, the mixed wheel setup with the MX rocker link and lower shock mount, and in the secret menu option, which entails using the LX rocker link and the MX lower shock mount.

If you’re considering this bike and are planning on using it for steeper, rowdier trails, maybe with some bike park laps thrown into the mix, I’ll cut right to the chase: mixed wheels with the LX upper / MX lower links is the way to go. The lower bottom bracket height and smaller rear wheel makes ripping corners even easier, and the slack head angle gives it a surefooted, stable feel in the steeps.

On paper, 62.9-degrees is quite slack, but somehow the combo of those higher rise bars and a 45mm stem makes it much more manageable than I expected, even on slightly tamer terrain. I tried a 40mm stem for a few rides, but ended up going back to the 45, since it felt like that shifted my weight forward just enough to prevent any front wheel washouts. The increased stack compared to Trek's prior models is a welcome change, especially in the steeps - that puts you in a good attack position above the bike, rather than feeling like you're getting sucked down into the front wheel.


The weight that I griped about in the climbing section isn’t an issue at all while descending; if anything, it helps give the Fuel LX/MX an extra helping of stability. Other than that slack head angle, the other dimensions aren’t outliers - the moderate reach and chainstay length feel well matched, and help keep the bike manageable in twistier sections of trail. It's essentially a freeride bike, at least depending on your definition of that term - to me, freeriding involves rock rolls, jumps, drops, and plenty of steep chutes, and it's on those types of rides that the Fuel LX/MX feels most at home. I'm a big fan of bikes that you can chuck into nasty webs of roots or piles of rocks without worrying too much, and the Fuel LX/MX certainly fits that bill.

I mentioned the low decibel level of this bike earlier, but it’s worth noting again - I was really impressed by how little noise it emitted. No cable rattling, no chain slapping - just point it downhill, hang on, and enjoy the sound of silence. O-Chains and other chain flail / pedal kickback devices have been getting a good deal of attention lately, but the Fuel LX isn't a bike that's immediately calling out for one of those devices to be installed - it's nice and calm underfoot, and even awkward stairstep-like sections didn't unsettle it.

As far as overall suspension performance goes, it was free of any surprises - there’s plenty of ramp up for handling bigger hits, and I only had one harsh bottom out, and that was fully warranted - it happened after an extra-nasty drop into a hole. I wasn’t on the extremes for either the fork or shock, which is a good sign, since it means there’s room for adjustment depending on the trail conditions.

How Does It Compare?

I’m very familiar with the Raaw Madonna, so a comparison between that aluminum machine and the Trek seems appropriate.

When it comes to geometry, both bikes are very adaptable - both can be set up mixed wheel or dual 29”, and can accept angle or reach adjusting headsets. The Raaw’s chainstay length is longer, which I’d say is the most noticeable trait out on the trail. The Madonna has a centered, carvey feel to it; it’s almost like you’re turning it like you would on skis.

The Trek’s chainstays are a little shorter, which can make it easier to perform quick direction changes in the steeps, but the flip side is that there isn’t quite the same locked-in feeling when cornering. For flat out, high speed trails I’d give the slight edge to the Madonna when it comes to stability, but realistically neither bike has much of a speed limit.

Price is where the Madonna falters - it’s around $2,500 USD for the frame only, without a shock; building up a complete Raaw will end up being a good deal more expensive than a Trek. The Raaw’s not exactly a lightweight, but the Trek is even chunkier.


Which Model is the Best Value?

Out of all the alloy Fuel LX models, the base model one easily poses the best value - paying more will get you wireless shifting, but realistically that’s about it. The same goes for the carbon version - the $6,199 USD has essentially the same build as the alloy model here, other than the switch to a carbon bar and carbon-railed seat. It also weighs 4 pounds less… Is it worth spending $250 for each pound saved? That’s between you and your wallet, but no matter what, the base model of each frame material gets my vote.



Technical Report

Maxxis Tires: Trek’s bikes have typically been shod with Bontrager tires, with mixed results. Some versions were decent, others were quite sketchy in the wet and had to be swapped out almost immediately. For that reason, it’s good to see the proven Assegai / DHRII EXO+ tire combo, with MaxxGrip rubber up front and MaxxTerra out back. A DoubleDown casing would have been nice to see, at least for the rear.

SRAM Eagle 90 Drivetrain: The Eagle 90’s shifter feel isn’t anything to get too excited about, but it does the job, and the drivetrain works well - the shifting is very consistent, and even after multiple smacks into rocks and roots the derailleur is still hanging tough. 165mm cranks would also be on my wish list, at least for the LX model, since the bottom bracket height starts getting pretty low if you explore the secret geometry menu options.

Fox DHX2 Coil Shock: The DHX2 received a substantial update earlier this year, and now uses a monotube design with externally adjustable high and low speed compression and rebound. There's no hydraulic bottom out feature, but the Fuel LX's level of progression and the shock's foam bumper kept things feeling nice and smooth, even on bigger hits. The rebound knobs do need to be removed to swap out the spring, which is a minor inconvenience, but most riders will likely only be doing that once or twice.

Pinkbike's Take

The new Fuel lineup might be a little more complicated than it needs to be, but if you sort through the geometry charts and possible builds there are some real gems to be found. The Fuel LX EA90 is no lightweight, but I’ve been consistently impressed by its downhill performance - at its core this is a no-frills freeride / enduro bike that won’t hold anyone back.— Mike Kazimer

 From the consumer side it definitely looks more complex, but from the manufacturing side even with all these options they likely have still reduced their SKU's, what was previously three bikes has now been wrapped into one frame, the only parts that change are the Rocker links and lower shock mounts which are pretty small and easy to manufacture. Especially since it isn't a 1 piece CNC'd rocker link, instead it's two probably forged castings which will be quite a bit easier to make at scale.

Before they had three bikes, the Remedy, Gen 5 Slash and Fuel, each with their own corresponding parts, e.g different rocker links, different chainstays, different seatstays, different frames, etc. Now with the new model, you have one frame, one set of seatstays/chainstays, etc.

Now, with the new platform, there's one frame and one set of seatstays/chainstays. This gives them the ability to scale output to meet demand. For example, if they previously had a bunch of Slashes or Remedys sitting around unsold, that production capacity can now be pivoted to make more Fuels if that's what consumers are after. It makes a lot of sense from a business and manufacturing standpoint. The challenging part of getting that system to work is having the scale to implement it, which a company like Trek obviously has. It also dramatically simplifies warranty procedures as rather than having to carry three different frames and sets of frame specific parts all they need is Fuel/Fuel+ parts.

Honestly I expect more of the big brands to go in this direction. It maintains a wide range of choice for the consumer while reducing the total number of parts that need to be produced. It's similar to how Specialized combined the Stumpy and Stumpy EVO, but this approach feels less compromised. The rider gets much more granular control over the bike's setup with all the built-in adjustment options.
