What happens when a bunch of hardtail enthusiasts build a full-suspension bike? You end up with an over-forked singletrack smasher. The Reazon is Chromag’s latest bouncy bike and, in terms of rear wheel travel, it slots in between two existing models, the Darco and the Lowdown.

The Reazon rolls on two 29” wheels and has 135mm of rear wheel travel, very normal stuff. What’s out there is the 160mm fork at the helm. That’s crazy talk, right? When you point it down the guts of 30 year-old Whistler trails behind Chromag’s headquarters, it feels right at home.
Reazon Details
• Chromoly / aluminum frame
• Wheel size: 29"
• 135 mm rear travel, 160 mm fork
• 4-bar Horst-link suspension design
• 64° head angle
• 78° effective seat angle
• 438 mm chainstays
• Weight: 16.75 kg / 36.93 lb (M/L tested)

Narrow diameter chromoly tubes and industrial edges may not be as luxurious as carbon spaceships, but beauty is in the eye of the beholder. It’s elegant, in a simple way. There are no adjustments to be found, nearly all of the cables are external, and there’s ample mud clearance around the yokes.

Two build kits set the starting price of a Reazon at $7,200 CAD or $5,300 USD. As you’d expect, it’s laced with Chromag hardware where possible and then a gamut of SRAM and RockShox components. That’s not a bad deal, if you’re willing to cart around a slightly hefty ride.

Sometimes, you shouldn't be afraid to be different. The Reazon is unlike any other 135mm-travel trail bike out there, and in the right conditions, it's ideal.— Matt Beer





Frame Details

There’s just something soothing about a chromoly frame. The small diameter tubing offers a muted, calming sensation - the total opposite of some boxy carbon shapes. You get the sense you’re riding smoother lines and being less damaging to your bike.

The rear half is made from aluminum, as is the CNC’d rocker link. Chromag revised the bearing fitment in the aluminum rear end and removed the seatstay bridge for more tire clearance. Now, the hardware pulls the bearings into a seat and does away with the need for a circlip to hold them in place. The bearings have been moved from the rocker to the seatstay at that pivot, but the same style is used throughout the upper portion of the frame. Below, a new locking collet axle deters the pivot bolt from backing out.

All of the specifications are standard issue, such as the 148 Boost hub spacing, UDH dropout, 180 post brake mount, and ISCG tabs. At this time, the Reazon only accepts 29” wheels, but word from Chromag is that an MX rocker is on the way.

Suspension Design

Early full-suspension iterations from Chromag showed potential for another 4-bar Horst-link suspension design, similar to the Specialized Stumpjumper, but since they launched their first squishy bike, they’ve stuck with a more basic layout - a rocker link rotates around a gusseted seat tube pivot to compress a 185x55mm shock.

In terms of kinematics, the leverage ratio starts around 3:1 and follows a swooping arc down to 2:1, giving a supple ride with a consistent mid-stroke and just enough progression to take bigger hits. Chromag says that the Reazon works best with an air shock, but a coil-sprung option isn’t out of the question either. I'm sold on the performance of the Super Deluxe air shock and its tune, though. The rear suspension moves with elastic sense; always active and in motion, never clunking or hanging up.

The anti-squat starts out around 120% and drops down to around 100% once the rider settles into the sag. That’s not as high as some dual-link bikes, but a firm lockout gives the Reazon a helping hand to crush surfaced climbs. On the brakes, there’s a decent amount of anti-rise for this suspension design with the percentage hovering around the low 70s throughout the travel.





Geometry

If you’ve ever considered any of Chromag’s bikes before, you may notice they’ve taken on the stretched out approach. The M/L I’m testing sits in the middle of that spread and has a whopping 485mm reach - a number more true to most brands’ size L. In the range are 5 sizes that are spread out with 20mm gaps in reach, so you may be able to downsize for a more traditional fit.

For other measures, the Reazon is a fairly standard affair with a 64-degree head tube and 78-degree seat tube angle. The stack heights run slightly lower than where modern trends are heading, but it’s not out of the ordinary. Most of that is due to the shorter head tubes rather than a high BB, though.

The Reazon sits between Lowdown (440mm) and Darco (435mm) for travel, but also the chainstay lengths. Wagging behind the rider are 438mm chainstays across the board, which could be considered on the shorter side these days.

You’d better like what Chromag is serving because there are no substitutions on this menu. The Reazon is a set-and-forget bike in the geometry department; however, it is possible to run a 10mm longer or shorter fork.

Test Bike Setup

For the reasons above, no pun intended, this beastly trail bike is simple to set up. Chromag states that the shock can be set with a 25-32% sag window (14-18mm for 55mm shock stroke). I steered towards the upper end of that range where the psi is nearly equivalent to the rider’s weight in imperial mass units. 175 psi was the magic number for me, or about 30% seated sag. The rebound in the Super Deluxe Ultimate was set to -10 from closed, the LSC was closed one click from the mid position, and the HSC in two.

The seemingly unbalanced 160mm fork was pumped up to 90 psi and had two tokens inside. The rebound ran at -13, a few more clicks open compared to what the RockShox Trailhead app suggested for the 200 lb rider weight pressures. On the compression side, the LSC was on 2 clicks and LSC just one.

The bars were cut to 770mm and 15mm sat below the 40mm stem. I toyed with placing another 5mm spacer below the stem but only found that helpful on the steepest of trails.

The Reazon came on a number of big days out, including a full tour of Whistler's best trails outside the bike park. Those consist of everything from weird and scary old-school trails to freeride stunts. After realizing the Reazon's strengths, I thought it would be fitting to drag it along on a trip back to my hometown of St. John's, Newfoundland, where the trails are equally janky, rough, and scenic as Whistler.




Climbing

What’s better for climbing, a heavy bike that tracks the ground or a featherweight that skips around? I guess that depends on what kind of trails you're riding. The Reazon is portly for its travel, that’s for sure, and it’s not going to win any cross-country races, but traction at the rear wheel is superb, and that is worth its weight in gold when you get off the bridlepaths.

Flicking the climb switch on the Super Deluxe shock basically turns the Reazon into a hardtail - the threshold is very firm. Without that switch closed, there’s heaps of traction. This comes with at a small compromise in pedalling support in the open mode, however, it does allow you to stay seated over stutter bumps on rough singletrack.

Compared to Chromag’s Lowdown, their enduro rig, climbing the Reazon isn’t as taxing to navigate. The head tube angle of the Reazon is reigned in and helps maintain solid front wheel tracking. 438mm chainstays are on the shorter side these days, but I found those, in conjunction with the 29” rear wheel, to offer a sweet spot: easy to pivot directions yet staying on line.

You’ll want to keep the movement going, though, because the low-engagement hub and suspension do make stop-start moves a bit clunky. Some of the energy expelled into the cranks is lost by both mechanisms. Ground clearance is another consideration for erring on the side of more pressure in the shock and less than 30% sag.

The best way to speed up the Reazon’s uphill performance would be to switch out the wheels and tires. You could easily drop 300 grams in that zone. Swapping for a faster rolling MaxxTerra compound of the DHR II tire would vastly improve the rolling resistance as well.




Descending

When every other carbon bike could be considered a ball peen hammer, this bike is a rubber mallet. It isn’t as precise, but that can have benefits. On the downhills, it’s hard to feel like you’re ever on the wrong line with the Reazon. Instead of ricocheting off rooty cambers, the bike tends to conform to them. There’s a whole host of other reasons why the Reazon is a singletrack steamroller.

The suspension adds in this bouncy element, keeping the rear wheel flowing without hanging up much. It provides a constant connection of the rear wheel to the ground. No, it doesn’t boast a maze of links or ramble on with bogus marketing claims; it’s just the right amount of everything rolled into a straightforward 4-bar recipe.

Again and again, the Super Deluxe shock delivers. The matchup makes for light action off the top, a smooth progression curve, and excellent bottom-out control. The shock always seems to be in motion. If I didn’t know any better, I might guess that the Reazon has a few more millimeters of travel than stated.

Without a doubt, the big wheels facilitate rolling over sharp edges, too. The caveat here is that the larger diameter hoop out back can take extra effort to initiate into turns. Despite the Reazon having short-ish chainstays, it takes a bit of wrestling to get it to change direction. It could be its long and low nature (both BB and stack height) doesn’t grant much leverage to lean it over, however, a taller bar height can help here.

On top of all that, the blanketed ride quality of the chromoly frame soaks up vibrations and makes you think you’re riding smoother than you are. Besides the odd ting from a rock flicking up and hitting the narrow diameter downtube, the Reazon is a silky smooth operator.

But isn’t chromoly flexy? I’d say it has a softer feel, yes, but it’s not unsettling - and remember, the rear triangle is aluminum. I think riders making those claims often confuse flex with the damped feedback from the frame. And that can be a positive attribute. It’s a soothing ride when you’re clobbering down a rock and root-infested forest floor.





Technical Report

Chromag Phase 30 wheels: Between a few tours around Whistler and a week in Newfoundland, aluminum met sharp granite multiple times. The rims withstood their fair share of strikes, and rightfully should because they aren’t light at 2,160g. Nothing is bulletproof and the spokes did need more attention than most alloy wheelsets. And if you’re the corner slapper, you’d better put a few extra psi in there because they can burp air easily.

SRAM 90 Eagle Drivetrain: The battery haters have been silenced - SRAM now has that clever Transmission chain-cassette timing available in cable form. The system is much crisper than GX Eagle (non-Transmission spec), but it does take more “ummf” on the lever to make that shift happen.

Chromag Trailmaster saddle: This big, cushy couch of a saddle has a long, ultra-wide nose. If needed, it allows the rider to slide forward, placing more weight on the front wheel to tackle the steepest climbs without sitting on an uncomfortable knife-edge. That being said, this zone could be too wide for smaller riders as they may hit their thighs on it while pedalling in their regular seated position.




Which Model is the Best Value?

First of all, I feel that steel frames are often misjudged as being cheap, or homemade, which isn’t untrue - it’s an easier material to work with. The tubing can’t be shaped into as sophisticated shapes as carbon (or even aluminum for that matter), but this is a quality frame nonetheless. It’s been designed and built with purpose and that purpose is to hit hard.

Another sticking point for steel frames is the price-to-weight ratio. In this case, it’s not great, but not the end of the world. A size M/L Reazon frame, without a shock, weighs in at 4.3 kg (9.5 lb).

When talking about the build options more specifically, there are just two choices: the premium SRAM X0 at $9450 CAD / $7300 USD and the SRAM 90, which is considerably less for $7200 CAD / $5300 USD.

The alternative is to take matters into your own hands and start from scratch with a frame/shock kit for $3600 CAD / $2750 USD.

In my eyes, the build that we reviewed makes the most sense. SRAM’s 90 T-type drivetrain gives you the benefits of the T-type shifting, but in a more pragmatic approach that aligns with the nature of the Reazon.

After that, you can’t get any better in the RockShox house with Ultimate suspension at both wheels (unless you count Flight Attendant), plus sharp Maven Silver brakes and a matching assortment of Chromag aluminum finishing kit.

How Does It Compare?

You’d think that heavy-weight, short-travel trail bikes might be far and few between, but there a a number of them, including the Raaw Jibb, Commencal Tempo, and Privateer 141. They’re all made of metal, can take a thrashing, and are usually pushed past their comfort zone.

I’ve spent the most time on the Privateer 141, which has more even travel numbers than the Reazon; 6mm more rear travel and a shorter 150mm fork. The entire frame is aluminum, it can accept either rear wheel diameter, and has a chainstay length adjustment.

In terms of geometry, the 141 is in a similar camp, but perhaps a touch more square: the stack is taller, and the head angle is a half-degree steeper. The Reazon is drastically different. It carries on with Chromag’s low, stretched out front end geometry.

Maybe the greatest factor separating the two is the suspension performance, though. The 141 starts with a high leverage ratio and becomes very progressive. I had trouble finding a sweet spot for air pressure, often feeling underequipped or over-sprung, whereas the Reazon was dead simple to setup and get along with in all scenarios.

Choosing which to lay down the cash for may depend on the region you live in. Talking cold, hard American cash, the 141 is slightly cheaper, at $4,949 USD. The build kit is sensible with powerful Hayes brakes, strong wheels made by Hunt, and a popular tire combo from Maxxis. One sticking point is the lack of a UDH, which Privateer passed on in favor of the chainstay flip-chip. That sticking point alone may be enough to sway consumers towards the Reazon for future-proofing.

+ Quiet
+ Calm and comfortable ride quality
+ Suspension performance delivers

Pinkbike's Take

From a company known for their hardtail backbone, the Reazon takes the sting out of the trail. It’s calm, comfortable, and quiet. The top-notch suspension performance of the Super Deluxe and leverage rate combo almost tack on another 10mm of travel, it’s that good.

The Reazon’s chunkier build and unorthodox front-to-rear travel pairing will suit the type of rider who prefers to constantly push a short-travel bike down trails that it shouldn’t be on. — Matt Beer
