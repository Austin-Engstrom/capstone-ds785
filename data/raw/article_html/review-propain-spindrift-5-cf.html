The requirements for a "daily driver" vary wildly depending on where and how you ride. On one end of the spectrum you have sporty short travel bikes built to cover ground, and on the other reside the big dogs - long travel pedal rigs meant to handle just about anything. The Propain Spindrift is up there with the longest travel bikes out there shy of a full-on DH rig, but targets efficiency and liveliness despite the long-travel figures.

Spindrift Details

• 180mm travel, 180-190mm fork
• Carbon fiber frame (alloy bike differs)
• Mixed or 29" wheels

Frame Details

Propain took the 4th generation Spindrift and refined some key details for the 5th iteration of the bike. In-frame storage, cable clamps, and multiple routing options are on the table, along with wheel size adjustment and an accessory mount. The frame storage hatch isn't the largest opening I've seen, but is still large enough to house a pump and accessories.

For those who hate their mechanic or love a tidy looking cockpit, you can route the cables through the headset. For those who want to stick with the tried and true method, there's fully-internal cable routing as well. The latter option has entry and exit clamps to keep things quiet, as an added bonus. Regardless of which option you go with, the bike comes with stainless steel headset bearings, a premium option that's nice to see on a high end bike.

All of the frame bearings are double sealed, with robust covers that should keep grit and grime at bay. A threaded bottom bracket, 31.6mm seatpost, and UDH rear hanger keep maintenance and parts availability easy, too.

Locked-in cable routing.
Nicely guided through the frame.

There's a cleverly designed rear brake mount that allows you to run 180mm or 200mm post mount, without an adapter. A small detail, but very clean and well thought out.

The rear wheel size can be adjusted via a flip chip at the seatstay rocker pivot, and is a fairly easy job if the bike is in a stand. Four keyed spacers hold the pivot axle in place, and can be flipped to the alternate position in only a few minutes.

Though there is an alloy version of the bike, the details, kinematic, and even geometry numbers differ enough to warrant a different deep dive. To that end, all my thoughts here are more relevant to the carbon bike, not the aluminum alternative.


Geometry

The last generation of Spindrift kept things lively with a 64.5° head tube angle, and neutral reach numbers for a given size. The new model slackens out by a full degree, and reach numbers increase by about 10mm per size. The stack heights have actually decreased a bit for the new generation, likely due to the fork raking out and taking away from that front end height. This is all comparing carbon to carbon, as the alloy frame does differ slightly.

For any given size, the alloy frame is slightly shorter in reach and steeper in head angle, to give the alloy bikes a more agile feel. The carbon frame has a 78° seat tube angle across all sizes, at an assumed pedaling height. Chainstay lengths are 445mm across all sizes, and seemingly isn't effected by changing the flip chip position.


I've ridden both the Large and Extra Large Spindrift, and found that the Large was my preferred size. The XL felt a bit long for my preference, and was also a bit awkwardly tall in terms of standover and seat tube length. That standover figure is a good bit higher than equivalent bikes in this travel range, and is worth keeping in mind as a sizing parameter. The same goes for the seat tube length, as that dimension may limit your dropper post length quite a bit if sizing up.


Suspension Design

The Spindrift uses Propain's PRO10 suspension system, which utilizes two counter-rotating links to drive a floating shock. Every build uses a 230x65mm shock to deliver 180mm of rear wheel travel.

Like the geometry, the kinematics vary slightly by frame material, with the alloy bike sporting slightly higher anti-squat and a more progressive leverage curve. Both bikes have unusual anti-squat characteristics, with the percentage increasing as you move through the travel, then falling off sharply at around 60% of travel.

Test Bike Setup

There's a case to be made for integrating all sorts of adjustment into a bike, but on the other hand I'm always happy to just hop on a frame that has one setup to hone in. The Spindrift was an easy one, thanks to a bang-on coil weight and a fork I'm familiar with. I ran a 500# coil for the duration of the test, which put me right around my ideal 28% sag target. I pressurized the 180mm Fox 38 to 106psi, with one volume spacer installed.

Thanks to the a-la-carte nature of Propain's ordering process, there weren't too many sticking points when it came to the build kit of the review bike. I essentially went with the Factory CF build, but swapped the XX drivetrain for the X0 variant. The one element I didn't have a say on was the tire spec, hence the EXO+ Assegai out front. I ran it for the majority of the test, but I'd be a little hesitant to opt that casing on a bike with intentions like the Spindrift's.

Testing Info

The Spindrift's travel numbers may seem to indicate a bike that requires the nastiest terrain to shine, but I was surprised to find that it's pretty happy just about everywhere. Folks living in areas with legit features and mixed terrain could probably justify it as a do it all one bike, thanks to the pedaling quality and easy to live with frame.



Climbing

The Spindrift doesn't just climb well for a 180mm bike, it holds its own against some of the short-travel trail bikes I've tested. By no means is the coil-sprung long travel rig going to fool you into signing up for an XC race on it, but it's far from frustrating to pedal all day on mellower trails. More appropriately, it makes sizable pedals to access gnarly terrain a far easier proposition than expected, thanks to a supportive and even-keeled pedaling characteristic, especially on road grades.

Technical climbing feels impressive as well, but can require a bit of a learning curve to intuit how the bike reacts to drivetrain inputs and shifts in body weight. Likely due to the rather high anti-squat numbers, the Spindrift stands up tall when you're putting power through the pedals, and can feel lurchy in awkward tech climbs if you're not keeping an even cadence.

Again, I found this was something you could begin to intuit pretty quickly, but compared to more compliant long travel bikes that just hum along and soak up the bumps through a climb, the Spindrift was a bit more energetic and demanding. I think this trait is offset by the general efficiency, making for a positive overall feel. Rear wheel traction isn't quite as stellar as some softer-feeling bikes in this category, but again, that's the tradeoff for a firm feel when on the pedals. I never once used the climb switch on this bike, which is quite impressive for something this leggy.



Descending

The Spindrift occupies an unusual corner of the bike world, with a lot of travel and pedaling characteristics that don't really fit that mold. A lot of people would probably expect this bike to be an absolute plow on the trails, but in keeping with the climbing performance the descending feels a bit more efficient and taut than you'd expect. I'll be saying it a lot here, but the Spindrift offers a ton of support through the travel, which lends a lively feel to the bike, even on mellower trails.

Though that support keeps things lively and fun, it can bite back a bit if you're really trying to push the bike through rougher terrain. I found that on trails that serve up quick successive compressions, the Spindrift could feel a bit unsettled when you really pushed through the feet. I'd chalk this up to the fairly high anti-squat number more than anything specific to the shock, as the sensation was akin to the chain tug you get on some shorter-travel bikes. That said, the Spindrift can still carry plenty of speed through the chop, it's just biased a bit towards efficiency.

Propain didn't change the geometry of the Spindrift much when compared to the last generation, and for the most part I think that's the right call. Reach and head angle are quite good for the intended purpose, the stack is solid if a little low, the bottom bracket is low enough, and the seat angle feels ideal for the bike's purpose. If I could change anything, it would be the rear center length and the standover / seat tube length. As I mentioned in the sizing section above, the standover might keep people from sizing up on the bike, and the seat tube length will limit dropper stroke for most riders out there. Though the Large bike felt quite balanced front to rear, I found the XL to be a bit short out back for how much the front center grows - scaled chainstays would definitely help.


The bike is remarkably quiet on the way down the hill, with cables, drivetrain, and in-frame storage all nicely handled by smartly integrated details. All of those little details are very cleanly executed, and it makes it a breeze to maintain through the wet and dust alike. Long term ownership feels like a safe bet here, thanks to well-shielded bearings and easily accessed components.

How does it compare?

It's easy to get hung up on travel numbers, but to me anything in the 170/180mm range should be targeting the same things. Stability at speed, forgiving suspension feel on big hits, and traction should all be plentiful on bikes in this category, and I think these two options pose an interesting comparison on a few of those points.

The Spindrift's supportive suspension is what gives it such efficient climbing chops, but the Slash doesn't fall too far behind. As a benefit of the high pivot suspension layout, the bike can lean on higher anti-squat values without a tradeoff on the descents, and a straight seat tube makes for a great pedaling position regardless of seat height. That's one of the Spindrift's weak points, as the seat tube length and standover will keep some from running as long a dropper as they should be able to on a bike ostensibly focused on the descents.

On the way down, the Slash's composure and bump eating performance certainly win on rough and natural terrain, but the Spindrift offers pop and support where you wouldn't expect it. If your riding errs on the mellower side of things and you just want the long travel for forgiveness or clout, then I'd opt for the Propain. If you can tolerate extra drivetrain maintenance and some idler noise on the climbs, then the Trek is the choice for going fast and hitting nasty lines.


Which Model is the Best Value?

To me, this is one of Propain's major selling points, with custom build options allowing you to select from myriad parts for each and every component. There isn't grotesque upcharge for those items either, negating the buy cheap and upgrade method that often applies to buying complete bikes. It's worth playing with the configurator, just for the lust factor alone.

For those who don't want to parse out each and every component on the bike, I'd go with the Swedish Gold build. I really like the component selection on my Factory-ish build, but for $2,500 less you'll have an undeniably excellent bike with robust and high performance parts. The Öhlins damping is up there with the best, and the GX T-Type drivetrain and Saint brakes will keep on trucking through bike park season and beyond.


Technical Report

Magura MT7 Brakes: I've long been a fan of the tunable power you can get out of the MT7, and still consider them one of the better performing brakes on the market. The finicky nature of keeping them in great shape isn't for everyone, and the plastic lever architecture remains a weak point, but if you keep them happy they'll keep you happy. I tend to prefer the sharpness and ergonomics of the HC-W "Loic" lever, but the HC-3 is nothing to complain about. The Performance pads have a decent wear life and great bite, but if you want all-out stopping power, it's hard to beat the Race compound.

Fox DHX2 Shock: The Fox DHX2 is a great damper, with a wide range of adjustability that can cater to all sorts of rider preference and terrain requirements. Some folks may get lost in the sauce with so many parameters to play with, but if you take the time to learn what each adjuster can accomplish, you can achieve something really impressive. Sadly, there was one hitch with the shock on this bike, in the form of a noticeable topout clunk. I warrantied this with Propain, and they sent a replacement out, but unfortunately that replacement had an even more pronounced topout. This is likely due to a bad build on Fox's end, as the DHX2 has some finicky steps to get things right. Another warranty should alleviate the problem, but also shouldn't be necessary.

I tested the bike with some other coil shocks to make sure that the frame wasn't the culprit, and found that topout wasn't an issue with other options installed.

Crankbrothers Synthesis Carbon Wheels: I really like the feel of these wheel, and have been impressed by the durability across every set I've spent time on. The stock Crankbrothers hubs aren't the highest engagement, but I think they're enough for most use cases. On this build, the wheels came with Industry Nine Hydra 1 hubs, which I'd love to see swapped out for the far-better Hydra 2. Those are still fairly new, so the inventory may take time to shift over, but the improved durability and drag of the new design is a huge step in the right direction.



Pros

+ Remarkably efficient climber
+ Handles big hits nicely, stays fun
+ High quality frame hardware, weather sealing


Cons

- Awkward tech climbing characteristics
- Standover and seat tube length limit sizing
- Rear center does not scale with size


Pinkbike's Take

The Spindrift is a bit of a paradox, with travel numbers that say one thing and a ride feel that says another. Propain delivered a long travel pedal bike that feels efficient and lively, even on mellower trails. You have to give up a bit of capability on the gnarliest descents, but ultimately the tradeoff makes for a unique and fun package. —Dario DiGiulio
