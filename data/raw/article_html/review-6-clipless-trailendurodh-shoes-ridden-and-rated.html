Picking the right pair of riding shoes for your needs can make a big difference to your riding. Shoes must find a balance of stiffness, weight, durability, protection, comfort and traction - both on and off the bike. Each shoe will have different biases and each rider will have different priorities.

With so many options on the market, we set out to find which shoes offer the best all-round performance. We're looking at shoes aimed at anything from trail to enduro and gravity riding here, so don't expect any carbon soles or toe studs. These shoes would typically be used with 140 mm travel bikes and up. They should all work for everything from enduro and downhill racing to casual trail riding and the odd bit of hike-a-bike.

I've been testing six pairs of clipless shoes over the last year to help you pick out the best options. If flat pedals are more your thing, Jessie wrote a roundup of those recently.


Tester: Seb Stott
Location: Tweed Valley, Scotland
Height: 6'3" / 191cm
Weight: 187 lbs / 85 kg, kitted
Shoe Size: UK 11 / EU 46
Preferred riding style: Trail, enduro & eMTB
Preferred pedal: Crankbrothers Mallet E or Mallet DH


What to look for

Shoe stiffness is a major talking point and a big differentiator between XC-style shoes and the more gravity-biased ones we have here. But a recent study suggests a stiffer shoe doesn't necessarily help with power delivery or pedalling efficiency after all. Stiffer shoes can feel more comfortable on long descents, especially when used with a smaller pedal platform, whereas more flexible soles may make it easier to locate the pedal mechanism when clipping in and make life easier when walking. In general, I'd suggest a stiffer sole if you favour smaller platform pedals or a more flexible one for broader pedals. If you plan to carry or push your bike over uneven ground, a flexible sole is your friend.


For gravity-focussed riders, the position of the cleat is important, with more room to adjust cleat position fore and aft and laterally being a bonus. A recent poll revealed that a significant fraction of Pinbkbike readers are setting their cleats as far back as they'll go for trail and enduro riding. Fortunately, the current crop of gravity shoes all make it possible to adjust the cleats much farther back than many shoes did a few years ago. Most people should be able to put their cleats where they want them with all of these shoes.


The closure mechanism is an important consideration too. You want something that holds the foot securely while remaining comfortable and adjustable. Boa dials, Velcro straps and good-old-fashioned laces all have pros and cons, with the former generally being the most expensive and most fragile, while laces are cheap and replaceable but aren't as good for fine-tuning the fit.

The outsole (tread) should offer plenty of grip when walking over soft and hard ground, or even when riding unclipped over rough terrain. But the tread mustn't get in the way when it comes time to clip back in.

Fit is another consideration. I have relatively normal-proportioned feet and I didn't have any issues with comfort or shape with any of these shoes. If you have unusually wide or narrow feet there's no substitute for trying before buying.

How the shoes were tested

To keep things simple and fair, I tested all six shoes with the same pedals - Crankbrothers Mallet DH. I have used several of these shoes with Shimano pedals too, and generally, what works with one will work with another. Even a Shimano shoe can be used with a Crankbrothers pedal and visa-versa. It's not like Apple and Android.

I've had several of these shoes on test for over a year, with plenty of regular ride time. I've also tested them head-to-head on the same day to make the differences more apparent, even riding with different shoes on each foot for the most direct comparison possible.


I also measured what I'm calling the cleat channel width (left) and cleat slot length (right) to put some objective numbers on the adjustment range of the cleat position. You can also get a sense of this by looking at the photo above underneath the video, which shows the cleats in my preferred position.

Fizik Gravita Versor

BUY AT:
MIKE'S BIKES (USA)

The Gravita is designed to cater to everything from trail riding to downhill, but the shoes feel more casual and less secure than the rest. The cut is low and the upper material is thin, making the ankle and upper foot more exposed to rock strikes and debris. The ripstop fabric feels hardwearing despite these being the lightest shoes on test, but there's noticeably less padding around the upper foot.
Fizik Gravita Versor Details
• Closure type: laces
• Weight: 1005 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 36-48 EU
• Cleat channel width: 41 mm
• Cleat slot length: 32 mm
• MSRP: $149.99 USD
• More info at fizik.com


The laces do a decent job of securing the foot for sprinting on the bike, although there is some heel lift when walking up steep slopes and the foot feels less locked-down than other shoes. There is a version with an extra Velcro strap (the Gravita Tensor), which should resolve this issue.

The cleat slot isn't the longest but it has just enough adjustment to get the cleats where I wanted them. The sole's low-profile tread and tapered cleat channel make it easy to clip in and out, and the shoes grip the pedals nicely when not yet clipped in. Walking on hard surfaces is very comfortable, although the tread is not well suited to mud. The sole is on the flexier side, which makes it easier to feel what's going on under your feet, but adds to the casual feel.

Pros
+ Lightweight
+ Decent grip on pedals and hard ground
+ Flexible sole is good for walking and clipping in
Cons
- Low cut and thin upper leave feet exposed
- Laces allow for some heel lift



Northwave Enduro Mid 2

BUY AT:
BACKCOUNTRY (USA)

In contrast to the Fizik, these shoes feel robust and secure, with exceptional ankle protection and a padded upper. This lends them to people who ride in loose, rocky terrain. By the same token, these are the heaviest shoes on test - over 200 g heavier than the lightest. That's not especially noticeable on the bike but it can be when walking, and it represents a weight difference that would be considered substantial when comparing components.

Enduro Mid 2Details
• Closure type: SLW X-Dial and Velcro strap
• Weight: 1213 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 38-48
• Cleat channel width: 40 mm
• Cleat slot length: 37 mm
• MSRP: $197.99 USD
• More info at northwave.com


The Northwave SLW X-Dial works similarly to the more common Boa system; it holds the foot securely alongside the Velcro strap and allows quick and easy adjustments. It's not covered by a no-questions-asked warranty like the Boa system, but at £11 for a pair of dials, it's not expensive to replace if needed. There's minimal heel lift and the tread grips well in the mud. Unfortunately, the rubber compound is slightly slippery on hard, wet surfaces like roots and rocks.

The sole is stiff by the standards of this test and quite thick too; this creates a numb feeling when walking on uneven ground or when locating the pedal to clip back in. The cleat channel allows the cleats to be set very far back in the shoe, but the rubber sole gets in the way of the pedal when the cleats are set all the way back in the slot, making it harder to clip in. Nevertheless, I was still able to get the cleats in the position I wanted them (as pictured) with no issues getting clipped in.

There is a slightly concave section under the ball of the foot and a slightly raised arch in the middle, which feels strange at first but I found the fit very comfortable.

Pros
+ Secure closure system
+ Robust and protective upper
+ Tread grips well in soft ground
Cons
- Hard compound and stiff sole aren't ideal on slippery hard surfaces
- Heavy



Fox Union Boa

BUY AT:
COMPETITIVE CYCLIST (USA)

These shoes are the most race-focussed here. They have a stiff sole and firm, unyielding upper; with dual Boa dials allowing for a particularly snug fit. The cleat channel is wide and long, making it possible to get the cleats very far back without any issues getting clipped in. It's hard to measure this precisely, but I'd say the cleats can be set further back on these than any other shoe on test - much further back than I would want them. They have a tough toe box and some extra protection around the side of the shoe, although the inner ankle bone is more exposed than with some other shoes.

Fox Union Boa Details
• Closure type: Dual Boa
• Weight: 1148 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 37-47
• Cleat channel width: 42 mm
• Cleat slot length: 36 mm
• MSRP: $249.95 USD
• More info at foxracing.com


They're heavy and stiff and the tread isn't optimised for walking, so there are better options if you plan to mix your DH racing with more casual riding, especially if a hike-a-bike is on the cards.

While Boa dials are replaceable and covered by a generous guarantee, they are prone to damage in my experience and having four dials per pair increases the chances of damage. Moreover, my pair developed a tear in the sewn-in lace guide on the opposite side of the shoe from the ratchet. The mechanism still works, but this part isn't replaceable if it wears through altogether.

The lace-up version (£160), which has a thick Velcro strap for a secure fit, is likely a better bet for durability and value.

Pros
+ Super secure fit
+ Lots of protection in the toe and side

Cons
- Dual Boa closure may invite repairs and mine are starting to wear through
- Stiff sole is not ideal or walking
- On the heavy side



Endura MT500 Burner

BUY AT:
BACKCOUNTRY (USA)

These shoes come with two sets of laces. This could be used to fine-tune the fit in the front and rear parts of the shoe separately, but the second set is really a spare or an alternate option to choose between a muted or funky colourway. They can be tucked away under the Velcro strap, which also helps to secure the fit after the laces are tied. This is a practical and reliable system which holds the foot securely, although it takes a bit longer to get the fit just right and stash the laces neatly when compared to Boa dials.

Endura MT500 Burner Details
• Closure type: Dual laces and Velcro
• Weight: 1092 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 38-47
• Cleat channel width: 47 mm
• Cleat slot length: 40 mm
• MSRP: $159.99 USD
• More info at endurasport.com


The fit is snug, with no heel lift and a locked-in feel once you've got the laces sorted. There's generous padding to keep your ankle bone protected, yet they remain impressively mid-pack weight-wise. The cleat channel is especially roomy, making it easy to position the cleat where you want it. In fact, there's more room to adjust cleat position than any other shoe here, although like with the Northwave, the tread gets in the way with the cleat set as far back as it goes. There's still more than enough space to get the cleat in a very rearward position where it's easy to clip in and out.

The tread offers excellent grip on all surfaces and the sole is a nice balance of stiffness and flex, making it easier to locate the pedal underfoot.

The ergonomic EGM insole feels odd at first. The raised metatarsal button in the middle of the shoe feels a bit like there's a lollipop stick running laterally across the middle of the shoe. Endura have big claims for these insoles but I found that while I got used to it after a while and found the shoes comfortable, I didn't notice any particular benefit. You could always swap them for an ordinary insole if they bother you.

Pros
+ Secure foot-hold once laces are sorted
+ Good ankle protection and walking grip
+ Flexible sole and big cleat slot make it easy to clip in
+ Well-priced (even for a lace-up shoe)
Cons
- Ergonomic insole feels strange at first
- Laces take longer to adjust than Boa dials



Crankbrothers Mallet Trail Boa

BUY AT:
EVO (USA)

While Crankbrothers' latest clipless shoe is nominally aimed at trail riders, it is in many ways an improvement over the original Mallet DH and Enduro shoes. They are slightly lighter, though there's only 68 g in it on my scales, and they are noticeably stiffer, but this doesn't seem to detract from walking comfort or on-pedal feel.

Mallet Trail Boa Details
• Closure type: Boa and Velcro strap
• Weight: 1018 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 37-49
• Cleat channel width: 41 mm
• Cleat slot length: 34 mm
• MSRP: $219.99 USD
• More info at crankbrothers.com


The tread offers extra grip for walking at the toe and heel, but this doesn't affect the pedal area so clipping in is just as slick. The cleat channel is even wider and the cleat slots run far enough back that gravity riders should still be able to get the cleat where they want. The Trail shoe's upper has a slightly less padded feel, making it easier to get a firm hold, but there is still some ankle protection. The shoes feel comfortable and secure on or off the bike, but being fussy, the Velcro straps are a bit too long and stick out when tightened down firmly.

They come with Crankbrothers cleats pre-installed, which speeds installation and makes the value proposition a little sweeter for Crank Brothers pedal fans. However, I still prefer to add an extra cleat spacer underneath for easier engagement, as I do with most of the other shoes. I can confirm they play nicely with Shimano Saint pedals, too, just as Shimano's shoes do with Crankbrothers pedals.

These shoes are still expensive, and I broke the Boa dial by catching it on a protruding root. You can get a replacement for free under Boa's lifetime guarantee, but it'll take longer to fix than laces. A lace version is available but, at £170, it's still expensive and lacks a Velcro strap for a secure hold.

Pros
+ Plenty of room to adjust cleat position and easy access with CB or Shimano cleats
+ Good walking grip and pedal feel
+ Secure hold
+ Lightweight
Cons
- A Boa dial broke on my shoes



Shimano GE9

BUY AT:
COMPETITIVE CYCLIST (USA)

These are the kind of shoes you happily forget about while riding. They don't have any strange features or quirks; they just work.

They have a relatively flexible sole and soft upper padding, but not so soft that they feel vague. They have a decent amount of protection but they're respectably light.

Shimano GE9 Details
• Closure type: Boa and Velcro
• Weight: 1034 grams (EU 46)
• Sizes: 38-48
• Cleat channel width: 41 mm
• Cleat slot length: 27 mm
• MSRP: $225 USD
• More info at shimano.com



The flexible sole and tread make them especially comfortable for walking, with good grip in all conditions and minimal heel lift. When pedalling and sprinting on the bike, I didn't notice any downsides when compared to the stiffer-soled shoes on test. The cleat slot is the shortest on test but it's well-placed. There's enough room to move the cleat back for gravity-focussed riding and I can't imagine many people will want to position it further forward than the slot allows. The extra-long channel running the length of the sole makes it easy to find the pedal mechanism - even with pedals from Shimano's rival pedal brand. The cut-out in the middle of the foot also offers somewhere for the "eggbeater" mechanism to go when your foot lands too far forward on the pedal, making it easier to push on during one of those "ride it out" moments.

They are expensive and Boa dials are a potential point of failure (though I had no issues in this case), but the cheaper GE7 is available for those who prefer laces, and it retains the velcro strap for secure fit.

Pros
+ Supremely comfortable yet secure-feeling
+ Grippy and compliant sole, whether on the pedal on the ground
+ Easy to locate and engage the pedal mechanism
Cons
- Small (but well-positioned) cleat adjustment window



What did we learn?

At the risk of sounding like an industry shill, all these shoes are pretty good. One of my first jobs as a bike journalist in 2015 was a shoe group test and I remember drilling out cleat slots and cutting the rubber around the cleat channel with a Stanley knife to get them to fit. These days, most shoes in the trail/gravity category work great out of the box - even if you like to set your cleats quite far back as I do. The only thing you may need to play with is cleat spacers and/or pedal pins to get the contact force between the pedal and shoe to your liking.

That's not to say there's nothing separating them.

A common theme is the choice of closure system. Boa-style ratchet dials are the quickest to take on and off or adjust tension, but two of the shoes in this test had issues with them. One required a replacement dial, which was free under Boa's lifetime warranty but isn't quick or easy to replace, while another suffered non-fatal but non-repairable damage to the lace guide opposite the dial. Laces may be the smart-money choice, ideally in combination with a Velcro strap to quickly cinch up the uppers on the fly. Some of the shoes on test have cheaper alternatives like this.

The low-cut Fizik Gravita feels exposed around the ankle and upper foot. The Northwave Enduro Mid 2 is heavy and a little slippery underfoot on hard, wet surfaces. The Fox Union is great for DH duties but its stiff sole and lack of toe tread mean it lacks versatility, and mine developed some damage on the Boa lace guide too. The Endura MT500 Burner is quirky with its ergonomic insoles, but you can replace these if they don't suit you. If you can live with laces it's a great all-round shoe with good protection at a fair price. The Crankbrothers Mallet Trail Boa is light, stiff and secure on the foot, without losing any of the downhill capabilities of the Mallet DH shoe. But the Boa dial broke on my set. The lace-up version is still expensive and lacks a Velcro closure. That leaves the Shimano GE9. It's a similarly impressive all-rounder to the Mallet Trail but with a slightly softer upper and more compliant sole, which makes it great for walking with little or no compromise on the bike. The GE7 is probably the smart money choice as it swaps the Boa for laces but keeps the Velcro strap.

The Shimano GE9 would be my top pick, but any of the top three are well worth considering. The Endura MT500 Burner would be my value choice, as it's both well-priced and should be reliable with its doubled-up laces instead of ratchet dials.
